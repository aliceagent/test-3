# The Sassoon Family: Jewish Moguls Who Built Shanghai

---

Walk through Shanghai today and you'll see gleaming skyscrapers, luxury hotels, and a skyline that rivals New York or Hong Kong. But look closer at the historic Bund—the famous waterfront boulevard—and you'll notice elegant colonial-era buildings bearing names like "Sassoon House" and "Cathay Hotel." These architectural treasures tell the story of a remarkable Jewish family whose vision and enterprise helped transform Shanghai from a modest port into one of Asia's greatest commercial centers.

The Sassoons—sometimes called the "Rothschilds of the East"—built a business empire spanning from Baghdad to Bombay to Shanghai, becoming one of the most influential Jewish families in Asian history. Their story is one of exile and opportunity, East-West cultural synthesis, and the complex legacy of colonialism and trade.

## From Baghdad to Bombay: The Sassoon Origins

The Sassoon story begins not in China but in Baghdad, Iraq, where the family had been prominent for generations.

### David Sassoon: The Patriarch

**David Sassoon** (1792-1864) was born into a wealthy and scholarly Baghdad Jewish family that served as treasurers (*sarraf*) to the Ottoman governors. His father, Sheikh Sassoon ben Saleh, was the chief treasurer and leader of Baghdad's Jewish community.

The family lived comfortably, deeply rooted in Jewish tradition and Middle Eastern culture. David received extensive Jewish education, mastering Hebrew, Aramaic, and Talmud, while also learning Arabic and Persian for business.

But in 1829, everything changed. A new Ottoman governor, Dawud Pasha, began persecuting prominent families and seizing their wealth. The Sassoons, wealthy and visible, became targets. Sheikh Sassoon was imprisoned, and though eventually released, the family's safety was shattered.

David Sassoon fled Baghdad in 1829, eventually settling in **Bombay (Mumbai), India**, then under British colonial rule. He was around 37 years old, an exile starting over in a foreign land.

### Building an Empire in India

Rather than seeing exile as tragedy, David Sassoon saw opportunity. Bombay was emerging as a major trading port under British rule, and David recognized the potential for building commercial connections between India, the Middle East, and China.

He established **David Sassoon & Co.** in 1832, initially trading in cotton, textiles, and later opium—a controversial but legal and enormously profitable commodity at the time. The firm grew rapidly, benefiting from:

- British colonial trade networks and legal protection
- David's Middle Eastern connections and linguistic abilities (Arabic, Persian, Hebrew)
- The family's business acumen and willingness to take risks
- Strategic positioning as intermediaries between British merchants and Asian markets

David remained deeply connected to Jewish life. He built synagogues in Bombay and Pune, supported Jewish education, and maintained Orthodox observance. His home was simultaneously a center of commerce and Jewish learning—business conducted downstairs, Torah study upstairs.

By the 1840s, the Sassoons had become one of India's wealthiest families, with branch offices throughout India, Persia, and China.

## Expanding to Shanghai: The China Trade

The real transformation of the Sassoon fortune came with China.

### The Opium Trade

In the 19th century, British merchants (and those operating under British protection) engaged extensively in the opium trade with China. Opium grown in India was shipped to China, where demand was enormous despite Chinese government prohibitions.

This trade was morally troubling—it enriched Western merchants while devastating Chinese society through addiction. The Qing Dynasty's attempts to stop it led to the Opium Wars (1839-1842 and 1856-1860), which Britain won, forcing China to open ports to foreign trade and cede Hong Kong.

The Sassoons were major players in this trade, though they framed it as simply meeting market demand within legal frameworks (legal under British law, if not Chinese). This remains the most controversial aspect of their legacy.

### Shanghai: The Treaty Port

After the First Opium War, Shanghai became a "treaty port"—forced open to foreign trade and settlement. The city rapidly transformed from a modest walled town into China's commercial hub.

David Sassoon sent his sons to establish offices in China's treaty ports, particularly Shanghai and Hong Kong. His son **Elias David Sassoon** (E.D. Sassoon) opened the Shanghai branch in 1845, and later his grandson **Edward Elias Sassoon** expanded operations dramatically.

But it was **Sir Victor Sassoon** (1881-1961), great-grandson of David, who became most identified with Shanghai and left the most visible legacy.

## Victor Sassoon: The Man Who Built Modern Shanghai

**Victor Sassoon** was born in Britain into already immense wealth. He inherited the family business empire but faced a critical decision: should he remain in comfortable Britain or venture to Shanghai, the wild, growing boomtown where opportunities seemed limitless?

He chose Shanghai.

### Vision for a Modern City

Arriving in Shanghai in the 1920s, Victor Sassoon saw a city poised to become Asia's greatest metropolis. He invested enormous sums in real estate development, constructing some of Shanghai's most iconic buildings:

**Sassoon House and the Cathay Hotel**: Completed in 1929, this Art Deco masterpiece on the Bund became Shanghai's most prestigious address. The Cathay Hotel (now the Peace Hotel) hosted international celebrities, politicians, and businesspeople. Victor kept a suite at the top, from which he oversaw his empire.

**Hamilton House, Grosvenor House, Embankment Building**: Victor developed multiple major commercial and residential buildings, shaping Shanghai's skyline.

**Affordable Housing**: Unusually for a wealthy developer, Victor also invested in affordable housing for Shanghai's working class, constructing apartment blocks that improved living conditions for thousands.

Victor's vision was to make Shanghai the "Paris of the East"—a cosmopolitan city combining Eastern and Western cultures, commerce, and sophistication.

### A Cosmopolitan Life

Victor Sassoon embodied cosmopolitan culture. He was:
- **British by nationality**: Born in Britain, educated at Cambridge
- **Jewish by heritage**: Proud of his Jewish identity, though not religiously observant
- **Iraqi by ancestry**: Descended from Baghdad's Jewish community
- **Indian by business**: Managing operations across South Asia
- **Chinese by choice**: Making Shanghai his home and primary focus

He moved in elite international circles—photographed with Chiang Kai-shek, friendly with British diplomats, hosting parties attended by Shanghai's multinational elite.

He was also passionate about horse racing, breeding champion horses and developing Shanghai's racecourse into one of Asia's premier racing venues. The racecourse occupied prime land in central Shanghai (now People's Square).

### Jewish Life in Sassoon's Shanghai

Under Victor Sassoon's era, Shanghai's Jewish community flourished. There were actually two distinct Jewish communities:

**Sephardic Jews** (from Middle Eastern origins like the Sassoons): Wealthy merchants and businesspeople, primarily from Baghdad and Bombay, who led the community institutionally and economically.

**Ashkenazi Jewish refugees**: Thousands of European Jews fleeing Russian pogroms (early 20th century) and later Nazi persecution (1930s-1940s) found refuge in Shanghai, one of the few places that didn't require entry visas.

Victor Sassoon supported both communities, contributing to synagogues, schools, and charitable institutions. His presence helped make Shanghai a safe haven for tens of thousands of Jewish refugees during the Holocaust—a fact that saved countless lives.

## The End of an Era

Victor Sassoon's Shanghai glory ended with World War II and the Chinese Civil War.

### Japanese Occupation (1937-1945)

When Japan invaded China and occupied Shanghai in 1937, foreign businesses came under pressure. Victor moved to India for the war's duration, though his Shanghai properties were maintained.

### Communist Victory (1949)

The real end came with the Communist victory in China's civil war. The new People's Republic of China nationalized foreign businesses and assets. Victor Sassoon lost his Shanghai empire virtually overnight—billions of dollars in property seized without compensation.

He relocated to the Bahamas, living comfortably on remaining assets but never returning to Shanghai. He died in 1961, a man out of time—the last of the great colonial merchant princes.

The Sassoon buildings remained, though renamed and repurposed. The Cathay Hotel became the Peace Hotel. Sassoon House became government offices. The name "Sassoon" was erased, remembered only by historians and older residents.

## Legacy and Memory

Today, the Sassoon legacy is being rediscovered and reassessed:

### Architectural Heritage

The Sassoon buildings are now recognized as architectural treasures. The Peace Hotel has been restored to luxury status, attracting tourists curious about old Shanghai glamour. Preservation efforts celebrate the architectural and cultural heritage these buildings represent.

### Complex Historical Assessment

Modern historians wrestle with the Sassoon legacy's complexity:

**Positive aspects**:
- Economic development: The Sassoons invested in infrastructure, housing, and businesses that employed thousands
- Cultural contribution: They helped make Shanghai a cosmopolitan center
- Jewish community support: They built institutions and provided refuge
- Architectural legacy: Their buildings remain Shanghai landmarks

**Problematic aspects**:
- Opium trade: Profiting from a trade that devastated Chinese society
- Colonial exploitation: Operating within and benefiting from colonial structures that disadvantaged Chinese sovereignty
- Inequality: Immense wealth built partly on cheap Chinese labor and unequal economic relationships

### Jewish Historical Significance

For Jewish history, the Sassoons represent:
- Jewish success in Asian diaspora contexts
- The adaptability of Jewish commercial culture across civilizations
- The complexity of Jewish participation in colonialism (Jews as both victims of oppression in Europe and participants in colonial enterprises in Asia)
- A remarkable family story of exile, rebuilding, and achievement

### Chinese Perspective

From a Chinese perspective, the Sassoons are increasingly studied as part of understanding Shanghai's complex modern history—neither purely villains nor heroes, but significant actors in the city's transformation. The colonial period's legacy remains contested, but the buildings themselves are valued as historical heritage.

## Conclusion

The Sassoon family's story arc is dramatic: from Baghdad's Jewish community to Bombay's trading empire to Shanghai's skyline. They were exiles who became moguls, Middle Eastern Jews who shaped Asian cities, traditional believers who became cosmopolitan sophisticates.

Victor Sassoon's Art Deco towers still stand on the Bund, reminding visitors that Shanghai's modern history was built by people from everywhere—including Iraqi Jewish refugees who saw opportunity where others saw only chaos.

For Chinese readers, the Sassoons offer a window into the complex Jewish role in modern Asian history—not just victims seeking refuge (though Sassoon-era Shanghai did shelter Holocaust refugees), but also active builders who invested in Asian cities and participated in the region's economic transformation.

For Jews worldwide, the Sassoons demonstrate Judaism's global reach and Jews' capacity to succeed in diverse contexts—though also raising uncomfortable questions about how that success was sometimes achieved.

The name "Sassoon" still echoes in Shanghai's streets, in Bombay's synagogues, and in the memories of those who knew old Shanghai. It's a name connecting Baghdad to Bombay to Shanghai, Torah study to opium trading to Art Deco architecture—a quintessentially Jewish story of diaspora, adaptation, success, loss, and enduring legacy.

The moguls who built Shanghai left more than buildings—they left questions about wealth and responsibility, integration and identity, tradition and modernity that resonate far beyond one family's extraordinary journey.
