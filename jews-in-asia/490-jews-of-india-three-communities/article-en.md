When people think of Jewish history, India rarely comes to mind. Yet for over two millennia, Jewish communities have thrived on the Indian subcontinent, creating unique traditions that blend Jewish law with Indian culture. Unlike many places where Jewish history is marked by persecution and expulsion, India offered Jews remarkable acceptance, allowing them to flourish while maintaining their distinct identity.

India's Jewish story is actually three stories—three distinct communities with separate origins, customs, and histories: the ancient **Cochin Jews** of Kerala, the mysterious **Bene Israel** of Maharashtra, and the modern **Baghdadi Jews** of Bombay and Calcutta. Together, they represent one of Judaism's most fascinating and peaceful diaspora experiences.

## The Cochin Jews: Ancient Traders on India's Spice Coast

### Origins in Legend and History

The **Cochin Jews** (also called Malabari Jews) settled on India's southwestern Malabar Coast, in what is now Kerala state. Their history stretches back so far that their origins blend history with legend.

According to community tradition, the first Jews arrived in India during the reign of King Solomon (10th century BCE), drawn by trade in spices, precious woods, and peacocks mentioned in the Bible: "Once every three years the ships of Tarshish came bringing gold, silver, ivory, apes, and peacocks" (1 Kings 10:22).

More reliably documented is a major Jewish settlement following the destruction of the Second Temple in 70 CE. Jewish refugees fleeing Roman persecution may have joined existing Jewish traders in India, establishing permanent communities.

### The Copper Plates: Royal Recognition

The Cochin Jewish community possesses an extraordinary historical treasure: **copper plates** inscribed in ancient Tamil script, granting privileges to the Jewish leader Joseph Rabban by the Kerala ruler Bhaskara Ravi Varma (traditionally dated to around 379 CE, though historians debate the exact date).

These copper plates, which the community preserved for centuries as its charter, granted:
- Land for Jewish settlement in the town of Anjuvannam (Cranganore)
- Tax exemptions
- The right to use royal symbols (lamps, parasols, drums)
- Legal autonomy for the Jewish community

This royal recognition allowed Cochin Jews to live securely and prosper for over a thousand years—a remarkable contrast to Jewish experiences in medieval Europe.

### White Jews and Black Jews: A Complex Division

Cochin's Jewish community developed an internal division that remains controversial: **Paradesi ("Foreign") Jews** and **Malabari Jews**, colloquially known as "White Jews" and "Black Jews."

**Paradesi Jews**: Descended from later arrivals—Sephardic Jews from Spain and Portugal (15th-16th centuries) and Middle Eastern Jews. They built the famous Paradesi Synagogue in Cochin (1568), which became the community's architectural crown jewel.

**Malabari Jews**: The original Indian Jewish population, speaking Malayalam and more culturally integrated into Kerala society.

This division created a painful hierarchy. Paradesi Jews, lighter-skinned and claiming purer Jewish lineage, refused to intermarry with Malabari Jews, relegated them to inferior synagogue seats, and excluded them from certain community honors.

This caste-like division (possibly influenced by Hindu caste concepts) contradicts Jewish principles of equality and remains a troubling aspect of Cochin Jewish history. It highlights how even tolerant diaspora communities could develop internal prejudices.

### Cultural Integration

Despite internal divisions, Cochin Jews integrated remarkably into Kerala culture:
- **Language**: They spoke Malayalam, writing it in Hebrew characters (Judeo-Malayalam)
- **Dress**: Women wore saris; men wore traditional Kerala clothing
- **Names**: Many adopted Indian names alongside Hebrew ones
- **Food**: Kerala Jewish cuisine blended kosher law with Indian spices and coconut-based dishes
- **Music**: Synagogue melodies incorporated Indian musical modes

Yet they maintained Jewish identity:
- Observing Sabbath and Jewish festivals
- Studying Torah and Talmud
- Maintaining synagogues and mikvaot (ritual baths)
- Circumcising sons and observing Jewish life cycle rituals

### Decline and Emigration

Cochin's Jewish community thrived for centuries but declined dramatically in the 20th century:

**Colonial period**: Under Portuguese and later British rule, the community continued but gradually shrank due to emigration and economic changes.

**Creation of Israel (1948)**: The vast majority of Cochin Jews emigrated to Israel, seeing it as the fulfillment of ancestral dreams. Entire villages of Jews departed, leaving behind centuries-old synagogues.

By the 1950s-60s, the once-vibrant community dwindled to a few dozen elderly members. The magnificent Paradesi Synagogue, famous for its hand-painted Chinese tiles and Belgian chandeliers, became more museum than active house of worship.

Today, fewer than twenty Jews remain in Cochin, mostly elderly. The synagogue is maintained as a heritage site, attracting tourists who marvel at this unique chapter of Jewish history.

## The Bene Israel: India's Mysterious Jews

### Origins Shrouded in Mystery

The **Bene Israel** ("Children of Israel") are India's largest historical Jewish community, numbering around 50,000 at their peak. They lived primarily in Maharashtra state, centered around Bombay (Mumbai) and the Konkan coastal region.

Their origins remain mysterious. According to community tradition, their ancestors were Jews from the Galilee region who fled persecution around 175 BCE, possibly during the Seleucid persecutions. Their ship was wrecked near the Konkan coast. Seven men and seven women survived and settled in India.

Isolated from other Jewish communities for perhaps 1,500 years, they lost most Jewish knowledge—Hebrew language, Torah texts, much of Jewish law. Yet they miraculously retained core Jewish identity:
- Observing Sabbath (lighting oil lamps, resting from work)
- Monotheism (worshipping one God)
- Circumcising sons on the eighth day
- Observing some dietary restrictions
- Reciting the *Shema* prayer in a corrupted form

They knew themselves as *Shanwar Telis* ("Saturday oil-pressers")—their occupation and Sabbath observance defining their identity.

### Rediscovery and Revival

In the 18th century, Cochin Jews traveling north encountered the Bene Israel and recognized their Jewish customs. This contact began the Bene Israel's reconnection with worldwide Jewry.

**David Rahabi**, a Cochin Jew, visited Bene Israel villages in the 1700s, teaching them Hebrew and basic Jewish law. This started a gradual revival of Jewish knowledge.

British colonization of India accelerated the process. The British recognized Bene Israel as Jews, allowing them to move to Bombay and participate in urban economic opportunities. Bene Israel increasingly adopted mainstream Jewish practices, built synagogues, and sent children to Jewish schools.

By the 19th-20th centuries, Bene Israel were well-integrated into Bombay's social and economic life. Many served in the British Indian Army. Some became professionals, educators, and civil servants. They were known for loyalty, discipline, and strong family values.

### Cultural Synthesis

Bene Israel developed fascinating cultural blends:

**Language**: They spoke Marathi, India's regional language in Maharashtra, giving them names like Rajpurkar, Samson, and Malekar.

**Customs**: They celebrated Jewish festivals but with Indian cultural flavors. Elijah's chair at circumcisions was decorated with Indian flowers; wedding canopies (*chuppot*) incorporated Indian design elements.

**Prophet Elijah Veneration**: They particularly revered the prophet Elijah, celebrating an annual pilgrimage to a coastal site they called "Elijah's Cave"—a practice with both Jewish and Indian pilgrimage overtones.

**Music**: Their synagogue music blended Middle Eastern Jewish melodies with Indian musical traditions.

### Recognition Controversy

When large numbers of Bene Israel began emigrating to Israel starting in the 1950s, a painful controversy erupted: were they "really" Jewish?

Some Israeli rabbis, noting their long isolation and uncertain origins, questioned their Jewish status, creating barriers to religious marriage and community acceptance. This deeply wounded the Bene Israel, who had maintained Jewish identity for millennia despite enormous odds.

Eventually, Israeli Chief Rabbis ruled that Bene Israel are fully Jewish, resolving the controversy officially—though social prejudices lingered longer.

### Present Status

Today, most Bene Israel live in Israel (about 50,000-70,000), with smaller communities in India (fewer than 5,000), the United States, and Canada. In Israel, they've largely integrated into Israeli society while maintaining some distinct customs and a strong community identity.

In Mumbai, several Bene Israel synagogues still function, though the community has drastically shrunk. These synagogues serve as both religious centers and heritage sites.

## The Baghdadi Jews: Modern Merchant Princes

### Latest Arrivals

The **Baghdadi Jews** are India's most recent Jewish community, arriving in the 18th-19th centuries from Iraq, Syria, Persia, and other Middle Eastern countries.

Unlike the ancient Cochin Jews or mysterious Bene Israel, Baghdadi Jews came as traders and entrepreneurs during British colonial expansion. As discussed in the previous article about the Sassoon family, they became enormously successful in commerce, establishing business empires in Bombay, Calcutta, and beyond.

### Two Major Centers

**Bombay (Mumbai)**: The Baghdadi center of commerce. Families like the Sassoons, Gubbays, and Ezras built trading companies, cotton mills, and real estate empires. They constructed magnificent synagogues—Magen David Synagogue, Knesset Eliyahoo, Tipherets Israel—which still grace Mumbai's landscape.

**Calcutta (Kolkata)**: Another major Baghdadi center. The community built beautiful synagogues (Magen David, Beth El, Neveh Shalome) and established schools and charitable institutions.

### Cultural Distinctiveness

Baghdadi Jews maintained more separation from Indian culture than the Cochin or Bene Israel communities:

- They spoke English, Arabic, or Judeo-Arabic, not Indian languages
- They dressed in Western or Middle Eastern style, not Indian clothing
- They operated in colonial British commercial networks
- They sent children to English-medium schools
- They remained culturally closer to Middle Eastern Jewish traditions

This wasn't hostility to India—many Baghdadi Jews loved India and considered it home. But their recent arrival, mercantile orientation, and operation within British colonial structures created different integration patterns than the older communities.

### Departure and Legacy

Like other Indian Jewish communities, most Baghdadi Jews emigrated to Israel, Britain, or the Americas in the mid-20th century, particularly after Indian independence (1947) and the nationalization policies that followed.

Their synagogues remain architectural jewels, maintained by tiny remnant communities and heritage organizations. In both Mumbai and Kolkata, these synagogues attract tourists and historians interested in India's multicultural past.

## Common Threads: Why India Was Different

Across all three communities, India offered Jews something rare in diaspora history: **acceptance**.

Unlike Europe's persecutions, the Inquisition's forced conversions, or the Middle East's dhimmi restrictions, India generally allowed Jews to live freely, practice their religion, and prosper economically.

Several factors explain this:

**Hindu Pluralism**: Hinduism's acceptance of multiple paths to the divine created cultural space for religious diversity. If there are countless forms of worship, Judaism was simply one more.

**No Christian or Islamic Supersessionism**: The theological claim that Christianity or Islam superseded Judaism didn't exist in Hindu or Buddhist contexts, removing a major source of anti-Jewish sentiment elsewhere.

**Merchant Class Respect**: As traders and merchants, Jews filled valuable economic roles that earned respect rather than resentment.

**Small Population**: Jews never threatened anyone demographically or politically, reducing potential for conflict.

**Caste System Navigation**: Some scholars suggest Jews navigated India's caste system by fitting into merchant/trader castes, giving them an accepted social position.

## Conclusion

India's Jewish communities—the ancient Cochin Jews, the mysterious Bene Israel, and the entrepreneurial Baghdadi Jews—represent three distinct threads of the global Jewish story, all finding a peaceful home in the Indian subcontinent.

For Chinese readers, India's Jewish experience offers interesting parallels to the Kaifeng Jews' Chinese experience: both show how Jewish communities could integrate into Asian societies while maintaining identity. Both demonstrate that Jewish history extends far beyond Europe.

For Jews worldwide, India's Jewish communities are a reminder that diaspora didn't always mean persecution, that Judaism could flourish in non-Western contexts, and that Jewish identity proved remarkably resilient even through centuries of isolation (as with the Bene Israel).

Today, these communities have largely disappeared from India, with most members now living in Israel. But their legacy remains—in the synagogues of Cochin, Mumbai, and Kolkata, in the memories of elderly community members, in the cultural synthesis of Indian Jewish cuisine and music, and in the historical record of how Jews and Indians lived together peacefully for millennia.

The story of India's Jews is ultimately a story of coexistence—proof that religious difference need not breed conflict, that diversity can enrich rather than threaten society, and that even a tiny minority can thrive when given the chance to live freely and contribute their gifts to the societies that welcome them.
