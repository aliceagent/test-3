# Jewish Life in Singapore, Hong Kong, and Southeast Asia Today


---

While Asia is home to the world's smallest Jewish populations, vibrant Jewish communities thrive in cities across Southeast Asia. For travelers, expatriates, and local Jews, these communities offer synagogues, kosher food, holiday celebrations, and educational programs—proof that Jewish life can flourish anywhere committed Jews build it.

This guide introduces the major Jewish communities of Southeast Asia, their histories, institutions, and practical resources for connecting to Jewish life in this dynamic region.

## Singapore: Asia's Model Jewish Community

With approximately 2,000-2,500 Jews, Singapore hosts one of Southeast Asia's most organized and welcoming communities.

### History

Jewish settlement in Singapore began in the 1830s, shortly after the British established the colony. Early Jewish merchants, primarily Baghdadi Jews from India and Iraq, traded in textiles, real estate, and rubber. The Manasseh Meyer family and David Sassoon & Co. were prominent community leaders and philanthropists.

Singapore's Jews integrated successfully into colonial society while maintaining Jewish identity. The community built its first synagogue, Maghain Aboth (Shield of Our Fathers), in 1878, which still stands in Waterloo Street—Singapore's oldest synagogue and a national monument.

### Organizations and Synagogues

**Maghain Aboth Synagogue**  
The historic Orthodox synagogue serves Sephardic and Ashkenazi Jews. Beautiful architecture, active services on Shabbat and holidays, and a welcoming atmosphere for visitors.

**Chesed-El Synagogue**  
Also Orthodox, located in Oxley Rise. Known for its friendly community and Shabbat hospitality.

**United Hebrew Congregation (Liberal/Progressive)**  
For Jews seeking egalitarian, liberal services. Offers alternative religious expression in a traditionally Orthodox community.

**Jewish Welfare Board (JWB)**  
The umbrella organization coordinating community activities, education, and social services. The JWB runs programs for children, teens, and adults, and provides support for transient Jews and tourists.

### Kosher Food

Singapore offers surprisingly robust kosher infrastructure:

- **Shemesh Kosher**: Full-service kosher restaurant (meat and dairy) serving Middle Eastern and Israeli cuisine
- **Carmel Delicatessen**: Kosher grocery with imported products
- **Kosher certification**: Some hotels and caterers offer kosher options for events
- **Shabbat meals**: Community members often host guests; contact synagogues to arrange

### Schools and Education

While no full-time Jewish day school exists, the community offers supplementary education programs, Hebrew classes, and Bar/Bat Mitzvah preparation through synagogues and the JWB.

### Connecting

Visitors should contact the Jewish Welfare Board or synagogues in advance. Singaporean Jews are famously hospitable and often invite travelers for Shabbat meals. High Holidays see large gatherings, and advance registration is recommended.

## Hong Kong: International Hub

Hong Kong's Jewish community numbers around 5,000-6,000, predominantly expatriates working in finance, law, and business, along with families from Israel, America, Australia, and Europe.

### History

Jewish merchants, especially from Baghdad, arrived in Hong Kong in the mid-19th century. The Sassoon family, Kadoorie family, and others became titans of Hong Kong business, involved in real estate, utilities (the Kadoories founded China Light & Power), and hospitality (the Peninsula Hotel).

The Ohel Leah Synagogue, built in 1902 by Jacob Sassoon and named for his mother, remains the community's architectural and religious centerpiece.

### Synagogues and Organizations

**Ohel Leah Synagogue (Orthodox)**  
Beautiful historic building in Mid-Levels. Active Orthodox services and programs.

**Chabad of Hong Kong**  
Dynamic Chabad presence with multiple centers offering Shabbat dinners, holiday programs, classes, and outreach. Chabad has become the community's most visible institution, attracting young professionals and families.

**United Jewish Congregation (Progressive/Reform)**  
Egalitarian services and events for liberal Jews.

**The Jewish Community Centre (JCC)**  
Located in the Ohel Leah complex, the JCC provides educational programs, cultural events, and social gatherings.

### Kosher Dining

- **Kosher restaurants**: Limited, but Chabad operates a kosher restaurant/café
- **Kosher products**: Available at specialty stores and through Chabad
- **Hotels**: Some luxury hotels can arrange kosher catering for events

### Education

**Carmel School**: Hong Kong's Jewish day school, offering Jewish studies alongside secular academics. Serves families seeking comprehensive Jewish education in an international setting.

### Connecting

Chabad is especially welcoming to travelers and offers easy online sign-up for Shabbat meals. Ohel Leah and UJC also host guests. High Holidays require advance registration and tickets.

## Other Southeast Asian Communities

### Thailand (Bangkok, Chiang Mai)

**Bangkok** has an estimated 500-1,000 Jews, mostly expatriates and tourists. Chabad of Thailand is highly active, offering Shabbat meals, holiday services, and kosher restaurants in popular tourist areas (Sukhumvit Road). The Chabad House is a lifeline for Israeli backpackers and Jewish travelers.

**Chiang Mai** has a small Chabad presence serving the digital nomad and expat community.

### Philippines (Manila)

A tiny Jewish community exists in Manila, centered around Chabad and informal gatherings. Services are held on High Holidays; kosher food is limited.

### Vietnam (Ho Chi Minh City, Hanoi)

Chabad operates in both cities, primarily serving Israeli tourists and expatriates. Shabbat dinners and holiday programs are available; kosher dining options are limited but growing.

### Indonesia (Jakarta, Bali)

Indonesia's Jewish presence is minimal due to political sensitivities and lack of diplomatic relations with Israel. Small informal Jewish gatherings occur among expatriates, but there are no public synagogues. Bali attracts Israeli tourists, and informal Chabad-style Shabbat meals sometimes occur discreetly.

### Malaysia (Kuala Lumpur, Penang)

Malaysia historically had small Jewish communities, but political and religious climate make open Jewish practice difficult today. Expatriate Jews connect discreetly, but there is no active synagogue. Some Jews visit Penang's historic Jewish cemetery, remnant of a vanished community.

## Practical Tips for Jewish Life and Travel in Southeast Asia

### Before You Go

1. **Contact local Chabad or synagogues**: Email ahead for Shabbat hospitality, kosher food, and community updates
2. **Pack essentials**: Bring tefillin, siddur, tallit, and any ritual items you need
3. **Check holiday dates**: Plan travel around Jewish holidays if you want to celebrate with a community

### Shabbat Observance

- **Hotels**: Many luxury hotels accommodate Shabbat needs (Shabbat elevators, room key alternatives)
- **Meals**: Sign up for community meals; bring non-perishable kosher snacks as backup
- **Eruv**: Ask local rabbis about eruv status in each city

### Kosher Food

- **Packaged goods**: Bring from home or buy from kosher shops in Singapore/Hong Kong
- **Fruits, vegetables, raw foods**: Widely available and permissible
- **Restaurants**: Verify kosher supervision; when in doubt, stick to vegetarian or fish (with proper scales)
- **Chabad**: Usually the most reliable source for hot kosher meals

### Security

Jewish institutions in Asia generally maintain low profiles due to regional politics (Israel-Muslim world tensions). Security is taken seriously at synagogues; respect protocols and avoid drawing unnecessary attention.

## Why Asian Jewish Communities Matter

These communities prove that Judaism is not confined to historically Jewish lands. Whether you're a traveler seeking Shabbat hospitality, an expatriate building a new life, or a curious learner exploring Jewish presence worldwide, these communities embody the eternal promise: "Wherever ten Jews gather, the Divine presence dwells."

From Singapore's grand synagogues to Bangkok's backpacker Shabbat dinners, Southeast Asian Jews maintain tradition, adapt creatively, and welcome strangers. They honor the past while building the future—one Shabbat, one kosher meal, one connection at a time.
