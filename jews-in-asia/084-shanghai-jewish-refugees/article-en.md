## Introduction

When the world closed its doors to Jews fleeing Nazi persecution in the late 1930s, one city remained open without restriction: Shanghai. While country after country imposed strict immigration quotas or turned away desperate refugees entirely, Shanghai — then an international settlement under partial Chinese sovereignty — asked for no visas, no sponsorships, no proof of wealth. Between 1938 and 1941, approximately 20,000 Jewish refugees found sanctuary in this bustling Chinese port city, making it the largest haven for Jews escaping the Holocaust outside of Palestine.

This remarkable chapter of history created an unexpected bridge between the Jewish and Chinese peoples. For many survivors, Shanghai was the city that saved their lives when the rest of the world looked away. Today, as interest in Judaism grows among Chinese people, understanding this shared history provides a powerful foundation — a reminder that China once played a heroic role in Jewish survival during history's darkest hour.

## Historical Context: The World Closes Its Doors

### The Refugee Crisis of 1938

After *Kristallnacht* (the "Night of Broken Glass") in November 1938 — when Nazi mobs destroyed thousands of Jewish businesses and synagogues across Germany and Austria — the urgency for Jewish emigration became desperate. This occurred during the late Republic of China era (民国时期, 中華民國, 1912-1949), shortly before the Second Sino-Japanese War would intensify.

The Evian Conference, convened in July 1938 by U.S. President Franklin Roosevelt, brought together 32 countries to discuss the refugee crisis. The result was devastating: nearly every nation present refused to increase immigration quotas. Australia famously declared, "We don't have a racial problem, and we're not desirous of importing one." The United States maintained its restrictive quota system. Britain severely limited entry to Palestine. Door after door slammed shut.

### Why Shanghai Was Different

Shanghai was unique in the world for three critical reasons:

1. **No visa requirement**: As an international settlement governed by a complex arrangement between Chinese authorities and foreign concessions, Shanghai was one of the last places on Earth where refugees could arrive without entry papers.

2. **Chinese sovereignty**: While the International Settlement and French Concession operated with considerable autonomy, ultimate sovereignty remained with China. Chinese officials, facing their own national crisis with Japanese invasion, chose not to close the gates.

3. **Existing Jewish infrastructure**: Small communities of Sephardic and Russian Jews had established themselves in Shanghai since the mid-1800s, providing some support structure for newcomers.

Between 1938 and 1941, when Japan (which had occupied Shanghai in 1937) finally closed entry, approximately 20,000 Central European Jews — primarily from Germany, Austria, and Poland — arrived in Shanghai by ship, often with nothing more than a single suitcase and the clothes on their backs.

## Life in Hongkou: The Shanghai Ghetto

### The Early Years (1938-1941)

The first wave of refugees arrived during the Republic of China era (民国时期) to find a city already strained by war, poverty, and massive internal displacement from the Sino-Japanese conflict. Most settled in the Hongkou district (虹口区), a densely populated area that would later become designated as the "Shanghai Ghetto."

Despite extreme hardship, the refugee community demonstrated remarkable resilience:

**Economic survival**: Refugees opened small businesses — cafés, bakeries, tailoring shops, and medical practices. The intersection of Chusan Road and Wayside Road became known as "Little Vienna," lined with Central European establishments. Chinese neighbors often became customers, creating small economic partnerships.

**Cultural life**: The community established schools for children (including religious instruction), theaters, newspapers (in German and Yiddish), sports clubs, and even a symphony orchestra. Music and culture became lifelines that preserved dignity and hope.

**Religious observance**: Several synagogues were established, including the Ohel Moishe Synagogue (still standing today as a museum). Maintaining Jewish practice — Shabbat, kashrut where possible, High Holidays — provided spiritual anchor amid displacement.

**Relationship with Chinese neighbors**: Despite language barriers and cultural differences, many Chinese residents showed kindness to the refugees. Stories abound of Chinese shopkeepers extending credit, landlords showing patience with late rent, and ordinary Shanghai residents sharing food during times of scarcity.

### The Designated Area (1943-1945)

In February 1943, during the middle years of the Republic of China era (民国时期), under pressure from their Nazi allies, the Japanese authorities issued the "Proclamation Concerning Restriction of Residence and Business of Stateless Refugees" — effectively creating a confined ghetto area in Hongkou. All "stateless refugees" (meaning Jews who had arrived after 1937) were required to relocate to a small, overcrowded area of less than one square mile.

Life became significantly harder:

- **Overcrowding**: Seven or eight families might share a single house designed for one.
- **Poverty**: Movement outside the designated area required special passes, making it difficult to work or operate businesses.
- **Disease**: Overcrowded conditions led to outbreaks of dysentery, typhoid, and tuberculosis.
- **Hunger**: Food became scarce. The Joint Distribution Committee (JDC), an American Jewish relief organization, sent funds that literally kept the community alive.

Yet even in these conditions, the community maintained schools, religious services, and cultural activities. Rabbi Meir Ashkenazi and other spiritual leaders continued teaching and providing pastoral care. The refugees lived under the same privations as their Chinese neighbors, and often Chinese residents showed remarkable compassion, sometimes at personal risk.

### Chinese Heroes

While the Japanese occupied Shanghai, some Chinese officials and ordinary citizens quietly helped Jews in ways both large and small:

**Ho Feng Shan (何鳳山)**: The Chinese consul-general in Vienna in 1938-1940, Dr. Ho issued thousands of visas to Shanghai against the orders of his superior, the Chinese ambassador. He is now honored as "Righteous Among the Nations" by Yad Vashem. His actions directly enabled many Jews to escape Europe.

**Sun Ke (孫科)**: Son of Sun Yat-sen and then-president of the Legislative Yuan, Sun Ke publicly stated in 1938 that China would welcome Jewish refugees, helping establish the moral framework for sanctuary.

**Ordinary Shanghai residents**: Countless unnamed Chinese neighbors shared food, hid refugees during Japanese searches, and showed everyday kindness that made survival possible.

## Liberation and Legacy

### The End of the War (1945)

When Japan surrendered in August 1945, the Shanghai ghetto was liberated. Unlike Jewish communities across Europe — where two-thirds of the Jewish population had been murdered — virtually all of Shanghai's Jewish refugees survived. This is an almost miraculous statistic: a Jewish community under Axis control that emerged intact.

### The Dispersion (1945-1950)

After the war, most refugees chose to leave Shanghai:

- Many emigrated to the newly established State of Israel (1948)
- Others went to the United States, Canada, or Australia
- Some returned to Europe to search for surviving family members
- A small number remained in Shanghai through the Communist revolution, though most eventually left in the early 1950s

By 1955, the vibrant Jewish community of Shanghai had essentially disappeared, its members scattered across the globe.

### Remembering Shanghai

For those who lived through it, Shanghai remained a place of profound gratitude. W. Michael Blumenthal, who would become U.S. Secretary of the Treasury, arrived in Shanghai as a refugee child in 1939. He later wrote: "Shanghai was our refuge when nobody else wanted us... The Chinese people were kind to us."

In 2007, the Shanghai Jewish Refugees Museum opened in the restored Ohel Moishe Synagogue in Hongkou. Today, elderly survivors and their descendants return to Shanghai to walk the streets of their childhood, to pay respects at the museum, and to thank the city that gave their families life.

## Cultural Connections: Exile and Sanctuary

### Shared Values Between Jewish and Chinese Thought

The Shanghai refugee experience resonates with deep themes in both Jewish and Chinese traditions:

**The stranger and the refugee**: The Torah commands 36 times to care for the stranger (*ger*), more than any other commandment. "You shall not oppress a stranger, for you know the feelings of the stranger, having yourselves been strangers in the land of Egypt" (Exodus 23:9). Shanghai's open door embodied this principle — offered by Chinese neighbors who themselves knew displacement and war.

**Humanity in crisis (仁)**: The Confucian concept of *ren* (仁) — often translated as "benevolence" or "humaneness" — describes the moral obligation to show compassion to those in need. The Chinese people of Shanghai, themselves suffering under occupation and war, extended *ren* to Jewish refugees who had nowhere else to go.

**Exile as a test**: Jewish history is marked by exiles — from Egypt, from Babylonia, from Spain, from across Europe. Each exile tested the people's ability to maintain identity while adapting to new circumstances. The Shanghai refugees maintained religious practice, established schools and synagogues, and passed Jewish tradition to the next generation even in the most unlikely place.

**Diaspora communities**: Just as Chinese emigrants established communities across Southeast Asia and the world (華僑), maintaining language, customs, and identity while integrating economically, so too did Jewish refugees in Shanghai create a parallel community structure — preserving heritage while building new lives.

## Practical Application: What This History Teaches Today

### 1. The Moral Obligation to Refugees

The Shanghai story challenges us to consider: when the world faces refugee crises today — whether from war, persecution, or disaster — what is our responsibility? The Torah doesn't merely suggest helping the stranger; it commands it. The Chinese people of Shanghai, in their darkest hour, modeled this.

### 2. Small Acts of Kindness Matter

Not everyone can be Ho Feng Shan, issuing thousands of life-saving visas. But the unnamed Chinese shopkeeper who extended credit, the landlord who waived late rent, the neighbor who shared food — these small acts collectively made survival possible. We each have power to help, even in small ways.

### 3. Shared Suffering Can Build Bridges

The refugees and their Chinese neighbors were both victims of war and occupation. This shared suffering created empathy and connection across vast cultural differences. Even when we don't share language or customs, we share humanity.

### 4. Memory as Moral Duty

Judaism places enormous emphasis on *zachor* — remembering. "Remember that you were slaves in Egypt" (Deuteronomy 5:15). The survivors of Shanghai have worked tirelessly to ensure this history is not forgotten, building museums and recording testimonies. Why? Because memory prevents complacency. It reminds us that when we close our doors to the desperate, people die. And when we open them, we save lives.

## Key Takeaways

- **Shanghai was the only place in the world between 1938-1941 that accepted Jewish refugees without visas or restrictions**, saving approximately 20,000 lives.

- **Chinese officials and ordinary citizens played heroic roles**, from Ho Feng Shan issuing visas in Vienna to Shanghai neighbors sharing food and shelter during the darkest years.

- **Despite extreme hardship in the Hongkou ghetto (1943-1945), virtually all of Shanghai's Jewish refugees survived** — a nearly miraculous outcome compared to European Jewry's devastation.

- **The refugee community maintained Jewish religious and cultural life** even under occupation, establishing synagogues, schools, and cultural institutions that preserved identity.

- **This history creates a profound bond between Jews and Chinese people** — a reminder of shared humanity and China's role as a place of sanctuary when the world turned away.

- **The moral lessons remain urgent today**: the obligation to welcome refugees, the power of small acts of kindness, and the duty to remember so history does not repeat.
