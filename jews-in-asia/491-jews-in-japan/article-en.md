The story of Jews in Japan is one of the most unusual chapters in the Jewish diaspora—a tale of merchants, refugees, wartime intrigue, and an audacious plan to settle tens of thousands of Jews in Japanese-controlled territory. While Japan has never had a large Jewish population, the encounters between Japanese and Jewish cultures reveal fascinating attitudes, desperate survival strategies, and unexpected moments of rescue.

## Early Encounters: Meiji-Era Traders

The first significant Jewish presence in Japan emerged during the Meiji era (1868-1912), when Japan opened to the West after centuries of isolation. Jewish merchants, primarily from Baghdad and Russia, arrived in the treaty ports of Yokohama, Kobe, and Nagasaki.

The Baghdad Jewish community was particularly influential. Families like the Sassoons—sometimes called "the Rothschilds of the East"—established trading networks spanning from India to Shanghai to Japan. These Sephardic merchants dealt in silk, cotton, and other goods, serving as cultural bridges between East and West.

Russian Jews also came to Japan, many fleeing pogroms and persecution in the late 19th and early 20th centuries. Some settled temporarily before moving on to America or other destinations; others stayed and established small but vibrant communities.

By the early 20th century, synagogues had been established in Kobe (1912) and Tokyo. The communities were tiny—perhaps a few hundred families at most—but they contributed to Japan's modernization and international commerce.

## The Fugu Plan: A Controversial Scheme

The most remarkable—and morally complex—episode in Japanese-Jewish history is the so-called "Fugu Plan" (河豚計画, *fugu keikaku*). Named after the poisonous pufferfish that is a delicacy when properly prepared but deadly if mishandled, the plan reflected Japanese uncertainty about how to "handle" Jewish refugees.

### Origins and Motivations

In the late 1930s, as Nazi persecution intensified, tens of thousands of European Jews sought refuge anywhere they could. Some Japanese military and political figures proposed an audacious scheme: invite Jewish refugees to settle in Japanese-controlled Manchuria (occupied China), where they would:

1. Bring capital and expertise to develop the region
2. Generate goodwill from wealthy American Jews (whom Japanese planners believed controlled U.S. finance and policy)
3. Serve Japanese imperial interests while providing humanitarian refuge

The plan's architects included Captain Koreshige Inuzuka and Colonel Norihiro Yasue, both considered Japan's "Jewish experts." They had studied European antisemitic literature (including the fraudulent *Protocols of the Elders of Zion*) but drew different conclusions than the Nazis: if Jews were as powerful as antisemites claimed, perhaps they should be befriended rather than destroyed.

### Reality and Implementation

The Fugu Plan was never officially adopted or systematically implemented. However, its influence shaped Japanese policy toward Jewish refugees during World War II.

Unlike most of the world, Japan did not completely close its doors to Jewish refugees. Several thousand Jews found temporary refuge in Japanese-controlled territories, particularly Shanghai, which did not require entry visas and became home to roughly 20,000 Jewish refugees by 1941.

The most famous case of Japanese assistance involved Chiune Sugihara, the Japanese consul in Kaunas, Lithuania. In the summer of 1940, Sugihara issued thousands of transit visas to Jewish refugees—against orders from Tokyo—allowing them to escape through the Soviet Union to Japan and onward to safety. His actions saved an estimated 6,000 lives. (Sugihara was later recognized as Righteous Among the Nations by Yad Vashem.)

### The Shanghai Ghetto

After Japan allied with Nazi Germany, pressure mounted to adopt more antisemitic policies. In 1943, under German pressure, Japanese authorities in Shanghai established a "Designated Area for Stateless Refugees"—essentially a ghetto where Jewish refugees without other nationality were required to live.

Conditions were harsh: overcrowding, disease, poverty, and restrictions on movement. Yet compared to ghettos in Nazi Europe, the Shanghai ghetto was not a death trap. Japanese authorities did not implement systematic murder, and most refugees survived the war.

Survivors later reported that many ordinary Japanese people showed kindness and sympathy. Some Japanese officials quietly resisted German demands for harsher measures. Japan never adopted the Final Solution, even as Germany's ally.

## Why Did Japan's Approach Differ?

Several factors explain Japan's relatively benign (though still opportunistic and discriminatory) treatment of Jews:

**No antisemitic tradition**: Japanese culture had no historical precedent of antisemitism. Jews were exotic curiosities, not ancient scapegoats.

**Pragmatism over ideology**: Japanese leaders prioritized strategic interests over Nazi racial ideology. They saw potential benefit in Jewish refugees, not just threat.

**Distance from European influence**: Geographic and cultural distance from European antisemitism meant Japanese attitudes were shaped more by geopolitics than by Christian anti-Jewish teachings.

**Individual courage**: People like Sugihara acted from conscience, even at personal risk.

## Post-War Jewish Life

After World War II, most Jews left Japan. The Shanghai refugees scattered to Israel, America, Australia, and elsewhere. The small communities in Tokyo and Kobe shrank further.

Today, Japan's Jewish community numbers only about 2,000 people—mostly expatriates working for multinational companies, diplomats, and students. The Kobe synagogue was demolished in 1995 after the Great Hanshin earthquake. Tokyo's Jewish Community Center serves as the hub for religious and cultural life.

Despite the tiny community, interest in Judaism remains. Some Japanese people study Hebrew, visit Israel, or explore Jewish mysticism and wisdom. Books about Jewish success and Jewish thought sell well in Japan, though sometimes promoting stereotypes.

## Lessons and Reflections

The story of Jews in Japan teaches several lessons:

**Rescue is possible**: Even in the darkest times, individuals and governments can choose compassion over complicity. Sugihara's visas and Japan's refusal to implement genocide saved thousands.

**Opportunism is not virtue**: The Fugu Plan was rooted in self-interest and stereotypes, not genuine humanitarianism. Viewing refugees as tools rather than people is morally compromised—even when it accidentally saves lives.

**Diaspora reaches everywhere**: From Baghdad merchants in Meiji Japan to Holocaust refugees in Shanghai, Jews have built temporary homes in the most unlikely places. The Jewish people's survival depends on this adaptability.

**Memory matters**: Chiune Sugihara was unemployed and forgotten after the war, punished by his government for disobedience. Only decades later was he honored. Remembering righteous acts—and teaching them—ensures they inspire future generations.

The Jewish-Japanese encounter reminds us that history is rarely simple. Heroes and villains coexist; rescue and exploitation intertwine; strangers become neighbors in unexpected corners of the world. Even in faraway Japan, the Jewish story continues.
