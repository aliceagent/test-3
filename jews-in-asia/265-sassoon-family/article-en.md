## Introduction

When you walk along Shanghai's famous Bund today, you're walking through the legacy of one of the most remarkable Jewish families in history. The Sassoon family — often called "the Rothschilds of the East" — transformed the commercial landscape of Asia while maintaining their Jewish identity across generations and continents. Their story is one of exile and reinvention, of holding fast to ancient traditions while building modern empires, and of how a single family helped shape the cities of Bombay (Mumbai), Shanghai, and Hong Kong into global trading centers.

Unlike the Rothschilds of Europe, whose influence was primarily financial, the Sassoons built their fortune on trade — particularly in cotton, opium, and real estate. Their journey began in Baghdad during the early Qing Dynasty (清朝) and reached its zenith during China's tumultuous late imperial period and early Republic era. Today, their architectural legacy still stands in the form of grand buildings, synagogues, and charitable institutions across Asia.

## From Baghdad to Bombay: The Foundation of an Empire

### The Baghdad Roots

The Sassoon family's story begins in Baghdad around 1750 — during what corresponds to China's mid-Qing Dynasty (清朝中期, 1644-1912). For generations, the Sassoons were prominent members of Baghdad's wealthy Jewish community, serving as chief treasurers (*nasi*) to the Ottoman governors of Iraq. They were respected scholars, community leaders, and successful merchants who maintained close ties with other Sephardic Jewish families throughout the Ottoman Empire.

The family patriarch, Sheikh Sassoon ben Saleh (1750-1830), was known for his learning and piety. He ensured that his sons received both traditional Jewish education and training in commerce. His son, David Sassoon (1792-1864), would become the founder of the family's Asian empire.

### The Flight to India

In 1829, political upheaval and persecution of Jews in Baghdad forced David Sassoon to make a life-changing decision. The new Ottoman governor, Dawud Pasha, was notoriously anti-Jewish and had several Jewish leaders imprisoned or executed. David fled to the Persian Gulf, then to Bushire (in present-day Iran), and finally settled in Bombay (Mumbai) in 1832 — a journey similar to how Chinese merchant families fled political turmoil to establish themselves in safer territories.

Bombay, under British rule, offered religious freedom and commercial opportunity. The British East India Company had transformed the city into a major trading hub, and David immediately recognized its potential. He arrived with his family, his books of Jewish law, and an intimate knowledge of trade routes stretching from the Mediterranean to the Indian Ocean.

### Building the Foundation

David Sassoon established his first firm, "David Sassoon & Co.," in 1832. He initially traded in pearls, spices, and precious metals, but quickly expanded into cotton and textiles. His strategy was brilliant: he leveraged his family's connections throughout the Middle East and Asia, establishing branches in Calcutta, Canton (Guangzhou), Hong Kong, and Shanghai.

Crucially, David maintained strict Jewish observance. He closed his businesses on Shabbat and Jewish holidays, required his employees to be literate in Hebrew, and established synagogues and Jewish schools in every city where he did business. His motto was said to be: "Trade is my business; faith is my life."

## The Sassoon Trading Empire in China

### Arrival in Shanghai (1845)

The Sassoon family first established a presence in China in 1845 — during the final years of the Daoguang Emperor's reign (道光帝, 1820-1850). They arrived in Shanghai just three years after the Treaty of Nanking opened Chinese ports to foreign trade following the First Opium War.

David sent his second son, Elias David Sassoon (1820-1880), to Shanghai to establish a branch office. The timing was perfect: Shanghai was about to explode into one of the world's most important commercial centers, and the Sassoons would be at the heart of that transformation.

### The Opium Trade Controversy

It must be acknowledged that much of the Sassoon fortune was built on the opium trade — a dark chapter in both British and Chinese history. The family imported opium from India to China, where British merchants exploited the addictive drug to balance their trade deficit with the Qing Empire.

From a modern perspective, this trade was morally indefensible. It caused immense suffering in China and contributed to the Qing Dynasty's decline. However, at the time, opium trading was legal under British law and sanctioned by the colonial government. The Sassoons, like other British merchants (including Jardine Matheson and Dent & Co.), participated in this trade as part of the broader economic system.

Later generations of Sassoons would shift away from opium as it became increasingly controversial, diversifying into cotton, silk, tea, real estate, and banking. By the early 20th century, the family's business interests in China were primarily in textiles and property development.

### The Cathay Hotel and Sassoon House

Perhaps the most iconic symbol of the Sassoon legacy in Shanghai is the Cathay Hotel (now the Peace Hotel on the Bund). Built by Victor Sassoon (1881-1961) in 1929 — during the Republic of China era (中华民国时期) — it was the most luxurious hotel in Asia. Its Art Deco design, rooftop gardens, and jazz clubs made it the social center of Shanghai's International Settlement.

Victor Sassoon, great-grandson of David, famously said: "There is only one race greater than the Jews, and that's the Derby." He was a flamboyant businessman, a decorated war hero (he flew combat missions in World War I), and a passionate horse racing enthusiast. Despite his cosmopolitan lifestyle, he remained deeply committed to Jewish causes and Zionism.

The Cathay Hotel hosted celebrities, diplomats, and refugees. During World War II, it became a temporary haven for Jewish refugees fleeing the Holocaust, many of whom found shelter in Shanghai when almost no other country would accept them.

## The Hong Kong Connection

### E.D. Sassoon & Co.

When David Sassoon's sons split the family business in the 1860s (a common pattern in Chinese family businesses as well), Elias David Sassoon established his own firm: E.D. Sassoon & Co. He chose Hong Kong as his headquarters, making it the Asian center of his commercial empire.

The firm dominated trade in cotton, silk, and opium throughout the late Qing Dynasty. They built warehouses along the Hong Kong waterfront and established the Sassoon Docks, which facilitated trade between British India and China.

### Sassoon Road and Peak Residences

The Sassoon name remains visible in Hong Kong today. Sassoon Road, running through the wealthy Peak district, was named after the family. They owned extensive real estate in the colony and were major philanthropists, donating to hospitals, schools, and public works.

## Jewish Identity Across Continents

### Maintaining Tradition in the East

Despite their wealth and assimilation into British colonial society, the Sassoons remained deeply Jewish. They built synagogues wherever they established businesses:

- **Magen David Synagogue** in Calcutta (1884) — still standing
- **Ohel Rachel Synagogue** in Shanghai (1920) — now a protected heritage site
- **Synagogue at Bombay** (now Mumbai)

David Sassoon insisted that all business correspondence be written in Judeo-Arabic (Arabic written in Hebrew characters), ensuring his employees maintained Hebrew literacy. He supported yeshivas in Baghdad, Jerusalem, and Bombay. He regularly sent financial aid to poor Jews in the Land of Israel.

### Philanthropy and Social Responsibility

The Sassoon family embodied the Jewish concept of *tzedakah* (righteous giving). They established:

- The David Sassoon Library and Reading Room in Bombay (1847)
- Schools for poor Jewish children
- Hospitals for all communities, regardless of religion
- Relief funds for victims of famines in India and China

This commitment to community welfare mirrored Confucian ideals of the benevolent merchant (仁商) who uses wealth to benefit society.

## The Decline and Dispersal

### World War II and the Fall of Shanghai

The Sassoon empire in Asia began to crumble with the Japanese invasion of China in 1937. Victor Sassoon, seeing the writing on the wall, began liquidating his Shanghai holdings in the late 1930s. By 1941, when Japan occupied the International Settlement, the Sassoon business interests in China were effectively finished.

After World War II, the Communist revolution in China (1949) nationalized all foreign assets. The Sassoon buildings were confiscated, and the family's century-long presence in China ended.

### From the East to Israel and Beyond

Many Sassoons, including Victor, became ardent Zionists. Victor donated generously to Jewish causes in Palestine and later to the State of Israel after 1948. He moved to the Bahamas in his later years but maintained close ties to the Jewish state.

Other branches of the family settled in England, where they integrated into British aristocracy. Some married into British nobility, gradually assimilating over generations — a common pattern among wealthy diaspora families.

## Parallels with Chinese Merchant Families

### Similarities to Shanxi Merchants (晋商)

The Sassoon family's story shares striking parallels with successful Chinese merchant families, particularly the Shanxi merchants (晋商) of the Qing Dynasty:

1. **Multi-generational businesses**: Both maintained family control across centuries
2. **Ethics-based commerce**: Confucian merchants emphasized *yi* (义, righteousness) in business; the Sassoons emphasized Jewish law
3. **Charitable giving**: Both traditions required the wealthy to support education and the poor
4. **Diaspora networks**: Shanxi merchants established branches across China; Sassoons across Asia
5. **Education as foundation**: Both emphasized literacy and learning for their children

### The Difference: Diaspora Identity

However, there's a crucial difference: Chinese merchant families operated within their homeland and culture. The Sassoons were perpetual diaspora people — exiled from Baghdad, foreigners in India, aliens in China, refugees from the Nazis. Their identity was anchored not in territory but in Torah, Shabbat, and Jewish peoplehood.

This rootless existence gave them flexibility and adaptability but also a profound longing for a homeland — which helps explain why many became passionate Zionists.

## The Sassoon Legacy Today

### Architectural Heritage

Walk through Mumbai, Shanghai, or Hong Kong, and you'll see Sassoon buildings:

- The Sassoon Docks in Mumbai (still functioning)
- The Peace Hotel on Shanghai's Bund
- The former Ohel Rachel Synagogue in Shanghai (recently restored)

These structures stand as reminders of an era when Jewish merchants were integral to Asia's modernization.

### A Model of Diaspora Success

The Sassoon family demonstrates that Jewish success in diaspora is possible when:
- Jewish identity remains central
- Ethical principles guide business (though they failed in the opium trade)
- Tzedakah and community support are priorities
- Education and learning are non-negotiable values

They also show the fragility of diaspora existence. Within a century, political upheaval, war, and nationalism scattered them from their Asian empire.

### Questions of Ethics

Modern Jews studying the Sassoon story must grapple with difficult questions:
- Can we celebrate their philanthropy while acknowledging the opium trade's harm?
- Is commercial success a worthy goal if it comes at others' expense?
- How do we balance survival in hostile environments with moral responsibility?

These are not easy questions, and Jewish tradition has debated them for centuries. The Talmud teaches: "Let the honor of your fellow be as dear to you as your own" (*Pirkei Avot* 2:10). The Sassoons excelled at honoring fellow Jews but failed to extend that honor to the Chinese victims of the opium trade.

## Practical Application

### Lessons for Modern Life

What can we learn from the Sassoon family's story?

1. **Identity and Success Are Not Opposites**: You can be deeply rooted in Jewish tradition and fully engaged with the modern world.

2. **Philanthropy Is an Obligation, Not an Option**: Wealth brings responsibility. The Sassoons understood that their success imposed duties to the less fortunate.

3. **Adaptability Without Assimilation**: The family adapted to different cultures and languages but never abandoned Shabbat, kosher laws, or Jewish education.

4. **Build Institutions, Not Just Fortunes**: The Sassoons left behind synagogues, libraries, and schools — structures that outlasted their commercial empire.

5. **Ethical Failures Have Long Consequences**: Their involvement in the opium trade remains a stain on their legacy, reminding us that financial success means little if it harms others.

### For Jews in Asia Today

The Sassoon story is particularly relevant for Jews living in Asia today:

- **Small communities can have large impact**: There were never more than a few hundred Sassoons in China, yet they shaped cities and saved lives.
- **Maintain connections to global Jewry**: The Sassoons supported Jews in Baghdad, Jerusalem, and Mumbai even while based in Shanghai.
- **Prepare for instability**: Diaspora history teaches that even successful communities can vanish overnight due to politics, war, or persecution.

## Key Takeaways

- The Sassoon family built a commercial empire across Asia from the 1830s to 1940s, maintaining strict Jewish observance while succeeding in trade and real estate.
- They arrived in China during the late Qing Dynasty, establishing businesses in Shanghai, Hong Kong, and other treaty ports.
- Their wealth was built partly on the morally problematic opium trade but later diversified into cotton, silk, real estate, and banking.
- They embodied Jewish values of *tzedakah* (charity), building synagogues, schools, libraries, and hospitals throughout Asia.
- The family's legacy includes iconic buildings like Shanghai's Peace Hotel and Mumbai's Sassoon Docks, which still stand today.
- Their story demonstrates both the possibilities and fragilities of Jewish diaspora life: commercial success, cultural adaptation, but also vulnerability to political upheaval.
