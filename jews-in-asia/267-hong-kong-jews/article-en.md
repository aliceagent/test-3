## Introduction

When you think of Hong Kong, images of towering skyscrapers, bustling markets, and Victoria Harbour likely come to mind. But hidden within this global metropolis is a Jewish community that has shaped the city's development for over 170 years. From the philanthropic Kadoorie family to Holocaust refugees who found sanctuary here during World War II, Hong Kong's Jewish story is one of resilience, integration, and profound impact on both Jewish and Chinese history.

Unlike many Jewish communities worldwide that faced persecution, Hong Kong's Jews thrived in an environment of religious tolerance and economic opportunity. They became architects of the city's infrastructure, pioneers of its industries, and bridges between East and West. Today, while the community is small — numbering around 5,000-6,000 — its legacy is monumental.

## The First Jewish Arrivals: Sephardic Pioneers (1840s-1850s)

Hong Kong became a British colony in 1842 following the First Opium War — during China's late Qing Dynasty (清朝, 1644-1912). Almost immediately, Jewish merchants from Baghdad, India, and other parts of the Sephardic diaspora recognized the territory's strategic importance as a gateway to China and Asia.

The first known Jewish resident was Elias David Sassoon, who arrived in 1844. The Sassoon family — often called "the Rothschilds of the East" — had built a trading empire from Baghdad to Bombay, and Hong Kong became a crucial link in their network. They traded in tea, silk, and opium, amassing enormous wealth that would later fund Jewish communities throughout Asia.

By the 1850s, enough Jews had settled in Hong Kong to establish a kehillah (organized community). In 1857, they leased land in Happy Valley for a Jewish cemetery, which still exists today as the oldest non-Christian burial ground in Hong Kong. Two years later, in 1859, they rented a space for prayer services, marking the beginning of formal Jewish religious life in the colony.

### The Ohel Leah Synagogue: A Landmark of Faith

In 1901, the Sassoon family funded the construction of the Ohel Leah Synagogue on Robinson Road, Mid-Levels. Named after Lady Leah Gubbay Sassoon, it became the spiritual heart of Hong Kong's Jewish community. The synagogue follows Sephardic rites and remains active today, hosting daily prayers, Shabbat services, and holiday celebrations.

The building itself is a testament to Jewish integration into Hong Kong society. Its architecture blends European synagogue design with colonial Hong Kong style, featuring tall windows, high ceilings, and intricate woodwork. Inside, Torah scrolls brought from Baghdad and India connect worshippers to centuries of Jewish tradition.

## The Kadoorie Family: Builders of Modern Hong Kong

While the Sassoons laid the foundation, the Kadoorie family transformed Hong Kong's landscape in the 20th century. Elly Kadoorie and his brother Ellis arrived in Hong Kong in the 1880s from Baghdad via India. They started as traders but quickly expanded into real estate, utilities, and manufacturing.

Their most significant contribution came in 1918 when they co-founded China Light & Power (CLP), which brought electricity to Hong Kong and the New Territories. Before CLP, most of Hong Kong relied on kerosene lamps and coal. The Kadoories' vision electrified the city, powering its transformation into a modern metropolis.

### The Peninsula Hotel and Hospitality Empire

In 1928, the Kadoorie family opened The Peninsula Hotel in Kowloon, which became one of Asia's most iconic luxury hotels. Known as "The Grande Dame of the Far East," The Peninsula set new standards for hospitality and elegance. Today, The Hongkong and Shanghai Hotels Limited — controlled by the Kadoorie family — operates luxury properties worldwide.

### Lawrence and Horace Kadoorie: Philanthropy and Legacy

The second generation of Kadoories continued their family's commitment to both business excellence and communal responsibility. Sir Elly Kadoorie's sons, Lawrence and Horace, became legendary figures in Hong Kong.

**Lawrence Kadoorie (1899-1993)** was a visionary businessman and philanthropist. After the Japanese occupation of Hong Kong during World War II, he dedicated himself to rebuilding the colony. He established agricultural programs that helped refugees and displaced farmers restart their lives, providing tools, seeds, and training.

The Kadoorie Farm and Botanic Garden in the New Territories, originally created to assist refugees, now serves as an environmental education center. Lawrence also funded schools, hospitals, and community centers that served both Jewish and Chinese communities without discrimination.

**Horace Kadoorie (1902-1995)** managed the family's business interests with equal dedication. Together, the brothers rebuilt China Light & Power after the war and expanded it into one of Asia's largest energy companies. They also supported Jewish institutions worldwide, including synagogues, schools, and relief organizations.

### A Distinctive Approach to Tzedakah

The Kadoorie family's philanthropy embodies the Jewish concept of tzedakah — not mere charity, but righteous giving that empowers others. Unlike donor-recipient models, their projects taught skills and created sustainable livelihoods. This approach mirrors the Talmudic principle that "the highest form of charity is to help someone become self-sufficient" (Maimonides' Eight Levels of Tzedakah).

Their giving crossed religious and ethnic boundaries. In Chinese Confucian thought, this resonates with the value of 仁 (rén, benevolence) — extending compassion beyond one's immediate circle. The Kadoories demonstrated that Jewish values and Chinese ethics could harmonize beautifully.

## Holocaust Refugees: Hong Kong as Sanctuary (1938-1941)

As Nazi Germany tightened its grip on Europe in the late 1930s, desperate Jewish families sought refuge anywhere that would accept them. While many nations closed their doors, Hong Kong — though under no obligation — became an unexpected haven.

Between 1938 and 1941, approximately 1,000-1,500 Jewish refugees from Germany and Austria found sanctuary in Hong Kong. The British colonial government, while not actively recruiting refugees, permitted entry more liberally than most Western countries. Local Jewish families, particularly the Kadoories and Sassoons, provided financial support, housing, and employment opportunities.

### The International Committee for Granting Relief to European Refugees

In 1938, prominent Hong Kong Jews established a relief committee to assist arriving refugees. They secured accommodations in boarding houses, found jobs, and helped families adjust to life in a radically different culture. Many refugees worked in trades they knew from Europe — tailoring, baking, watchmaking — contributing skills that enriched Hong Kong's economy.

Refugee children attended local schools, and families worshipped at Ohel Leah Synagogue alongside established Sephardic families. Despite language barriers and cultural differences, there was a profound sense of Jewish unity and mutual responsibility.

### The Japanese Occupation (1941-1945)

This sanctuary ended abruptly on December 8, 1941, when Japan invaded Hong Kong just hours after attacking Pearl Harbor. During the three years and eight months of occupation, Hong Kong's entire population — Jews included — endured severe hardship.

While Japan was allied with Nazi Germany, it did not implement the Final Solution in Hong Kong. However, many Jewish refugees were interned in Stanley Camp along with other British subjects and foreigners. Conditions were harsh: starvation rations, disease, forced labor, and constant fear. Some refugees who had already escaped the Holocaust now faced new terrors.

The Kadoorie family was interned, and their properties were confiscated. Yet after liberation in August 1945, they returned to rebuild not with bitterness but with renewed commitment to Hong Kong's future.

## Post-War Revival and Growth

After World War II, Hong Kong's Jewish community rebuilt rapidly. Refugees who had survived internment chose to stay, establishing businesses and raising families. The Ohel Leah Synagogue was renovated, and religious life resumed with renewed vigor.

In the 1950s and 1960s, Hong Kong experienced massive economic growth as refugees from mainland China (fleeing Communist rule after 1949) poured into the territory. The Jewish community grew modestly, with new arrivals from Europe, America, Australia, and Israel. Some came for business opportunities; others were attracted by Hong Kong's unique position as a free port and gateway to Asia.

### Establishment of Modern Jewish Institutions

**Jewish Recreation Club (JRC):** Founded in 1956, the JRC became a social and cultural hub for Hong Kong's Jewish community. It offers sports facilities, cultural programs, and a kosher restaurant — one of the few in East Asia.

**Carmel School:** Established in 1991, this Jewish day school provides students with both secular education and Jewish studies. Children learn Hebrew, celebrate Jewish holidays, and receive religious instruction while also following Hong Kong's curriculum.

**United Jewish Congregation (UJC):** In addition to the Sephardic Ohel Leah Synagogue, the Ashkenazi-rite United Jewish Congregation was established to serve Jews from Eastern European backgrounds. Today, both congregations coexist peacefully, reflecting Hong Kong's cosmopolitan spirit.

**Chabad of Hong Kong:** Arriving in the 1980s, Chabad-Lubavitch outreach has been instrumental in providing religious services to Jewish visitors and residents. They operate a synagogue, mikva (ritual bath), and educational programs, ensuring that even Jews with minimal background can connect to their heritage.

## The Handover and Modern Era (1997-Present)

On July 1, 1997, the United Kingdom returned Hong Kong to Chinese sovereignty, marking the end of 156 years of colonial rule. This transition raised concerns worldwide, but for Hong Kong's Jewish community, it highlighted their unique position: long-standing ties to both British institutions and Chinese culture.

The Basic Law — Hong Kong's mini-constitution — guaranteed religious freedom, and the new Hong Kong Special Administrative Region government upheld this commitment. Jewish institutions continued operating without interference, and kosher food imports remained unrestricted.

### A Thriving Yet Small Community

Today, Hong Kong's Jewish population is estimated at 5,000-6,000, including long-term residents, expatriates on temporary assignments, and Israeli businesspeople. The community is diverse: Sephardic families who trace their lineage to the 1850s, Ashkenazi families from Europe and America, Israelis working in technology and finance, and converts married into Jewish families.

Despite its small size, the community maintains:
- Two active synagogues (Ohel Leah and UJC)
- A Chabad center
- Carmel School
- A kosher restaurant and grocery
- Regular cultural events and holiday celebrations

### The Kadoorie Legacy Continues

The Kadoorie family remains deeply involved in Hong Kong's development. The Kadoorie Agricultural Aid Association (KAAA) continues agricultural and environmental work. The Hongkong and Shanghai Hotels operates The Peninsula and other luxury properties globally. Proceeds from these enterprises fund charitable projects in Hong Kong and beyond.

In 2021, the family donated HK$200 million to establish the Kadoorie Biological Sciences Building at the University of Hong Kong, supporting cutting-edge research. This commitment to education and science reflects timeless Jewish values.

## Cultural Bridges: Jews and Chinese Together

One of the most remarkable aspects of Hong Kong's Jewish story is the deep mutual respect between Jews and Chinese. Unlike many places where Jews faced suspicion or hostility, Hong Kong's Jews were welcomed, valued, and integrated.

### Shared Values

Both Jewish and Chinese cultures emphasize education, family, hard work, and communal responsibility. The Confucian ideal of the 君子 (jūnzǐ, noble person) who serves society resonates with the Jewish concept of a mensch — someone of integrity and compassion. These shared values created natural common ground.

### Contributions to Chinese Society

Jewish philanthropy in Hong Kong has always extended to the broader Chinese community. Kadoorie schools, medical clinics, and agricultural projects served people of all backgrounds. This generosity earned deep respect and affection.

Similarly, Chinese neighbors protected Jewish families during the Japanese occupation, sharing scarce food and offering shelter when possible. These bonds of friendship, forged in hardship, endure across generations.

### Modern Connections

Today, Hong Kong serves as a meeting point for Jewish and Chinese professionals. Israeli tech companies partner with Hong Kong investors. Jewish educators teach Chinese students Hebrew and Middle Eastern history. Chinese entrepreneurs visit Israel to study innovation ecosystems.

This dynamic exchange benefits both communities, creating bridges of understanding that extend far beyond business into culture, art, and mutual learning.

## Visiting Jewish Hong Kong Today

If you visit Hong Kong, you can explore its rich Jewish heritage:

**Ohel Leah Synagogue (70 Robinson Road, Mid-Levels):** Visitors are welcome for services. Contact in advance to arrange a tour.

**Jewish Cemetery (Happy Valley):** The oldest Jewish cemetery in East Asia, with gravestones dating back to 1857. A quiet place for reflection on the generations who built this community.

**The Peninsula Hotel (Salisbury Road, Kowloon):** Experience the luxury and elegance of the Kadoorie family's most famous creation.

**Chabad of Hong Kong:** Offers Shabbat dinners, holiday programs, and educational classes. A welcoming space for Jewish visitors and locals alike.

## Conclusion: A Legacy of Light in the East

Hong Kong's Jewish community may be small, but its impact has been profound. From pioneering merchants who helped build a colonial trading port to philanthropists who electrified a modern metropolis, Jews have been integral to Hong Kong's success story.

The Kadoorie family exemplifies the best of Jewish values: using wealth not for personal glory but to uplift others, investing in education and infrastructure, and treating all people with dignity regardless of background. Their legacy is not measured in buildings alone but in lives changed, communities strengthened, and bridges built between cultures.

For Chinese readers encountering this history, consider it an invitation: Jewish history is not distant or foreign. It is interwoven with your own city's story, your neighbors' contributions, and shared values that transcend ethnicity and religion.

And for Jewish readers, Hong Kong's story reminds us that even in the most unexpected places — thousands of miles from Jerusalem — Torah values can flourish, communities can thrive, and light can shine brightly.

## Key Takeaways

- Hong Kong's Jewish community began in the 1840s with Sephardic merchants from Baghdad and India during China's Qing Dynasty (清朝)
- The Kadoorie family transformed Hong Kong through China Light & Power, The Peninsula Hotel, and extensive philanthropy
- Holocaust refugees found sanctuary in Hong Kong (1938-1941) before the Japanese occupation
- The community today numbers 5,000-6,000 with active synagogues, a Jewish school, and vibrant cultural life
- Jewish and Chinese values of education, family, and communal responsibility create natural bonds of friendship and mutual respect
