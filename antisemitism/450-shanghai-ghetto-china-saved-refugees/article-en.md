# Shanghai Ghetto: How China Saved 20,000 Jewish Refugees


---

While six million Jews perished in the Holocaust, there is a remarkable chapter in this dark history where China played a heroic role: the story of Shanghai, which became a refuge for approximately 20,000 Jewish refugees fleeing Nazi persecution when almost every other country in the world closed its doors.

This is not a well-known story outside of Jewish and Shanghai history circles, but it represents one of the most significant humanitarian acts of the World War II era. At a time when the United States, Britain, Canada, Australia, and most other countries refused Jewish refugees, Shanghai—already struggling with Japanese occupation and its own refugee crisis—took them in, no questions asked.

For Chinese readers, this is your history too. It's a story of compassion, survival, and the complicated relationship between Chinese residents and Jewish refugees during one of the most chaotic periods in modern Chinese history.

## Why Shanghai?

In the 1930s, Shanghai was unique in a crucial way: it required no visa for entry. The international settlements in Shanghai—areas governed by foreign powers under unequal treaties—operated independently of Chinese government control. This meant Jews fleeing Europe could board a ship to Shanghai without needing permission from any government.

This became critical after 1938. Following *Kristallnacht*, the violent pogrom across Nazi Germany, Jews desperately sought escape. But at the Évian Conference in July 1938, 32 countries met to discuss Jewish refugees, and nearly all refused to increase their immigration quotas. The Australian delegate infamously said, "We don't have a racial problem, and we don't want to import one."

Only the Dominican Republic offered to accept refugees, and that offer was largely symbolic. But Shanghai—by bureaucratic accident, not by deliberate policy—remained open.

## The Journey to Shanghai

Getting to Shanghai meant an arduous, expensive, and dangerous journey. Refugees had to:

1. Escape from Nazi-controlled territories (increasingly difficult as borders closed)
2. Make their way to ports in Italy or other neutral countries
3. Pay for expensive ship passage (often using their life savings or selling everything they owned)
4. Endure a journey of 4-6 weeks, often in crowded conditions
5. Arrive in a city where they knew no one, didn't speak the language, and had few resources

Despite these obstacles, approximately 20,000 Jews made it to Shanghai between 1938 and 1941. They came primarily from Germany, Austria, and Poland, though refugees arrived from across Nazi-occupied Europe.

## Who Were the Refugees?

The Shanghai Jewish refugees were diverse:

**Professionals and intellectuals**: Doctors, lawyers, musicians, engineers, teachers, and businesspeople who had been driven from their professions in Europe

**Families**: Parents desperate to save their children, often leaving behind extended family who couldn't or wouldn't leave

**Observant Jews**: Rabbis and religious community members who maintained traditional practice

**Secular Jews**: Many were highly assimilated Germans who had considered themselves German first until the Nazis stripped their citizenship

**Children**: Some came with parents; others arrived on *kindertransport* programs, sent alone by parents who hoped to follow but often never did

They brought little—perhaps a suitcase, some family photographs, musical instruments, or precious items they hoped to sell. The Nazis had stripped them of most possessions and savings.

## Shanghai's Jewish Communities

When the refugees arrived, Shanghai already had two established Jewish communities:

### Sephardic Jews

Wealthy Jews from Baghdad, including the famous Sassoon and Kadoorie families, had come to Shanghai in the 19th century and built business empires. They were British subjects, spoke English, and were part of Shanghai's elite. The Sassoons owned hotels, real estate, and trading companies. The Kadoories were philanthropists who built hospitals and schools.

These wealthy Sephardic Jews played a crucial role in helping the refugees, funding relief organizations, building housing, and creating aid networks.

### Russian Jews

About 5,000 Jews from Russia had fled to Shanghai after the Russian Revolution and civil war. They had established communities, synagogues, and businesses. They served as a cultural bridge between the new refugees and Chinese society.

### The Refugees

The new arrivals from Central Europe were the largest group and the most desperate. Unlike the established communities, they arrived penniless and traumatized.

## Life in Shanghai: 1938-1941

The first years were characterized by struggle but also relative freedom.

### Housing

Initially, refugees were housed in camps called *Heime* (homes), funded by international Jewish relief organizations and wealthy Sephardic Jews. Conditions were crowded—dozens of people sharing rooms—but they were safe.

As refugees established themselves, many moved into the Hongkou district, a poor area north of Suzhou Creek that had been heavily damaged during the 1937 Battle of Shanghai. Rents were cheap because much of it was in ruins. This area would later become the Shanghai Ghetto.

### Work and Business

Refugees couldn't work in professional jobs (partly due to Japanese restrictions, partly due to lack of credentials in China). So they became entrepreneurial:

- Opening small shops and cafés
- Providing services (hairdressing, tailoring, shoe repair)
- Teaching music and languages
- Manufacturing goods (pastries, sausages, leather goods)
- Publishing German-language newspapers

The "Little Vienna" café district emerged in Hongkou, where refugees recreated aspects of European culture—coffee houses with newspapers, pastry shops, small theaters, and musical performances.

### Relations with Chinese Residents

The relationship between Jewish refugees and Chinese residents of Hongkou was complex:

**Economic interaction**: Jews rented from Chinese landlords, bought from Chinese merchants, hired Chinese workers. This created economic connections.

**Cultural barriers**: Language differences (refugees spoke German or Yiddish, residents spoke Shanghainese or Mandarin) and cultural unfamiliarity limited deeper connection.

**Shared hardship**: Both communities lived in poverty in Hongkou. Both were victims of Japanese occupation in different ways.

**Some friendships**: Despite barriers, friendships formed. Chinese residents sometimes shared food with refugees. Chinese doctors treated Jewish patients. Children played together.

**Some exploitation**: Some Chinese landlords charged inflated rents; some merchants overcharged refugees who didn't know local prices.

**Mutual curiosity**: For many Chinese, Jews were exotic foreigners. For Jews, Chinese culture was entirely new. This created both fascination and misunderstanding.

Survivors recall Chinese neighbors with mixed feelings—some remember kindness, others remember indifference or hostility. The relationship was complicated by the broader chaos of occupied Shanghai.

## The Shanghai Ghetto: 1943-1945

In February 1943, under pressure from Nazi Germany (which was allied with Japan), the Japanese authorities established the "Designated Area for Stateless Refugees" in Hongkou—effectively a ghetto. Jews who had arrived after 1937 were required to move there if they didn't already live there, and those inside needed special passes to leave.

### Why Did Japan Create the Ghetto?

Japan was Nazi Germany's ally but had very different attitudes toward Jews:

- Some Japanese officers believed the antisemitic "Protocols of the Elders of Zion" but interpreted it to mean Jews were powerful and should be cultivated as allies (the "Fugu Plan")
- Japan didn't share Nazi racial ideology or genocidal intent
- But under German pressure, Japan implemented some anti-Jewish measures

The result was a strange compromise: a ghetto that restricted movement but didn't have the death, starvation, and brutality of Nazi ghettos in Europe.

### Life in the Ghetto

**Restrictions**: Refugees needed passes to leave for work, which were sometimes granted, sometimes denied arbitrarily. This made employment difficult.

**Crowding**: Approximately 20,000 refugees crowded into less than one square mile of already-damaged urban area.

**Poverty**: Most refugees lived in extreme poverty, relying on relief organizations for food rations.

**Disease**: Malnutrition, poor sanitation, and overcrowding led to outbreaks of typhus, tuberculosis, and other diseases.

**But not death camps**: Crucially, this was not Auschwitz. There were no gas chambers, no systematic murders, no mass shootings. Jews in Shanghai suffered from poverty, disease, and restrictions—but they survived.

The Japanese guards were sometimes harsh, sometimes indifferent, occasionally even sympathetic. Some could be bribed. The system was arbitrary but not genocidal.

### Community and Resilience

Despite hardship, the refugee community maintained cultural life:

**Education**: Schools operated in German, teaching children subjects from Torah to mathematics.

**Religious life**: Multiple synagogues served different denominations and traditions.

**Arts and culture**: Theater groups performed, musicians gave concerts, artists held exhibitions.

**Newspapers**: German-language newspapers kept the community informed and connected.

**Mutual aid**: Relief organizations distributed food, medical care, and assistance.

This cultural vitality was itself resistance—maintaining humanity and dignity in inhuman conditions.

## The End of World War II

In August 1945, Japan surrendered. American forces occupied Shanghai. The ghetto restrictions ended immediately.

But liberation brought new uncertainties:

**Where to go?** Most refugees' homes in Europe were destroyed or occupied. Family members they'd left behind were likely dead.

**Displaced Persons status**: Refugees were now stateless, needing new countries to accept them.

**Cold War beginning**: China was entering civil war between Nationalists and Communists, making long-term residence uncertain.

### The Diaspora

By 1949, when the Communists took power, almost all the Jewish refugees had left Shanghai:

- **Israel** (established 1948): Many emigrated there, finally reaching a Jewish state
- **United States**: Some obtained visas after years of waiting
- **Australia, Canada, South America**: Various countries accepted refugees
- **Europe**: A few returned, though most couldn't face returning to the continent of their persecution

The Russian and Sephardic Jewish communities also largely left China after 1949, though some families remained into the 1950s.

By the mid-1950s, Shanghai's Jewish population, which had peaked at 25,000-30,000, had dwindled to almost nothing.

## The Chinese Heroes

While most refugees interacted with ordinary Chinese residents, several Chinese individuals played particularly heroic roles:

### He Fengshan (何凤山)

He Fengshan was the Chinese consul-general in Vienna from 1938-1940. Despite orders from his superiors to limit visa issuance, He issued thousands of visas to Shanghai for Jews desperate to escape Austria. His actions saved an estimated 5,000-10,000 lives.

He lost his position because of his actions, but he never sought recognition. His role wasn't widely known until decades after his death. In 2000, Israel recognized him as "Righteous Among the Nations"—the honor given to non-Jews who saved Jews during the Holocaust.

### Wang Tiya (王体雅)

Wang was a Chinese police officer in Shanghai who secretly provided information and protection to Jews in the ghetto, warning them of Japanese inspections and helping them avoid arrest. His story is less documented but represents the quiet acts of courage that saved lives.

### Ordinary Shanghai Residents

Countless unnamed Chinese residents helped in smaller ways: landlords who didn't report refugees who violated ghetto rules, merchants who extended credit, doctors who treated patients who couldn't pay, neighbors who shared food during shortages.

These acts weren't always purely altruistic—economic interest played a role, and not all interactions were positive. But in a time and place of extraordinary hardship, many Chinese residents showed humanity toward desperate strangers.

## Why China's Role Matters

The Shanghai refuge was unique in several ways:

**Scale**: 20,000 refugees represents a significant percentage of those who escaped Nazi-controlled Europe. While this is a small fraction of those who died, every life saved matters.

**Timing**: Shanghai remained open when almost everywhere else was closed. For refugees who arrived 1938-1941, Shanghai was often the only option.

**Survival rate**: Almost all refugees who reached Shanghai survived the war. This contrasts starkly with Jews who remained in Europe, where two-thirds died.

**Moral example**: At a time when wealthy, powerful nations turned away Jewish refugees (including ships full of refugees that were sent back to Europe and death), Shanghai—a poor, occupied, war-torn city—took them in.

This is a point of pride for Chinese history and a rebuke to those countries that closed their doors.

## Remembering Shanghai's Jews

### The Shanghai Jewish Refugees Museum

Located in Hongkou at the site of the Ohel Moshe Synagogue, this museum documents the history of Jewish refugees in Shanghai. It includes:

- Photographs and documents
- Personal testimonies
- Recreated living spaces
- Interactive exhibits

The museum has become a site of Jewish heritage tourism, with descendants of refugees visiting to see where their parents or grandparents survived.

### The "Righteous Among Nations" Honors

Israel has recognized Chinese diplomats and individuals who saved Jews as "Righteous Among Nations," planting trees in their honor at Yad Vashem, Israel's Holocaust memorial.

### Continuing Connections

Some descendants of Shanghai refugees maintain connections to Shanghai, visiting regularly or supporting cultural exchanges. Chinese Jewish history has become a bridge between Chinese and Jewish communities.

## Lessons from This History

The Shanghai story offers several important lessons:

**Bureaucratic accidents can save lives**: Shanghai was open not because of a deliberate humanitarian policy but because of a quirk of international governance. Yet it saved 20,000 people.

**Ordinary people can be heroes**: From He Fengshan risking his career to issue visas, to Chinese neighbors sharing food, to Sephardic Jews funding relief—ordinary people made extraordinary choices.

**Refugees contribute**: The Jewish refugees, despite their suffering, contributed to Shanghai—opening businesses, providing services, enriching culture. Refugees are not just recipients of charity but potential contributors.

**Shared humanity transcends difference**: Chinese and Jewish refugees had little in common culturally or linguistically, yet they lived alongside each other, helped each other, and sometimes built genuine relationships.

**Historical memory matters**: For decades, this history was little known. Now it's being recovered and commemorated, ensuring the courage and suffering of both Chinese helpers and Jewish refugees is not forgotten.

## Conclusion

The Shanghai Ghetto was not truly a ghetto in the Nazi sense—it was a restricted area characterized by poverty and hardship, but not by systematic murder. The 20,000 Jews who found refuge in Shanghai were among the lucky ones of the Holocaust era. They survived.

They survived partly because of geographical accident, partly because of the courage of Chinese diplomats like He Fengshan, partly because Japanese occupation policy differed from Nazi policy, and partly because ordinary Shanghai residents, themselves struggling, made space for desperate strangers.

When the history of the Holocaust is told, the focus is rightly on the six million who died and the nations that participated in or enabled their murder. But there is also this story: the story of a Chinese city that, in its own complicated way, saved 20,000 Jewish lives.

As the Talmud teaches: "Whoever saves a single life is considered to have saved the entire world." Shanghai saved 20,000 worlds.
