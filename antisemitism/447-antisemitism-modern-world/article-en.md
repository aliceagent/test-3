# Antisemitism in the Modern World: From the Far Right to the Far Left


---

Many people assume that antisemitism is primarily a problem of the far right—neo-Nazis, white supremacists, and fascist movements. While these groups are indeed antisemitic, Jew-hatred in the 21st century is far more complex. It appears across the political spectrum, takes different forms in different contexts, and has adapted to modern technologies and political movements.

Understanding contemporary antisemitism requires recognizing that it doesn't fit neatly into traditional political categories. A white supremacist in the United States, a left-wing activist in Europe, an Islamist militant in the Middle East, and a conspiracy theorist on social media may all express antisemitic views—but their ideologies, justifications, and targets may differ significantly.

This article examines how antisemitism manifests in the modern world, from the far right to the far left, and across different cultural and political contexts.

## Far-Right Antisemitism

### White Supremacy and Neo-Nazism

The most traditional form of modern antisemitism comes from explicit white supremacist and neo-Nazi movements. This antisemitism is:

**Racial**: Viewing Jews as a distinct, inferior race that corrupts white European civilization

**Conspiratorial**: Believing Jews secretly control governments, banks, media, and international organizations

**Eliminationist**: Often calling for removal or destruction of Jews

These movements idolize Hitler, deny or celebrate the Holocaust, and promote explicitly antisemitic ideology. They're found primarily in North America, Europe, Russia, and parts of Latin America.

### Recent Examples

**Charlottesville (2017)**: White supremacists marched chanting "Jews will not replace us" and held a rally that turned deadly. The antisemitism was explicit and central to the event.

**Pittsburgh Tree of Life Synagogue (2018)**: A gunman motivated by antisemitic conspiracy theories about Jewish organizations helping immigrants killed eleven worshippers. He believed Jews were orchestrating the "replacement" of white Americans.

**Poway, California (2019)**: Another white supremacist opened fire in a synagogue, killing one and wounding three, citing similar replacement conspiracy theories.

**Halle, Germany (2019)**: A neo-Nazi attempted to storm a synagogue on Yom Kippur, killing two people outside when he couldn't breach the door.

### The "Great Replacement" Theory

Contemporary far-right antisemitism often centers on "replacement" conspiracy theories: the idea that Jews are orchestrating mass immigration and multiculturalism to "replace" white European populations. This updates traditional antisemitic conspiracy theories for the immigration politics of the 21st century.

## Far-Left Antisemitism

Left-wing antisemitism is more controversial to identify because it rarely presents itself as antisemitism. Instead, it often appears as anti-Zionism, anti-imperialism, or anti-capitalism—causes that are not inherently antisemitic but can serve as vehicles for Jew-hatred.

### Anti-Capitalism and "Banker" Conspiracy Theories

Some left-wing discourse about capitalism veers into antisemitism when it:

- Focuses obsessively on "Rothschilds" and Jewish bankers rather than systemic analysis
- Portrays capitalism as a Jewish conspiracy rather than an economic system
- Uses imagery of hook-nosed bankers controlling the world
- Suggests that eliminating Jewish influence would solve economic problems

These tropes recycle old antisemitic ideas in progressive language. The problem isn't criticism of capitalism or banks—it's when that criticism singles out Jews as uniquely responsible.

### Anti-Zionism Crossing Into Antisemitism

Much debate centers on when criticism of Israel becomes antisemitic. Anti-Zionism (opposition to Jewish statehood) is not automatically antisemitic—Jews themselves debate Zionism. But it crosses into antisemitism when it:

- Denies Jews alone the right to self-determination while supporting it for other peoples
- Holds Israel to standards applied to no other nation
- Demonizes Israel as uniquely evil, comparing it to Nazi Germany
- Blames all Jews worldwide for Israeli government actions
- Uses antisemitic imagery (Jews as child-killers, blood-drinkers, etc.) in anti-Israel rhetoric
- Calls for Israel's destruction while offering no vision for what happens to its Jewish population

### Examples of Left-Wing Antisemitism

**UK Labour Party Controversy (2015-2020)**: Under Jeremy Corbyn's leadership, the Labour Party faced repeated accusations of tolerating antisemitism among members and officials. Incidents included denying antisemitism, downplaying Holocaust memorial, conspiracy theories about Israel, and creating a hostile environment for Jewish members. An investigation found the party had violated equality law.

**Women's March Split (2018-2019)**: The U.S. Women's March organization faced controversy when some leaders' associations with Louis Farrakhan (leader of the Nation of Islam, known for antisemitic statements) and their own antisemitic comments came to light, leading to organizational breakdown.

**Academic Boycotts**: Some academic boycotts of Israel have raised concerns when they target only Israeli Jewish institutions while not similarly boycotting other countries with serious human rights issues, or when they create hostile environments for Jewish students and scholars.

## Islamist Antisemitism

A particularly virulent form of contemporary antisemitism comes from Islamist movements (not to be confused with Islam as a religion or Muslims generally).

### Characteristics

**Theological**: Interpreting Islamic texts to portray Jews as eternal enemies of Muslims

**Political**: Linking opposition to Israel with broader antisemitic conspiracy theories

**Apocalyptic**: In extreme cases, viewing the elimination of Jews as a religious imperative

**Imitative**: Importing European antisemitic texts like the Protocols of the Elders of Zion

### Examples

**Hamas Charter (1988)**: The original charter quoted the Protocols of the Elders of Zion and blamed Jews for the French Revolution, World Wars, and numerous other events. (A revised version from 2017 softened some language but maintained rejection of Israel's existence.)

**Hezbollah**: Hassan Nasrallah, leader of Hezbollah, has made numerous antisemitic statements beyond criticism of Israel, including claims that Jews are spreading corruption and that they gathered in Israel so they would be easier targets.

**Iranian Regime**: Official Iranian media frequently publish Holocaust denial, antisemitic cartoons, and conspiracy theories. The government has sponsored Holocaust denial conferences.

**Terror Attacks**: ISIS, Al-Qaeda, and other jihadist groups have explicitly targeted Jews, with attacks on Jewish institutions in France, Belgium, Tunisia, India, and elsewhere, often accompanied by explicitly antisemitic rhetoric.

### Important Distinction

It's crucial to distinguish between:

- **Islamist antisemitism**: Specific ideological movements that incorporate Jew-hatred
- **Muslim individuals**: The vast majority of whom are not antisemitic and many of whom oppose Islamist movements
- **Criticism of Islamist antisemitism**: Which is necessary and not itself Islamophobic

Conflating these leads either to ignoring real antisemitism or to unfairly stigmatizing all Muslims.

## Social Media and Online Antisemitism

The internet has dramatically changed how antisemitism spreads.

### Characteristics of Online Antisemitism

**Anonymity**: People express views online they wouldn't voice publicly

**Amplification**: A small number of antisemites can seem like many through multiple accounts and bot networks

**Radicalization**: Algorithms can create rabbit holes leading from mild conspiracy theories to extreme antisemitism

**Coordination**: Antisemites organize harassment campaigns, doxing, and threats

**Coded language**: Using symbols and dog whistles to evade content moderation (triple parentheses around Jewish names, "globalist" as code for Jew, etc.)

### Platforms and Movements

**4chan/8chan**: Anonymous imageboards where explicit antisemitism is normalized and extreme ideologies incubate

**Gab**: A social network that markets itself as "free speech" and has become home to neo-Nazis and white supremacists

**Telegram**: Encrypted messaging where extremist groups organize

**YouTube**: While the platform moderates content, its recommendation algorithm has been criticized for leading users toward extremist content

**Twitter/X**: Harassment campaigns frequently target Jewish journalists, activists, and public figures

### QAnon and Contemporary Conspiracy Theories

QAnon, the conspiracy theory movement claiming that a cabal of Satan-worshipping pedophiles runs the world, recycles classic antisemitic tropes even when not explicitly mentioning Jews. The "cabal" language, focus on "globalists," and other elements echo Protocols of the Elders of Zion.

Similarly, conspiracy theories about COVID-19, vaccines, and 5G technology often eventually blame Jews or use Jewish individuals (like George Soros) as proof of conspiracy.

## Institutional and Political Antisemitism

Beyond individual prejudice, antisemitism appears in institutions and political movements.

### United Nations

The UN has passed more resolutions condemning Israel than all other countries combined. While legitimate criticism of Israel is appropriate, the disproportionate focus—while ignoring or minimizing atrocities by other nations—suggests bias. UN bodies have sometimes featured explicitly antisemitic rhetoric from delegates.

### BDS Movement

The Boycott, Divestment, and Sanctions movement against Israel is debated. Supporters argue it's legitimate political protest against occupation. Critics argue it applies unique standards to the Jewish state, echoes historical antisemitic boycotts, and many BDS advocates support eliminating Israel entirely rather than changing specific policies.

### Intersectionality Debates

Some progressive spaces have excluded Jews or Jewish issues from "intersectional" frameworks, treating Jews as white oppressors rather than as a minority with their own history of persecution. This has led to Jewish students and activists sometimes feeling unwelcome in spaces that claim to oppose all bigotry.

## Campus Antisemitism

University campuses have become sites of significant antisemitic incidents:

- Swastikas and antisemitic graffiti
- Disruption of Jewish events
- Speakers promoting Holocaust denial or explicit antisemitism
- Jewish students reporting hostile environments
- Incidents where criticism of Israel involves harassment of Jewish students

This comes from both far-right sources (such as white supremacist flyering campaigns) and far-left sources (such as protests that cross into harassment).

## Measuring Modern Antisemitism

Various organizations track antisemitic incidents:

The **Anti-Defamation League** reports that antisemitic incidents in the United States reached near-historic levels in recent years, including assaults, harassment, and vandalism.

**European Jewish Congress** surveys show increasing numbers of European Jews considering emigration due to antisemitism.

**The Community Security Trust** in the UK recorded its highest-ever number of antisemitic incidents in recent years.

Global surveys show that traditional antisemitic beliefs (Jews have too much power, Jews are more loyal to Israel than their home countries, etc.) remain widespread in many countries.

## The Horseshoe Theory

Some scholars describe contemporary antisemitism as "horseshoe-shaped"—the far left and far right, though opposites in most ways, converge in their antisemitism. Both may:

- Engage in conspiracy theories about Jewish power
- Demonize Israel beyond reason
- Minimize or deny antisemitism
- Exclude Jews from coalition spaces

This doesn't mean left and right antisemitism are identical—their ideological frameworks and specific expressions differ significantly. But the convergence is notable.

## Why Modern Antisemitism Persists

Several factors explain antisemitism's persistence:

**Adaptability**: Antisemitism morphs to fit different ideologies. It can be religious or secular, racial or political, right-wing or left-wing.

**Explanatory power**: Conspiracy theories about Jewish control offer simple explanations for complex problems.

**Israel**: The Israeli-Palestinian conflict provides a contemporary focus for antisemitism, though this is often a pretext rather than the real cause.

**Social media**: Technology enables rapid spread of antisemitic content and coordination of harassment.

**Historical amnesia**: As Holocaust survivors die and memory fades, constraints on antisemitic expression weaken.

**Scapegoating**: Economic anxiety, social change, and political instability create demand for scapegoats, and Jews remain a convenient target.

## Recognition and Response

Fighting modern antisemitism requires:

**Recognition**: Acknowledging antisemitism across the political spectrum, not just from opposing political camps

**Education**: Teaching about antisemitism's history, forms, and impacts

**Platform accountability**: Pressuring social media companies to enforce policies against hate speech

**Coalition building**: Jews working with other minorities and allies to oppose all forms of bigotry

**Security**: Physical protection for Jewish institutions and individuals

**Political will**: Leaders rejecting antisemitism even when it comes from allies or supporters

**Nuance**: Distinguishing legitimate criticism from bigotry, while also not dismissing antisemitism claims reflexively

## Conclusion

Modern antisemitism is not a relic of the past or the province of one political extreme. It appears on the far right in the form of white supremacy and "replacement" conspiracies. It appears on the far left when anti-capitalism or anti-Zionism slide into demonization of Jews. It appears in Islamist movements that blend anti-Israel politics with theological antisemitism. It appears in online spaces where anonymity and algorithms create radicalization pipelines.

For readers in countries like China with limited Jewish presence, this might seem like a distant issue. But the patterns of scapegoating, conspiracy thinking, and how prejudice adapts to new contexts are universally relevant. The mechanisms that produce antisemitism can produce hatred of any minority—Muslims, immigrants, religious groups, ethnic minorities, LGBTQ+ people, or others.

Understanding modern antisemitism means recognizing that:

- Bigotry transcends traditional political categories
- Good intentions don't prevent prejudice
- Conspiracy thinking is dangerous regardless of target
- Vigilance is required across all political and social movements
- No society is immune

The fight against antisemitism is part of the broader fight against hatred, dehumanization, and conspiracy thinking that threaten pluralistic, democratic societies everywhere. It requires honesty about where prejudice appears, including in movements and communities we otherwise support. Only through such honesty can we build the solidarity necessary to oppose hatred in all its forms.
