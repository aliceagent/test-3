# The Protocols of the Elders of Zion: The Most Dangerous Forgery in History

*Section: Antisemitism*

In 1903, a Russian newspaper published a sensational document claiming to reveal a secret Jewish conspiracy to control the world. Titled "The Protocols of the Elders of Zion," it purported to be the minutes of secret meetings where Jewish leaders outlined their plan to destroy Christian civilization, manipulate economies, control governments, and establish a Jewish world empire.

The document was a complete fabrication. It was plagiarized from earlier works of fiction, created by Russian secret police to justify antisemitic policies, and filled with internal contradictions and absurdities. Multiple investigations proved it was a forgery.

Yet the Protocols became one of the most influential books of the 20th century, inspiring pogroms, shaping Nazi ideology, and continuing to circulate today in both print and digital form. It has been called "the deadliest text of the modern age."

Understanding how this forgery was created, spread, and weaponized is essential for understanding not only antisemitism but also how disinformation and conspiracy theories function in the modern world.

## The Origins: A Russian Forgery

The Protocols were fabricated around 1902-1903 by agents of the Okhrana, the secret police of Tsarist Russia. The exact authors are uncertain, but historians believe the primary creators were Pyotr Rachkovsky, head of the Okhrana's foreign branch in Paris, and his associate Matvei Golovinski.

### Why Create It?

Russia at the turn of the 20th century was in turmoil. Revolutionary movements were gaining strength. Industrialization was disrupting traditional society. The regime needed a scapegoat to redirect popular anger away from the Tsar and toward a common enemy.

Jews were the perfect target: a visible minority, already subject to discrimination, associated with both revolutionary socialism (because many revolutionaries were Jewish) and capitalism (because some prominent bankers were Jewish). The Protocols provided a unified conspiracy theory that could explain all of Russia's problems as the result of Jewish machinations.

### The Plagiarism

The Protocols were not even originally written material. They were clumsily plagiarized from several earlier sources:

**"Dialogue in Hell Between Machiavelli and Montesquieu" (1864)**: Written by French satirist Maurice Joly, this book was a political satire criticizing Napoleon III. It had nothing to do with Jews. Large sections were copied almost word-for-word into the Protocols, with "Jews" substituted for Joly's characters.

**"Biarritz" (1868)**: A German novel by Hermann Goedsche (writing as "Sir John Retcliffe") featured a fictional scene of rabbis meeting in a cemetery to plot world domination. This scene was later circulated as "fact" and influenced the Protocols.

**Earlier antisemitic pamphlets**: Various Russian and French antisemitic texts contributed ideas and phrases.

The plagiarism was sloppy. Sometimes pronouns don't match. Sometimes the logic breaks down mid-paragraph. This is not a sophisticated work—it's a cut-and-paste job by people who didn't care about coherence, only about impact.

## The Content: What the Protocols Claim

The Protocols are structured as 24 "protocols" or chapters, supposedly recording the secret plans of the "Elders of Zion" (a completely fictional council of Jewish leaders).

The alleged Jewish conspiracy includes:

- **Economic manipulation**: Creating economic crises, controlling gold supplies, manipulating stock markets, and owning banks to impoverish gentiles
- **Media control**: Owning newspapers and publishers to shape public opinion and suppress truth
- **Political subversion**: Promoting democracy and liberalism to weaken governments, while secretly pulling the strings of rulers
- **Educational corruption**: Controlling universities to spread false ideas and undermine traditional morality
- **Social degeneracy**: Promoting alcoholism, sexual immorality, and cultural decay to weaken Christian societies
- **Revolutionary movements**: Creating and funding revolutions to destabilize governments
- **Future tyranny**: Planning to eventually reveal themselves and establish a Jewish world dictatorship

The irony is brutal: the Protocols accuse Jews of exactly what authoritarian regimes actually do—control media, suppress dissent, manipulate information, and concentrate power.

## Early Exposure and Spread

The Protocols were first published in the Russian newspaper Znamya in 1903, then included in Sergei Nilus's book "The Great in the Small" in 1905. They initially received limited attention.

But after the Russian Revolution of 1917, which overthrew the Tsar, White Russian émigrés (anti-Bolsheviks) spread the Protocols internationally, claiming the revolution proved the Jewish conspiracy was real. The fact that some Bolshevik leaders were ethnically Jewish was cited as "evidence," ignoring that most Jews opposed the Bolsheviks and that the revolutionaries were atheists who rejected all religion, including Judaism.

## The Times Investigation

In 1921, Philip Graves, a correspondent for The Times of London, was shown a book by a Russian émigré in Constantinople. It was Joly's "Dialogue in Hell." Comparing it to the Protocols, Graves documented the plagiarism in a series of articles.

The Times demonstrated conclusively that the Protocols were a forgery, showing parallel passages and explaining the historical context. Other investigations followed, all reaching the same conclusion.

But the truth didn't matter to those who wanted to believe. As the saying goes, "A lie can travel halfway around the world while the truth is putting on its shoes."

## The Protocols Go Global

Despite being exposed as a fake, the Protocols spread widely:

### United States

Henry Ford, the automobile magnate, was an avid believer. He published his own antisemitic newspaper, The Dearborn Independent, which serialized the Protocols and distributed them widely. Ford later apologized, but not before half a million copies were distributed.

The Protocols influenced American antisemitic movements and contributed to restrictive immigration laws in the 1920s that limited Jewish refugees from Europe.

### Germany

The Protocols were translated into German and became a bestseller in the 1920s. Adolf Hitler cited them in Mein Kampf:

> "To what extent the whole existence of this people is based on a continuous lie is shown incomparably by the Protocols of the Wise Men of Zion... For once this book has become the common property of a people, the Jewish menace may be considered as broken."

Hitler knew or should have known they were fake—the exposure was widely publicized. But he called them genuine anyway because they were useful. Nazi propaganda minister Joseph Goebbels explicitly stated that even if they were fake, they described "reality" as the Nazis saw it.

The Protocols became required reading in German schools under the Nazis. They helped prepare the psychological ground for the Holocaust by depicting Jews as an existential threat requiring elimination.

### Arab World

Starting in the 1930s and accelerating after the establishment of Israel, the Protocols spread throughout the Arab world. They were translated into Arabic, published by governments, taught in schools, and quoted by political leaders.

The Protocols provided a ready-made explanation for the establishment of Israel and the ongoing conflict: not as a complex political situation with multiple causes, but as proof of a Jewish conspiracy to control the Middle East and eventually the world.

## Persistence Despite Debunking

What makes the Protocols particularly insidious is their resistance to debunking. Multiple investigations have proven them fake:

- The 1921 Times investigation showed the plagiarism
- A 1934-1935 trial in Switzerland ruled them a forgery
- Historians have documented their creation by the Okhrana
- The United States Senate issued a report in 1964 calling them "fabricated"
- Countless scholars have analyzed and debunked them

Yet they continue to circulate because:

**Confirmation bias**: People who already believe in Jewish conspiracy theories see the Protocols as confirmation rather than seeking to verify their authenticity.

**Conspiracy thinking**: When you expose the Protocols as fake, believers claim the exposure is *itself* part of the cover-up. In this framework, denying the Protocols proves they're true, because of course the conspirators would deny it.

**Availability**: The Protocols are free, widely translated, and easy to find online. Rebuttals require more effort to locate and understand.

**Simplicity**: The Protocols offer a simple explanation for complex problems. Economic troubles? Jewish conspiracy. Political instability? Jewish conspiracy. War? Jewish conspiracy. This simplicity is appealing compared to actually understanding complicated historical and social forces.

## Modern Manifestations

The Protocols remain dangerously active today:

### Middle East

The Protocols are frequently quoted in sermons, political speeches, and media across parts of the Middle East. The 2002 Egyptian television series "Horseman Without a Horse" dramatized the Protocols as historical fact, airing during Ramadan when viewership was highest. A 2003 Syrian television series, "The Diaspora," similarly treated the Protocols as authentic.

While some Arab intellectuals and leaders have rejected the Protocols as fabricated, they remain influential in popular discourse.

### Internet and Social Media

The Protocols are freely available on countless websites, often presented without context or with claims that their exposure as a fake is itself a conspiracy. They're cited in online forums, social media posts, and conspiracy theory videos.

Contemporary conspiracy theories about "globalists," "international bankers," or "cultural Marxists" often echo Protocols themes while avoiding explicit mention of Jews—a process called "coded antisemitism."

### White Nationalist and Extremist Groups

Neo-Nazis, white supremacists, and other extremist movements cite the Protocols as "proof" of their worldview. Some acknowledge they're a forgery but claim they're nonetheless "true" in describing Jewish behavior.

### Unlikely Believers

The Protocols have been cited by figures across the political spectrum, from far-right fascists to far-left anti-imperialists, from Islamic fundamentalists to Christian nationalists. This demonstrates that antisemitic conspiracy thinking transcends traditional political categories.

## The Structure of the Conspiracy Theory

The Protocols exemplify how conspiracy theories function:

**Unfalsifiability**: Any evidence against the conspiracy is explained as part of the conspiracy. If scholars say it's fake, that proves the conspiracy's power. If Jews deny it, that proves their guilt.

**Totality**: The conspiracy explains everything. Every event, every problem, every social change—all part of the plan. This eliminates the need for complex analysis.

**Hidden enemy**: The conspirators are everywhere and nowhere, controlling things in secret. You can't see them, which proves how powerful they are.

**Elect status**: Believers see themselves as enlightened truth-seekers against the deceived masses. This provides psychological satisfaction.

**Solution**: The conspiracy theory implies a solution—eliminate or neutralize the conspirators. This is where conspiracy theories become genocidal.

## How to Counter the Protocols

Fighting the Protocols and similar disinformation requires multiple approaches:

**Education**: Teaching critical thinking, media literacy, and how to identify forgeries and propaganda.

**Historical context**: Explaining the actual history of the Protocols' creation and the pattern of antisemitic scapegoating.

**Alternative explanations**: Providing accurate, evidence-based explanations for complex social and political phenomena.

**Inoculation**: Exposing people to weakened versions of conspiracy theories alongside refutations, so they develop resistance.

**Platform accountability**: Pressuring social media companies and publishers to not amplify known disinformation.

**Personal conversations**: One-on-one dialogue can sometimes reach people that public debunking cannot, though this requires patience and skill.

## Why This Matters for Non-Jews

The Protocols are an antisemitic text, but their danger extends beyond the Jewish community.

First, conspiracy thinking affects all of society. People who believe the Protocols often believe other false and dangerous ideas, making them vulnerable to manipulation by demagogues and extremists.

Second, the techniques used to spread the Protocols—plagiarism, fabrication, emotional manipulation, exploiting social anxieties—are used against many groups. Understanding how it worked against Jews helps identify when it's used against others.

Third, societies that embrace conspiracy theories over evidence and reason are unstable and dangerous for everyone. The same impulses that led people to believe the Protocols contributed to catastrophic events like the Holocaust and countless pogroms.

Fourth, the Protocols demonstrate that even obviously false information can have real-world impact. In the internet age, this lesson is more relevant than ever.

## Conclusion

The Protocols of the Elders of Zion is the most successful and dangerous forgery in history. Despite being proven fake over a century ago, it continues to poison minds, fuel hatred, and inspire violence.

It serves as a case study in how disinformation works:

- Created by those in power to scapegoat a minority
- Plagiarized from unrelated sources
- Spread by true believers and cynical opportunists alike
- Resistant to debunking through conspiracy logic
- Adaptable across cultures and contexts
- Simple enough to be compelling, vague enough to apply to any situation

For readers in China and other countries without significant historical Jewish presence, the Protocols might seem like a distant, foreign issue. But the patterns it represents—scapegoating, conspiracy thinking, the weaponization of forged documents, the persistence of disinformation despite evidence—these are universal.

In a world of information overload, the lesson of the Protocols is clear: verify sources, question narratives that sound too convenient, resist the temptation to blame complex problems on a single malevolent group, and remember that the most dangerous lies are often the ones people *want* to believe.

As historian Norman Cohn wrote, the Protocols served as a "warrant for genocide." Understanding how a clumsy forgery could serve such a function is essential to ensuring it never happens again—to Jews or to any other people.
