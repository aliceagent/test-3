# The Holocaust: A Concise History for Chinese Readers


---

Between 1933 and 1945, Nazi Germany and its collaborators systematically murdered six million Jews—two-thirds of Europe's Jewish population. This genocide, known as the Holocaust (or in Hebrew, the *Shoah*, meaning "catastrophe"), represents one of the darkest chapters in human history.

For readers encountering this history for the first time, especially those from regions without direct experience of the Holocaust, this article provides a concise overview of what happened, how it happened, and why it matters today.

## Before the Holocaust: Jews in Europe

Before World War II, approximately 9.5 million Jews lived in Europe. They had lived there for centuries, contributing to European culture, science, business, and arts despite facing periodic persecution.

By 1933, Jewish communities were particularly well-established in:
- **Poland**: 3.3 million Jews, forming vibrant communities in cities and towns
- **Soviet Union**: 3 million Jews, living primarily in western regions
- **Romania**: 750,000 Jews
- **Germany**: 525,000 Jews, highly integrated into German society
- **Hungary**: 445,000 Jews
- **France, Netherlands, Greece, Czechoslovakia**, and other countries with significant Jewish populations

Many European Jews were secular and integrated into their societies. Others maintained traditional religious lifestyles. They spoke various languages: Yiddish, German, Polish, Russian, Hungarian, and others. They worked in all professions and belonged to all social classes.

What they had in common was being Jewish—and that made them targets when the Nazis came to power.

## Hitler's Rise to Power (1933)

Adolf Hitler and the Nazi Party came to power in Germany in January 1933, not through violent revolution but through democratic elections followed by political maneuvering. Hitler was appointed Chancellor by President Hindenburg, then quickly consolidated absolute power.

Nazi ideology was explicitly antisemitic from the beginning. Hitler's book *Mein Kampf* (1925) blamed Jews for Germany's defeat in World War I, described Jews as racial inferiors and parasites, and portrayed the conflict between "Aryans" and Jews as existential. But initially, few believed Hitler would actually implement his genocidal vision.

## The Path to Genocide: 1933-1941

The Holocaust didn't begin with gas chambers. It was a gradual process of dehumanization, disenfranchisement, and escalating violence.

### Legal Persecution (1933-1939)

**1933**: Boycott of Jewish businesses; Jews dismissed from civil service and universities; burning of books by Jewish authors.

**1935**: The Nuremberg Laws stripped Jews of German citizenship, forbade marriage between Jews and non-Jews, and defined who counted as Jewish based on ancestry.

**1938**: *Kristallnacht* (Night of Broken Glass) – coordinated nationwide pogrom where Nazis destroyed 7,500 Jewish businesses, burned 1,400 synagogues, killed 91 Jews, and arrested 30,000 Jewish men who were sent to concentration camps.

**1939**: Jews forced into ghettos; forbidden from many occupations; subject to curfews and movement restrictions.

Each step made Jews more vulnerable, isolated, and poor. Each step was designed to make Jews want to leave Germany—but increasingly, no country would take them. At the 1938 Évian Conference, 32 countries met to discuss Jewish refugees, and nearly all refused to increase immigration quotas.

### World War II Begins (September 1939)

When Germany invaded Poland, it gained control over Poland's 3.3 million Jews—the largest Jewish population in Europe. The Nazis immediately implemented harsh anti-Jewish measures, herding Jews into ghettos—sealed-off sections of cities where Jews were forced to live in overcrowded, unsanitary conditions with minimal food.

The largest was the **Warsaw Ghetto**, where 450,000 Jews were crammed into 1.3 square miles. Thousands died of starvation and disease. Similar ghettos were established across occupied Poland.

### Invasion of Soviet Union (June 1941)

When Germany invaded the Soviet Union in June 1941, the genocide entered a new phase. Mobile killing squads called *Einsatzgruppen* followed the German army, rounding up Jewish communities and shooting them in mass executions.

At **Babi Yar** (Ukraine), 33,771 Jews were shot in two days in September 1941. Across occupied Soviet territory, 1.5 million Jews were murdered this way—shot and buried in mass graves, often forced to dig their own graves first.

## The "Final Solution" (1941-1945)

In late 1941, Nazi leadership decided on the complete annihilation of European Jewry—what they called the "Final Solution to the Jewish Question."

### The Wannsee Conference (January 1942)

At a villa in Wannsee, outside Berlin, Nazi officials met to coordinate the genocide. They discussed logistics: how to round up Jews across Europe, how to transport them, how to kill them efficiently, what to do with their property.

The conference lasted 90 minutes. It was bureaucratic, administrative, almost boring. This was the banality of evil—mass murder planned like a business meeting.

### The Death Camps

The Nazis built six dedicated extermination camps, all in occupied Poland:

**Auschwitz-Birkenau**: The largest and deadliest. Approximately 1.1 million people (mostly Jews) were murdered there. It combined a concentration camp (for slave labor) with extermination facilities (gas chambers and crematoria).

**Treblinka**: 900,000 murdered. Almost all were killed immediately upon arrival.

**Belzec**: 600,000 murdered.

**Sobibor**: 250,000 murdered.

**Chelmno**: 320,000 murdered.

**Majdanek**: 80,000 murdered.

### How the Killing Happened

Jews from across Europe were transported in sealed cattle cars, packed so tightly that many died before arriving. Journeys could take days without food, water, or sanitation.

Upon arrival, prisoners were divided: most (especially children, the elderly, and women with children) were sent immediately to gas chambers. Those selected for labor were worked to death, starved, beaten, and subjected to horrific conditions.

**Gas chambers** were disguised as shower rooms. Prisoners were ordered to undress and told they were going to bathe. They were packed into sealed rooms, and Zyklon B poison gas was released. Death came within 20 minutes. Bodies were then burned in crematoria or open pits.

The camps operated with industrial efficiency. Auschwitz could murder and cremate 6,000 people per day at peak operation.

### Collaboration and Bystanders

The Holocaust required the participation or complicity of many people beyond Nazi Germans:

**Collaborators**: Police, administrators, and volunteers in occupied countries helped identify Jews, guard ghettos, and sometimes participated directly in killings. France's Vichy government, for instance, rounded up Jews for deportation.

**The SS and German military**: Hundreds of thousands of Germans directly participated in genocide.

**Ordinary Germans**: Millions knew at least something about mass killings. Some disapproved but stayed silent. Some approved. Few actively resisted.

**Allied governments**: The United States, Britain, and other Allies knew about the extermination camps by 1942 but did not make stopping the Holocaust a military priority. They refused to bomb the railways leading to Auschwitz or the gas chambers themselves.

**Neutral countries**: Switzerland, Sweden, and others limited Jewish immigration, sending refugees back to their deaths.

**The Vatican**: Pope Pius XII did not publicly condemn the Holocaust, prioritizing neutrality over moral witness.

Yet there were also **rescuers**—individuals and groups who risked their lives to save Jews. Oskar Schindler in Czechoslovakia, Raoul Wallenberg in Hungary, the people of Denmark who smuggled most of Danish Jewry to neutral Sweden, countless families who hid Jews. These "Righteous Among the Nations" remind us that choices existed, even under Nazi terror.

## Life and Death in the Camps

Daily life in concentration camps was designed to dehumanize and destroy:

- Prisoners wore striped uniforms and were identified by numbers tattooed on their arms
- Starvation rations: watery soup and a small piece of bread
- Forced labor: 12+ hour days in factories, mines, or construction
- Random beatings and executions
- No sanitation, leading to rampant disease
- Medical experiments: Nazi doctors performed horrific experiments on prisoners, especially children, without anesthesia

Many prisoners died not in gas chambers but from starvation, exhaustion, disease, or being worked to death. Those who could no longer work were sent to be killed.

## Resistance

Despite impossible odds, Jews resisted:

**Warsaw Ghetto Uprising (April-May 1943)**: When Nazis came to liquidate the ghetto, Jewish fighters fought back with smuggled weapons. They held out for nearly a month before being crushed. Most fighters died, but they showed that Jews would not go passively.

**Sobibor and Treblinka uprisings**: Prisoners in these death camps organized revolts, killing guards and allowing some prisoners to escape.

**Partisans**: Some Jews escaped to forests and joined resistance movements, fighting the Nazis through sabotage and guerrilla warfare.

**Spiritual resistance**: Maintaining religious practice, education, cultural activities, and human dignity in the face of dehumanization was itself a form of resistance.

## Liberation and Aftermath (1944-1945)

As Allied forces advanced in 1944-1945, they began liberating camps:

**July 1944**: Soviet forces liberated Majdanek, finding evidence of mass murder.

**January 1945**: Soviet forces liberated Auschwitz, finding 7,000 surviving prisoners—emaciated, sick, barely alive.

**April-May 1945**: American and British forces liberated camps in Germany, including Bergen-Belsen, Buchenwald, and Dachau. Soldiers were shocked by what they found: piles of corpses, starving survivors, and evidence of systematic extermination.

Many survivors were too weak to survive liberation. Thousands died in the weeks after being freed.

## The Numbers

**Six million Jews murdered**, including:
- 3 million from Poland
- 1 million from Soviet territories
- 560,000 from Hungary
- 280,000 from Romania
- 165,000 from Germany
- 140,000 from Czechoslovakia
- 100,000 from Netherlands
- 77,000 from France
- 60,000 from Greece
- And Jews from every other European country

This represented **two-thirds of European Jewry** and **one-third of all Jews worldwide**.

Additionally, the Nazis killed:
- Approximately 200,000 Roma (Gypsies)
- Tens of thousands of people with disabilities
- Thousands of political prisoners, homosexuals, Jehovah's Witnesses, and others deemed undesirable

But the Holocaust was specifically the systematic attempt to annihilate the Jewish people.

### Impact on Jewish Demographics

Entire communities were wiped out. In Poland, where Jewish civilization had flourished for centuries, 90% of Jews were murdered. The Yiddish-speaking world of Eastern Europe—its language, culture, and traditions—was destroyed.

## Why Did It Happen?

The Holocaust was not inevitable. It required:

**Ideology**: The Nazi belief that Jews were subhuman and a threat to "Aryan" civilization.

**Bureaucracy**: A modern state apparatus to identify, transport, and kill millions efficiently.

**Technology**: Railways, industrial killing methods, record-keeping systems.

**Propaganda**: Years of dehumanizing Jews, making ordinary Germans see them as enemies rather than neighbors.

**War**: The chaos and violence of war created conditions where mass murder could occur.

**Complicity**: The participation, cooperation, or silence of millions.

**Vulnerability**: Jews were a minority without a state or army to defend them.

## Aftermath and Legacy

### Trials and Justice

The **Nuremberg Trials** (1945-1946) prosecuted major Nazi leaders for crimes against humanity. Many were executed; others imprisoned. Additional trials continued for decades, though most perpetrators were never prosecuted.

### Survivors

Survivors faced enormous challenges: trauma, grief (most had lost entire families), physical illness from camp conditions, and nowhere to go (their homes were destroyed or occupied by others).

Many were placed in **Displaced Persons camps** in Europe. Eventually, most emigrated to Israel (established 1948), the United States, or other countries.

### "Never Again"

The Holocaust gave rise to new concepts in international law:
- **Genocide**: A new term for the crime of attempting to destroy a people
- **Crimes against humanity**: Acts of mass atrocity recognized as international crimes
- **Universal jurisdiction**: The principle that certain crimes are so severe that any country can prosecute them

The phrase "Never Again" became a pledge to prevent future genocides—though tragically, genocides have occurred since (Cambodia, Rwanda, Bosnia, Darfur, and others).

### Creation of Israel

The Holocaust dramatically increased support for a Jewish state. Survivors needed refuge, and the world had seen what happens when Jews have no homeland. In 1948, Israel was established, providing a place where Jews could defend themselves.

### Ongoing Impact

The Holocaust remains central to Jewish identity and consciousness. "Yom HaShoah" (Holocaust Remembrance Day) is observed annually. Holocaust museums and memorials exist worldwide. Survivors and their descendants carry the trauma.

For the world, the Holocaust raised profound questions: How could a civilized nation commit such atrocities? How do ordinary people become complicit in evil? What are our obligations to prevent mass atrocity? These questions remain urgent.

## Holocaust Denial and Distortion

Despite overwhelming evidence—documents, photographs, films, survivor testimony, perpetrator confessions, and the camps themselves—some people deny the Holocaust happened or minimize its scale.

Holocaust denial is a form of antisemitism, an attempt to rehabilitate Nazi ideology by denying its greatest crime. It persists in some regions and online spaces, making Holocaust education even more crucial.

## Lessons for Today

The Holocaust teaches several crucial lessons:

**Prejudice can escalate**: What begins as discrimination can end in genocide.

**Bystander silence enables evil**: "All that is necessary for evil to triumph is for good people to do nothing."

**Bureaucracy can serve atrocity**: Modern institutions can be weaponized for mass murder.

**Dehumanization precedes violence**: Before you can kill people en masse, you must convince yourself they aren't fully human.

**Vigilance is necessary**: Democracy and human rights can be destroyed. Protecting them requires constant effort.

## Why This Matters for Chinese Readers

For readers in China, the Holocaust may seem distant—a European event with no direct Chinese connection. But there are important connections:

**Shanghai's Jewish refugees**: Before and during the Holocaust, Shanghai accepted approximately 20,000 Jewish refugees when other countries refused them. This history is explored in the next article.

**Universal lessons**: The Holocaust demonstrates what can happen when hatred goes unchecked. These lessons apply to any society and any minority group.

**Genocide prevention**: Understanding the Holocaust helps recognize warning signs of mass atrocity and the importance of international intervention.

**Historical knowledge**: The Holocaust is perhaps the most documented genocide in history. It provides insight into human capacity for both evil and goodness.

## Conclusion

The Holocaust was the systematic, bureaucratic, state-sponsored persecution and murder of six million Jews by Nazi Germany and its collaborators. It represents the most extreme manifestation of antisemitism and one of history's greatest crimes.

It was not an accident, an explosion of passion, or a byproduct of war. It was deliberate, planned, and executed with modern industrial efficiency. It required the active participation of many and the passive complicity of many more.

The survivors who rebuilt their lives, established communities, and insisted on telling their stories demonstrated remarkable resilience. Their testimony ensures we remember what happened and remain vigilant against hatred.

As survivor Elie Wiesel wrote: "For the dead and the living, we must bear witness." Understanding the Holocaust is part of that witness—acknowledging what humans are capable of at their worst, honoring those who were murdered, and committing to ensuring such atrocities never happen again.
