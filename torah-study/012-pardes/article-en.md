## Introduction

Have you ever noticed how a great poem can mean different things each time you read it? Or how a classic novel reveals new insights as you grow older? The same words on the page, yet each reading unlocks a different door.

Jewish tradition teaches that the Torah works exactly this way — but with a systematic framework for understanding its multiple layers. This framework is called **PaRDeS** (פַּרְדֵּס), a Hebrew acronym that literally means "orchard" or "paradise." Just as an orchard contains many trees bearing different fruits, the Torah contains multiple levels of meaning waiting to be discovered.

This approach to sacred text emerged during the medieval period — roughly corresponding to China's Song and Ming Dynasties (宋明, ~1000-1500 CE) — when Jewish scholars formalized what had been an oral tradition for centuries. The four levels of PaRDeS became the foundation for how Jews study and understand their most sacred text.

Understanding PaRDeS transforms your relationship with Torah from passive reading to active exploration. It shows you that when two people "disagree" about what a verse means, they may actually both be right — they're simply examining different levels of the same truth.

## The Four Levels of PaRDeS

### P (פ) — Pshat: The Simple Meaning

**Pshat** means the straightforward, literal meaning of the text. It answers the question: "What is this verse actually saying?"

When the Torah says "In the beginning, God created the heavens and the earth" (Genesis 1:1), the pshat is exactly that — a statement about the origin of the universe. No hidden meanings, no allegories, just the plain sense of the words.

The 11th-century French rabbi **Rashi** (Rabbi Shlomo Yitzchaki) is the undisputed master of pshat commentary. His explanations, printed on every page of the Talmud, clarify confusing words, resolve grammatical difficulties, and explain the simple narrative flow. Rashi lived during what corresponds to China's Northern Song Dynasty (北宋, 960-1127 CE).

**Why pshat matters:** Without understanding the simple meaning first, you're building a house without a foundation. All deeper interpretations must rest on a solid grasp of what the text actually says.

**Example:** When Torah commands to place "totafot between your eyes" (Deuteronomy 6:8), the pshat tells us this refers to tefillin (phylacteries) — the black leather boxes Jewish men wear during morning prayers. The Oral Torah, transmitted alongside the Written Torah, clarifies that "between your eyes" means on the forehead, not literally between the eyeballs.

### R (ר) — Remez: The Hint

**Remez** means "hint" or "allusion." This level looks for what the text is pointing toward — meanings that aren't stated explicitly but are hinted at through unusual word choices, numerical patterns, or subtle linguistic cues.

Hebrew is uniquely suited for remez because:
- Every Hebrew letter has a numerical value (gematria)
- Torah scrolls contain no vowels, allowing multiple pronunciations
- Similar-sounding words create intentional wordplay
- The precise choice of every word and letter is deliberate

**Example:** The Torah uses two different words for "and he said" when God speaks to Moses. Sometimes it's *vayomer* (ויאמר) and sometimes *vayedaber* (וידבר). The remez level notices this variation and asks: Why the different words? Traditional interpretation suggests *vayedaber* (from the root "to speak") indicates stricter speech, while *vayomer* (from the root "to say") indicates gentler communication.

Another example: When Jacob's name changes to Israel (Genesis 32:29), the numerical value (gematria) of the letters becomes significant. Scholars notice patterns where names appear in specific numbers throughout the text, hinting at deeper connections.

**Chinese parallel:** This resembles how classical Chinese poetry uses tonal patterns and character radicals to create layers of meaning. A character's component parts can hint at meanings beyond the surface definition.

### D (ד) — Drash: The Interpreted Meaning

**Drash** (or *Drush*) comes from the root word meaning "to seek" or "to interpret." This is the homiletic, interpretive level — where rabbis ask: "What is this text teaching us about how to live?"

Drash is found primarily in the **Midrash**, the vast collection of rabbinic interpretations compiled over centuries (roughly 200-500 CE, corresponding to China's Three Kingdoms through Jin Dynasty period, 三国-晋朝). The Midrash fills in narrative gaps, resolves apparent contradictions, and extracts moral lessons.

**Why drash matters:** The Torah isn't just history — it's instruction. Drash bridges the gap between ancient text and contemporary life, showing how timeless wisdom applies to your specific situation today.

**Example:** The Torah says Abraham's servant found Rebekah by a well (Genesis 24). The simple pshat is: that's where she happened to be. The drash asks: Why do so many crucial Torah encounters happen at wells? Isaac meets Rebekah at a well, Jacob meets Rachel at a well, Moses meets his wife Zipporah at a well. 

The Midrash teaches: Wells represent depth — you must dig beneath the surface to find living water. These righteous individuals recognized depth in their future spouses, looking past surface appearances to find spiritual substance.

Another famous drash: When Torah says "an eye for an eye" (Exodus 21:24), the oral tradition and Talmud explain this means monetary compensation equal to the value of an eye — not literal physical retaliation. This interpretation has been the accepted understanding since Sinai.

**Chinese parallel:** This mirrors how Confucian scholars interpreted the Analects, finding contemporary applications of ancient wisdom. Just as Confucius's students asked how his teachings applied to their governance and family relationships, the Midrash asks how Torah applies to daily life.

### S (ס) — Sod: The Secret, Mystical Meaning

**Sod** means "secret" or "mystery." This is the mystical, kabbalistic level — exploring the hidden spiritual dimensions of the text that aren't accessible through rational analysis alone.

Sod is primarily found in the **Zohar** ("Book of Splendor"), the foundational text of Jewish mysticism written in 13th-century Spain (corresponding to China's Yuan Dynasty, 元朝). The Zohar reveals how every Torah verse contains encoded information about the structure of the universe, the nature of the divine, and the inner workings of the soul.

**Why sod matters:** It reminds us that reality has dimensions beyond what we can see and measure. The Torah isn't just a historical document or moral guide — it's a blueprint of creation itself.

**Example:** The opening words of Genesis — *Bereishit bara Elohim* ("In the beginning, God created") — contain, according to Kabbalah, encoded information about the ten sefirot (divine emanations), the process of creation, and the relationship between the infinite God and finite universe.

When the Torah describes the Tabernacle (Exodus 25-40) with precise measurements and materials, sod sees this as a map of the spiritual structure of the cosmos. The menorah's seven branches correspond to seven divine attributes, the cherubim on the ark represent the marriage of divine wisdom and understanding.

**Important note:** Traditional Judaism teaches that sod should only be studied after mastering pshat, remez, and drash. It's compared to entering an orchard (*pardes*) — without proper preparation, you risk misunderstanding or misapplication. The Talmud tells a cautionary tale of four sages who "entered the orchard" of mystical study: one died, one went insane, one became a heretic, and only Rabbi Akiva "entered in peace and left in peace."

**Chinese parallel:** This resembles the esoteric dimensions of Daoist philosophy, where texts like the *Dao De Jing* (道德经) can be read at a simple philosophical level or as encoded teachings about spiritual cultivation, energy flow, and the hidden structure of reality. Both traditions recognize that sacred texts operate simultaneously on mundane and transcendent levels.

## Why PaRDeS Means "Orchard"

The acronym PaRDeS spells the Hebrew word for "orchard" or "paradise garden" (related to the English word "paradise"). This is no accident.

An orchard contains many trees, each bearing different fruits. Some fruits grow close to the ground, easy to pick (pshat). Others require reaching and effort (remez and drash). Still others grow at the very top, accessible only to those who've learned to climb (sod).

But here's the profound part: **It's all the same orchard.** These aren't four different Torahs — they're four ways of experiencing the same divine revelation. Just as an apple tree has roots, trunk, branches, and fruit — all essential parts of one organism — the four levels of PaRDeS are all essential dimensions of the same Torah.

The Oral Torah teaches that God gave both the Written Torah (the Five Books) and the Oral Torah (the explanations and interpretations) together at Mount Sinai, around 1300 BCE during what corresponds to the late Shang Dynasty (商朝) in China. The Oral Torah has always included this multi-layered understanding.

## How PaRDeS Works in Practice

Let's see all four levels applied to one verse:

**Genesis 1:1 — "In the beginning, God created the heavens and the earth."**

**Pshat (Simple):** This verse tells us that God created the physical universe. There was a beginning to time, and God initiated it. The heavens and earth encompass all of physical reality.

**Remez (Hint):** The Hebrew word *bereishit* ("in the beginning") can be rearranged to form *brit esh* ("covenant of fire"), hinting that creation itself is a covenant between God and the universe. The first word contains the letters of the alphabet in a particular order, suggesting structure and order in creation.

Some notice that the first sentence has exactly seven Hebrew words and twenty-eight letters (7×4), hinting at the seven days of creation and the four-fold structure of existence (mineral, plant, animal, human).

**Drash (Interpretation):** The Midrash asks: Why does Torah begin with the letter *bet* (ב), the second letter of the alphabet, rather than *aleph* (א), the first? One interpretation: Just as the letter *bet* is closed on three sides but open on one, we are meant to look forward (toward the future and how to live) but not probe too deeply into what came before creation, above the heavens, or beneath the earth. Focus on your purpose in this world.

Another drash: The word *bereishit* can mean "for the sake of *reishit* (the beginning/first)." God created the world for the sake of Torah (called *reishit* in Proverbs 8:22) and for the sake of Israel (called *reishit* in Jeremiah 2:3). This teaches that creation has purpose — it exists for the sake of Torah wisdom and the people who carry it.

**Sod (Secret):** Kabbalah teaches that *bereishit* encodes the ten sefirot (divine emanations). The word contains six letters, representing the six directions of physical space that God created. The numerical value of *bereishit* (913) equals the combined value of significant divine names, revealing the hidden structure through which the infinite God contracted to create a finite universe.

Each word in this opening verse corresponds to a stage in the spiritual process of creation, from initial divine will (beginning) through the manifestation of physical reality (earth).

## The Balance: Not Ignoring Any Level

Jewish tradition teaches that all four levels are essential. You can't pick and choose.

A beautiful wordplay illustrates this: If you remove the *samech* (ס, representing *sod*) from PaRDeS, you're left with PeReD (פרד), which means "mule" — a sterile hybrid animal. A person who studies only the literal level without the mystical dimension is like a mule: they can carry loads but can't bear fruit. They understand information but miss the transformative spiritual power.

Conversely, someone who jumps straight to mystical meanings without grounding in pshat and drash risks floating into fantasy, disconnected from the actual text and tradition.

The ideal is to enter the orchard fully — tasting fruit from every level, each enriching your understanding of the others.

## PaRDeS and the Oral Torah

The existence of four levels of interpretation is inseparable from the concept of the Oral Torah (see Article #11: "What is the Oral Torah?").

When Moses received the Ten Commandments at Sinai, he received far more than the written words. He received the explanations, the interpretations, the multiple meanings. This is why two qualified rabbis can both be right even when they seem to disagree — they may be addressing different levels of PaRDeS.

The Talmud records thousands of legal debates. These aren't contradictions — they're explorations of the same reality from different angles. One rabbi focuses on pshat and legal precedent; another emphasizes the drash and moral implications; a third brings in kabbalistic sod to illuminate both positions.

This is what makes Torah study uniquely dynamic. You're not just learning ancient history — you're entering an ongoing conversation spanning 3,300 years, where every generation adds its insights while remaining faithful to the original revelation.

## Practical Application: How to Use PaRDeS

**When studying any Torah verse:**

1. **Start with Pshat:** What is the simple, straightforward meaning? Don't rush past this. Consult Rashi's commentary. Make sure you understand the basic narrative, the grammar, the context.

2. **Notice Remez:** Are there unusual words? Repetitions? Numerical patterns? What might the text be hinting at? This is where learning Hebrew becomes invaluable — patterns invisible in translation jump out in the original.

3. **Explore Drash:** What is this teaching me about how to live? What moral or spiritual lesson emerges? Read classical Midrash to see what the sages learned from this verse. How does it connect to other parts of Torah?

4. **Approach Sod with caution:** If you're ready and properly prepared, explore the mystical dimensions. Study under a qualified teacher. The Zohar, Tanya, and other kabbalistic works reveal these depths — but not as a replacement for the other levels, only as a crown upon them.

**When two interpretations seem to conflict:**

Ask yourself: Are they really contradicting each other, or are they addressing different levels of PaRDeS? Often, what appears as disagreement is actually complementary insights — like describing a sculpture from different angles.

**For daily living:**

Let PaRDeS remind you that reality itself has layers. The simple facts of a situation (pshat) matter, but so do the subtle hints and patterns (remez), the deeper meaning and purpose (drash), and the spiritual dimension invisible to the eye (sod).

## Chinese Textual Traditions: A Parallel Framework

Chinese scholarly tradition developed remarkably similar approaches to sacred texts, suggesting that multi-layered interpretation is a universal characteristic of deep wisdom literature.

**The Confucian Classics:** Scholars distinguish between:
- **文** (*wen*): The literal text
- **義** (*yi*): The righteous meaning and moral application
- **微言大義** (*weiyan dayi*): "Subtle words, great meaning" — the hidden profound teachings encoded in simple phrases

This parallels pshat (literal), drash (moral application), and sod (hidden meaning).

**The Daoist Tradition:** The *Dao De Jing* operates on multiple levels:
- Surface level: Practical advice on governance and living
- Philosophical level: Teachings about the nature of reality
- Esoteric level: Instructions for spiritual cultivation and immortality practices

Daoist masters teach that the same verse can guide a ruler in statecraft, a scholar in philosophy, and a mystic in meditation — depending on which level the reader has accessed.

**Buddhist Sutras:** Mahayana Buddhism developed the doctrine of the "two truths":
- **世俗諦** (conventional truth): How things appear
- **勝義諦** (ultimate truth): How things truly are

Advanced practitioners perceive both simultaneously — similar to how PaRDeS teaches that all levels are true concurrently.

## The Living Orchard: PaRDeS Today

Understanding PaRDeS transforms how you approach not just Torah, but all wisdom literature. It teaches you to ask:
- What is the simple meaning? (Don't skip this!)
- What is being hinted at?
- How does this apply to my life?
- What deeper truth is hidden here?

This framework prevents two common errors:

**Fundamentalism:** Thinking there's only one literal meaning, missing the richness of deeper layers.

**Relativism:** Thinking every interpretation is equally valid, losing the anchor of pshat and the discipline of authentic tradition.

PaRDeS offers a middle path: The text has a simple meaning that anchors everything (pshat), but it also contains infinite depths (remez, drash, sod) that unfold across generations. Both the simplicity and the depth are real. Both are divine.

When you study a verse, you're not alone. You enter an orchard cultivated by centuries of sages. Their commentaries are like ladders helping you reach higher fruits. The orchard is alive — each generation plants new trees while tending the ancient ones.

## Conclusion: Entering the Orchard

The Talmud tells of four great sages who "entered the *pardes*" — the orchard of mystical Torah study. It was dangerous: one died, one lost his mind, one became a heretic. Only Rabbi Akiva "entered in peace and left in peace."

This story isn't meant to scare you away from deep study. It's meant to teach you respect.

The Torah is not a casual read. It's an orchard of infinite depth. Approach it with humility. Start with pshat. Learn from qualified teachers. Master each level before climbing to the next.

But do climb. The fruits at every level are real, nourishing, and meant for you.

The beauty of PaRDeS is that it never ends. Even the greatest scholars return to verses they've studied a thousand times and discover new dimensions. The orchard keeps yielding fruit.

In the words of the Mishnah (Pirkei Avot 5:22): *"Turn it over and over, for everything is in it."* 

The entire universe is encoded in Torah. All wisdom, all truth, all meaning. Four levels, infinite depths, one divine orchard.

Welcome to the *pardes*.

## Key Takeaways

- **PaRDeS** is an acronym for the four levels of Torah interpretation: Pshat (simple), Remez (hint), Drash (interpretation), Sod (secret)
- The word means "orchard" — suggesting many trees bearing different fruits from the same ground
- All four levels are true simultaneously; they complement rather than contradict each other
- Start with Pshat (simple meaning) before exploring deeper levels
- PaRDeS is inseparable from the Oral Torah — both were given together at Sinai
- Similar multi-layered approaches exist in Chinese textual traditions (Confucian, Daoist, Buddhist)
- This framework prevents both fundamentalism (only literal) and relativism (anything goes)
- Torah study is entering a living orchard cultivated across generations
