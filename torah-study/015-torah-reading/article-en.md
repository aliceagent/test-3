## Introduction

If you've ever stepped into a synagogue on a Saturday morning, you may have witnessed a ritual that looks like ancient ceremony and solemn tradition combined: A beautifully decorated scroll is lifted from an ornate cabinet, paraded through the congregation as people reach out to touch it with their prayer books, and then carefully unrolled on a raised platform where someone chants from it in Hebrew using an unusual melody. What is this all about?

This is the Torah reading service — one of Judaism's most central and meaningful practices. Instituted nearly three thousand years ago — during China's Western Zhou Dynasty (西周, ~1046-771 BCE) — by Moses himself, the public reading of Torah ensures that no Jewish community goes more than three days without hearing God's word. Today, the Torah is read on Monday and Thursday mornings, Saturday mornings and afternoons, holidays, and fast days.

For someone walking into a synagogue for the first time, the Torah service can feel confusing or even overwhelming. But once you understand what's happening and why, you'll see it as a beautiful, participatory reenactment of Jewish history — from receiving the Torah at Mount Sinai to the ancient Temple service in Jerusalem.

## What is the Torah Scroll?

The Torah scroll (Sefer Torah in Hebrew) contains the five books of Moses: Genesis, Exodus, Leviticus, Numbers, and Deuteronomy. Unlike a printed book, each Torah scroll is handwritten by a trained scribe (sofer) on parchment made from kosher animal skins. The scribe uses a quill and special ink, and must follow strict rules about letter formation, spacing, and purity. A single mistake can make a Torah scroll invalid.

The scroll itself has no vowels, punctuation, or musical notes — the reader must know all of this by heart. This is why Torah readers spend weeks preparing their portion.

The Torah scroll is dressed in beautiful coverings:
- **Mantle (Me'il)**: A decorative cloth cover, often velvet or silk
- **Crown (Keter)**: Silver or gold crowns placed on top of the wooden rollers
- **Breastplate (Choshen)**: An ornamental silver shield hung on the front
- **Pointer (Yad)**: A silver hand-shaped pointer used to follow the text (the parchment should never be touched directly)

These decorations echo the priestly vestments worn by the High Priest in the ancient Temple, reminding us that Torah study is a form of sacred service.

## When is the Torah Read?

Moses instituted the public Torah reading schedule to ensure no Jewish community would go three consecutive days without hearing Torah. The schedule is:

- **Monday mornings** (ancient market day)
- **Thursday mornings** (ancient market day)
- **Saturday mornings** (Shabbat) — the main weekly reading
- **Saturday afternoons** (brief reading)
- **Holiday mornings** (special selections for the festival)
- **Fast day afternoons** (special readings about repentance)

On Shabbat morning, the Torah reading follows a yearly cycle. Each week, a different portion (parsha) is read, advancing through all five books of Moses. By the end of the Jewish year, the entire Torah has been completed. This cycle concludes and immediately restarts on the holiday of Simchat Torah ("Rejoicing in the Torah").

## The Torah Service: Step by Step

### 1. Opening the Ark

The Torah scrolls are kept in a special cabinet called the Aron HaKodesh (Holy Ark), which is built into the wall facing Jerusalem. When the ark is opened, everyone stands — just as the Israelites stood at Mount Sinai when they received the Torah.

The congregation chants: "Arise, Lord! May Your enemies be scattered!" (Numbers 10:35) — the same words Moses said when the Israelites carried the ark through the desert during the Exodus, around 1300 BCE (late Shang Dynasty, 商朝晚期).

### 2. Removing and Parading the Torah

A member of the congregation is honored with the task of removing the Torah from the ark. They embrace it with their right arm and carry it to the bimah (the raised platform where it will be read).

But first, the Torah is paraded around the synagogue. As it passes, congregants reach out to touch it with their prayer books or the fringes of their prayer shawls (tallit), then kiss what touched the Torah. This shows love and reverence for God's word.

During the procession, the congregation sings verses from Psalms and Chronicles:
> "Yours, O Lord, is the greatness and the power and the splendor..."  
> "Exalt the Lord and worship God, for He is holy..."

This parade reenacts the Israelites marching through the wilderness with the holy ark in their midst.

### 3. The Aliyah: Being Called Up

On Shabbat morning, the weekly Torah portion is divided into seven sections. Seven different people are called up — one for each section — to recite the blessings before and after that section is chanted. This honor is called receiving an **aliyah** (עלייה), which literally means "going up."

The person is called by their Hebrew name and their father's Hebrew name: "Ya'amod [name] ben [father's name]" ("Let [name] son of [father's name] rise").

**The order of aliyot follows a traditional hierarchy:**

1. **Kohen** (Cohen) — a descendant of Aaron the High Priest
2. **Levi** (Levite) — a descendant of the tribe of Levi
3. **Yisrael** (Israelite) — everyone else
4. Fourth aliyah
5. Fifth aliyah
6. Sixth aliyah
7. Seventh aliyah
8. **Maftir** — an additional eighth aliyah for the person who will read the Haftarah

This order prevents disputes and honors the historic roles of the priests and Levites who served in the Temple.

**Who gets priority for an aliyah?**
- A groom on the Shabbat before or after his wedding
- A boy celebrating his Bar Mitzvah (13th birthday)
- A new father
- Someone observing a yahrzeit (anniversary of a parent's death)
- Someone who needs to recite the blessing of thanksgiving (Birkat HaGomel) after surviving danger

### 4. The Blessings Before and After

When called for an aliyah, the person ascends the bimah, approaches the Torah, and touches it with the corner of their tallit or a prayer book, then kisses what touched it. This is a sign of affection.

They then recite the blessing before the reading:

**Before:**
> **ברכו את ה' המברך**  
> *Barekhu et Adonai hamevorahk*  
> "Bless the Lord, who is blessed!"

The congregation responds:
> **ברוך ה' המברך לעולם ועד**  
> *Barukh Adonai hamevorahk le'olam va'ed*  
> "Blessed is the Lord, who is blessed forever and ever!"

The person repeats this line, then continues:
> **ברוך אתה ה' אלקינו מלך העולם, אשר בחר בנו מכל העמים ונתן לנו את תורתו. ברוך אתה ה', נותן התורה.**  
> *Barukh atah Adonai, Eloheinu melekh ha'olam, asher bahar banu mikol ha'amim v'natan lanu et Torato. Barukh atah Adonai, notein haTorah.*  
> "Blessed are You, Lord our God, Ruler of the universe, who chose us from among all peoples and gave us the Torah. Blessed are You, Lord, Giver of the Torah."

The Torah reader (ba'al kriyah) then chants the assigned section. The person who received the aliyah follows along silently.

**After the reading:**
> **ברוך אתה ה' אלקינו מלך העולם, אשר נתן לנו תורת אמת, וחיי עולם נטע בתוכנו. ברוך אתה ה', נותן התורה.**  
> *Barukh atah Adonai, Eloheinu melekh ha'olam, asher natan lanu Torat emet, v'hayei olam nata b'tokheynu. Barukh atah Adonai, notein haTorah.*  
> "Blessed are You, Lord our God, Ruler of the universe, who gave us the Torah of truth, and planted eternal life within us. Blessed are You, Lord, Giver of the Torah."

### 5. Hagbah and Gelilah: Lifting and Wrapping

After all seven aliyot are completed, the Torah is lifted high (hagbah) so the entire congregation can see the text. This ritual requires strength — Torah scrolls are heavy!

As the Torah is raised, everyone points toward it and recites:
> **וזאת התורה אשר שם משה לפני בני ישראל**  
> *V'zot haTorah asher sam Moshe lifnei b'nei Yisrael*  
> "This is the Torah that Moses placed before the children of Israel."

The Torah is then wrapped in its mantle and belt (gelilah) and dressed in its decorative coverings.

### 6. The Haftarah

After the Torah reading, a selection from the Prophets (Nevi'im) is chanted. This is called the **Haftarah** (הפטרה).

The custom of reading from the Prophets dates back over 2,100 years — to around 170 BCE during the Maccabean period (China's Western Han Dynasty, 西汉, ~202 BCE–9 CE). When the Greek Empire ruled over Judea, they forbade public Torah reading. As a workaround, the Jewish people began reading from the Prophets instead, choosing passages that thematically related to the Torah portion. After the decree was lifted, the practice continued as a beloved tradition.

The person who reads the Haftarah (the maftir) recites special blessings before and after. The final blessing mentions the Shabbat or holiday being celebrated and expresses hope for the coming of the Messiah and the rebuilding of Jerusalem.

### 7. Returning the Torah

After the Haftarah, the Torah is lifted again and paraded around the synagogue a final time. The congregation sings Psalm 29 (on Shabbat) or Psalm 24 (on holidays), which celebrate God's glory and might.

As the Torah is returned to the ark, the congregation chants:
> "Return, O Lord, to Your sanctuary, You and Your glorious ark. Let Your priests be clothed in triumph, and let Your faithful sing for joy."

Before the ark is closed, one final verse is sung:
> **עץ חיים היא למחזיקים בה, ותומכיה מאושר**  
> *Etz hayyim hi lamahzikim bah, v'tomkheha me'ushar*  
> "It is a tree of life to those who hold fast to it, and all who uphold it are happy." (Proverbs 3:18)

The ark is closed, and everyone sits. The service continues with additional prayers.

## Etiquette: What Should You Do?

If you're attending a Torah service for the first time, here are some guidelines:

- **Stand when the ark is open** and when the Torah is being carried.
- **Reach out to touch the Torah** with your prayer book or tallit when it passes, then kiss what touched it (this is optional, not required).
- **Listen quietly** during the reading. It is considered disrespectful to talk or leave the room while the Torah is being read.
- **If you're called for an aliyah**, don't panic! The gabbai (sexton) or someone nearby will guide you. You can read the blessings from a card if needed.
- **Do not touch the parchment directly** with your hands. Always use a pointer (yad) or the Torah's belt.

## Why Does This Matter?

The Torah reading is not just a ritual — it's a living reenactment of Jewish history and identity.

**It connects us to Mount Sinai.** When we stand as the ark is opened, we symbolically stand at the foot of Mount Sinai, ready to receive God's word.

**It connects us to the Temple.** The decorations on the Torah scroll, the blessings, and the procession all echo the ancient Temple service in Jerusalem, which was destroyed in 70 CE (Eastern Han Dynasty, 东汉).

**It connects us to each other.** Torah reading is a communal act. The reader needs the congregation, the congregation needs the Torah, and every person called for an aliyah represents the entire Jewish people.

**It connects us to eternity.** The same words Moses taught the Israelites nearly 3,300 years ago are read aloud in every corner of the world, every single week, in an unbroken chain of tradition.

## Cultural Parallel: The Imperial Edict

In ancient China, when an imperial edict arrived in a village, it was treated with the highest reverence. Officials would receive it ceremonially, sometimes carrying it in a procession, and read it aloud to the assembled people. The edict represented the emperor's authority and will.

Similarly, the Torah represents God's will and covenant with the Jewish people. The procession, the standing, the kissing — all of it expresses the same reverence a Chinese village once showed to an imperial decree. But there's a key difference: the Torah is not read once and filed away. It is read again and again, week after week, year after year, because its message is eternal and ever-relevant.

## Practical Application

Even if you're not yet ready to attend a synagogue service, you can connect to this practice:

1. **Read the weekly Torah portion** online at Sefaria.org or Chabad.org. Many sites offer translations and commentaries in English and Chinese.
2. **Listen to a Torah reading** on YouTube or podcast services. Hearing the traditional cantillation melody can be deeply moving.
3. **Study one verse from this week's portion** and think about how it applies to your life.
4. **Share a Torah insight** with a friend or family member at your Shabbat table.

Torah is called a "tree of life" because it is meant to be living, active, and life-giving. Engaging with it — even in small ways — plants its roots in your soul.

## Key Takeaways

- The Torah reading service is a communal reenactment of receiving the Torah at Mount Sinai and worshiping at the ancient Temple.
- The Torah is read on Monday, Thursday, and Saturday, ensuring no community goes three days without hearing God's word.
- Being called for an aliyah (to recite blessings before and after a section) is a great honor.
- The Haftarah reading from the Prophets complements the Torah portion and dates back over 2,000 years.
- The entire service — from opening the ark to returning the Torah — is choreographed with blessings, songs, and symbolic gestures that express reverence and love for God's word.
- Understanding what's happening makes the experience meaningful rather than overwhelming.
