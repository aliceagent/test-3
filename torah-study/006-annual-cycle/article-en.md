## Introduction

Imagine finishing a book you love — and instead of putting it on the shelf, you immediately turn back to page one and start again. For Jews around the world, this happens every year with the Torah. On a holiday called Simchat Torah (literally "Rejoicing with the Torah"), we finish reading the last verses of Deuteronomy and, without pause, begin again with the opening words of Genesis: "In the beginning, God created the heavens and the earth."

This isn't just tradition — it's a statement about what the Torah means to us. The annual cycle of Torah reading, practiced for over a thousand years, reflects the Jewish belief that divine wisdom is inexhaustible. No matter how many times you read it, there's always something new to discover.

## The History of the One-Year Cycle

The practice of reading the entire Torah publicly dates back over two thousand years — to around the time of China's Han Dynasty (漢朝, 202 BCE–220 CE). The Talmud records that Ezra the Scribe, who returned from Babylonian exile (around 458 BCE, during China's Spring and Autumn period 春秋), instituted regular public Torah readings on Mondays, Thursdays, and Shabbat.

However, there wasn't always a single universal cycle. In ancient Israel and Babylon, two different systems existed:

### The Babylonian Annual Cycle
Jewish communities in Babylon (modern-day Iraq) divided the Torah into 54 portions (called *parshiyot*), one for each Shabbat of the year. This system ensured the entire Torah was completed in exactly one year. This is the practice that eventually became universal.

### The Palestinian Triennial Cycle
In the Land of Israel, communities followed a three-year cycle, reading the Torah more slowly. Each Shabbat they would read approximately one-third of what the Babylonian communities read. This meant it took three years to complete the Torah.

By the 12th century — during what corresponds to China's Song Dynasty (宋朝, 960–1279 CE) — the Babylonian annual cycle had become the dominant practice worldwide. The great medieval commentator Rashi (Rabbi Shlomo Yitzchaki, 1040–1105 CE), who lived in France, wrote his famous Torah commentary based on the annual cycle, which helped standardize it across all Jewish communities.

## Why One Year? The Spiritual Rhythm

The one-year cycle isn't arbitrary — it's deeply connected to the Jewish calendar and the rhythm of spiritual life:

### 1. Alignment with the Festivals
The Torah portions are strategically timed to align with Jewish holidays. For example:
- During Passover (Pesach), we read about the Exodus from Egypt
- During Shavuot, we read about the giving of the Torah at Mount Sinai
- During Sukkot, we read Moses's final blessings to the people

This creates a powerful resonance: as we live through each holiday, we're also reading the Torah passages that describe its origins.

### 2. Annual Renewal
Jewish spirituality operates on an annual cycle. Each year brings:
- Rosh Hashana (New Year) and Yom Kippur (Day of Atonement) — a time for self-examination
- The harvest festivals (Sukkot, Passover, Shavuot) — connecting us to the land and history
- Simchat Torah — celebrating our relationship with divine wisdom

Finishing and restarting the Torah reading cycle on Simchat Torah mirrors this pattern of renewal. It teaches that spiritual growth isn't linear — it's cyclical. We return to the same texts each year, but we ourselves have changed, so we discover new meanings.

### 3. Communal Unity
The annual cycle means that Jews everywhere are reading the same Torah portion each week. A Jew in Shanghai, Jerusalem, New York, and Johannesburg all study the same *parsha* on the same Shabbat. This creates a global conversation — what one community learns can enrich another.

This unity is similar to how traditional Chinese culture had common classical texts (like the Analects of Confucius 論語) that scholars across China studied together, creating shared wisdom and language.

## Simchat Torah: The Day We Finish and Begin

Simchat Torah falls immediately after Sukkot, in the autumn season (September or October). In Israel, it's observed on the same day as Shmini Atzeret (the eighth day of assembly); outside Israel, it's celebrated the following day.

### What Happens on Simchat Torah?

1. **Completing the Torah** — The final portion of Deuteronomy (chapters 33-34) is read. It includes Moses's blessings to each tribe and his death.

2. **Immediately Beginning Again** — Without pause, we read the opening of Genesis: the creation story. This demonstrates that Torah study has no end.

3. **Hakafot (Processions)** — All the Torah scrolls are taken from the ark and carried around the synagogue seven times. People dance and sing, often for hours. Unlike most synagogue services, this is loud, joyful, and celebratory.

4. **Everyone Gets Called Up** — On a normal Shabbat, only seven people are called up to the Torah. On Simchat Torah, everyone — men, women, and children — is honored. Some communities call children up in groups, holding a large tallit (prayer shawl) over them like a canopy.

### The Symbolism of Finishing and Starting

The Talmud asks: Why do we immediately begin reading Genesis again after finishing Deuteronomy? The answer given is:

> "To show that the Torah is beloved to us like a new object and not like an old command which a person no longer treasures. Since it is brand new to us, we all run to greet it."  
> — Talmud, Sukkah 55b (paraphrased)

This reflects a key Jewish idea: **The Torah is infinitely deep.** Each time you return to it, you bring new life experience, greater maturity, different questions — and so you find different answers.

It's similar to how a scholar might read the *Dao De Jing* (道德經) or the *I Ching* (易經) multiple times throughout life, discovering new layers of meaning at each stage.

## Chinese Cultural Parallel: The Cyclical Nature of Study

Traditional Chinese philosophy, particularly Confucianism and Daoism, also emphasizes returning to foundational texts:

### Confucian Self-Cultivation (修身)
Confucius taught that learning is a lifelong process. The Analects say:
> "To study and not think is a waste. To think and not study is dangerous."  
> — Analects 2:15

Just as Confucian scholars would study the Five Classics (五經) repeatedly throughout their lives, deepening their understanding with age and experience, Jews return to the Torah each year.

### Daoist Cycles
Daoism teaches that nature moves in cycles — the seasons, the moon, yin and yang. The *Dao De Jing* emphasizes returning to the source:
> "Returning is the movement of the Dao."  
> — Dao De Jing, Chapter 40

The Jewish one-year Torah cycle embodies this same principle: returning to the beginning is not going backward — it's moving forward on a spiral. You're back at the starting point, but at a higher level of understanding.

## Practical Application: How This Works Today

### Weekly Torah Portions (Parshiyot)
The Torah is divided into 54 portions. Each portion has a name (usually the first distinctive Hebrew word in that section). For example:
- **Bereishit** (בראשית) — "In the beginning" (Genesis 1-6)
- **Noach** (נח) — "Noah" (Genesis 6-11)
- **Lech Lecha** (לך לך) — "Go forth" (Genesis 12-17)

Every Shabbat, the weekly portion is read aloud in the synagogue during the morning service. The reading is divided into seven sections, and different congregants are honored by being called up for each section.

### Reading the Same Portion Worldwide
Because all Jewish communities follow the same calendar, a family in Beijing and a family in Tel Aviv will discuss the same *parsha* at their Shabbat table. This creates:
- **Unity** — a shared focal point for Jews worldwide
- **Depth** — thousands of years of commentary on each portion
- **Relevance** — each generation finds new applications to current events

### What You Can Do
Even if you're not part of a Jewish community yet, you can connect to this cycle:
1. **Find the weekly portion** — Websites like Chabad.org and Sefaria.org list the current week's parsha
2. **Read it in translation** — Start with a good English or Chinese translation
3. **Read one commentary** — Try Rashi's commentary (it's concise and foundational)
4. **Reflect on one idea** — What speaks to you this week?

Even reading just a few verses each week connects you to this ancient rhythm of study.

## The Deeper Lesson: Torah Study Has No End

The annual cycle teaches a profound lesson: **Wisdom isn't something you master and move on from — it's something you live with.**

In modern education, we think of learning as linear: elementary school → middle school → high school → university → graduation. You "finish" and get a degree.

Jewish tradition offers a different model: learning as a spiral. You return to the same material repeatedly, but each time you're different — older, wiser, facing new questions. The text hasn't changed, but *you* have. And so it reveals new depths.

The Talmud expresses this beautifully:
> "Turn it over and turn it over, for everything is in it."  
> — Pirkei Avot 5:22

This is why the Torah is never "finished" — only continuously renewed.

## Key Takeaways

1. **The one-year Torah reading cycle** has been the standard practice for Jewish communities worldwide for over 1,000 years, ensuring that the entire Torah is read publicly from beginning to end each year.

2. **Simchat Torah**, the holiday marking the completion and restart of the cycle, is one of the most joyful celebrations in Judaism — featuring dancing, singing, and communal participation.

3. **The annual rhythm** aligns Torah study with the Jewish calendar, creating resonance between the weekly readings and the festivals throughout the year.

4. **Global unity** — Jews everywhere read the same portion each week, creating a shared conversation and collective wisdom across all communities.

5. **Infinite depth** — The cycle embodies the belief that the Torah can never be fully "mastered." Each return brings new insights, reflecting the spiral nature of wisdom.

6. **Cultural resonance** — Like Confucian and Daoist teachings about returning to foundational texts, Judaism sees repeated study not as repetition but as deepening.
