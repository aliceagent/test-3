## Introduction

What makes a truly great leader? In a world obsessed with power, status, and self-promotion, Judaism offers a radically different answer. The Torah's greatest leader — Moses (Moshe Rabbeinu) — is described not by his accomplishments, his miracles, or his authority, but by a single, surprising quality: humility.

"Now Moses was a very humble man, more humble than anyone else on the face of the earth" (Numbers 12:3).

This is remarkable. Nearly three and a half thousand years ago — during what corresponds to China's late Shang Dynasty (商朝, ~1600-1046 BCE) — Moses led an enslaved nation to freedom, spoke directly with God, split the sea, brought down divine law from Mount Sinai, and guided two million people through forty years in the wilderness. Yet the Torah's highest praise is not for his power, but for his humility.

What can Moses teach us about leadership that applies to our lives today — whether we lead a company, a family, a community, or simply our own selves?

## The Leader Who Didn't Want to Lead

Moses's leadership journey began with profound reluctance. When God appeared to him at the burning bush and commanded him to confront Pharaoh and liberate the Jewish people, Moses didn't leap at the opportunity. Instead, he responded with a series of objections:

"Who am I that I should go to Pharaoh?" (Exodus 3:11)

"What if they don't believe me?" (Exodus 4:1)

"I am not a man of words... I am slow of speech and tongue" (Exodus 4:10)

"Please, Lord, send someone else" (Exodus 4:13)

This was not false modesty or a negotiating tactic. Moses genuinely felt inadequate for the task. He had a speech impediment. He had been a shepherd in the desert for forty years, far from the centers of power. He did not see himself as leadership material.

Yet precisely this reluctance — this awareness of his own limitations — made him fit to lead.

**Chinese Parallel:** This echoes the Confucian ideal expressed in the *Analects* (论语): "The superior person is modest in speech but exceeds in actions" (14:27). Like the wise ministers of ancient China who accepted leadership only when pressed by duty, Moses's humility was his qualification, not his disqualification.

## Humility: The Foundation of True Leadership

Why does the Torah emphasize Moses's humility above all else?

Because humility is not weakness — it is strength rooted in self-awareness and truth. The arrogant leader demands honor because they fear they don't deserve it. The humble leader serves without needing recognition because their worth is already secure.

Rabbi Avraham Yeshaya Karelitz (the Chazon Ish) taught that true humility doesn't mean denying your gifts or accomplishments. A humble person may know they are great — the difference is they don't *demand* honor because of it. They don't need external validation to feel complete.

Moses understood his greatness clearly. He knew he spoke with God face to face. He knew no prophet before or after would reach his level. Yet this knowledge never translated into arrogance or entitlement. He led not for personal glory, but to serve God and the Jewish people.

Modern psychology confirms this ancient wisdom. Research shows that narcissistic leaders — who appear supremely confident — are often deeply insecure. Their arrogance is a mask for inner emptiness. In contrast, leaders with authentic self-esteem (like Moses) are more resilient, less defensive, and more effective because they don't need constant validation.

## Going Out to His People: The Power of Empathy

Even before God called him, Moses demonstrated the essential quality of a leader: identification with those he would serve.

Raised in Pharaoh's palace with wealth, power, and privilege, Moses had every reason to remain comfortable. He could have enjoyed the luxuries of Egyptian royalty and ignored the suffering of the Hebrew slaves. But the Torah tells us:

"When Moses grew up, he went out to his people and saw their burdens" (Exodus 2:11).

He *went out*. He left the palace. He chose to identify with the oppressed, the downtrodden, the powerless — his own people.

This empathy — the ability to feel another's pain as your own — is essential to leadership. If your subordinates, colleagues, or community feel that you don't understand their struggles, they will never truly follow you. But when people know their leader sees them, hears them, and genuinely cares about their welfare, they will go to extraordinary lengths.

**Chinese Parallel:** This mirrors the principle of benevolent governance (仁政) taught by Mencius (孟子): "The people are the most important element in a state; the ruler is of slight importance" (7B14). A true leader prioritizes the welfare of those they serve above personal comfort — just as the sage-kings Yao (尧) and Shun (舜) were revered for their selfless devotion to the people.

## Taking Responsibility When No One Else Will

When Moses saw an Egyptian taskmaster beating a Hebrew slave, he didn't wait for authorization or committee approval. The Torah says:

"He turned this way and that and saw there was no man, so he struck down the Egyptian" (Exodus 2:12).

The phrase "saw there was no man" is often interpreted to mean: he saw that no one else was taking responsibility. In the *Ethics of our Fathers* (Pirkei Avot 2:5), Hillel teaches: "In a place where there is no man, strive to be a man."

A leader is not necessarily someone with perfect credentials or exceptional talent. A leader is someone who sees a problem that must be solved — and steps up to solve it, even at personal risk.

Moses took responsibility. He acted when no one else would. This willingness to own the problem, to say "I'll make it happen," is the defining trait of leadership.

**Chinese Parallel:** The *Doctrine of the Mean* (中庸) teaches: "The superior person does what is proper to their position and does not wish to go beyond it" (14:1). Yet when duty calls and the situation demands it, the virtuous person rises to the occasion — like Zhuge Liang (诸葛亮) accepting the burden of guiding the Shu Han kingdom despite his preference for quiet scholarship.

## Learning to Delegate: The Counsel of Jethro

Even the greatest leaders cannot do everything alone. Moses learned this crucial lesson from an unlikely source: his father-in-law Jethro, a Midianite priest.

After the Exodus, Moses sat from morning until evening judging every dispute among the people. When Jethro saw this, he said:

"What you are doing is not good. You will surely wear yourself out, and these people as well. The task is too heavy for you; you cannot do it alone... Select capable men from all the people... and appoint them as officials over thousands, hundreds, fifties, and tens. Have them serve as judges for the people at all times" (Exodus 18:17-22).

Moses, the humble leader, listened. He accepted advice from someone outside his tradition, someone who wasn't even Jewish. He restructured the entire judicial system based on this counsel.

This teaches three critical leadership lessons:

1. **Listen to advice, even from unexpected sources.** Pride blinds. Humility opens you to wisdom wherever it comes from.

2. **Delegation is not weakness — it's multiplication.** By empowering others, Moses didn't lose authority; he gained capacity.

3. **A leader's job is not to do everything, but to build systems that work.** Moses created a sustainable structure that outlasted him.

**Chinese Parallel:** The *I Ching* (易经, Book of Changes) teaches that wise rulers surround themselves with capable ministers and delegate appropriately. Emperor Taizong (唐太宗) of the Tang Dynasty famously said, "With bronze as a mirror, one can correct one's appearance; with history as a mirror, one can understand the rise and fall of states; with people as mirrors, one can see one's strengths and weaknesses." Moses exemplified this principle by accepting Jethro's mirror.

## Speaking Truth to Power: Advocacy Before God

One of Moses's most remarkable qualities was his willingness to argue with God on behalf of the Jewish people — even when they deserved punishment.

After the sin of the Golden Calf, God said to Moses: "Now leave Me alone, and My anger will burn against them and I will destroy them. Then I will make you into a great nation" (Exodus 32:10).

God offered Moses an out: destroy this rebellious generation and start fresh with Moses as the new Abraham. It was an extraordinary offer. Moses could have become the sole patriarch of a new covenant.

But Moses refused. Instead, he pleaded:

"Why, Lord, should Your anger burn against Your people, whom You brought out of Egypt?... Remember Your servants Abraham, Isaac, and Israel... Turn from Your fierce anger; relent and do not bring disaster on Your people" (Exodus 32:11-13).

Moses put the welfare of his people above his own potential greatness. This is the essence of servant leadership.

Later, after God forgave the people but declared that He would not dwell among them, Moses went even further: "If Your presence does not go with us, do not bring us up from here" (Exodus 33:15). Moses was willing to give up the entire journey to the Promised Land if it meant the people would lose God's presence.

**Chinese Parallel:** This recalls the Confucian duty of *zhong* (忠) — loyalty and faithfulness — but directed toward the people rather than the throne. Like the upright minister Hai Rui (海瑞) of the Ming Dynasty, who risked execution by criticizing the emperor to protect the common people, Moses placed righteousness above personal safety.

## The Menorah vs. The Pyramid: A New Model of Leadership

The ancient world was built on hierarchy. The Mesopotamian ziggurats and Egyptian pyramids were architectural statements: wide at the base (the masses), narrow at the top (the king), with the ruler believed to be the meeting point of heaven and earth.

Judaism rejected this model. Every human being is created in the image of God (Genesis 1:27). Therefore, no one is inherently entitled to rule over another without consent. Leadership exists not to elevate the leader, but to serve the people.

The symbol of biblical Israel — the menorah (seven-branched candelabrum) — is an inverted pyramid. It is wide at the top and narrow at the base. The leader does not stand above the people; the leader stands beneath them, supporting and serving.

Moses embodied this. His title in the Torah is not "King Moses" or "Lord Moses," but "Moshe eved Hashem" — Moses, the servant of God (Deuteronomy 34:5). This title appears eighteen times in the Hebrew Bible. Only one other leader receives it: Joshua, Moses's successor, and only twice.

To lead in the Jewish tradition is to serve.

**Chinese Parallel:** Laozi's (老子) *Tao Te Ching* (道德经) teaches: "The highest good is like water... The sage stays behind, thus he is ahead. He is detached, thus at one with all" (Chapter 8). "The reason rivers and seas can be kings of the hundred valleys is that they are good at staying low" (Chapter 66). Moses, like water, nourished from below rather than dominating from above.

## The Lessons for Us Today

Moses lived over three millennia ago, yet his leadership principles remain timeless. Whether you lead a multinational corporation or simply your own life, here are the lessons Moses offers:

### 1. **Lead with Humility**
Know your worth, but don't demand recognition. Let your actions speak. The arrogant leader is eventually rejected; the humble leader is enduringly respected.

### 2. **Empathize Deeply**
Go out to your people. Understand their struggles. Make their concerns your concerns. People follow leaders who see them and care.

### 3. **Take Responsibility**
Don't wait for perfect conditions or complete authority. See what needs to be done, and step up — even when it's risky or uncomfortable.

### 4. **Listen and Delegate**
Accept advice, even from unlikely sources. Build systems. Empower others. Sustainable leadership multiplies capacity, it doesn't hoard it.

### 5. **Advocate for Those You Serve**
Put the welfare of your team, community, or family above your personal advancement. True leaders lift others, not themselves.

### 6. **Serve, Don't Dominate**
Leadership is not about status or power. It's about service. The greatest leaders make others greater.

## Conclusion

Moses transformed leadership. Before him, leaders were conquerors and kings. After him, the highest calling of a leader was to be a servant.

He showed that humility is not the opposite of strength — it is the foundation of it. That empathy is not sentimentality, but strategic. That delegation is not abdication, but multiplication. That true greatness lies not in standing above others, but in lifting them up.

Nearly three and a half thousand years later, Moses's example still challenges us: Will we lead for ourselves, or for those we serve? Will we seek honor, or will we quietly do what's right? Will we rule from above, or serve from within?

The answer defines not just our leadership, but our character.

## Key Takeaways

- **Humility is strength:** True leaders don't need external validation because their worth is secure.
- **Empathy builds loyalty:** People follow leaders who genuinely understand and care about their struggles.
- **Responsibility defines leadership:** Leaders step up when others won't, even without authority or perfect skills.
- **Delegation multiplies impact:** Listening to advice and empowering others builds sustainable success.
- **Service, not status:** The greatest leaders serve their people, not themselves.
