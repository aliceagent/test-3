## Introduction

When you open a Torah scroll today, you're not just reading ancient text—you're joining a conversation that has spanned nearly a thousand years. And the voice you'll hear most clearly belongs to a French rabbi who lived during what corresponds to China's Northern Song Dynasty (北宋, 960-1127 CE): Rabbi Shlomo Yitzchaki, known simply as Rashi.

If you've ever wondered how to truly understand the Torah, Rashi is your guide. His commentary appears in virtually every printed Torah today, sitting right alongside the biblical text itself. For nearly 900 years, Jewish children have learned Torah through Rashi's eyes, and scholars still debate the meaning of his every word.

But who was Rashi? What made his commentary so revolutionary? And why, out of thousands of Jewish scholars across the centuries, does his voice still echo the loudest?

## The Man Behind the Commentary

### A Medieval Scholar in Champagne

Rashi was born around 1040 CE in Troyes, the capital of the County of Champagne in northern France—during the same era when China's Song Dynasty was flourishing with its own renaissance of scholarship and literature. Like the great Chinese scholars who devoted their lives to understanding the Confucian classics, Rashi dedicated himself to unlocking the meaning of Jewish sacred texts.

His full name was Shlomo Yitzchaki (Solomon son of Isaac), but he became known by the acronym "Rashi" (רש"י), which stands for Rabbi Shlomo Yitzchaki. According to tradition, his father was a winemaker, though Rashi himself showed extraordinary scholarly talent from childhood.

At age 17, Rashi married and soon traveled to Worms, Germany, to study at the great yeshiva (Torah academy) there. He learned from the leading rabbis of his generation, absorbing centuries of oral tradition about how to read and understand the Talmud and Torah. After years of intensive study, he returned to Troyes around age 25, where he founded his own yeshiva and began answering legal questions for the Jewish community.

### Living Through Crisis

Rashi lived during turbulent times. In 1096, when he was in his fifties, the People's Crusade swept through the Rhineland, massacring Jewish communities and destroying the great academies where Rashi had studied. His own teachers' sons were among those murdered.

Despite this tragedy, Rashi continued teaching and writing until his death on July 13, 1105, at age 65. He left behind three daughters (all of whom were educated in Torah, remarkable for that time), and through them, his intellectual legacy passed to his grandsons, who became some of the greatest Talmud scholars of the next generation.

## What Made Rashi Revolutionary?

### The Problem He Solved

Before Rashi, studying the Talmud and Torah was nearly impossible without a living teacher. The texts were dense, written in terse Hebrew and Aramaic, filled with technical legal terminology and obscure references. You had to travel to a major academy and study directly at the feet of a master rabbi to have any hope of understanding.

Rashi changed everything. He wrote comprehensive commentaries on both the Torah (the Five Books of Moses) and the Babylonian Talmud that made these sacred texts accessible to anyone who could read Hebrew—even a bright child of five, as one description puts it.

### Two Approaches in One

Rashi's genius lay in how he balanced two different ways of reading Scripture:

**Peshat (פשט)** — The plain, straightforward meaning of the text. What does it say on the surface?

**Derash/Midrash (דרש/מדרש)** — The deeper, interpretive meaning drawn from rabbinic tradition.

As Rashi himself explained: "I am only concerned with the plain sense of Scripture (*peshuto shel mikra*) and with such Midrashic interpretations that explain the words of Scripture in a manner that fits in with them."

This was revolutionary. Earlier commentators often piled on layer after layer of mystical or allegorical interpretation. Rashi insisted on first understanding what the text actually says, then carefully selecting from the vast sea of rabbinic tradition only those interpretations that genuinely illuminated the plain meaning.

### A Practical Example

Let's see Rashi's method in action. The Torah opens with the words: "*Bereishit bara Elohim*" — "In the beginning, God created..."

Rashi immediately asks: Why does the Torah start with the story of Creation? Shouldn't a law book begin with the first commandment given to the Jewish people?

He answers by quoting a Midrash: The Torah begins with Creation to establish that God owns the entire world. Therefore, when He gave the Land of Israel to the Jewish people, it was His right to do so, and no nation can claim the Jews stole the land.

Notice what Rashi does here: He doesn't just explain *what* the verse says (that's obvious). He explains *why* it's there, using a Midrashic tradition that addresses the question a thoughtful reader would naturally ask.

## Rashi's Commentary on the Talmud

While Rashi's Torah commentary is beloved, his commentary on the Talmud may be even more important. The Talmud is an immense, complex work—a record of rabbinic debates spanning centuries, written in a mixture of Hebrew and Aramaic, jumping between legal discussions, stories, and ethical teachings with barely any explanation.

Rashi's Talmud commentary provides:

- **Definitions** of difficult words and technical terms
- **Explanations** of the logical flow of arguments
- **Punctuation** for the unpunctuated text (e.g., "This is a question," "He says this in surprise")
- **Connections** to other parts of the Talmud
- **Translations** of Aramaic phrases into Hebrew, and Hebrew into Old French (giving modern linguists a window into medieval French vocabulary!)

Without Rashi's commentary, the Talmud would remain a closed book for most people. As one scholar wrote: "Rashi democratized Talmudic scholarship. Prior to his work, the only way to master a tractate was to travel to an academy and study at the feet of a master. With Rashi's work, anyone could master any Talmudic topic through talent and effort."

Since the first printing of the Talmud in the 1520s, Rashi's commentary has appeared on every page, always positioned next to the central text—a mark of its absolute indispensability.

## Rashi's Influence: Then and Now

### The Jewish World

Rashi's influence on Jewish learning is impossible to overstate:

- More than **300 supercommentaries** have been written analyzing Rashi's Torah commentary—books devoted entirely to explaining Rashi's explanations
- Every Jewish child learning Torah for the first time learns "Chumash with Rashi" (Torah with Rashi's commentary)
- Rashi's grandson Rashbam, great scholars like the Maharal of Prague, and even modern Hasidic masters like the Lubavitcher Rebbe devoted thousands of pages to understanding Rashi's choice of words
- According to Jewish law, one can fulfill the weekly Torah study requirement by reading Rashi's commentary instead of the standard Aramaic translation

### Beyond Judaism

Rashi's influence extended far beyond the Jewish world:

- Christian monk **Nicholas de Lyra** (1270-1349) relied heavily on Rashi when writing his influential biblical commentary *Postillae Perpetuate*
- De Lyra's work, in turn, was a primary source for **Martin Luther's German translation of the Bible**—meaning Rashi indirectly influenced Protestant Christianity
- Renaissance Christian Hebraists studied Rashi's commentaries as essential to understanding the Hebrew Bible
- Rashi's use of Old French vocabulary (he translated hundreds of Hebrew words into his spoken language) has become an invaluable resource for linguists studying medieval French

## A Chinese Parallel: The Scholarly Tradition

Rashi's life and work bear striking similarities to China's great commentators on the Confucian classics.

Just as Chinese scholars like Zhu Xi (朱熹, 1130-1200) wrote authoritative commentaries on the *Four Books and Five Classics* that became inseparable from the texts themselves, Rashi's commentary became the indispensable companion to Torah study. Both men lived during the Song Dynasty era, both sought to clarify difficult ancient texts for students, and both balanced respect for tradition with clear, practical explanation.

Like the Chinese tradition of writing "subcommentaries" on major works, the Jewish world produced hundreds of commentaries analyzing Rashi's commentaries—a testament to the depth and importance of his work.

Both traditions recognize that a classic text needs a great teacher to unlock it—and both Rashi and Zhu Xi became those teachers for countless generations.

## How to Read Rashi Today

### Start with the Basics

If you're new to Torah study, here's how to approach Rashi:

1. **Read the Torah verse first** in your own language—understand what's happening
2. **Look at Rashi's comment**—often he'll explain a single difficult word, or point out something you might have missed
3. **Ask yourself why Rashi chose to comment here**—Rashi rarely wastes words; if he comments, there's a reason
4. **Don't expect to understand everything at once**—Rashi sometimes references Talmudic discussions or earlier verses; you'll catch more as you read more

### Rashi's Clarity

One of Rashi's greatest gifts is his clarity. He writes in simple, straightforward Hebrew. Unlike some commentators who show off their learning with complex arguments, Rashi explains things so a child can understand—but with such precision that scholars debate the implications of his every word.

### Modern Tools

Today, you can study Rashi more easily than ever:

- **Sefaria.org** provides free, searchable access to the entire Torah with Rashi's commentary in Hebrew and English
- Many **bilingual printed editions** feature Rashi's commentary alongside translations
- **Online classes** walk through Rashi's commentary verse by verse

## Practical Application: Learning to Think Like Rashi

Rashi teaches us not just *what* to think about the Torah, but *how* to think:

### 1. Pay Attention to Every Word

Rashi notices when a word is unusual, when the Torah uses one synonym instead of another, when a verse seems repetitive. He teaches us that every word in Torah is chosen with care.

**Example**: When the Torah says God "formed" (*vayitzer*, ויצר) man, it uses two letter *yud*s (ייצר) instead of one. Rashi explains this hints at the two inclinations in human nature—the inclination toward good and the inclination toward evil.

### 2. Ask Questions First, Then Seek Answers

Rashi often begins a comment with a question: "Why does it say this?" "What's the problem here?" He models active, questioning engagement with the text.

### 3. Connect Past and Present

Rashi frequently connects the Torah's stories to later Jewish history and to our lives today. The Torah isn't just ancient history—it's a living guide.

### 4. Balance Tradition and Plain Meaning

Rashi shows us how to respect centuries of rabbinic interpretation while still asking, "But what does the text actually say?"

## Key Takeaways

- **Rashi (1040-1105 CE) was a French rabbi** who lived during China's Northern Song Dynasty and wrote the most influential commentary on the Torah and Talmud in Jewish history
- **His commentary made sacred texts accessible** to anyone, not just advanced scholars—revolutionizing Jewish education
- **He balanced *peshat* (plain meaning) and *derash* (interpretive tradition)**, selecting carefully from rabbinic sources to illuminate the text without obscuring it
- **His Talmud commentary is printed on every page** of the Talmud, and his Torah commentary is the foundation of countless other works
- **His influence reached beyond Judaism** to Christian biblical scholarship and linguistics
- **Learning to read Rashi teaches you to think critically and carefully** about sacred texts—a skill applicable to any deep reading
