# How to Tie Tzitzit: A Step-by-Step Guide with Diagrams

---

The first time you hold the four strings and corner of a tallit in your hands, you might feel a bit overwhelmed. Eight strings, specific knots, wrapping patterns—it can seem complicated. But thousands of years of Jewish tradition have preserved this beautiful mitzvah, and with a little patience and practice, you'll find that tying tzitzit becomes almost meditative.

This guide will walk you through the process step by step, explaining not just *how* to tie tzitzit, but *why* we tie them this way.

## What Are Tzitzit?

Tzitzit (ציצית) are the specially knotted fringes that Jewish men attach to four-cornered garments. The Torah commands this practice in two places: Numbers 15:37-41 and Deuteronomy 22:12. The purpose is profound yet simple: "Look at them and remember all the commandments of the Lord" (Numbers 15:39).

Every time you see or touch your tzitzit, you're meant to remember your connection to God and your commitment to living according to His commandments. It's a physical reminder of spiritual obligations—something to keep you mindful throughout your day.

## Before You Begin: What You'll Need

**Materials:**
- A four-cornered garment (tallit katan or tallit gadol)
- Tzitzit strings (usually sold in packs of four sets)
- Scissors
- Good lighting
- Patience!

**Important:** Most tzitzit strings you purchase will already be doubled over, creating the eight strings you need from four actual strands. One of these strands (the shamash) will be longer than the others—this is the string you'll use for wrapping.

## Understanding the Structure

Before we begin tying, it helps to understand what we're creating:

Each corner will have:
- **7-8-11-13 wraps** (according to Ashkenazi custom)
- **5 double knots** (at the start and between each set of wraps)
- **Four sections** divided by the knots

These numbers aren't random. The gematria (numerical value) of the wraps and knots, plus the eight strings themselves, equals 613—representing all 613 commandments in the Torah.

## Step-by-Step Instructions

### Step 1: Threading the Strings

Take your four strings (which will become eight when doubled) and thread them through the hole in the corner of your garment. Pull them through until you have seven shorter strings of equal length on one side, and one significantly longer string (the shamash) on the other.

The shamash should be about twice as long as the others—this is the string that will do all the wrapping.

### Step 2: The First Double Knot

Gather all eight strings together and tie a simple overhand knot close to the corner of the garment (about 4-5 centimeters from the edge). Then tie a second identical knot on top of the first—this creates your first "double knot."

Make sure this knot is firm but not so tight that it damages the fabric. These double knots define the structure of your tzitzit.

### Step 3: The First Section (7 Wraps)

Now we begin the wrapping pattern. Take the shamash (the long string) and wrap it around the other seven strings **seven times**, moving downward from the double knot.

Keep the wraps close together but not overlapping. Each wrap should sit neatly below the previous one. The wraps should be snug but not overly tight.

When you've completed seven wraps, tie another double knot exactly as you did before.

### Step 4: The Second Section (8 Wraps)

Immediately after the double knot, begin wrapping again with the shamash. This time, make **eight wraps** around the other seven strings.

Complete with another double knot.

### Step 5: The Third Section (11 Wraps)

Continue the pattern: **eleven wraps** with the shamash around the other strings, followed by another double knot.

By now, you should be getting a rhythm—wrap, wrap, wrap, double knot.

### Step 6: The Fourth Section (13 Wraps)

The final section requires **thirteen wraps** with the shamash, followed by the final double knot.

### Step 7: The Final Touches

After your last double knot, you should have several centimeters of loose strings hanging down. Tradition requires that the hanging portion (from the last knot to the end of the strings) be at least the same length as the wrapped portion (from the corner to the last knot).

Trim all eight strings to the same length if needed, creating a neat, even fringe.

### Step 8: Repeat for All Four Corners

You've completed one corner! Now repeat this entire process for the other three corners of your garment.

## Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

**Mistake #1: Wrong shamash length**  
If your shamash runs out before you finish, you measured incorrectly. The shamash should be roughly twice as long as the other strings. Always check before you start.

**Mistake #2: Inconsistent wrapping**  
Try to keep your wraps uniform in tightness and spacing. This takes practice, so don't worry if your first set looks a bit irregular.

**Mistake #3: Knots too loose**  
Double knots should be firm enough to stay in place but not so tight they damage the garment or strings.

**Mistake #4: Counting errors**  
It's easy to lose count, especially during the 11 and 13-wrap sections. Some people count aloud, others use their fingers to mark groups of five. Find what works for you.

## Different Customs: Sephardi vs. Ashkenazi

The method described above follows Ashkenazi custom (7-8-11-13). Sephardi communities often use a different pattern:

- **Sephardi method:** 10-5-6-5 wraps

Both are valid and fulfill the mitzvah. If you're converting or new to Judaism, ask your rabbi which custom your community follows.

Some Sephardi communities also tie an additional knot within some wrapping sections, creating a more complex pattern. The principle remains the same: specific numbers of wraps separated by knots.

## The Deeper Meaning

Why all this precision? Why these specific numbers?

Jewish tradition finds multiple layers of meaning:

**Numerical significance:**  
The word tzitzit (ציצית) has a gematria value of 600. Add the eight strings and five knots, and you get 613—the total number of Torah commandments.

**Visual reminder:**  
According to the Talmud, the Hebrew word for tzitzit shares a root with the word meaning "to look" or "to see." These fringes catch your eye throughout the day, redirecting your attention to spiritual matters.

**Humility and dignity:**  
The tzitzit hang below, toward the earth, reminding us of humility. Yet they're attached to our garments, elevating everyday clothing into ritual objects.

## A Chinese Cultural Parallel: The Mindfulness of Knot-Tying

Chinese culture has a rich tradition of decorative knot-tying (中国结, Zhongguo jie), where intricate knots serve as both art and symbol. Like tzitzit, Chinese knots carry meaning beyond their physical form—they represent luck, harmony, and connection to tradition.

The meditative act of tying Chinese knots, where each loop and crossing must be precise yet flows naturally with practice, mirrors the experience of tying tzitzit. Both practices require patience, attention to detail, and ultimately create something both beautiful and meaningful.

In both traditions, the knots we tie connect us to our ancestors who tied the same patterns, linking past and present through simple string.

## Caring for Your Tzitzit

**Daily wear:**  
Tzitzit strings will naturally fray and break over time—this is normal. When a string breaks, you don't necessarily need to replace the entire set. Jewish law provides specific guidance on when tzitzit remain kosher despite some damage.

**Replacement:**  
Many men replace their tzitzit annually before Rosh Hashanah, starting the new year with fresh strings. Others replace them as needed when they become too worn.

**Washing:**  
You can wash a garment with tzitzit attached. Some people prefer to remove the tzitzit first, but it's not required. Air-drying is gentler on the strings than machine drying.

## When You First Wear Them

The first time you put on a tallit with tzitzit you've tied yourself, there's something special about it. These aren't just strings—they're strings *you* tied, following an ancient pattern, connecting yourself to centuries of Jewish practice.

You might feel a sense of accomplishment, maybe a bit of pride in getting the pattern right. That's good. But more importantly, you should feel connected—to God, to Torah, to the Jewish people across time and space who have all performed this same mitzvah.

## Learning by Doing

Like many Jewish practices, tying tzitzit is best learned hands-on. If possible, ask someone experienced to guide you through your first set. There are also many video tutorials available online that can show you the wrapping motion more clearly than written instructions ever could.

Don't be discouraged if your first attempts aren't perfect. The mitzvah is in the intention and the action, not in achieving aesthetic perfection. Your tzitzit are kosher even if they're not beautiful (as long as the basic requirements are met).

With practice, you'll develop muscle memory, and your hands will know the pattern without your mind needing to count every wrap.

## The Mitzvah of Tzitzit in Daily Life

Once you've learned to tie tzitzit, you'll appreciate them differently when you wear them. You'll notice the weight of the strings, the way they move as you walk, the reminder they provide dozens of times throughout your day.

When you grab them during the Shema prayer, reciting "You shall see them and remember all the commandments," those words will have deeper meaning. These are the strings you tied. You know their structure. You understand their symbolism.

This is the beauty of hands-on mitzvot—they engage not just your mind and heart, but your hands and body as well.

## Conclusion: Strings That Connect

In a world of mass production and instant gratification, there's something profoundly countercultural about sitting down with eight strings and tying them in a specific, ancient pattern. It requires time, attention, and care.

But that's precisely the point. Tzitzit aren't meant to be convenient—they're meant to be mindful. The act of tying them yourself, string by string, wrap by wrap, knot by knot, is itself a meditation on connection: connection to Torah, to tradition, to the Divine.

So take your time. Count carefully. Enjoy the process. And when you're done, you'll have created something both humble and holy—a few strings that carry infinite meaning.
