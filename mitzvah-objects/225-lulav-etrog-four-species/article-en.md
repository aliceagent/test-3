# The Lulav and Etrog: Sukkot's Four Species Explained

## Introduction

---

During the festival of Sukkot, Jewish communities around the world perform one of the most visually distinctive mitzvot: holding and waving the Four Species—lulav (palm branch), etrog (citron), hadass (myrtle), and aravah (willow). This seemingly simple agricultural ritual connects us to ancient Temple practices, the Land of Israel, and profound spiritual truths about Jewish unity and relationship with God.

For someone encountering this tradition for the first time, the Four Species might appear unusual—why these specific plants? Why wave them in six directions? What makes an etrog kosher or pasul (invalid)? This article will guide you through the halachic requirements, symbolic meanings, and practical how-to of this beautiful mitzvah.

## Biblical Source and Historical Context

The Torah commands us explicitly: "On the first day you shall take the fruit of beautiful trees, branches of palm trees, boughs of leafy trees, and willows of the brook, and you shall rejoice before the Lord your God for seven days" (Leviticus 23:40).

Our oral tradition identifies these four species precisely:
- **Pri etz hadar** (fruit of a beautiful tree) = **etrog** (citron)
- **Kapaot temarim** (palm branches) = **lulav**
- **Anaf etz avot** (boughs of leafy trees) = **hadass** (myrtle)
- **Arvei nachal** (willows of the brook) = **aravah**

During the Temple period, these species were taken on all seven days of Sukkot within Jerusalem, but only on the first day in outlying areas. After the Temple's destruction, Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai instituted that we take them everywhere for all seven days (except Shabbat) as a remembrance of the Temple service.

This practice connects Chinese Jews to a ritual older than the Ming Dynasty (1368-1644 CE)—in fact, older than the Tang Dynasty (618-907 CE). When Jewish people were celebrating Sukkot in ancient Jerusalem during the Second Temple period, China was in the late Zhou Dynasty and early Han Dynasty era.

## The Four Species: Detailed Requirements

### 1. The Lulav (Date Palm Branch)

**Halachic Requirements:**
- Must be from a date palm tree
- The tiyomet (central spine) must be intact and closed
- Minimum length: one tefach (handbreadth, approximately 9-10 cm)
- Optimal length: at least 4 tefachim (approximately 40 cm)
- Cannot be dried out, split, or broken
- Must be straight; cannot be bent like a sickle

**Practical Note:** The lulav usually comes bound with the hadassim and aravot. Check that the binding doesn't crack the spine.

### 2. The Etrog (Citron)

The etrog is often considered the "beauty" of the Four Species and has the most stringent requirements.

**Halachic Requirements:**
- Must be from a citron tree (not a hybrid lemon)
- Minimum size: k'beitzah (egg-sized)
- Cannot have black or white spots covering most of the surface
- The pitom (protrusion on top) should ideally be intact, but if it fell off naturally, it remains kosher
- Cannot be cracked, punctured, or missing a piece
- Should have a beautiful appearance

**Special Concern:** Many commercial citrons are grafted. Sephardic communities are generally more lenient about grafted etrogim than Ashkenazi communities, who prefer etrogim with clear non-grafted lineage (often from Calabria, Italy, or Israel).

**Chinese Agricultural Parallel:** The meticulous care in selecting an unblemished etrog resembles the traditional Chinese practice of selecting perfect peaches or pomegranates for ancestral offerings—both cultures recognize that what we offer in spiritual contexts should be unblemished and beautiful.

### 3. The Hadass (Myrtle Branches)

**Halachic Requirements:**
- Need three branches minimum
- Each branch must have leaves growing in groups of three from the same point on the stem (meshulash)
- Leaves must cover the majority of the branch length
- Minimum length: 3 tefachim (approximately 30 cm)
- Cannot be dried out

**Common Issue:** Check that the leaves are indeed growing in triplets, not pairs. Not all myrtle varieties qualify halachically.

### 4. The Aravah (Willow Branches)

**Halachic Requirements:**
- Need two branches minimum
- Must be from a variety with elongated leaves and a reddish stem
- Leaves should be smooth-edged (not serrated)
- Cannot be dried out
- Minimum length: 3 tefachim (approximately 30 cm)

**Shelf Life:** Willows dry out fastest. Many people replace them during the intermediate days of Sukkot (Chol HaMoed) to ensure they remain fresh.

## How to Hold and Wave the Four Species

### The Proper Assembly

1. **The lulav** is held in the right hand with the spine facing you
2. **The hadassim** (3 myrtle branches) are placed on the right side of the lulav
3. **The aravot** (2 willow branches) are placed on the left side of the lulav
4. All three are bound together with palm leaves or special holders
5. **The etrog** is held in the left hand with the pitom (top) facing down

### The Blessing

On the first day (or your first time fulfilling the mitzvah that year), recite two blessings:

1. **"Baruch Atah Hashem, Elokeinu Melech ha'olam, asher kidshanu b'mitzvotav v'tzivanu al netilat lulav"**
   ("Blessed are You, Lord our God, King of the universe, who has sanctified us with His commandments and commanded us concerning the taking of the lulav")

2. **"Baruch Atah Hashem, Elokeinu Melech ha'olam, shehecheyanu v'kiyemanu v'higiyanu laz'man hazeh"**
   ("Blessed are You, Lord our God, King of the universe, who has kept us alive, sustained us, and brought us to this season")

On subsequent days, only the first blessing is recited.

### After the Blessing: Invert the Etrog

Immediately after the blessing, turn the etrog right-side up (pitom facing upward) and hold the Four Species together.

### The Waving: Na'anuim

While reciting Hallel during the morning prayers, wave the Four Species in six directions at specific verses:

**During "Hodu LaShem ki tov" (Psalm 118:1):**
- East (front)
- South (right)
- West (back)
- North (left)
- Up (skyward)
- Down (earthward)

**Repeat during "Ana Hashem hoshia na" (Psalm 118:25)**

**The Pattern:** 
- Extend forward and pull back three times for each direction
- Some hold the species stationary and shake them; others move their entire arms

**Symbolic Meaning:** We acknowledge that God is everywhere—in all six directions. Some interpret this as acknowledging God's control over nature: the four cardinal directions represent wind and weather, while up and down represent rain and earth.

## Profound Symbolism: Four Types of Jews

The Midrash offers a beautiful teaching about Jewish unity through these four species:

- **Etrog** has both taste (it's edible) and fragrance = Jews with both Torah knowledge and good deeds
- **Lulav** (date palm) produces fruit (dates) but has no fragrance = Jews with Torah knowledge but lacking in good deeds
- **Hadass** (myrtle) has fragrance but no taste = Jews with good deeds but lacking in Torah knowledge
- **Aravah** (willow) has neither taste nor fragrance = Jews lacking both Torah knowledge and good deeds

The mitzvah requires all four species together. We cannot fulfill it with only the "best" Jews (etrogim). Jewish peoplehood requires unity—we bind ourselves together with all types of Jews, recognizing that each contributes something essential to the collective.

**Chinese Philosophical Parallel:** This resembles the Confucian concept of social harmony (和 hé), where different types of people with varying virtues and roles form a complete society. Just as Confucius taught that a gentleman (君子 jūnzǐ) possesses multiple virtues (仁 rén, 義 yì, 禮 lǐ), Judaism teaches that the complete Jewish people requires all types working together.

## The Four Species and the Human Body

Another interpretation connects each species to parts of the human body:
- **Lulav** (tall and straight) = the spine
- **Etrog** (heart-shaped) = the heart
- **Hadass** (small oval leaves) = the eyes
- **Aravah** (elongated leaves) = the lips/mouth

When we perform this mitzvah, we sanctify our entire being—our physical structure (spine), our emotions (heart), our perception (eyes), and our speech (mouth)—in service of God.

## Practical Tips for First-Time Users

### Where to Purchase

- **Before Sukkot:** Jewish communities set up Four Species markets (often called "lulav markets" or "arba minim sales")
- **Online:** Many organizations ship complete sets worldwide
- **Pricing:** Ranges from basic sets (~$40-60 USD) to mehudar (beautified) sets with pristine etrogim ($100-300+ USD)

### Storage

- **Keep the etrog in its protective box** with padding to prevent bruising
- **Mist the lulav, hadass, and aravah daily** to keep them fresh
- **Store in a cool location**, but not refrigerated (except the aravot, which some refrigerate in a sealed bag)

### If You're in China

Finding Four Species in mainland China can be challenging. Contact:
- Chabad of Beijing, Shanghai, or Hong Kong
- Jewish communities in major cities who often import sets collectively
- Online vendors who ship internationally (order well in advance)

### Children and the Mitzvah

Children who have reached the age of chinuch (education in mitzvot, typically around age 6-7 for boys) should be trained in this mitzvah. Many families purchase a second, less expensive set for children to practice with.

## Special Days: Hoshana Rabbah

On the seventh day of Sukkot (Hoshana Rabbah), we circle the bimah (synagogue platform) seven times while holding the Four Species and reciting special prayers called Hoshanot. We also take a separate bundle of five aravot (willows) and strike them on the ground, symbolically beating away the last traces of our sins from the High Holiday season.

This day is considered the final seal of judgment that began on Rosh Hashanah. There's a custom to stay up all night studying Torah, similar to Shavuot.

## The Deeper Meaning: Joy Before God

The Torah's language is specific: "You shall rejoice before the Lord your God for seven days" (Leviticus 23:40). The Four Species are instruments of joy, not somber religious obligation.

Sukkot is called "Z'man Simchateinu" (The Time of Our Joy). After the intensity of Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur, after we've done the internal work of repentance and emerged forgiven, we celebrate with physical, tangible mitzvot—dwelling in the sukkah, holding beautiful plants from the Land of Israel.

**Chinese Harvest Festival Parallel:** This timing parallels the Mid-Autumn Festival (中秋節 Zhōngqiū Jié), which also occurs in autumn and celebrates the harvest with joy, family gatherings, and thanksgiving. Both festivals acknowledge human dependence on successful harvests and express gratitude to Heaven/God for sustenance.

## Common Questions

**Q: What if I'm left-handed?**
A: Left-handed individuals should hold the lulav in their left hand and the etrog in their right hand—the "right hand" in halacha means your dominant hand.

**Q: Can women perform this mitzvah?**
A: Yes. Although it's a time-bound positive commandment, from which women are generally exempt, many women have the custom to fulfill this mitzvah, and they may recite the blessing.

**Q: What if my etrog's pitom breaks off?**
A: Consult a rabbi immediately. If it broke off after you first took the Four Species on Yom Tov, it's likely still kosher. If it was missing from the start, it depends on whether it fell off naturally or was broken.

**Q: I missed the first day of Sukkot. Can I still say Shehecheyanu?**
A: Yes, say it on the first day you perform the mitzvah.

**Q: Can I use the same set as someone else?**
A: On the first day of Sukkot (or the first day it's taken when it falls on Shabbat), you should ideally own the set. Borrowed sets can be used on subsequent days. There's a halachic mechanism called "matanah al menat lehachzir" (a gift on condition of return) that allows sharing.

## Conclusion: Ancient Ritual, Timeless Message

The Four Species unite ancient agricultural practice, Temple service, botanical precision, and profound spiritual symbolism. When you wave the lulav and etrog during Sukkot, you're participating in a ritual performed by Jews for over 3,000 years—in the Babylonian exile, in medieval Spain, in the ghettos of Eastern Europe, in modern Israel, and now in Jewish communities worldwide, including China.

This mitzvah teaches us that holiness dwells in physical objects, that beauty matters in worship, that Jewish unity requires all types of Jews, and that our relationship with God engages our entire body—spine, heart, eyes, and mouth.

As you hold these four species together, remember: you too are part of a greater whole, bound together with Jews of all types, across all generations, rejoicing before God during the season of our joy.

**Chag Sameach—Happy Sukkot!**
