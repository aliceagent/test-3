## Introduction

Imagine receiving your monthly salary and immediately setting aside a portion — not for savings, not for investments, but for giving to those in need. This practice, called *ma'aser kesafim* (מעשר כספים), or "tithing money," has been a cornerstone of Jewish financial ethics for centuries. But here's the interesting question that confounds even experienced practitioners: Should you give 10%, or should you aim for 20%? And what happens if you can't afford either?

Unlike the agricultural tithes commanded explicitly in the Torah during the time when the Temple stood in Jerusalem (corresponding to China's Warring States through Han Dynasty period, 戰國至漢朝), monetary tithing developed as a rabbinic practice and custom. Yet it has become so deeply woven into Jewish life that many observant Jews consider it as binding as biblical law itself. This article explores the origins, requirements, and practical considerations of ma'aser kesafim — helping you navigate one of Judaism's most impactful yet flexible obligations.

## The Biblical Roots: From Fields to Finances

### Agricultural Tithes in Ancient Israel

The Torah commands several forms of agricultural tithing. During the First Temple period (957-586 BCE, corresponding to China's Spring and Autumn period, 春秋時期), Jewish farmers were required to give:

1. **Ma'aser Rishon** (First Tithe): 10% of produce to the Levites
2. **Ma'aser Sheni** (Second Tithe): Another 10% set aside for consumption in Jerusalem
3. **Ma'aser Ani** (Poor Tithe): 10% given to the poor in the third and sixth years of the seven-year agricultural cycle

These tithes served both spiritual and social purposes — supporting the priestly class who had no land inheritance, funding religious infrastructure, and creating a systematic welfare network for the vulnerable.

### The Transition to Monetary Giving

After the destruction of the Second Temple in 70 CE (during China's Han Dynasty, 漢朝), when agricultural tithes could no longer be brought to Jerusalem, the question arose: What about wealth earned through commerce, crafts, or other non-agricultural means? Medieval rabbinic authorities grappled with this question. The practice of ma'aser kesafim emerged as a *minhag* (custom) rather than strict law, but gained such widespread acceptance that many authorities treated it with near-biblical seriousness.

The foundational text comes from the Talmud (Taanit 9a), which states: "Tithe so that you may become wealthy" (*aser bishvil shetitasher*). This cryptic statement — a play on words in Hebrew where "tithe" (*aser*) and "become wealthy" (*titasher*) share the same root — suggests that giving tzedakah is not only morally right but creates a cycle of blessing and prosperity.

## The 10% Standard: The Minimum Obligation

### What the Law Requires

According to most halachic authorities, **10% of net income** is considered the baseline for ma'aser kesafim. This applies to:

- **Salary and wages** after taxes
- **Business profits** after expenses
- **Gifts of money** (subject to rabbinic debate)
- **Inheritance** (also debated — some say yes, others say no)
- **Investment gains** in most opinions

The 10% is calculated on your *net* income — what you actually receive after mandatory taxes and business expenses, not gross revenue. The Shulchan Aruch (Yoreh Deah 249:1) codifies this as the normative practice.

### Historical Context

The 10% standard has deep resonance in Jewish tradition. Abraham gave a tenth of his war spoils to Melchizedek, the priest-king of Salem (Genesis 14:20). Jacob vowed to give a tenth of all that God would give him (Genesis 28:22). These precedents, though not formal commandments, established a cultural expectation that carried through millennia.

In Chinese tradition, the concept of *yi* (義, righteousness) similarly emphasizes moral duty toward society. Confucian thought taught that the prosperous have responsibilities to support social harmony — a parallel that makes ma'aser kesafim feel culturally familiar to many Chinese learners of Judaism.

## The 20% Ideal: Going Beyond the Baseline

### The Upper Limit

While 10% is the floor, the Talmud (Ketubot 50a) and later codes teach that **20% is considered the upper limit** of voluntary charitable giving. Why a cap? Because Jewish law seeks balance. The Shulchan Aruch rules that one should not give away so much that they themselves might become dependent on charity — a situation that ultimately reduces the total amount of good they can do in the world.

Giving between 10% and 20% is seen as *middah chasidus* — the practice of the pious. Those who can afford it and wish to go beyond the minimum are praised, but it's not obligatory.

### Who Should Aim for 20%?

Some authorities suggest that those with comfortable incomes and no financial stress should strive toward 20%. Rabbi Moshe Feinstein, one of the 20th century's preeminent halachic authorities, wrote that people who can afford it without hardship should aim higher than the baseline.

Conversely, those struggling financially are not expected to impoverish themselves. Some rabbinic opinions even exempt those in genuine need from ma'aser entirely, allowing them to focus on stabilizing their own situations first.

## Practical Questions: The Details Matter

### What Counts as Income?

**YES — typically requires ma'aser:**
- Monthly salary
- Freelance income
- Bonuses and commissions
- Business profits after expenses
- Rental income (net, after property costs)
- Interest and dividends (some opinions)

**DEBATED — consult a rabbi:**
- Inheritance (many say exempt, some say tithe)
- Large one-time gifts (divided opinions)
- Lottery or gambling winnings (complex question)
- Capital gains from selling a home
- Retirement account withdrawals (depends on structure)

**EXEMPT — generally:**
- Return of a loan you made
- Refunds or reimbursements
- Selling personal items at a loss

### Where Should You Give?

Ma'aser funds can go to any legitimate charitable cause, Jewish or otherwise. Traditional priorities include:

1. **Supporting Torah study** — yeshivas, Jewish schools, scholars
2. **Helping the poor** — food, housing, medical care
3. **Communal institutions** — synagogues, mikvahs, Jewish community centers
4. **Israel-related causes**
5. **Other worthy charities** — medical research, disaster relief, etc.

Many authorities recommend prioritizing local Jewish poor and institutions, but there is no strict requirement. The key is that the money genuinely helps others.

### Can You Use Ma'aser for Family Needs?

This is a common question. **Generally, no** — you cannot use ma'aser money for obligations you already have:

- ❌ Your children's Jewish education (that's your parental duty)
- ❌ Your own synagogue membership (that's a community obligation)
- ❌ Buying your own ritual objects (tefillin, mezuzah, etc.)

However, **some leniencies exist:**
- ✅ Helping poor relatives beyond your strict obligation
- ✅ Paying for others' Jewish education if they can't afford it
- ✅ Buying ritual items for communal use

When in doubt, consult a knowledgeable rabbi, as these questions can be nuanced.

## Special Situations and Leniencies

### Starting Out: Young Professionals and Students

Young adults just entering the workforce, burdened with student debt or struggling to establish themselves, are in a unique situation. Many authorities say:

- You can start with less than 10% and work up as your income stabilizes
- Student loan repayment reduces your "net income" for ma'aser calculation
- Saving for essential future needs (housing down payment) can sometimes be prioritized

The goal is to establish the *habit* of giving, even if the percentage starts smaller.

### Business Owners and Entrepreneurs

For those running businesses, ma'aser is calculated on **profit**, not revenue. All legitimate business expenses, salaries to employees, and operational costs are deducted first. The question becomes more complex with retained earnings, reinvestment, and growth capital — situations where rabbinic guidance is essential.

### Investment Income and Passive Gains

There's debate about capital gains and investment returns:
- **Conservative view**: All profit is income subject to ma'aser
- **Lenient view**: Only realized gains that you withdraw and use personally

Again, this is an area where personal consultation is valuable.

## The Psychology of Giving: Why It Changes You

### Training in Generosity

Ma'aser kesafim is not just about the money — it's about transforming your relationship with wealth. By systematically giving away a significant portion of your income, you internalize several key truths:

1. **Your wealth is not entirely yours** — it's a trust from God
2. **Prosperity carries responsibility** — not just privilege
3. **Security comes from faith** — not from hoarding every dollar

In traditional Chinese philosophy, the concept of *de* (德, virtue) similarly emphasizes that true prosperity includes moral character and social contribution. Wealth without virtue is hollow.

### The Paradox of Abundance

Jewish tradition teaches that giving actually *increases* your prosperity, not decreases it. This seems counterintuitive from a purely economic standpoint, but countless Jews attest to experiencing unexpected blessings after committing to regular tzedakah. Whether you understand this as divine providence, the psychological benefits of generosity, or the social capital built through communal support — the pattern holds.

## Setting Up Your Ma'aser Practice

### Practical Steps

1. **Calculate your baseline**: Determine your monthly net income
2. **Choose your percentage**: Start with 10% or whatever is sustainable
3. **Set up automatic transfer**: Create a separate bank account or fund
4. **Track your giving**: Keep records for both spiritual and tax purposes
5. **Review annually**: Adjust as your financial situation changes

### Record Keeping

Some people maintain a detailed *tzedakah ledger*, noting:
- Income received
- Ma'aser amount calculated
- Organizations supported
- Date and amount of each gift

This practice has the added benefit of creating accountability and mindfulness around both earning and giving.

### Making It Meaningful

Rather than viewing ma'aser as a "tax," try to see it as an opportunity:
- Research organizations carefully and feel proud of your support
- Involve family members in deciding where to give
- Visit or volunteer with organizations you support
- Reflect on gratitude for what you have left after giving

## Cultural Parallels: Jewish and Chinese Wisdom

### Confucian Social Responsibility

The Confucian concept of *ren* (仁, benevolence or humaneness) shares common ground with Jewish tzedakah. Both traditions teach that the prosperous have duties toward society, that moral character is inseparable from wealth, and that a harmonious community requires economic justice.

During the Song Dynasty (宋朝, 960-1279 CE, roughly contemporary with medieval European Jewry), Chinese scholar-officials were expected to support education, relief for the poor, and public works — much like Jewish communal leaders were expected to be exemplary in tzedakah.

### Buddhist Dana and Jewish Tzedakah

Buddhist *dana* (generosity) emphasizes giving freely without expectation of return, which parallels the Jewish ideal that the highest form of charity is anonymous, preserving the recipient's dignity. Both traditions see giving as spiritually transformative for the giver, not just helpful for the receiver.

## Key Takeaways

- **Ma'aser kesafim** is a deeply rooted Jewish practice of giving 10-20% of income to charity
- **10% is the baseline** standard; 20% is considered the upper limit to avoid impoverishing yourself
- **Calculate on net income** after taxes and necessary business expenses
- **Flexibility exists** for those struggling financially, just starting out, or facing special circumstances
- **Ma'aser transforms you** spiritually and psychologically, not just materially helping others
- **Consult a rabbi** for complex situations involving inheritance, investments, or business income
- **The practice builds character** — teaching responsibility, gratitude, and trust in divine provision
