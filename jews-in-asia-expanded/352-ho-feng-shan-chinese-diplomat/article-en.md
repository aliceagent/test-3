## Introduction

Between 1938 and 1940, as Nazi Germany tightened its grip on Austria and began the systematic persecution of Jews, one Chinese diplomat in Vienna made a decision that would save thousands of lives. Dr. Ho Feng-Shan (何凤山), the Republic of China's consul-general in Vienna, issued visas to Shanghai—thousands of them—to Jews desperate to escape Nazi-occupied Austria. He did this despite opposition from his own superiors, despite personal financial risk, and despite the clear danger it posed to his diplomatic career.

For decades, his heroism went almost entirely unknown. It wasn't until the late 1990s, years after his death, that historians began to piece together the full scope of what he had done. Today, Ho Feng-Shan is recognized as one of the "Righteous Among the Nations" at Yad Vashem in Israel, the only Chinese diplomat to receive this honor. His story is a powerful testament to individual moral courage in the face of bureaucratic indifference and systematic evil—and it represents a unique moment when China became a place of refuge for Jews fleeing the Holocaust.

## The Historical Context: Jews Trapped in Vienna

After Nazi Germany annexed Austria in March 1938 in what became known as the *Anschluss*, the approximately 180,000 Jews living in Vienna suddenly found themselves under Nazi racial laws. Almost overnight, they were stripped of their citizenship, their property, their livelihoods. Jewish businesses were "Aryanized"—stolen by the state and given to non-Jews. Jewish professionals were barred from their professions. Jewish children were expelled from schools. And most ominously, the Gestapo began rounding up Jews for deportation to concentration camps.

This was during what corresponds roughly to the Chinese Second Sino-Japanese War period (1937-1945, 抗日战争时期), when China itself was fighting for survival against Japanese invasion. The Republic of China government, led by Chiang Kai-shek, had relocated to Chongqing after the fall of Nanjing in 1937. China was in no position to take on international refugee crises. Yet it was precisely in this period that one of its diplomats would perform extraordinary acts of humanitarian rescue.

The desperate Jews of Vienna faced an impossible catch-22: most countries required immigrants to show proof of departure—an exit visa or evidence that they had a destination—before they would grant entry. But the Nazi authorities would not grant exit visas unless Jews could prove they had somewhere to go. It was a vicious circle designed to trap Jews in Austria where they could be systematically persecuted and eventually murdered.

## Shanghai: The Last Open Door

In 1938, as nation after nation closed their doors to Jewish refugees—the United States maintained strict quotas, Britain severely limited immigration to Palestine, Latin American countries tightened restrictions—Shanghai remained one of the last places on Earth that required no visa for entry. This was not due to Chinese generosity, but rather an accident of history: Shanghai's International Settlement and French Concession operated outside Chinese sovereignty, a legacy of the "unequal treaties" imposed on China by foreign powers in the 19th century.

Anyone could land in Shanghai without documentation. By 1939, approximately 18,000 Jewish refugees had made their way to Shanghai, creating a vibrant exile community in the Hongkou district. But even to get to Shanghai, Jews first needed to escape Austria—and that meant having papers, any papers, that would satisfy the Nazi bureaucrats and allow them to leave.

## Ho Feng-Shan: The Man Behind the Visas

Dr. Ho Feng-Shan was born in 1901 in Yiyang, Hunan Province (湖南益阳). He came from a poor family but was intellectually gifted, earning a scholarship to Yali School (雅礼中学), a Christian mission school in Changsha. He later studied at the University of Munich in Germany, where he earned his doctorate in political economy. His education in Germany gave him fluency in German and a deep understanding of European politics—knowledge that would prove crucial in his posting to Vienna.

In 1937, Ho was appointed consul-general of the Republic of China in Vienna. He arrived at his post just months before the Nazi annexation of Austria, and he would serve in Vienna through some of the darkest years of the Holocaust.

According to his daughter, Dr. Manli Ho, her father was motivated by simple humanitarian principle. He saw Jews being persecuted, and he knew he had the power to help them. As a Christian (he had converted during his time at the mission school), he later wrote that his faith compelled him to act. In his memoirs, he wrote simply: "I thought it only natural to feel compassion and to want to help."

## The Visa Operation: Courage Under Bureaucratic Opposition

When Jews began approaching the Chinese consulate seeking visas to Shanghai, Ho Feng-Shan recognized immediately what was needed. Even though Shanghai technically did not require a visa for entry, having a Chinese visa in their passport would satisfy the Nazi authorities and allow Jews to obtain the essential exit permit to leave Austria. The visa didn't even need to be genuine in the bureaucratic sense—it simply needed to exist on paper.

Ho began issuing visas by the hundreds, then by the thousands. He charged only a minimal fee (some accounts suggest he often waived fees entirely for those who could not pay). He operated, in effect, a humanitarian assembly line, processing applications as quickly as possible while Nazi persecution intensified week by week.

But Ho's actions soon attracted the attention of his superior, Chen Jie (陈介), the Chinese ambassador to Germany based in Berlin. Ambassador Chen ordered Ho to stop issuing visas. The reasons were partly political—Chen did not want to antagonize Germany, with which China was attempting to maintain diplomatic relations despite the war with Japan—and partly practical. Chen argued that Shanghai was already overwhelmed with refugees and could not absorb more.

Ho Feng-Shan defied the order. He continued issuing visas.

The bureaucratic conflict escalated. In 1939, the landlord of the Chinese consulate building, under pressure from the Nazi authorities, demanded that the consulate vacate the premises. This was almost certainly retaliation for Ho's visa activities. Rather than close down or reduce operations, Ho purchased a new building with his own personal funds and continued the visa operation from the new location.

Think about what this means: Ho spent his own money—money he could not afford to lose—to continue saving Jewish lives. This was not a symbolic gesture or a mere bureaucratic maneuver. This was personal financial sacrifice in service of conscience.

## How Many Were Saved?

The exact number of Jews saved by Ho Feng-Shan's visas remains uncertain, and historians continue to research the question. The numbers cited range from around 1,000 to as many as 4,000 or more. Part of the difficulty in establishing an exact count is that many of the visa records were destroyed during the war, and Ho himself kept no publicity about his actions.

What we know for certain:
- Ho issued visas continuously from 1938 to 1940, when he was reassigned to a different posting
- Surviving visa numbers show that he issued visas numbered into the thousands (one researcher found evidence of visa #1906 dated October 1938, and another found evidence of visas in the #3,000-4,000 range by 1940)
- Many Jews used the Chinese visa to escape to other countries once they left Austria—the visa was a ticket out, not necessarily a ticket to Shanghai specifically
- Multiple survivor testimonies confirm that Ho's visas were essential to their escape

Eric Goldstaub, one of the Jews saved by Ho's visas, testified: "He was an angel to us...We knew we had a certificate and could get out."

## After the War: A Forgotten Hero

In 1940, Ho Feng-Shan was reassigned from Vienna, and his visa-issuing operation came to an end. He continued his diplomatic career, serving in various posts for the Republic of China government, including as ambassador to Egypt, Mexico, Bolivia, and Colombia. After the Communist victory in the Chinese Civil War, Ho chose to remain with the Nationalist government and eventually retired to the United States, settling in San Francisco.

Remarkably, Ho never spoke publicly about what he had done in Vienna. His own children knew almost nothing of his wartime actions. In his memoirs, *Forty Years of My Diplomatic Life* (《外交生涯四十年》), published in 1990, he devoted only a brief passage to the Vienna period, mentioning the visa operation almost in passing.

Why the silence? Ho's daughter suggests several reasons: modesty, humility, and a sense that he had simply done what any decent person would do. There may also have been political calculation—Ho's defiance of his ambassador's orders was technically insubordination, and he may not have wanted to call attention to it. Additionally, in the complex post-war political landscape of Taiwan and the Chinese diaspora, Ho may have felt that publicizing his assistance to European Jews would serve no practical purpose.

## Discovery and Recognition

It was not until 1997, two years before Ho's death, that his story began to emerge publicly. Eric Goldstaub, who had been saved by a Ho-issued visa, was researching his own family history when he realized that the Chinese consul who had saved him was still alive. Goldstaub contacted Ho and shared his story with Holocaust researchers and the Israeli organization Yad Vashem.

Investigations revealed the scope of Ho's actions. In 2000, one year after his death, Ho Feng-Shan was posthumously honored by Yad Vashem as "Righteous Among the Nations"—the highest honor Israel bestows on non-Jews who risked their lives to save Jews during the Holocaust. His daughter Manli Ho received the award on his behalf in a ceremony at the Israeli consulate in San Francisco.

The memorial at Yad Vashem in Jerusalem now bears Ho's name alongside the names of other rescuers from around the world. He is the only Chinese diplomat to receive this recognition.

In 2005, the Israeli postal service issued a stamp in Ho's honor. In 2007, the Holocaust Memorial Center in Farmington Hills, Michigan, dedicated an exhibit about Ho Feng-Shan. In mainland China, Ho's hometown of Yiyang has erected a memorial, and his story is now taught in some Chinese schools as an example of humanitarian courage.

## The Meaning of Ho's Actions: Lessons in Moral Courage

What makes Ho Feng-Shan's story particularly powerful is not just the number of people he saved (though that alone would be remarkable), but the nature of his moral choice. He acted:

**Without expectation of reward or recognition.** Ho never publicized his actions. For decades, even his own family did not know. He did not use his rescue work to build a post-war career or reputation. He simply did what he believed was right and then moved on.

**Against direct orders from his superiors.** Ho understood that issuing the visas contradicted his ambassador's instructions and potentially endangered his career. He issued them anyway. This is civil courage in its purest form—the willingness to disobey unjust orders even at personal cost.

**At personal financial expense.** When the consulate was forced to relocate, Ho used his own money to continue operations. Diplomats are not wealthy people, and this sacrifice was real.

**In a context of overwhelming evil.** It's easy, in retrospect, to say "of course you would help people fleeing genocide." But in 1938-1940, the full horror of the Holocaust was not yet known, and the Nazi propaganda machine was working overtime to portray Jews as dangerous enemies. Ho saw through the propaganda and recognized human beings in desperate need.

## Parallel Lessons: Yiyi (义) and Confucian Righteous Courage

For readers from a Chinese cultural background, Ho's story resonates deeply with traditional Confucian values, particularly the concept of *yi* (义), often translated as "righteousness" or "moral duty." *Yi* is one of the cardinal virtues in Confucianism, representing the moral obligation to do what is right even when it is difficult, costly, or personally disadvantageous.

The great Confucian philosopher Mencius (孟子, 372-289 BCE) taught: "Life is what I want; righteousness (*yi*) is also what I want. If I cannot have both, I will forsake life and choose righteousness." (生，亦我所欲也；义，亦我所欲也。二者不可得兼，舍生而取义者也。)

Ho Feng-Shan did not have to choose between life and righteousness in such dramatic terms, but he did have to choose between career security and moral obligation. He chose *yi*.

In Jewish thought, there is a parallel teaching. The Talmud states: "Whoever saves a single life, it is as if they saved an entire world" (*Mishnah Sanhedrin* 4:5). This teaching reflects the Jewish understanding that every human being contains infinite worth, created *b'tzelem Elohim* (in the image of God). When Ho issued those visas, he was not just saving individuals; he was saving worlds—entire families, future generations that would not have existed without his intervention.

## Ho Feng-Shan and Other Righteous Diplomats

Ho was not alone in using diplomatic power to rescue Jews, though he may have been unique among Chinese officials. His contemporary, Raoul Wallenberg of Sweden, issued protective passports to Hungarian Jews and is credited with saving tens of thousands. Aristides de Sousa Mendes of Portugal issued visas in defiance of his government, saving thousands of Jews fleeing France. Chiune Sugihara, the Japanese consul in Lithuania (whose story is told in Article #351), issued transit visas that saved approximately 6,000 Jews.

What these diplomats had in common was a willingness to see suffering human beings rather than abstract "refugees" or political problems. They recognized that the power of their position came with moral responsibility, and they used their stamps, their signatures, and their authority to write permission to live.

The parallel between Ho Feng-Shan and Sugihara Chiune is particularly striking: both were Asian diplomats serving in European posts, both witnessed the Nazi persecution of Jews, both defied their governments' orders, and both acted from a sense of simple moral duty rather than political or religious ideology. Their stories remind us that moral courage is not limited by culture, nationality, or religion. It is a universal human capacity.

## The Shanghai Connection: A Bridge Between Peoples

Ho's visas directed Jews to Shanghai, which adds another layer of meaning to his story. For approximately 18,000 Jewish refugees, Shanghai became a temporary home during the war years (1938-1945). The refugees created a rich cultural life in the Hongkou district, publishing newspapers in German and Yiddish, opening schools, cafes, and theaters. They called it "Little Vienna."

Chinese residents of Shanghai, despite their own hardships during the Japanese occupation, generally treated the Jewish refugees with kindness and acceptance. There are numerous accounts of Chinese neighbors sharing food with refugee families, Chinese doctors treating Jewish patients, and Chinese children playing with refugee children despite the language barrier.

This period represents a unique moment in Jewish-Chinese relations. While individual Chinese dynasties had occasionally welcomed small Jewish trading communities (notably the Kaifeng Jews, who arrived in China around the 11th century during the Song Dynasty), the Shanghai refuge period was the first time large numbers of Jews and Chinese lived side by side, and it occurred during a moment of shared suffering—China fighting Japanese invasion, Jews fleeing Nazi persecution.

The refugees who survived the war in Shanghai almost all eventually emigrated—most to Israel, the United States, or Australia—but many maintained warm memories of their time in Shanghai and of the Chinese people who sheltered them. This historical moment remains a source of positive connection between Chinese and Jewish communities today.

## Practical Application: Moral Courage in Everyday Life

Most of us will never face the kind of life-and-death decisions that Ho Feng-Shan confronted. But his story offers lessons that apply to smaller-scale moral challenges:

**The power of the individual.** Ho was one man with a stamp and a desk. He was not wealthy, not politically powerful, not commanding armies. But he had access to a tool—visa-issuing authority—and he chose to use it. Each of us has some form of power or access in our lives. The question is: what will we do with it?

**Courage means acting despite cost.** Ho knew he was risking his career and spending his own money. He did it anyway. Moral courage is not the absence of fear or concern about consequences; it's the decision to do what's right despite those fears.

**Bureaucracy is not destiny.** When Ho's superior ordered him to stop issuing visas, Ho had a choice: obey the bureaucracy or obey his conscience. He chose conscience. In any organization—corporate, governmental, or social—there are moments when "following orders" means participating in injustice. Ho's example reminds us that we are not morally obligated to obey unjust orders, and that individual resistance matters.

**Quiet heroism is still heroism.** Ho never sought recognition. He did what he believed was right and then moved on with his life. In our age of social media performance and "virtue signaling," Ho's example of humble, unpublicized goodness is particularly powerful. The truest moral acts are often those done without witnesses, without cameras, without expectation of praise.

## The Jewish Response: Remembering the Righteous

In Jewish tradition, there is a profound obligation to remember and honor those who helped Jews in times of persecution. The book of Esther, read on the holiday of Purim, emphasizes that the deliverance of the Jewish people was achieved through Mordechai and Esther, but also through the Persian King Achashverosh who was ultimately persuaded to protect the Jews. The message: we remember not only Jewish heroes but also non-Jewish protectors.

This is why Yad Vashem created the "Righteous Among the Nations" program. It is not merely about giving awards; it is about fulfilling a moral obligation to publicly acknowledge and honor those who demonstrated humanity and courage. As the Talmud teaches, "The righteous of all nations have a share in the World to Come" (*Tosefta Sanhedrin* 13:2).

For Chinese readers exploring Judaism, this aspect of Jewish culture is important to understand. Judaism places immense value on gratitude (*hakarat hatov*, הכרת הטוב, literally "recognizing the good") and on actively remembering and honoring benefactors. Ho Feng-Shan is remembered not because Jews need to flatter China or because of political considerations, but because Jewish ethics demands that we remember and honor those who saved Jewish lives.

## Visiting Ho Feng-Shan Memorials

If you are interested in learning more about Ho Feng-Shan, several sites preserve his memory:

**In China:**
- **Yiyang, Hunan Province** (Ho's birthplace): A memorial park dedicated to Ho, including exhibits about his life and his rescue work in Vienna.
- **Shanghai**: The Shanghai Jewish Refugees Museum in Hongkou District includes exhibits about the diplomats who issued visas to Shanghai refugees, including Ho Feng-Shan.

**In Israel:**
- **Yad Vashem, Jerusalem**: Ho's name is inscribed on the Wall of Honor among the Righteous Among the Nations. The museum includes his story in exhibits about rescuers.

**In Vienna:**
- **Berggasse 8**: A plaque marks the location where Ho Feng-Shan purchased property with his own money to continue the Chinese consulate's operations and visa issuance. The plaque, installed in 2014, reads in German and Chinese: "In this building, the Chinese Consul General Dr. Ho Feng-Shan issued thousands of life-saving visas to Jewish refugees 1938-1940."

## Key Takeaways

- **Ho Feng-Shan, Chinese consul in Vienna (1938-1940), issued thousands of visas to Shanghai that enabled Austrian Jews to escape Nazi persecution.**
- **He acted against direct orders from his superiors and used his own money to continue the rescue operation when the consulate was forced to relocate.**
- **His actions saved between 1,000-4,000 Jewish lives, making him one of the most significant Asian rescuers during the Holocaust.**
- **Ho never publicized his heroism and his story only became known in 1997, shortly before his death.**
- **He was posthumously recognized as "Righteous Among the Nations" by Yad Vashem in 2000, the only Chinese diplomat to receive this honor.**
- **Ho's story exemplifies the Confucian value of *yi* (义, righteousness) and the Jewish principle that "whoever saves a single life saves an entire world."**
- **The Shanghai refuge period (1938-1945) represents a unique moment of Jewish-Chinese solidarity during a time when both peoples faced existential threats.**
