## Introduction

In the heart of Shanghai's former International Settlement, on a quiet street now named Shaanxi North Road, stands a remarkable building that has witnessed a century of turbulent history. The Ohel Rachel Synagogue, built in 1920 during China's early Republican era (民国时期), served as the spiritual center for Shanghai's Sephardic Jewish community — a community that grew from a handful of families in the 1840s to thousands by the early 20th century. Today, this grand structure stands as a protected historical landmark, a silent testament to the remarkable Jewish presence in China and the complex interweaving of Jewish and Chinese history in Shanghai.

For Chinese learners of Judaism, the Ohel Rachel Synagogue offers more than architectural beauty. It represents a tangible connection to a Jewish community that once thrived on Chinese soil, built businesses, established schools, and contributed to Shanghai's transformation into a modern metropolis. Understanding this synagogue's story helps us grasp how Judaism adapted to life in Asia and how Jews became part of Shanghai's cosmopolitan fabric.

## The Sassoon Family and Shanghai's Sephardic Jews

To understand the Ohel Rachel Synagogue, we must first understand the remarkable family that built it: the Sassoons, often called the "Rothschilds of the East." The Sassoons were Baghdadi Jews — Sephardic Jews who traced their ancestry to Spain but had lived in Iraq for centuries. When persecution intensified under Ottoman rule in the 1820s, David Sassoon fled Baghdad with his family and established himself in Bombay (now Mumbai), India, in 1832.

From this base, the Sassoon family built a trading empire that spanned Asia, dealing in opium, textiles, and later real estate and banking. By the 1840s, they had established operations in Shanghai, then just beginning its transformation from a small Chinese port into an international trading hub following the Treaty of Nanking (1842).

One of David Sassoon's sons, Sir Jacob Elias Sassoon (雅各布·伊利亞斯·沙遜), moved to Shanghai and became one of the city's most prominent businessmen and philanthropists. Sir Jacob married Rachel Sassoon (瑞切爾·沙遜), and when she passed away in 1919, he decided to memorialize her by building a magnificent synagogue — hence the name "Ohel Rachel," which in Hebrew means "Rachel's Tent."

This tradition of memorial building echoes certain Chinese practices of honoring ancestors through architectural dedications, though in this case expressed through Jewish religious architecture. Just as Chinese families might build a memorial hall (纪念堂) or endow a temple in honor of a deceased relative, Sir Jacob created a house of worship that would serve the community while commemorating his beloved wife.

## The Building: Architecture and Design

The Ohel Rachel Synagogue was completed in 1920, during a period when Shanghai was experiencing rapid modernization and architectural innovation. The building was designed in the Moorish Revival style, a popular architectural fashion for synagogues in the late 19th and early 20th centuries that drew inspiration from Islamic architecture of medieval Spain and North Africa — lands where Jews had once flourished before the 1492 expulsion from Spain.

The synagogue's exterior features distinctive horseshoe arches, decorative stonework, and twin towers flanking the main entrance. Inside, the sanctuary could accommodate up to 700 worshippers. The interior was designed according to traditional Sephardic liturgical requirements: the bimah (reading platform) at the front of the sanctuary, the ark (aron kodesh) housing the Torah scrolls positioned against the eastern wall (facing Jerusalem), and a women's gallery upstairs, separated from the main floor according to Orthodox Jewish practice.

The architectural choice of Moorish Revival carried deep meaning for Sephardic Jews. It represented a visual connection to their ancestral past in Spain and the Islamic lands, even as they built new lives in East Asia. For Chinese observers then (and now), the building's arches and decorative elements might have seemed exotic yet somehow familiar — Islamic architectural influence had reached China through the Silk Road, and Chinese Muslims (回族) had built mosques with similar arched forms throughout Chinese history.

## The Community That Worshipped Here

At its peak in the 1930s and 1940s, Shanghai's Jewish community numbered between 20,000 and 25,000 people — making it one of the largest Jewish populations in Asia. This community consisted of several distinct groups:

1. **Sephardic Jews** from Baghdad, Bombay, and other parts of the British Empire, who had arrived in the 19th century and established themselves as successful merchants and business leaders. The Sassoons, Kadoories, Hardoons, and other Sephardic families built much of Shanghai's modern infrastructure.

2. **Russian Jews** who fled to Shanghai after the Bolshevik Revolution (1917) and during the Russian Civil War. By the 1930s, thousands of Russian Jews had created a vibrant community in Shanghai's Hongkou district.

3. **European Jewish refugees** who arrived in the late 1930s and early 1940s, fleeing Nazi persecution in Europe. Shanghai was one of the few places in the world that did not require a visa for entry, making it a life-saving refuge for approximately 18,000 Central European Jews.

The Ohel Rachel Synagogue primarily served the Sephardic community, conducting services according to the Sephardic minhag (custom) and offering Hebrew education for children. The synagogue also served as a community center, hosting weddings, bar mitzvahs, and community gatherings. For the Sephardic elite of Shanghai, this synagogue was not just a place of worship but a symbol of their established presence and success in the city.

## Historical Periods and Challenges

### The Golden Era (1920s-1930s)

During the 1920s and 1930s, corresponding to China's early Republican period and the rule of the Nationalist government, the Ohel Rachel Synagogue flourished. Regular Shabbat services, holiday celebrations, and life-cycle events filled the sanctuary. The Sephardic community maintained strong ties to Jewish communities in other parts of Asia and the British Empire, and the synagogue served as a hub for this network.

This period paralleled a time when Shanghai itself was booming, known as the "Paris of the East," with its international settlement, modern infrastructure, and cosmopolitan culture. The Jewish community contributed significantly to this development through business, philanthropy, and cultural institutions.

### The War Years (1937-1945)

The Japanese invasion of China in 1937 and the occupation of Shanghai in 1941 brought profound changes. While the Japanese initially did not target Jews specifically, the situation became more complicated as Japan allied with Nazi Germany. In 1943, under pressure from their German allies, the Japanese established the "Shanghai Ghetto" in Hongkou, requiring all stateless Jewish refugees (primarily the European Jews) to relocate there.

The Sephardic and Russian Jews, who held British, French, or other passports, were generally not forced into the ghetto, and the Ohel Rachel Synagogue continued to function during this period. However, the community faced food shortages, restrictions on movement, and the general hardships of wartime occupation.

Chinese neighbors often helped Jewish residents during these difficult years, sharing food and offering protection — acts of kindness that Jews who lived through that period never forgot. This kindness reflected traditional Chinese values of hospitality and compassion toward strangers, similar to the Jewish principle of *hachnasat orchim* (welcoming guests).

### The Communist Era (1949-1980s)

When the Communist Party established the People's Republic of China in 1949, most of Shanghai's Jewish community left China. The Sephardic families moved to Hong Kong, India, England, or Israel. Russian Jews emigrated to Australia, the United States, or Israel. By the early 1950s, only a handful of Jews remained in Shanghai.

The Ohel Rachel Synagogue, like many religious buildings in China during this period, was confiscated by the government. For decades, it served various secular purposes: at different times it was used as a warehouse, an office building, and reportedly even as a stable. The Torah scrolls, prayer books, and ritual objects were either taken by departing families or disappeared.

This period parallels the broader treatment of religious institutions in China during the Cultural Revolution (1966-1976), when temples, churches, and mosques throughout the country were closed, repurposed, or damaged. The synagogue's survival at all, even in altered form, is remarkable given the destruction many religious sites suffered during that era.

### Recognition and Protection (1990s-Present)

The 1990s brought significant changes. As China opened to the world and began protecting historical sites, the Shanghai government recognized the cultural and historical importance of the Ohel Rachel Synagogue. In 1994, it was designated as a protected historical building, and in 1998, it was declared a Shanghai Municipal Cultural Heritage site.

However, the building remains closed to regular public access, as it is currently used by the Shanghai municipal government's Educational Bureau. Occasional tours are permitted for special groups, and the building has been used for rare Jewish community events when visiting Jewish leaders come to Shanghai.

The exterior has been maintained, and the distinctive architecture remains visible to passersby, serving as a reminder of Shanghai's cosmopolitan past. For the small Jewish community that exists in Shanghai today (primarily expatriate business people and diplomats), the building represents a tangible connection to the vibrant Jewish life that once flourished in the city.

## The Synagogue Today and Questions About Its Future

The current status of the Ohel Rachel Synagogue raises important questions about cultural heritage, religious freedom, and historical memory. While the building is protected and maintained, it does not function as an active place of worship. Shanghai's current Jewish community, estimated at a few hundred people, primarily worship at the Shanghai Jewish Center (Chabad of Pudong) established in the early 2000s.

Some community members and international Jewish organizations have expressed hope that one day the Ohel Rachel Synagogue might be restored to religious use, similar to how some historic churches in China have been reopened for worship. Others suggest it could become a museum documenting Jewish life in Shanghai, similar to the Shanghai Jewish Refugees Museum in Hongkou.

These discussions reflect broader questions about how China balances preservation of historical religious sites with current policies on religious practice, and how multicultural heritage is acknowledged and honored in a country with a complex recent history regarding religion.

## What the Ohel Rachel Synagogue Teaches Us

For students of Judaism, particularly those in China, the Ohel Rachel Synagogue offers several important lessons:

**Judaism's Global Diaspora**: Jews have lived in virtually every corner of the world, adapting to local conditions while maintaining core religious practices and identity. The synagogue represents Judaism's presence in Asia, far from its Middle Eastern origins.

**Cultural Bridge-Building**: The Sephardic Jews of Shanghai built businesses, contributed to the city's development, and participated in civic life while maintaining their distinct Jewish identity. This demonstrates how Jewish communities have historically navigated between particularism (maintaining distinct identity) and universalism (engaging with the broader society).

**Historical Complexity**: The synagogue's story encompasses colonialism, refugee crises, war, revolution, and modernization — key themes of 20th-century history played out in one building. Understanding this helps us grasp how Jews experienced these global upheavals.

**Resilience and Memory**: Even though the building no longer functions as a synagogue, its architectural survival ensures that the story of Shanghai's Jewish community is not forgotten. In Jewish tradition, memory (*zachor*) is a religious obligation, and physical landmarks help communities remember their history.

**Shared Human Values**: The Chinese neighbors who helped Jews during the war years, the government's decision to protect the building as a historical site, and the ongoing conversations about its future all reflect shared human values of compassion, historical preservation, and respect for cultural diversity.

## Connections to Traditional Jewish Thought

The concept of building a synagogue in memory of a loved one connects to several Jewish values:

**Tzedakah (Charity) and Chesed (Loving-kindness)**: Sir Jacob's decision to build a community institution rather than a personal monument reflects the Jewish principle that the best memorial is one that serves others. This echoes the teaching in Pirkei Avot (Ethics of the Fathers): "The world stands on three things: Torah, worship, and acts of loving-kindness."

**Zichronah L'vracha (Memory for a Blessing)**: In Jewish tradition, when mentioning a deceased person, we often say "may their memory be a blessing" (*zichronah l'vracha* for a woman, *zichrono l'vracha* for a man). Sir Jacob ensured Rachel's memory would be a blessing by creating a space for Torah study, prayer, and community gathering — activities that in Jewish thought help repair the world (*tikkun olam*).

**Mikdash Me'at (A Small Sanctuary)**: The Talmud refers to synagogues as "mikdash me'at" — small sanctuaries — echoing the ancient Temple in Jerusalem. Even far from Jerusalem, in Shanghai, this synagogue represented the Jewish people's continuous connection to their spiritual center and to God.

## Practical Application: Visiting or Learning About the Synagogue

For those interested in Jewish history in Asia, here are some practical ways to engage with this topic:

**If You're in Shanghai:**
- The building is located at 500 North Shaanxi Road (陕西北路500号) in the former French Concession area
- While not open for regular public visits, occasionally special tours can be arranged through Jewish community organizations
- Visit the Shanghai Jewish Refugees Museum in Hongkou for broader context about Jewish life in wartime Shanghai
- The exterior of the synagogue is visible from the street and worth seeing for its architectural significance

**For Learners Anywhere:**
- Research the Sassoon family's broader contributions to Asia — they built hotels (the Peace Hotel in Shanghai), founded schools, and contributed to many philanthropic causes
- Read memoirs and oral histories from Jews who lived in Shanghai (many are available online or in libraries)
- Compare the Sephardic Jewish experience in Asia with Ashkenazi Jewish experiences in Eastern Europe — understanding different Jewish cultural traditions
- Reflect on how Jewish communities adapt to new environments while maintaining core religious identity

## Key Takeaways

- The Ohel Rachel Synagogue, built in 1920 in Shanghai, served the city's Sephardic Jewish community for decades and remains a protected historical landmark
- Built by Sir Jacob Sassoon in memory of his wife Rachel, the synagogue exemplifies how Jewish philanthropy combines personal memorial with community service
- The building's Moorish Revival architecture represents the Sephardic Jewish connection to their Spanish and Middle Eastern heritage
- Shanghai's Jewish community at its peak numbered over 20,000 people from diverse backgrounds — Sephardic, Russian, and Central European refugees
- The synagogue survived the tumultuous 20th century, including war, revolution, and decades of repurposing, and was designated a protected site in the 1990s
- Today it stands as a testament to Jewish presence in Asia and raises ongoing questions about religious heritage preservation
- The story demonstrates Judaism's global reach, the adaptability of Jewish communities, and the importance of historical memory
- Chinese kindness toward Jews during difficult periods reflects shared human values that transcend cultural boundaries
