## Introduction

In the summer of 1940, as Nazi terror spread across Europe, thousands of Jewish refugees found themselves trapped in Lithuania with nowhere to go. Most nations had closed their doors. But one man — a Japanese diplomat stationed in the Lithuanian city of Kaunas — chose to defy his own government's orders. For nearly a month, Chiune Sugihara (杉原千畝) worked from dawn until his hand cramped, writing visa after visa that would become passages to life. By the time he left Lithuania, he had issued transit visas to over 6,000 Jewish refugees, saving them from almost certain death in the Holocaust. Today, he is remembered as "The Japanese Schindler" — a title that barely captures the magnitude of his courage and compassion.

## The Historical Context

### Europe in 1940

By the summer of 1940 — during what corresponds to the early years of China's War of Resistance against Japan (抗日战争, 1937-1945) and the outbreak of World War II in Europe — Nazi Germany had conquered Poland, and the Soviet Union had occupied the Baltic states of Lithuania, Latvia, and Estonia. Jewish refugees who had fled eastward from Poland suddenly found themselves trapped. The Soviet authorities were preparing to close the borders, and escape routes to the West were cut off. The only hope was to obtain transit visas through the Soviet Union to reach Japan, and from there to find passage to other destinations like Shanghai, Curaçao, or eventually the United States.

### The Japanese Consulate in Kaunas

Chiune Sugihara was serving as the Japanese Vice-Consul in Kaunas, Lithuania's temporary capital. Born in 1900 in Gifu Prefecture, Japan, Sugihara was a brilliant linguist who spoke Russian, German, French, and English. He had worked in the Japanese diplomatic service in Manchuria and had been posted to Lithuania in 1939 to gather intelligence on German and Soviet movements.

When desperate Jewish refugees began gathering outside his consulate in July 1940, Sugihara faced an impossible choice: follow orders or save lives.

## The Visas: A Race Against Time

### The Refugees' Plea

The refugees who came to Sugihara represented a cross-section of European Jewry — rabbis and yeshiva students from the famous Mir Yeshiva, families with small children, professionals, intellectuals, and ordinary people whose only crime was being born Jewish. Many had walked hundreds of miles. They stood in lines outside the consulate, day and night, begging for help.

The visa they needed was relatively simple: a transit visa through Japan. With this document, they could obtain the Soviet transit visa necessary to cross Russia via the Trans-Siberian Railway, reach Vladivostok, and sail to Japan. From Japan, they hoped to reach other destinations. The Dutch consul in Kaunas, Jan Zwartendijk, had agreed to issue entry permits to Curaçao (a Dutch colony that didn't require entry visas), giving the refugees a technical "destination" that would allow them to get Soviet transit papers.

### Sugihara's Dilemma

Sugihara cabled the Japanese Foreign Ministry three times requesting permission to issue the visas. Each time, Tokyo refused. The official reasons were bureaucratic: the refugees didn't have final destinations, they lacked proper funds, they couldn't demonstrate they were merely "passing through" Japan. But the real reason was likely political: Japan was allied with Nazi Germany and didn't want to antagonize its Axis partner.

According to his wife Yukiko's later accounts, Sugihara spent sleepless nights wrestling with the decision. Finally, in late July 1940, he made his choice: *"I may have to disobey my government, but if I don't, I will be disobeying God."*

### The Writing Marathon

From late July through early September 1940, Chiune Sugihara transformed his consulate into a visa-issuing machine. He began writing visas at 5:00 AM and continued until late at night. His wife Yukiko massaged his aching hands. His young sons helped by stamping documents. Sugihara simplified the application process to its bare minimum, skipping formalities to save time.

He wrote by hand, in careful script, hundreds upon hundreds of visas. When refugees couldn't afford the visa fee, he waived it. When they had no photographs, he accepted whatever they had. When families were too large to list on one visa, he split them across multiple documents.

Even as the consulate was being closed and Sugihara was ordered to leave Lithuania, he continued writing visas. According to witnesses, he was still signing and throwing visas out of the train window as it pulled away from Kaunas station. His final act was to stamp blank sheets of consulate stationery and give them to Jewish representatives, trusting they would fill them out properly.

## The Rescue and Its Impact

### The Survivors' Journey

The visas Sugihara issued became lifelines. Thousands of Jews used them to board trains across the Soviet Union, traveling the Trans-Siberian Railway for over a week to reach Vladivostok. From there, they sailed to Tsuruga and Kobe, Japan. While most stayed in Japan only briefly before traveling onward, some remained for months. The Japanese authorities, though initially skeptical of these refugees, generally honored Sugihara's visas and allowed them entry.

From Japan, the refugees scattered:
- Many reached Shanghai, which uniquely required no entry visa and sheltered approximately 20,000 Jewish refugees during the war
- Some made it to British-controlled Palestine (modern-day Israel)
- Others eventually reached the United States, Canada, Australia, and South America
- A significant number of students from the Mir Yeshiva escaped to Shanghai and rebuilt their institution there, continuing their Talmudic studies throughout the war

### The Mir Yeshiva: A Community Saved

Among the most famous groups saved by Sugihara's visas were the students and rabbis of the Mir Yeshiva, one of the great centers of Talmudic learning in pre-war Europe. Nearly the entire yeshiva — hundreds of students and their families — escaped using Sugihara's documents. They eventually settled in Shanghai, where they established a makeshift yeshiva and continued their studies despite poverty and uncertainty. After the war, the Mir Yeshiva was re-established in Jerusalem and Brooklyn, where it continues to this day as one of the largest yeshivas in the world. Every single student, rabbi, and family member studying there today owes their existence to Chiune Sugihara's courage.

### The Numbers

Historians estimate that Sugihara issued between 2,139 and 2,500 visas during his time in Kaunas. However, because many visas covered entire families, and because some refugees shared visas, the actual number of people saved is estimated at 6,000 or more. Today, tens of thousands of descendants of those refugees live around the world — a testament to one man's decision to do what was right despite the consequences.

## The Price of Conscience

### Sugihara's Later Life

After leaving Lithuania, Sugihara continued to serve in the Japanese diplomatic service, with postings in Prague, Germany, and Bucharest. When the war ended, he was dismissed from the Foreign Ministry. The official reason was "downsizing," but many believe he was quietly punished for his disobedience in Lithuania.

For the next 25 years, Sugihara lived in relative obscurity. He worked at various jobs, including as a manager for an export company. He never spoke publicly about what he had done in Kaunas. His modesty was such that even his own children didn't fully understand the magnitude of his actions until years later.

### Recognition and Remembrance

It wasn't until 1968 that Sugihara's story began to emerge. A survivor named Yehoshua Nishri, who had received a Sugihara visa as a teenager, tracked him down in Japan through decades of research. Slowly, survivors began to reach out, thanking Sugihara and sharing their stories.

In 1985, Yad Vashem, Israel's Holocaust memorial authority, recognized Chiune Sugihara as "Righteous Among the Nations" — the highest honor Israel bestows on non-Jews who risked their lives to save Jews during the Holocaust. Sugihara traveled to Israel in 1985 to receive the honor. He planted a tree on the Avenue of the Righteous and was warmly embraced by the survivors he had saved.

Chiune Sugihara passed away in 1986 at the age of 86, finally receiving the recognition he deserved. Today, memorials to Sugihara stand in Lithuania, Japan, and Israel. His childhood home in Yaotsu, Japan, houses a museum dedicated to his life. Streets are named after him. His story is taught in schools across Japan as an example of moral courage.

## The Lessons of Sugihara's Story

### Courage in the Face of Authority

Sugihara's story raises profound questions about obedience and conscience. He was a loyal Japanese civil servant, trained to follow orders and honor hierarchy — values deeply embedded in Japanese culture. Yet when those orders conflicted with his sense of humanity and justice, he chose to disobey. In Jewish thought, this resonates with the principle that "Pikuach Nefesh" — saving a life — overrides nearly every other commandment. When confronted with the choice between bureaucratic rules and human lives, Sugihara chose life.

This parallels a concept familiar in Chinese philosophy: the tension between *li* (礼, ritual propriety and social order) and *ren* (仁, humaneness and compassion). When these values conflict, which takes precedence? Sugihara's answer was clear: compassion for human life transcends all.

### The Power of One Person

Sugihara's story demonstrates that individuals matter. He was not a government, not an army, not a wealthy philanthropist. He was simply a mid-level diplomat with a pen and a conscience. Yet his actions saved thousands of lives and created tens of thousands of descendants. In Jewish tradition, there is a teaching from the Talmud: *"Whoever saves one life, it is as if he saved an entire world"* (Sanhedrin 37a). Sugihara saved not one life, but six thousand worlds.

### Remembering the Righteous

In a world where so many stood by and did nothing — or worse, actively participated in evil — the memory of righteous individuals like Sugihara becomes a moral beacon. Judaism commands us to remember both the perpetrators of evil and the exemplars of good. The "Righteous Among the Nations" program at Yad Vashem serves this purpose: ensuring that heroes like Sugihara are not forgotten, and that their courage inspires future generations.

### The Importance of Speaking Up

Sugihara's decision to act came after listening to the desperate pleas of refugees and wrestling with his conscience. He didn't act rashly, but neither did he hide behind bureaucracy. In our own lives, we may never face such dramatic moral choices, but we regularly encounter smaller moments where we must decide: Do I speak up or stay silent? Do I help or look away? Sugihara's example reminds us that our choices matter.

## Sugihara and the Japanese Spirit

### Bushido and Compassion

Some observers have noted a connection between Sugihara's actions and the principles of Bushido — the samurai code of honor that emphasizes duty, loyalty, and self-sacrifice. While Bushido traditionally focused on loyalty to one's lord, Sugihara demonstrated loyalty to a higher moral code. His willingness to sacrifice his career for the sake of righteousness reflects the Bushido values of courage (*yū* 勇) and benevolence (*jin* 仁).

### A Different Japanese Narrative

During World War II, Japan was allied with Nazi Germany and committed its own atrocities in Asia. Sugihara's story offers a counter-narrative: proof that even within a system aligned with evil, individuals could choose differently. His actions remind us that no nation is monolithic, and that personal conscience can transcend national policy.

## Practical Lessons for Today

### How to Honor Sugihara's Memory

1. **Learn the stories** — Read accounts of Sugihara visa recipients and share them with others, especially young people
2. **Visit the memorials** — If you travel to Japan, Lithuania, or Israel, visit the sites dedicated to Sugihara's memory
3. **Support refugee causes** — Sugihara's compassion for refugees is needed today as much as ever; consider supporting organizations that help refugees and asylum seekers
4. **Speak up against injustice** — In your own sphere of influence, don't be a bystander when you witness injustice
5. **Teach moral courage** — Use Sugihara's story as a teaching tool for children about the importance of doing what's right even when it's difficult

- Are there situations in your life where you've had to choose between following rules and doing what you felt was right?
- What does true courage look like in everyday life?
- How can we cultivate the moral clarity to recognize when we must act?

## Connection to Jewish Values

### Pikuach Nefesh: The Sanctity of Life

Jewish law holds that saving a human life overrides almost every other commandment. This principle, known as *Pikuach Nefesh*, would have required any Jew to violate Shabbat, eat non-kosher food, or break other laws if necessary to save a life. Sugihara, though not Jewish, embodied this principle perfectly. He understood that human life transcends bureaucracy, politics, and even career.

### Kiddush Hashem: Sanctifying God's Name

In Jewish thought, *Kiddush Hashem* (sanctification of God's name) occurs when someone acts in a way that brings honor to God and reflects divine values. Sugihara's selfless actions sanctified the divine image in humanity. His choice demonstrated that even a non-Jew could fulfill the highest Jewish values — reminding us that righteousness is not limited by religion or ethnicity, but is a universal human capacity.

### Righteous Among the Nations

The title "Righteous Among the Nations" is based on the Jewish concept that righteous people exist among all nations, and that non-Jews who uphold moral law have a share in the World to Come. This recognition reflects Judaism's universal moral vision: that goodness is not exclusive to the Jewish people, and that we must honor and remember those from all backgrounds who risk everything to save human life.

## Conclusion

Chiune Sugihara was an ordinary man who made an extraordinary choice. When the machinery of genocide was in full operation and most of the world looked away, he picked up his pen and wrote visas until his hand cramped. He understood what many powerful leaders failed to grasp: that no order, no rule, no law is more important than the sanctity of human life.

Today, when we see the names engraved at Yad Vashem, when we meet the descendants of the Mir Yeshiva, when we read the testimonies of survivors who walked off trains in Japan clutching Sugihara's documents, we are reminded of a simple truth: **One person can make a difference.** One person with courage, compassion, and a pen can save a world.

As we reflect on Sugihara's legacy, we should ask ourselves: When our moment comes — when we are faced with a choice between convenience and conscience, between following orders and doing what is right — what will we choose? 

May we all have the courage of Chiune Sugihara.
