## Introduction

Imagine discovering that your people—who have lived for over 2,700 years in the hills of northeast India and Myanmar—are actually descendants of one of the Ten Lost Tribes of Israel. For the Bnei Menashe community, this is not a fictional story but their lived reality. These remarkable people have preserved ancient traditions, oral histories, and customs that point back to their Israelite origins—and in recent decades, thousands have made the journey "home" to Israel. Their story is one of the most extraordinary chapters in modern Jewish history, connecting the ancient biblical narrative to communities living today in Asia.

For Chinese readers, the idea of a people preserving their identity across millennia despite geographic isolation may resonate with China's own experiences—such as the Jewish community of Kaifeng (开封犹太人) who maintained distinct practices for centuries, or the many ethnic minorities in China's southwest who preserve unique traditions. The Bnei Menashe story shows that identity can survive against all odds, and that ancient promises can still be fulfilled in the modern world.

## Who Are the Bnei Menashe?

The Bnei Menashe (literally "Children of Menashe" or Manasseh in English) are a community of approximately 10,000 people who primarily belong to the Kuki-Chin-Mizo ethnic groups living in the Indian states of Manipur and Mizoram, as well as in Myanmar. They claim descent from the biblical Tribe of Manasseh, one of the Ten Lost Tribes that were exiled by the Assyrian Empire around 722 BCE—during what corresponds to China's Spring and Autumn Period (春秋时代, 770-476 BCE).

### The Biblical Background

To understand the Bnei Menashe story, we need to know what happened to the Ten Lost Tribes. After King Solomon's death around 930 BCE (during China's Western Zhou Dynasty, 西周, 1046-771 BCE), the united Kingdom of Israel split into two: the northern Kingdom of Israel (containing ten tribes) and the southern Kingdom of Judah (containing the tribes of Judah and Benjamin, plus the Levites).

In 722 BCE, the Assyrian Empire conquered the Northern Kingdom and exiled its inhabitants—scattering them across the Assyrian Empire. The Torah records this in 2 Kings 17:6: "In the ninth year of Hoshea, the king of Assyria captured Samaria and deported the Israelites to Assyria. He settled them in Halah, in Gozan on the Habor River and in the towns of the Medes."

The Tribe of Manasseh was one of these ten tribes. Manasseh was the elder son of Joseph—the biblical figure who rose to become viceroy of Egypt. The whereabouts of these exiled tribes became one of history's greatest mysteries, spawning countless theories and searches over the centuries.

## The Bnei Menashe Oral Tradition

What makes the Bnei Menashe claim remarkable is not just their assertion of Israelite descent, but the specific traditions they preserved for over 2,700 years before encountering any outside Jewish influence in the modern era.

### Ancient Customs and Songs

Before Christian missionaries arrived in their region in the 19th century, the Bnei Menashe maintained distinctive customs that paralleled biblical Israelite practices:

**The Passover-like "Sacrifice":** They observed an annual ritual involving the sacrifice of an animal, placing its blood on doorposts—strikingly similar to the original Passover commanded in Exodus 12. They called this ritual *Pawl Kut*.

**Day of Atonement Ritual:** They practiced a yearly day of fasting and atonement that bore resemblance to Yom Kippur, involving confession of sins and seeking divine forgiveness.

**Dietary Restrictions:** They avoided eating certain animals and had concepts of ritual cleanliness that paralleled kashrut laws, despite having no contact with Jewish communities.

**Oral Traditions of Exile:** Their folk songs and oral histories spoke of a journey from a distant land, crossing many rivers and mountains, leaving behind a place where they had been enslaved. Elders would recount that their ancestors came from "Manmasi" (a name linguistically linked to Manasseh) and that they were "scattered" from their original homeland.

### The Migration Story

According to Bnei Menashe oral tradition, after the Assyrian exile, their ancestors migrated eastward through Persia (modern-day Iran), Afghanistan, and eventually into the Tibet-China border regions—possibly through what is now Qinghai Province (青海省) or Sichuan Province (四川省). From there, they continued moving into Myanmar and northeast India over many centuries, finally settling in the hills of Manipur and Mizoram.

This migration story, preserved through oral transmission across countless generations, contains specific geographic markers and place names that researchers have found to be remarkably consistent across different Bnei Menashe villages that had no contact with each other.

## The Modern Awakening

For most of their history, the Bnei Menashe were not aware that other Jewish communities still existed in the world. When British Christian missionaries arrived in northeast India in the late 1800s and early 1900s, many Bnei Menashe converted to Christianity. However, when they heard the Bible stories, they experienced a profound recognition: these were *their* stories—the traditions their grandparents had told them about.

### Hillel Halkin's Investigation

In the 1990s, Israeli author Hillel Halkin traveled to Manipur and Mizoram to investigate the Bnei Menashe claims. He documented his findings in the book *Across the Sabbath River* (2002). Halkin interviewed elders, recorded oral traditions, and studied their customs. While he approached the subject with healthy skepticism, he concluded that the evidence of pre-contact Israelite traditions was compelling enough to warrant serious consideration.

### DNA and Linguistic Studies

Modern scientific research has produced mixed but intriguing results:

**Genetic Studies:** Some DNA testing has shown genetic markers among the Bnei Menashe that are found in Middle Eastern populations, though the evidence is not conclusive. Genetic admixture over 2,700 years makes definitive proof difficult.

**Linguistic Analysis:** Researchers have found Hebrew-like words and grammatical structures in the Kuki-Chin languages, though this could also result from biblical literacy after Christian contact. More compelling are the pre-Christian oral traditions that align with biblical narratives.

**Cultural Anthropology:** The strongest evidence comes from customs that predate missionary contact—practices that parallel biblical Judaism but differ from both Christianity and the local animist religions of the region.

## Recognition and Return

The journey from being an isolated community in India to recognition as Jews and immigration to Israel involved decades of determination and complex halachic (Jewish legal) deliberations.

### Conversion Process

Under Orthodox Jewish law, to be considered Jewish, one must either be born to a Jewish mother or undergo formal conversion. Since the Bnei Menashe lineage could not be definitively proven through maternal descent (halacha requires maternal Jewish lineage), the Chief Rabbinate of Israel required the Bnei Menashe to undergo formal conversion.

In 2005, the Sephardic Chief Rabbi of Israel, Rabbi Shlomo Amar, recognized the Bnei Menashe as descendants of Israel and approved their conversion and aliyah (immigration to Israel). This was a landmark decision that opened the door for organized immigration.

The conversion process involved:
- **Torah Study:** Intensive study of Jewish law, prayer, holidays, and history
- **Brit Milah (Circumcision):** For males not previously circumcised according to Jewish law
- **Mikveh (Ritual Immersion):** For both men and women
- **Acceptance of Mitzvot:** Formal commitment to observe the 613 commandments
- **Bet Din (Rabbinic Court):** Examination and approval by a panel of three rabbis

This conversion was not a rejection of their identity but a formal reconnection to the Jewish people after millennia of separation.

### Shavei Israel and the Aliyah Process

The organization primarily responsible for facilitating the Bnei Menashe return to Israel is **Shavei Israel** (meaning "Return to Israel"), founded and led by Michael Freund, an American-Israeli activist. Since the early 2000s, Shavei Israel has:

- Established community centers and synagogues in Manipur and Mizoram
- Provided Torah education and preparation for conversion
- Coordinated conversion processes with Israeli rabbinical authorities
- Arranged immigration logistics and absorption in Israel
- Provided financial assistance for travel and initial settlement

### Immigration Numbers

Since 2006, approximately **5,000 Bnei Menashe have made aliyah to Israel**. They have settled primarily in:

- **Northern Israel:** Towns in the Galilee and Golan Heights
- **Judea and Samaria:** Some communities in the West Bank settlements
- **Development Towns:** Places like Kiryat Shemona, Tiberias, and Carmiel

The Israeli government initially halted Bnei Menashe aliyah in 2007 due to political sensitivities (some were settling in disputed territories), but immigration resumed in 2012 and has continued steadily.

As of 2024, approximately **4,000-5,000 Bnei Menashe remain in India**, many of whom are in various stages of preparation, conversion, and waiting for government approval to make aliyah. The COVID-19 pandemic temporarily halted the immigration process but it has since resumed.

## Life in Israel

The absorption of the Bnei Menashe into Israeli society has been both inspiring and challenging.

### Successes

**Strong Work Ethic:** The Bnei Menashe have earned a reputation in Israel for being hardworking, law-abiding, and dedicated to building their new lives. Many work in agriculture, construction, and service industries.

**Military Service:** Young Bnei Menashe serve in the Israel Defense Forces (IDF) with pride, contributing to Israel's security alongside all other Israeli citizens.

**Community Cohesion:** They have maintained strong community bonds, establishing Bnei Menashe cultural centers and synagogues that blend their unique heritage with mainstream Jewish practice.

**Religious Commitment:** Many Bnei Menashe have become strictly observant Orthodox Jews, maintaining kosher homes, observing Shabbat, and sending their children to religious schools.

### Challenges

**Language Barrier:** Learning Hebrew has been difficult for the older generation, though younger people typically become fluent.

**Economic Integration:** Many Bnei Menashe arrived with limited formal education and have worked in blue-collar jobs. Economic mobility remains a challenge.

**Cultural Adjustment:** Adapting from small village life in rural India to modern Israeli urban or semi-urban environments required significant adjustment.

**Prejudice:** Some Bnei Menashe have faced discrimination or skepticism from Israeli Jews who question their authenticity or view them as "foreign." This has been painful for a community that sees themselves as finally returning home after millennia.

**Marriage and Acceptance:** Some mainstream Orthodox rabbis have been reluctant to officiate marriages with Bnei Menashe converts, though this has improved over time as the community has become more established.

## Cultural Parallels: The Jewish-Chinese Experience

The Bnei Menashe story offers fascinating parallels to Chinese experiences with identity, diaspora, and return.

### Diaspora Identity Preservation

Just as the **Kaifeng Jews (开封犹太人)** preserved distinct customs for centuries despite isolation from other Jewish communities, the Bnei Menashe maintained traditions across millennia. The Kaifeng community eventually assimilated, but they demonstrate that Jewish identity can survive in Chinese cultural contexts.

The concept of preserving cultural identity across vast distances and time periods is something Chinese people understand deeply. The overseas Chinese communities (华侨) throughout Southeast Asia, the Americas, and beyond have maintained Chinese cultural practices, language, and identity across generations—sometimes for centuries.

### The Power of Oral Tradition

In Chinese culture, the transmission of wisdom and history through oral tradition has deep roots—from Confucian teachings passed from master to student, to the epic oral traditions of minority peoples like the Yi (彝族) or Miao (苗族) in southwest China, who preserved their histories through song and story before developing written scripts.

The Bnei Menashe's preservation of their origin story through oral tradition across 2,700 years demonstrates the power of collective memory when a community is committed to remembering.

### The Journey Home

The Bnei Menashe concept of returning to an ancestral homeland after millennia resonates with Chinese concepts of 寻根 (xúngēn, "seeking roots"). Many overseas Chinese have made journeys back to ancestral villages in Guangdong, Fujian, or other provinces to reconnect with their origins.

The emotional power of "return" is universal, but it carries special weight for peoples with strong historical consciousness—including both Jews and Chinese.

## Controversies and Skepticism

Not everyone accepts the Bnei Menashe claims or their recognition as Jews.

### Academic Skepticism

Some scholars argue that:
- The similarities to biblical practices could result from Christian missionary influence and biblical literacy
- Oral traditions are unreliable over such vast timespans
- Genetic evidence is inconclusive
- Many peoples have claimed Lost Tribe descent without convincing evidence

### Political Dimensions

Critics have raised concerns that:
- The Israeli government supports Bnei Menashe immigration to bolster Jewish population in contested areas (West Bank settlements)
- The conversions are motivated by economic opportunity rather than genuine religious conviction
- The process sets a problematic precedent for ethnic-based immigration claims

### Halachic Debates

Within the Orthodox rabbinical world, debates continue about:
- Whether formal conversion was necessary (if they are truly descended from Israel)
- The validity of mass conversions in India versus individual conversions
- Whether maternal Jewish descent can be established through oral tradition alone

These debates reflect broader tensions within Judaism about who is a Jew, the role of conversion, and the relationship between ethnicity and religion.

## The Larger Question: The Ten Lost Tribes

The Bnei Menashe are one of several groups around the world claiming descent from the Lost Tribes:

- **Beta Israel (Ethiopia):** Ethiopian Jews, now mostly in Israel, recognized as descendants of the Tribe of Dan
- **Lemba (Zimbabwe/South Africa):** Claim descent from Jewish traders; DNA shows Cohen genetic markers
- **Pashtuns (Afghanistan/Pakistan):** Some Pashtun tribes have oral traditions of Israelite descent
- **Bene Ephraim (India):** Another South Indian group claiming descent from the Tribe of Ephraim
- **Igbo Jews (Nigeria):** West African community with Israelite traditions

The phenomenon of Lost Tribe claimants raises profound questions:
- How do we balance respect for oral tradition with demands for historical proof?
- What constitutes Jewish identity—genetics, practice, belief, or acceptance by the community?
- Can ancient promises of ingathering the exiles be fulfilled literally in the modern world?

## What We Can Learn from the Bnei Menashe

Regardless of one's position on the historicity of their claims, the Bnei Menashe story offers valuable lessons:

### The Power of Identity

The Bnei Menashe maintained a distinct identity for 2,700 years in the face of geographic isolation, cultural pressure, and the absence of any larger Jewish community. This demonstrates that identity is not merely about external reinforcement but about internal commitment to memory and meaning.

### The Reach of Ancient Promises

For believers, the return of the Bnei Menashe represents the beginning of the fulfillment of biblical prophecies about the ingathering of exiles. The prophet Isaiah (11:12) promised: "He will raise a banner for the nations and gather the exiles of Israel; he will assemble the scattered people of Judah from the four quarters of the earth."

That a people from the hills of northeast India and Myanmar would return to Israel after 27 centuries is seen by many as nothing short of miraculous—a sign that ancient promises remain alive.

### The Challenges of Return

The Bnei Menashe experience also reveals the difficulties of return. Coming "home" to a place you've never been, to a culture that has developed for 2,700 years without you, is profoundly challenging. The romantic idea of return meets the harsh realities of language barriers, economic struggle, and social integration.

Yet the Bnei Menashe persist, building lives, serving in the army, raising children as Israelis—demonstrating remarkable resilience and commitment.

### The Living Nature of Jewish Peoplehood

The Bnei Menashe story shows that Jewish peoplehood is not static but dynamic. It can expand to include those who were separated and can embrace diversity—from European Ashkenazim to Middle Eastern Sephardim to Ethiopian Beta Israel to Indian Bnei Menashe. This diversity is a source of strength and demonstrates that Jewishness is ultimately about covenant and commitment, not race or geography.

## Practical Takeaway: What This Means for You

If you are learning about Judaism as a Chinese reader, the Bnei Menashe story offers an important message: Jewish identity can transcend geography, culture, and time. Judaism is not limited to people of a particular ethnic background or from a specific region. It is a covenantal community that can include anyone willing to commit to its values, practices, and peoplehood.

The Bnei Menashe underwent formal conversion not because their story was rejected but because halachic standards require it. This same path is open to anyone, anywhere—including in China—who feels called to join the Jewish people. Conversion is not about erasing your cultural background but about adding a new layer of identity, joining an ancient story that continues to unfold.

The journey of the Bnei Menashe also reminds us that "home" is not always a place we've been but sometimes a place we're meant to be. Whether that's literal (making aliyah to Israel) or spiritual (connecting to Jewish tradition wherever you live), the concept of return—*teshuvah* in Hebrew—is at the heart of Jewish life.

## Key Takeaways

- **The Bnei Menashe are a community from northeast India and Myanmar who claim descent from the biblical Tribe of Manasseh, one of the Ten Lost Tribes exiled by Assyria in 722 BCE**

- **They preserved distinctive Israelite-like customs—including Passover-type rituals, atonement practices, and oral traditions of exile—for over 2,700 years before modern contact with Jewish communities**

- **Since 2005, the Israeli Chief Rabbinate has recognized them, requiring formal conversion to ensure halachic status, and approximately 5,000 have made aliyah (immigrated) to Israel**

- **Their integration into Israeli society has been both successful (strong work ethic, military service, religious commitment) and challenging (language barriers, economic struggles, social acceptance)**

- **The Bnei Menashe story demonstrates the power of oral tradition, the possibility of preserving identity across millennia, and the living, dynamic nature of Jewish peoplehood that can embrace those who return after centuries of separation**
