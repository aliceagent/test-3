## Introduction

When you walk past the historic buildings along Shanghai's Bund today, or explore the old colonial districts of Bombay (now Mumbai), you're treading paths once walked by one of the most remarkable Jewish communities in history—the Baghdadi Jews. These Sephardi merchants from Iraq created a commercial and cultural network that stretched from the Persian Gulf to the South China Sea, leaving synagogues, schools, and philanthropic institutions across Asia that stand to this day.

Their story is one of entrepreneurship, cultural adaptation, and deep Jewish commitment. Families like the Sassoons, Kadoories, and Hardoons didn't just succeed in business—they became pillars of Jewish life across the continent, building communities where none had existed before. For anyone interested in how Jewish tradition can thrive in the most unexpected places, the Baghdadi Jews offer an inspiring case study.

## Origins: The Cradle of Jewish Commerce

### Ancient Roots in Babylonia

The story begins not in the 19th century, but nearly 2,600 years earlier—during China's Spring and Autumn Period (春秋时代, 770-476 BCE)—when the Babylonian Empire conquered the Kingdom of Judah in 586 BCE. The Jewish community that developed in Babylonia (modern-day Iraq) became one of the most important centers of Jewish learning in history. The Babylonian Talmud, compiled between 200-500 CE (corresponding to China's Three Kingdoms through Jin Dynasty period), emerged from the great academies in Sura and Pumbedita near Baghdad.

For over two millennia, Iraqi Jews thrived as merchants, scholars, and craftsmen under various empires—Persian, Greek, Arab, and Ottoman. By the early 19th century, Baghdad's Jewish community numbered around 50,000, roughly one-third of the city's population. They spoke Judeo-Arabic, maintained rich traditions of Torah study, and had developed extensive trading networks throughout the Middle East and beyond.

### Why They Left: Push and Pull Factors

The great migration eastward began in the early 1800s, driven by both challenges and opportunities:

**Economic instability**: The Ottoman Empire's decline created uncertainty for Jewish merchants in Iraq. Political upheavals, changing tax regimes, and the rise of local warlords made business increasingly difficult.

**Trading opportunities**: British colonial expansion opened up new markets across Asia. Cities like Bombay, Calcutta, Singapore, and later Shanghai became booming commercial hubs where enterprising merchants could thrive.

**Religious freedom**: British-controlled territories generally offered greater religious tolerance than the Ottoman-controlled Middle East, where Jews faced increasing restrictions and occasional persecution.

The Baghdadi Jewish migration wasn't a desperate flight like the Spanish Expulsion of 1492 or the exodus from Nazi Europe. Rather, it was a calculated business expansion by families seeking opportunity while maintaining their Jewish identity and traditions.

## The Sassoon Dynasty: "The Rothschilds of the East"

### David Sassoon: The Patriarch

The archetypal story is that of David Sassoon (1792-1864), born in Baghdad into a prominent family of Nasi (Jewish community leaders). His father, Sheikh Sassoon ben Saleh, served as the chief treasurer to the pashas of Baghdad and the Nasi of the Jewish community. When political turmoil made life dangerous, David fled to Persia in 1828, then eventually settled in Bombay (now Mumbai) in 1832.

What David Sassoon did next would create a commercial empire rivaling the great trading houses of Europe. He established David Sassoon & Co., which would grow to dominate the trade between India, China, and Britain in cotton, opium, tea, and textiles. But David never forgot his Jewish roots—every business document bore Hebrew headings, the company observed Shabbat, and he required all employees to know Hebrew.

### Building Communities, Not Just Businesses

David Sassoon didn't just make money; he built Jewish infrastructure across Asia:

- **Bombay**: He constructed the magnificent Magen David Synagogue (1861), still one of India's most beautiful synagogues, with a capacity for over 3,000 worshippers.
- **Pune**: He established the Ohel David Synagogue (1863), which remains active today.
- **Jewish schools and hospitals**: He funded educational institutions to ensure that even poor Jewish children received Torah education alongside secular studies.

When David Sassoon died in 1864—corresponding to the late Qing Dynasty period in China (清朝, 1644-1912)—he left behind not just a fortune, but a thriving network of Jewish communities that had barely existed when he arrived.

### The Second Generation: Expanding to China

David's sons expanded the empire further east:

**Elias David Sassoon** (1820-1880) moved the company headquarters to London while maintaining operations throughout Asia.

**Abdullah (later Albert) Sassoon** (1818-1896) took control of the Bombay branch and expanded into Shanghai, opening a branch in 1845. He financed the construction of the Beth El Synagogue (1887) in Hong Kong and supported Jewish communities throughout China.

The Sassoon name became synonymous with quality and reliability across Asia. In Shanghai, "Sassoon Dock," "Sassoon House," and "Sassoon Road" commemorated their influence—though the family wasn't always resident there, they were key investors and philanthropists.

## The Kadoorie Family: From Baghdad to Bombay to Hong Kong

### Elly Kadoorie: The Humble Beginning

While the Sassoons are better known, the Kadoorie family's story is equally remarkable. Elly Silas Kadoorie (1865-1922) was born in Baghdad, moved to Bombay as a young man, and worked his way up in the Sassoon firm. In 1880, at age 15, he was sent to manage Sassoon operations in Hong Kong.

Rather than remain a company man, Elly struck out on his own, founding Elly Kadoorie & Sons in 1889. He partnered with his brother Ellis Kadoorie, and together they built an investment empire focused on real estate, hotels, and public utilities.

### Building Jewish Life in Hong Kong and Shanghai

The Kadoories became the primary benefactors of Jewish communities throughout East Asia:

**Hong Kong**: The Ohel Leah Synagogue (1902), named after Elly's wife Leah, remains the center of Hong Kong's Jewish community to this day. Built in Sephardi Orthodox style, it features beautiful stained glass, intricate woodwork, and a women's gallery upstairs—all preserved meticulously.

**Shanghai**: The family supported the Shanghai Jewish community extensively, providing funds for schools, relief organizations, and the famous Mir Yeshiva (a Lithuanian rabbinical academy) that fled to Shanghai during World War II.

**Education and Charity**: They established the Kadoorie Agricultural High School in Israel and numerous schools in China and India, reflecting the Jewish value of chinuch (education) and tzedakah (charity).

### Modern Legacy

Today, the Kadoorie family's descendants remain active in Hong Kong, with Sir Michael Kadoorie and his family controlling Hong Kong and Shanghai Hotels (including the iconic Peninsula Hotels chain) and CLP Holdings, one of Asia's largest power companies. The family maintains strong ties to the Jewish community and continues philanthropic work across the region.

## Silas Aaron Hardoon: The Enigmatic Real Estate Mogul

### The Mystery Man

Perhaps the most fascinating—and controversial—figure among the Baghdadi Jews was Silas Aaron Hardoon (1851-1931). Born in Baghdad, Hardoon arrived in Shanghai as a young man in 1874 and started as a rent collector for Sassoon's company. Through shrewd investments, he became Shanghai's wealthiest resident by the 1920s, owning massive tracts of land in what is now downtown Shanghai.

Hardoon's life was unconventional by any standard. He married Luo Jialing (羅迦陵), a Chinese-French Eurasian woman who practiced Buddhism. The couple adopted numerous Chinese orphans (sources vary from 11 to over 20 children), creating a unique multicultural household. Hardoon remained Jewish, observing Shabbat and supporting Jewish causes, while simultaneously sponsoring Buddhist temples and Confucian schools.

### The Aili Garden (爱俪园)

Hardoon's greatest creation was the Aili Garden (爱俪园, Garden of Loving Couples), a massive private estate in Shanghai combining Chinese, Japanese, and European landscape design. He used the grounds to host interfaith gatherings, Buddhist ceremonies, and Jewish celebrations—a remarkable example of religious pluralism in early 20th-century China.

After Hardoon's death in 1931, his widow Luo Jialing controlled the estate, but family disputes over inheritance became legendary. The Communist revolution of 1949 saw the property nationalized. Today, portions of the estate's former grounds house Shanghai Exhibition Center.

### Theological Controversy

Hardoon's syncretism troubled some in the Jewish community. Did supporting Buddhism while remaining Jewish constitute idol worship? The Shanghai Jewish community debated this question without reaching consensus. Hardoon maintained he never abandoned Jewish practice—he observed Shabbat, gave generously to Jewish causes, and was buried with Jewish rites.

His story raises profound questions about Jewish identity in diaspora: How much cultural adaptation is permissible? Can a Jew honor ancestral Chinese traditions without compromising monotheism? These questions remain relevant for Jews living in Asia today.

## The Synagogues They Built: Architectural Gems

### The Ohel Rachel Synagogue (上海拉结会堂), Shanghai

Completed in 1920 and named after Rachel Sassoon (wife of Jacob Elias Sassoon), this remains Shanghai's most prominent synagogue building. Built in Sephardi style with neo-classical elements, it served the Baghdadi Jewish community through the turbulent decades of war and revolution.

During World War II, when Shanghai became a refuge for European Jews fleeing the Holocaust (the only major city requiring no visa), the Ohel Rachel Synagogue became a lifeline. Though the Baghdadi and European Jewish communities remained somewhat separate due to cultural and linguistic differences, the synagogue provided crucial support.

After 1949, the Communist government closed the synagogue, converting it for use by various government offices. It's now a protected historical building under Shanghai Municipal Government control, with limited access for Jewish visitors and occasional services on major holidays.

### The Magen David Synagogue, Bombay (Mumbai)

Built by David Sassoon in 1861, this stunning synagogue in the Byculla neighborhood features:

- **Star of David designs** throughout the architecture
- **Stained glass windows** depicting Jewish themes
- **A women's gallery** upstairs, following Sephardi Orthodox tradition
- **Hebrew inscriptions** praising the Sassoon family's philanthropy

The synagogue remains active today, serving Mumbai's small but vibrant Jewish community. The building underwent restoration in 2009, preserving its 19th-century grandeur.

### The Ohel Leah Synagogue, Hong Kong

Completed in 1902, funded by the Kadoorie family, this synagogue is arguably the most beautiful Jewish building in East Asia:

- **Victorian architecture** with Jewish motifs
- **Gorgeous ark** (aron kodesh) containing Torah scrolls rescued from European communities destroyed in the Holocaust
- **Active congregation** serving Hong Kong's Jewish community of several thousand

The synagogue sits on a hill in Hong Kong's Mid-Levels district, and its distinctive architecture makes it a recognizable landmark. Friday night and Shabbat morning services draw locals, expatriates, and tourists from around the world.

## Daily Life: Maintaining Tradition in Exotic Lands

### Kashrut in Asia

How did these families keep kosher in cities where Jewish infrastructure didn't exist?

**Home slaughter**: Wealthier families employed their own shochtim (ritual slaughterers) or brought them from the Middle East.

**Imported foods**: Basic staples like matzah, wine, and certain preserved goods were imported from Europe or Bombay.

**Local adaptations**: Indian vegetables, rice, and certain local fish species were deemed acceptable by their rabbinic authorities.

### Shabbat Observance

The Sassoon firms famously closed on Shabbat, an unusual practice in the hyper-competitive trading world. David Sassoon instituted this policy from the start, willing to lose business rather than violate Jewish law. His descendants maintained this tradition for decades, proving that commercial success and religious observance weren't mutually exclusive.

In Shanghai, the Baghdadi Jewish community held Friday night services at home or in rented halls before the Ohel Rachel Synagogue was built. Children learned Hebrew, and families celebrated all Jewish holidays with traditional foods—sometimes incorporating local ingredients in creative ways.

### Language and Identity

Most Baghdadi Jews spoke:
- **Judeo-Arabic** at home
- **English** for business (crucial for dealings with British colonial authorities)
- **Some local languages** (Hindi/Urdu in India; Shanghainese or Pidgin in China) for daily interactions
- **Hebrew** for prayer and religious study

This multilingualism allowed them to navigate multiple cultural worlds while maintaining a distinct Jewish identity.

## The European Refugee Connection: World War II

### Shanghai: The Last Open Door

When the Nazis began persecuting Jews in the 1930s, most countries closed their borders. Shanghai, however, was an "open city" requiring no entry visa, controlled by various foreign powers with no central immigration authority. From 1938-1941, approximately 18,000-20,000 European Jews (mainly from Germany and Austria) fled to Shanghai—a story depicted in the PBS documentary "Shanghai Ghetto."

The Baghdadi Jewish community, though culturally very different from the Ashkenazi refugees, provided crucial support:

**Housing and jobs**: Wealthier Baghdadi families hired European Jews as tutors, secretaries, and managers.

**Relief organizations**: The Shanghai Ashkenazi Collaborating Relief Association (SACRA), founded by Horace Kadoorie and other Baghdadi leaders, distributed funds, food, and medicine.

**Synagogue access**: While the communities worshipped separately due to liturgical differences (Sephardi vs. Ashkenazi traditions), the Baghdadi synagogues provided space when needed.

### Cultural Tensions

It wasn't all harmonious. The Baghdadi Jews had lived in Asia for generations, were British subjects, spoke English, and saw themselves as colonial elites. The European refugees were often poor, desperate, and culturally foreign (speaking Yiddish and German rather than English).

Some refugees felt the Baghdadi Jews didn't do enough; some Baghdadis viewed the refugees as a burden. Yet despite tensions, the Baghdadi community's aid likely saved lives, and many refugees later recalled individual Baghdadi families' generosity with deep gratitude.

## The End of an Era: 1949 and Beyond

### The Communist Revolution

The establishment of the People's Republic of China in 1949 marked the end of the Baghdadi Jewish presence in Shanghai. The new government:

- **Nationalized businesses and real estate**, ending the economic basis of Jewish wealth
- **Restricted religious practice**, closing synagogues and converting them to other uses
- **Encouraged (sometimes forced) emigration** of foreigners

Most Shanghai Jews—Baghdadi and European alike—left between 1949-1952. Some went to Hong Kong (which remained British), others to Israel, Australia, the United States, or Britain.

### Similar Patterns Across Asia

In India, a different but parallel process unfolded. Indian independence in 1947 led to:

- **Economic nationalism** making it harder for foreign business families to operate
- **Emigration to Israel** (established in 1948) as many Baghdadi Jews felt pulled toward the new Jewish state
- **Emigration to Britain and the US** as opportunities abroad seemed greater than in post-colonial India

By the 1970s, the once-thriving Baghdadi Jewish communities of Bombay, Calcutta, and other Indian cities had shrunk dramatically.

### Hong Kong: The Survivor

Hong Kong's Jewish community—largely established by the Kadoories—survived and even thrived:

- **British colonial stability** lasted until 1997
- **Economic opportunities** in one of Asia's financial hubs
- **Strong institutions** (Ohel Leah Synagogue, Jewish Recreation Club, Carmel School)

Today, Hong Kong hosts a vibrant Jewish community of several thousand, with Chabad, Modern Orthodox, and Progressive congregations, kosher restaurants, and an active social scene. The Kadoorie family remains prominent, embodying continuity from the Baghdadi era to the present.

## Legacy: What They Left Behind

### Physical Heritage

**Synagogue buildings** in Shanghai, Mumbai, Hong Kong, Kolkata, and Pune—most protected as historical landmarks

**Street names**: Sassoon Road, Kadoorie Avenue, and other reminders of their civic contributions

**Hotels and institutions**: The Peninsula Hotels (Kadoorie family), Sassoon-founded schools and hospitals

### Cultural Contributions

The Baghdadi Jews demonstrated that Jewish tradition could flourish in Asian contexts. They:

- **Maintained halacha** (Jewish law) in challenging circumstances
- **Supported Torah education** by funding schools and importing teachers
- **Built institutions** serving both Jewish and general communities (hospitals, schools, charities open to all)
- **Showed religious tolerance** by respecting Asian cultures while remaining committed Jews

### Inspiring Diaspora Judaism

For modern Jews—especially those living in Asia today—the Baghdadi story offers practical lessons:

**You can succeed while staying observant**: The Sassoons proved that Shabbat observance and business success aren't contradictory.

**Build community infrastructure**: Wherever they went, Baghdadi Jews built synagogues, schools, and support networks before they were personally needed—investing in the next generation.

**Be generous to fellow Jews**: Despite cultural differences, they aided refugees and supported Jews worldwide.

**Engage respectfully with host cultures**: They learned local languages, employed local people fairly, and contributed to civic life without compromising their Jewish identity.

## Visiting Their Legacy Today

### Shanghai

- **Ohel Rachel Synagogue** (No. 500 North Shaanxi Road): Now a protected building, occasionally open for tours or special events. Contact the Shanghai Jewish Refugee Museum for access.
- **Hongkou Jewish Refugee Museum** (长阳路62号): Tells the story of the European Jewish refugees with exhibits on the Baghdadi community's role.

### Hong Kong

- **Ohel Leah Synagogue** (70 Robinson Road): Active congregation, visitors welcome for Shabbat services. Contact in advance.
- **Jewish Cemetery** (Happy Valley): Many Kadoorie family members and other prominent Baghdadi Jews are buried here.

### Mumbai (Bombay)

- **Magen David Synagogue** (Byculla): Active congregation, one of six remaining synagogues in Mumbai.
- **Keneseth Eliyahoo Synagogue** (Fort area): Built by the Sassoon family in 1884, beautifully maintained.
- **Sassoon Docks**: Historic fishing port named after the family (though not directly connected to Jewish history).

### Kolkata (Calcutta)

- **Magen David Synagogue** (built 1884): One of two remaining active synagogues in Kolkata.
- **Jewish Girls' School** (now Aliyah Museum): Preserves the history of Calcutta's Jewish community.

## Conclusion: Merchants, Builders, Jews

The Baghdadi Jews weren't just successful businessmen—they were builders of communities, bridges between civilizations, and living examples of diaspora Judaism at its finest. From the souks of Baghdad to the bunds of Shanghai, they carried with them not just commercial acumen but Torah scrolls, Shabbat candles, and a deep commitment to Jewish continuity.

Their legacy lives on in the synagogues that still stand, the institutions that bear their names, and the example they set for Jews navigating life in foreign lands. In an era when Jewish communities face challenges of assimilation and identity preservation, the Baghdadi story reminds us: it's possible to thrive economically, engage respectfully with other cultures, and remain profoundly, unapologetically Jewish.

As the Talmud teaches: "Wherever Jews go, they take their Judaism with them" (adapted from Megillah 29a). The Sassoons, Kadoories, and Hardoons proved this truth across the breadth of Asia, leaving behind not just wealth and buildings, but inspiration for generations to come.

## Key Takeaways

- **The Baghdadi Jews** were Sephardi merchants from Iraq who built commercial empires and Jewish communities across Asia from the 1830s-1940s
- **Key families** like the Sassoons, Kadoories, and Hardoons combined business success with deep commitment to Jewish practice and philanthropy
- **They built synagogues**, schools, and support networks in cities like Mumbai, Shanghai, and Hong Kong—many still standing today
- **During WWII**, Baghdadi Jews aided European Jewish refugees fleeing to Shanghai, despite cultural differences
- **Their legacy** demonstrates that Jewish tradition can thrive anywhere with commitment, creativity, and communal investment
