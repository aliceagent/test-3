## Introduction

If you're exploring Judaism from China or another part of Asia, you've likely encountered Christianity first—through missionaries, churches in your city, or simply its larger global presence. You may wonder: aren't Judaism and Christianity basically the same religion, with Jews simply not accepting Jesus as the messiah? The reality is both more complex and more interesting. While Christianity emerged from Judaism nearly two thousand years ago—during China's Han Dynasty (汉朝, 202 BCE-220 CE)—the two religions developed profoundly different understandings of God, humanity, salvation, and religious law. This article explores those key differences with respect and clarity, helping you understand what makes Judaism distinctive.

## The Nature of God: Unity vs. Trinity

### Judaism: Radical Monotheism

The foundational statement of Jewish belief is the *Shema* (שְׁמַע): "Hear, O Israel, the Lord is our God, the Lord is One" (Deuteronomy 6:4). This isn't merely a statement that there is only one God rather than many gods—it's a declaration of God's absolute, indivisible unity. God has no parts, no aspects, no divisions. God is not a composite being. This concept is called *yichud Hashem* (the unity of God) and is non-negotiable in Jewish theology.

The Rambam (Maimonides, 1138-1204 CE—during China's Song Dynasty 宋朝) codified this as the second of his Thirteen Principles of Faith: "I believe with perfect faith that the Creator, blessed be His name, is One. There is no unity like His in any way. He alone is our God—He was, He is, and He will be."

### Christianity: The Trinity

Christianity teaches that God exists as three distinct persons—Father, Son, and Holy Spirit—who are simultaneously one God. This doctrine of the Trinity holds that Jesus is both fully God and fully human, that he is the second person of the divine Trinity. From a Jewish perspective, this represents a fundamental departure from monotheism, regardless of how Christians frame it philosophically.

For a Jew, the idea that God could become human, or that a human could be God, contradicts the Torah's repeated insistence on God's transcendence and absolute difference from creation. "God is not a man" (Numbers 23:19) is taken literally. The worship of any being other than the One God—even if understood as an aspect or incarnation of God—is considered *avodah zarah* (foreign worship, idolatry).

## The Messiah: Different Expectations

### Judaism: A Future Human Leader

Judaism teaches that the *mashiach* (messiah, literally "anointed one") will be a fully human descendant of King David who will accomplish specific concrete tasks during his lifetime:

1. **Rebuild the Temple** in Jerusalem (Third Temple)
2. **Gather all Jews** from exile back to the Land of Israel
3. **Bring world peace**—an end to war and suffering
4. **Spread knowledge of God** throughout the world

These are observable, physical events. The Jewish messiah is not a divine being, not the son of God, and will not require faith to recognize—his identity will be confirmed by his accomplishments. If someone claiming to be the messiah dies without achieving these goals, Judaism considers that claim false. No amount of theological reinterpretation can change unfulfilled prophecy.

### Christianity: Jesus as Divine Savior

Christianity teaches that Jesus of Nazareth was (and is) the messiah, that he fulfilled messianic prophecy through his death and resurrection, and that his second coming will complete the messianic mission. Rather than political and military redemption, Christianity reinterprets messianic prophecy as spiritual redemption—salvation from sin through faith in Jesus.

From a Jewish perspective, the world Jesus left behind showed no signs of messianic fulfillment: the Temple remained destroyed, Jews remained scattered in exile, wars continued, and universal knowledge of God did not arrive. The promise of a "second coming" is, to Jews, an admission that the first coming did not accomplish what the prophets said the messiah would do. Judaism does not recognize the concept of a messiah who comes twice, dies, and returns.

## Original Sin and the Nature of Humanity

### Judaism: No Inherited Sin

Judaism does not believe in original sin. The story of Adam and Eve in the Garden of Eden is understood as the origin of human mortality and the struggle between good and evil inclinations, but *not* as the transmission of guilt to all subsequent generations. Each person is born pure, with both a *yetzer tov* (good inclination) and a *yetzer hara* (evil inclination). Sin is the result of individual choice, and each person is responsible only for their own actions.

The prophet Ezekiel explicitly states: "The soul that sins, it shall die. A son shall not bear the iniquity of the father, and a father shall not bear the iniquity of the son" (Ezekiel 18:20). This principle of individual moral responsibility runs throughout Jewish thought. You are not born guilty; you are born with potential for both good and evil, and your life's work is to choose good.

This has significant implications: Judaism has no concept of needing a savior to redeem humanity from inherited sin. *Teshuvah* (repentance)—direct return to God through acknowledgment of wrongdoing, remorse, and change—is always available. No intermediary is required.

### Christianity: Inherited Guilt and Redemption

Christianity teaches that all humans inherit Adam's sin and are thus born in a state of separation from God. This "fallen" condition cannot be remedied through human effort alone; salvation requires divine grace through faith in Jesus's atoning death. The crucifixion is understood as a cosmic sacrifice that reconciles humanity to God.

This fundamental difference shapes everything else: if you don't believe humanity is born sinful and spiritually dead, the entire Christian narrative of redemption becomes unnecessary. Judaism looks at the cross and sees a tragedy—the death of a Jewish teacher at Roman hands—not a salvific event.

## The Path to Salvation: Law vs. Faith

### Judaism: Covenant and Commandments

Judaism does not have a concept of "salvation" in the Christian sense—being saved from hell through faith. Instead, Judaism speaks of living righteously, fulfilling the covenant, and earning a place in *Olam HaBa* (the World to Come) through one's actions.

Jews are bound by the 613 commandments (*mitzvot*) of the Torah. These laws are not a burden to be escaped but a gift—a framework for living a holy life. Observing Shabbat, keeping kosher, putting on *tefillin*, giving charity, studying Torah—these actions draw a person closer to God and sanctify the material world.

For non-Jews, Judaism teaches that they are not required to convert or follow all 613 commandments. Instead, they are expected to follow the Seven Noahide Laws (basic moral principles like prohibitions on murder, theft, and idolatry). Anyone—Jew or non-Jew—who lives righteously according to their obligations has a place in the World to Come. Good deeds matter more than correct theology.

### Christianity: Salvation by Faith

Christianity, particularly Protestant Christianity, teaches *sola fide* (faith alone)—that salvation comes through faith in Jesus rather than through observance of religious law. In Christian theology, the Torah's commandments cannot save; they only reveal human inability to be righteous. Grace, received through faith, replaces the law as the means of salvation.

This represents a radical departure from Judaism. Where Christianity says the law is obsolete or fulfilled in Jesus, Judaism says the law is eternal and continues to bind the Jewish people. The Torah itself states: "This commandment which I command you today—it is not hidden from you, and it is not distant... The matter is very near to you—in your mouth and in your heart—to do it" (Deuteronomy 30:11-14). The commandments are neither impossible nor replaced.

## Covenant: Universal vs. Particular

### Judaism: Multiple Covenants

Judaism recognizes different relationships between God and different peoples. All humanity is bound by the Noahide covenant. The Jewish people specifically are bound by the covenant at Sinai, which involves additional responsibilities (*mitzvot*) in exchange for being God's "treasured nation" and "kingdom of priests" (Exodus 19:5-6).

This doesn't mean only Jews are saved or that Judaism is superior—it means Jews have specific obligations other nations don't share. Just as a doctor has different professional duties than a teacher, the Jewish people have a unique covenant relationship with God while other nations have their own path to righteousness.

Conversion to Judaism is possible but not required for non-Jews to have a relationship with God or a place in the World to Come. Judaism has never believed that "outside the synagogue there is no salvation."

### Christianity: Universal Call

Christianity teaches a universal covenant available to all through faith in Jesus, replacing the particular Jewish covenant. The New Testament presents the church as the "new Israel," and many Christian traditions teach that God's covenant with the Jewish people has been superseded or expanded to include all believers in Jesus.

Judaism strongly rejects this theology of *supersessionism* (replacement theology). The covenant at Sinai is eternal—God says so explicitly: "I will remember My covenant with Jacob, and also My covenant with Isaac, and also My covenant with Abraham I will remember, and I will remember the land" (Leviticus 26:42). An eternal covenant cannot be replaced or become obsolete.

## Scripture: What Is Authoritative?

### Judaism: Tanakh and Oral Torah

Jewish scripture consists of the *Tanakh*—Torah (Five Books of Moses), *Nevi'im* (Prophets), and *Ketuvim* (Writings)—what Christians call the "Old Testament," though Jews reject that terminology as it implies these texts are outdated.

Equally authoritative is the *Oral Torah*—the Mishnah, Talmud, and later rabbinic writings—which explain how to observe the written Torah's commandments. These texts record debates, legal rulings, and the continuous interpretation of Torah that adapts it to changing historical circumstances while maintaining its core principles.

The New Testament has no authority in Judaism. The writings about Jesus were composed decades after his death, in Greek, by people who never met him, and reflect a theology that departs fundamentally from Torah Judaism.

### Christianity: Old and New Testaments

Christianity accepts the Jewish scriptures but reads them through the lens of the New Testament, seeing Jesus as the fulfillment of messianic prophecy and the Torah as a shadow of the gospel to come. Many passages Jews read as referring to Israel or King David are reinterpreted by Christians as prophecies about Jesus.

This hermeneutical difference is significant. When Isaiah writes about the "suffering servant," Jews understand this as the people of Israel enduring exile and persecution; Christians read it as a prophecy about Jesus's crucifixion. These are incompatible readings stemming from fundamentally different theological frameworks.

## Religious Practice: Law-Centered vs. Faith-Centered

### Judaism: Orthopraxy

Judaism emphasizes *orthopraxy* (correct practice) as much as or more than *orthodoxy* (correct belief). What you *do* matters enormously. How you observe Shabbat, whether you keep kosher, whether you pray daily, whether you study Torah, whether you give charity—these actions shape your relationship with God and with the Jewish community.

There is room for theological diversity within Judaism as long as practice remains rooted in halacha (Jewish law). A Jew who doubts God's existence but keeps Shabbat is still engaging in Jewish life in a meaningful way. A Jew who believes deeply but ignores all commandments is living outside the covenant.

This is sometimes confusing for people raised in faith-centered religions, but it reflects Judaism's understanding that action sanctifies the world and brings holiness into daily life. You study Torah not merely to learn what God wants but to *do* it.

### Christianity: Orthodoxy

Christianity emphasizes *orthodoxy*—correct belief, particularly about Jesus's divine nature and salvific role. While different Christian denominations have different practices (liturgy, sacraments, church governance), shared belief in core doctrines (Trinity, incarnation, resurrection) defines Christian identity.

Good works are encouraged, but in most Christian theology, they are seen as the *fruit* of salvation, not the *means* of salvation. This creates a fundamental difference in religious emphasis: Christians ask "Do you believe in Jesus?" Jews ask "Do you keep the commandments?"

## Suffering and Atonement: Sacrifice vs. Repentance

### Judaism: Direct Return to God

Judaism teaches that repentance (*teshuvah*) is always available directly to anyone who turns to God with sincere remorse and the commitment to change. No priestly intermediary, no sacrifice, no savior is required. As the prophet Hosea declared: "Return, O Israel, to the Lord your God... Take words with you and return to the Lord. Say to Him: 'Forgive all sin and accept what is good; let our lips replace the offering of bulls'" (Hosea 14:2-3).

Even when the Temple stood and animal sacrifices were performed, these rituals were effective only when accompanied by genuine repentance. The sacrifice itself did not mechanically erase sin—it was the person's inner transformation that mattered. Yom Kippur, the Day of Atonement, is the holiest day of the Jewish year precisely because it offers complete forgiveness to anyone who sincerely repents and makes amends.

### Christianity: Vicarious Atonement

Christianity teaches that Jesus's death served as the ultimate sacrifice, atoning for all human sin. His suffering and death are understood as vicarious atonement—he took on the punishment humanity deserved. Faith in this sacrificial act is what reconciles people to God.

From a Jewish perspective, this concept is foreign to Torah theology. God does not demand human sacrifice—indeed, the Torah explicitly forbids it. The story of Abraham's binding of Isaac ends with God providing a ram instead, teaching that God does *not* want human sacrifice. The idea that God would require the death of an innocent person to forgive others contradicts divine justice: "Fathers shall not be put to death for their children, and children shall not be put to death for their fathers; each person shall be put to death for his own sin" (Deuteronomy 24:16).

## The Role of the Torah: Eternal vs. Fulfilled

### Judaism: The Torah Is Forever

"The Torah that Moses commanded us is the heritage of the congregation of Jacob" (Deuteronomy 33:4). For Jews, the commandments of the Torah are eternally binding. They cannot be replaced, fulfilled, or made obsolete. The Talmud teaches that even if a prophet performed miracles and predicted the future, if that prophet said to violate even one commandment of the Torah, the prophet must be rejected as false—because God's Torah does not change.

This is why Jews continue to observe Shabbat, keep kosher, circumcise their sons, and follow the intricate details of halacha. These are not outdated practices from an old covenant but the ongoing life of the covenant community. The Torah itself states: "You shall not add to the word which I command you, nor shall you take away from it" (Deuteronomy 4:2).

### Christianity: The Torah Is Fulfilled

Christianity teaches that Jesus "fulfilled the law" and that Christians are not bound by Torah commandments—particularly the ritual and dietary laws. While interpretations vary, most Christian traditions hold that faith in Jesus replaces observance of Torah law. The Apostle Paul wrote that Christians are "not under law but under grace" (Romans 6:14).

This is perhaps the deepest divide. From a Jewish perspective, the idea that God would give an eternal covenant, call it everlasting, and then replace it centuries later contradicts God's own nature. How can an eternal law have an expiration date? How can the God who never changes change the terms of the covenant?

## Practical Application: What This Means for Your Journey

If you're exploring both Judaism and Christianity, these differences matter:

1. **Ask yourself what you believe about God**: Is God absolutely one and indivisible, or is God a Trinity? Your answer shapes everything else.

2. **Consider the nature of humanity**: Are people born sinful and in need of saving, or are they born with moral potential and personal responsibility?

3. **Think about religious practice**: Are you drawn to a religion centered on faith and belief, or one centered on law and action? Neither is better—they are different paths.

4. **Research the historical context**: Understanding that Christianity emerged *from* Judaism but diverged in fundamental ways helps clarify why Jews do not accept Jesus as the messiah.

5. **Respect is essential**: You can understand the differences without denigrating either tradition. Many Jews and Christians maintain respectful dialogue, recognizing that they worship the same God of Abraham even while disagreeing profoundly about God's nature and requirements.

For Chinese learners, a useful comparison might be the relationship between Confucianism and Daoism (道家)—both emerged from the same ancient Chinese philosophical tradition, both address questions of how to live rightly, yet they offer profoundly different answers about human nature, social order, and the path to harmony with the Tao. Similarly, Judaism and Christianity share a common root in the Hebrew Bible but have grown into distinct religions with different theological frameworks.

## Key Takeaways

- **God's Unity**: Judaism's absolute monotheism differs fundamentally from Christianity's Trinitarian theology.
- **The Messiah**: Judaism awaits a future human leader who will bring observable world peace; Christianity believes Jesus was the divine messiah whose spiritual salvation is primary.
- **Sin and Redemption**: Judaism rejects original sin and teaches that repentance is always directly available; Christianity teaches inherited sin requiring faith in Jesus's atoning sacrifice.
- **Law vs. Faith**: Judaism emphasizes fulfilling commandments (mitzvot) as the path to righteousness; Christianity emphasizes faith in Jesus as the means of salvation.
- **Eternal Covenant**: Judaism teaches the Torah is forever binding; Christianity teaches it was fulfilled and replaced by the new covenant in Jesus.
