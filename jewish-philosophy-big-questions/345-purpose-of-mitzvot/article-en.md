## Introduction

When most people first learn about Judaism, one of the most startling discoveries is that Jewish law contains 613 commandments. Six hundred and thirteen! Some are familiar: don't murder, honor your parents, give to charity. But many seem arbitrary: don't mix wool and linen in clothing, don't plant two kinds of seeds in the same field, leave the corner of your field unharvested for the poor. For someone encountering this system for the first time—especially from a Chinese cultural background that may emphasize natural harmony and spontaneity—the question arises immediately: *Why so many rules? What's the point?*

This is not a modern question. Jewish thinkers have grappled with the purpose of the mitzvot (commandments) for thousands of years. From the medieval rationalist Maimonides (Rambam), who lived during China's Song Dynasty (宋朝, 960-1279 CE), to the mystical traditions of Kabbalah and Chassidut, to modern philosophers, Jews have asked: Why does God command us to do these things? What are the mitzvot meant to achieve?

This article explores three major approaches to understanding the purpose of mitzvot: the **rational approach** (mitzvot have logical reasons), the **obedience approach** (mitzvot are divine commands we follow regardless of understanding), and the **connection approach** (mitzvot create relationship with God). Each offers profound insights into what it means to live a Jewish life.

## The Rational Approach: Rambam and the Guide for the Perplexed

### Maimonides' Project

Rabbi Moshe ben Maimon (1138-1204 CE), known as Maimonides or the Rambam, was perhaps the greatest Jewish philosopher. Born in Spain during the Islamic Golden Age—a period roughly contemporary with China's Southern Song Dynasty (南宋, 1127-1279 CE)—he spent his life trying to harmonize Jewish law with Aristotelian philosophy. His magnum opus, *The Guide for the Perplexed* (Moreh Nevuchim), addressed Jews who were troubled by apparent contradictions between Torah and rational thought.

Maimonides believed that every mitzvah has a rational purpose. God, being perfectly wise, would not command something arbitrary or pointless. The mitzvot exist to perfect human beings in two ways:

1. **Perfection of the body**: Physical health, social order, and a stable society
2. **Perfection of the soul**: Correct beliefs about God, ethical character, and spiritual elevation

### Categories of Commandments

In the *Guide*, Maimonides categorizes the 613 mitzvot according to their purposes. Some examples:

**Social and Ethical Mitzvot**: These are relatively easy to understand. Don't murder, don't steal, don't bear false witness—these create a functioning society where people can trust one another and pursue higher goods. Honor your parents teaches gratitude and maintains generational continuity. Give charity (tzedakah) redistributes wealth and prevents grinding poverty. Pay workers on time prevents exploitation.

Even here, though, Judaism goes beyond pure pragmatism. The Torah commands us to leave the corners of our fields unharvested (pe'ah) and the gleanings for the poor—not merely as welfare, but as a system that preserves the dignity of the poor by allowing them to work for their sustenance.

**Ritual Mitzvot**: These are harder. Why keep kosher? Why observe Shabbat? Maimonides offers sophisticated explanations:

- **Kosher laws** train us in self-discipline and remind us that eating is not merely physical but spiritual. By distinguishing between permitted and forbidden foods, we sanctify the act of eating.
- **Shabbat** breaks the cycle of work and consumption, reminding us that the world belongs to God, not to us. It creates a rhythm of rest and reflection, preventing us from becoming enslaved to productivity.
- **Sacrifices** (when the Temple stood) channeled the ancient human impulse to worship through physical offerings into a carefully controlled system that prevented idolatry.

**Mysterious Mitzvot (Chukim)**: Some commandments seem to have no rational explanation. Why not mix wool and linen (sha'atnez)? Why send a mother bird away before taking her eggs? Maimonides argues that even these have reasons—sometimes historical (combating ancient idolatrous practices), sometimes pedagogical (teaching attentiveness to detail and obedience even when we don't understand).

### The Limits of Reason

Maimonides acknowledges that we may not grasp the full wisdom behind every mitzvah. Some reasons are beyond human comprehension. But he insists that searching for reasons is itself a mitzvah—it deepens our relationship with Torah and prevents us from viewing Judaism as mere superstition.

Critics of this approach worry that if we perform mitzvot only because they make sense to us, we'll abandon those whose rationale we don't grasp. If the reason for a mitzvah becomes obsolete, should we stop observing it? Maimonides would say no—the mitzvot are still binding because God commanded them—but the question remains.

## The Obedience Approach: The Kuzari and Kabbalat Ol Malchut Shamayim

### The Kuzari's Argument

Rabbi Yehuda Halevi (1075-1141 CE), a Spanish Jewish poet and philosopher contemporary with Maimonides but representing a very different approach, wrote *The Kuzari* during China's Northern Song Dynasty (北宋, 960-1127 CE). The book is structured as a dialogue between the King of the Khazars (a real kingdom that converted to Judaism) and a Jewish sage.

Halevi's central argument: Judaism is not based on philosophy but on **revelation**. The Jewish people stood at Mount Sinai and heard God speak. That collective experience—witnessed by 600,000 adult males plus women and children, an entire nation—is the foundation of Jewish faith. We don't follow the mitzvot because they make philosophical sense. We follow them because God, who revealed Himself to us, commanded them.

### Accepting the Yoke of Heaven

This approach is called *kabbalat ol malchut shamayim*—accepting the yoke of the Kingdom of Heaven. The word "yoke" might sound harsh to modern ears, but in the traditional context, it means acknowledging that we are servants of God, not autonomous individuals who pick and choose which divine commands appeal to us.

Halevi emphasizes that some mitzvot—especially the *chukim*, the inexplicable laws—are precisely the ones that define our relationship with God most profoundly. If we only followed commandments that made sense to us, we'd be following our own wisdom, not God's. It's the commandments we don't understand that require true faith and humility.

### The Shabbat Candle Test

Consider this thought experiment: Why do we light Shabbat candles? A rationalist might say: "To create a warm, peaceful atmosphere for the Shabbat meal. It enhances family bonding and sets a sacred mood."

But suppose you're a single person eating alone, or suppose bright electric lights have already made your home well-lit. Does the mitzvah still apply? According to the obedience approach, absolutely. We light candles because God commanded it—not because we've reasoned out a psychological benefit. The benefit may be real, but it's secondary. The primary reason is: *God said so*.

This approach resonates with certain aspects of Confucian thought, particularly the emphasis on *li* (禮, ritual propriety). Confucius taught that performing rituals correctly—even when their original meanings have been forgotten—maintains social harmony and personal cultivation. Similarly, Judaism values the careful observance of mitzvot regardless of whether we fully grasp their purpose.

### Critiques of the Obedience Approach

Critics worry that this approach can lead to mechanical, rote observance without spiritual engagement. If the only reason to perform a mitzvah is "because God said so," what happens to personal meaning, joy, and passion? Furthermore, in an age that values autonomy and critical thinking, simply appealing to divine authority may feel unsatisfying to many seekers.

Yet defenders of this approach argue that precisely because we live in an age of inflated individualism, we need the humility of obedience. Not everything is about us and our feelings. Sometimes spiritual growth requires submission to a wisdom greater than our own.

## The Connection Approach: Chassidut and Mystical Unity

### The Baal Shem Tov's Revolution

In the 18th century—during China's Qing Dynasty (清朝, 1644-1912 CE)—a new movement arose in Eastern Europe that transformed Jewish spirituality. The Baal Shem Tov (Master of the Good Name), founder of Chassidism, taught that the purpose of mitzvot is to create *devekut*—**connection, cleaving, or adhesion to God**.

According to Chassidic thought, the entire universe is God's creation, sustained moment by moment by divine energy. Every physical object contains a divine spark. When we perform a mitzvah using a physical object—wrapping tefillin made of leather, eating matzah made of grain, lighting Shabbat candles—we elevate that spark, reuniting it with its divine source.

### Mitzvot as Spiritual Technology

The mystics view mitzvot as a kind of **spiritual technology**—actions that accomplish something in the unseen worlds. When you give charity, you're not just helping a poor person; you're drawing down divine blessing into the world. When you observe Shabbat, you're not just resting; you're participating in God's rest, imitating the Creator's rhythm of creation and cessation.

This approach draws heavily on Kabbalah (Jewish mysticism), which teaches that the 613 mitzvot correspond to the 613 channels through which divine energy flows into the world. Some kabbalists even correlate the 613 commandments with the 248 limbs and 365 sinews of the human body (according to traditional anatomy), suggesting that each mitzvah perfects a different aspect of our spiritual physiology.

### The Joy of Connection

Unlike the obedience approach, which can feel austere, the Chassidic approach emphasizes *simcha*—**joy**. If the purpose of mitzvot is to connect with God, then performing them should be joyful. The Baal Shem Tov taught that one mitzvah performed with joy is worth more than many performed with a heavy heart.

This doesn't mean that understanding the reasons for mitzvot is unimportant. Rather, the Chassidic approach integrates all previous approaches: Yes, mitzvot may have rational purposes (Maimonides). Yes, we perform them because God commanded them (Halevi). But the deepest truth is that they are **relational acts**—ways of reaching toward the Divine.

### A Practical Example: Tzitzit

Consider the mitzvah of *tzitzit*, the fringes worn on a four-cornered garment. 

- **Rational approach (Rambam)**: The Torah explicitly states the purpose—"You shall see them and remember all of God's commandments" (Numbers 15:39). The tzitzit are a visual reminder to observe the mitzvot throughout the day.
- **Obedience approach (Kuzari)**: We wear tzitzit because God commanded it in the Torah. Even if we forget to look at them or don't feel particularly inspired, we fulfill the mitzvah by wearing them.
- **Connection approach (Chassidut)**: Each time we look at the tzitzit, we experience a moment of reconnection with God. The knots and windings contain mystical meanings that draw down divine light. Wearing them surrounds us with holiness.

All three perspectives are true. They're not contradictory but complementary—different dimensions of a single reality.

## Which Approach Is Correct?

Judaism has never demanded philosophical uniformity. All three approaches—and many more—coexist within traditional Jewish thought. Different communities, different rabbis, different individuals emphasize different understandings.

**Rationalists** (like Maimonides) appeal to Jews who value intellectual coherence and want their religious practice to make sense. This approach has particular strength in addressing skeptics and in apologetics.

**Traditionalists** (like the Kuzari) appeal to those who value humility, submission, and the authority of received tradition. This approach has particular strength in maintaining observance across generations and preventing erosion.

**Mystics** (like the Chassidim) appeal to those who seek passionate, experiential connection with the Divine. This approach has particular strength in making ritual feel alive and personally meaningful.

Most Jews, whether they realize it or not, draw on all three at different times. When we're intellectually curious, we look for reasons. When we're feeling disconnected, we rely on discipline and obedience. When we're spiritually open, we seek mystical connection.

## Practical Application

How does this play out in daily life?

### When You Don't Feel Like It

Suppose it's Friday evening, you're exhausted from work, and lighting Shabbat candles feels like one more chore. 

- The **rational** voice reminds you: This ritual creates sacred time, helps you transition from workweek to rest, and connects you to Jewish communities worldwide doing the same thing.
- The **obedience** voice says: Your feelings don't matter right now. God commanded this. Light the candles.
- The **connection** voice whispers: This is your weekly moment to invite the Shabbat Queen into your home, to step into sacred time. Just light the candles and see what happens.

All three voices can help you overcome inertia.

### When You Don't Understand

Suppose you're required to follow a mitzvah whose reason escapes you—say, the prohibition against mixing meat and milk.

- The **rational** voice searches: Maybe it's about not cooking a young animal in its mother's milk, which would be cruel. Maybe it's about maintaining separate categories, training us in mindfulness.
- The **obedience** voice accepts: This is part of the covenant. I may not understand it fully, but my ancestors kept this law for thousands of years. That continuity has meaning.
- The **connection** voice intuits: Perhaps this mitzvah operates on a spiritual level I can't fully grasp. By keeping it, I participate in something larger than myself.

### When You're Seeking Meaning

Suppose you've been observing mitzvot for years and want to go deeper.

- The **rational** voice studies: Read Maimonides' *Guide*, explore the wisdom traditions, understand the historical context.
- The **obedience** voice deepens: Focus on precision—are you performing each mitzvah exactly as required? Greater care can awaken new awareness.
- The **connection** voice opens: Add *kavanah* (intention) to your practice. Before performing a mitzvah, pause and dedicate it to God. Notice what shifts.

## The Chinese Philosophical Parallel

Interestingly, Chinese philosophy contains a similar spectrum of approaches to ritual practice (*li*, 禮).

**Confucius** emphasized the importance of performing rituals correctly, even if their original meanings were no longer clear—something like the obedience approach. Ritual propriety maintains social harmony and shapes character.

**Xunzi** (荀子, ~310-235 BCE), the rationalist Confucian, argued that rituals have clear social purposes—they channel human desires, prevent conflict, and create order. This parallels Maimonides' rational approach.

**Daoist thinkers**, by contrast, were often skeptical of elaborate rituals, preferring spontaneous harmony with the Way (Dao, 道). Yet some Daoist practices—like internal alchemy and meditation—bear resemblance to the Kabbalistic emphasis on inner transformation through prescribed practices.

The difference is that in Judaism, all three approaches operate within the framework of covenant—a unique relationship between God and the Jewish people. The mitzvot are not human inventions (like Confucian *li*) or natural patterns (like the Dao), but divine commands rooted in historical revelation.

## Key Takeaways

- **The 613 commandments (mitzvot)** are not arbitrary rules but a comprehensive system for living a holy life in covenant with God.

- **Maimonides (Rambam)** taught that mitzvot have rational purposes—perfecting body and soul, creating a just society, and fostering correct beliefs about God.

- **Yehuda Halevi (Kuzari)** emphasized obedience to divine command as the heart of Jewish practice, arguing that faith is based on revelation, not philosophy.

- **Chassidic mysticism** views mitzvot as spiritual technology for creating connection (*devekut*) with God, elevating sparks of holiness trapped in the physical world.

- All three approaches are valid and complement each other—Judaism has room for rationalist, traditionalist, and mystical understandings.

- In practice, most Jews draw on different approaches at different times, depending on where they are spiritually and what challenges they face.
