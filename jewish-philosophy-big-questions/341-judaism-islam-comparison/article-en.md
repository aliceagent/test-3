## Introduction

When you study world religions in China, you quickly learn that Judaism and Islam share profound connections. Both claim descent from Abraham. Both worship one God. Both have sacred texts, dietary laws, and daily prayers. A Chinese friend once asked me, "If they're so similar, why are they separate religions?" It's a fair question — and the answer reveals how two traditions can spring from common roots yet grow in profoundly different directions.

This article explores the key theological and practical differences between Judaism and Islam, with deep respect for both traditions. We're not debating which is "correct" — we're understanding what makes each unique.

## Shared Foundations: The Abrahamic Connection

Before we discuss differences, let's acknowledge what Judaism and Islam genuinely share:

**Common Ancestor:** Both traditions trace themselves back to Abraham (Ibrahim in Arabic). Muslims see themselves as descendants of Abraham through his son Ishmael, while Jews trace lineage through his son Isaac. This isn't just symbolic — it's family history.

**Strict Monotheism:** Both religions proclaim absolute belief in one God. No partners, no intermediaries, no physical form. This sets both apart from Christianity's Trinity and from polytheistic traditions.

**Sacred Law:** Both have comprehensive legal systems governing daily life. Jews follow *halacha* (Jewish law), Muslims follow *sharia* (Islamic law). Both systems cover prayer, food, family life, business ethics, and community obligations.

**Prophetic Tradition:** Both honor prophets who received divine revelation. Moses, Abraham, Noah, David — all these figures appear in both the Hebrew Bible and the Quran.

**Revealed Scripture:** Both believe God revealed His will through sacred texts — the Torah for Jews, the Quran for Muslims.

Given these profound similarities, what creates the distinction?

## Key Difference #1: The Nature of Prophecy and Revelation

### Judaism's View

In Jewish thought, prophecy reached its peak with Moses. The Torah states: "Never again has there arisen in Israel a prophet like Moses, whom the Lord knew face to face" (Deuteronomy 34:10). Moses received the Torah at Mount Sinai — both the Written Torah (the Five Books) and the Oral Torah (explanations and interpretations later written in the Mishnah and Talmud).

Later prophets like Isaiah, Jeremiah, and Ezekiel did bring divine messages, but their role was to remind the Jewish people to return to the Torah Moses already gave them. They added no new commandments. Jewish tradition teaches that prophecy ended with Malachi, the last biblical prophet (approximately 350 BCE — during China's Warring States period, 战国时代).

**Key Point:** For Jews, revelation is *closed*. The Torah is complete. No new Torah will come. The Messiah, when he arrives, will not bring new religious laws — he will restore the old ones.

### Islam's View

Muslims believe that while Moses was indeed a great prophet, Muhammad was the *final* prophet — the "Seal of the Prophets" (Khatam an-Nabiyyin). According to Islamic teaching, God sent prophets throughout history, but people corrupted their messages. The Quran was revealed to Muhammad to provide the final, uncorrupted message for all humanity.

From the Islamic perspective, the Torah and the Gospels contain truth but were altered over time. The Quran supersedes them as the final, perfect revelation.

**Key Point:** For Muslims, Muhammad's revelation *completes and corrects* previous scriptures. The Quran is the ultimate authority.

### The Chinese Parallel

Chinese readers might compare this to different schools of Confucian interpretation. Orthodox Confucians view the Five Classics as complete and perfect — later scholars like Zhu Xi (朱熹, Song Dynasty) interpreted them but didn't replace them. This is closer to the Jewish model. In contrast, if a new sage claimed to have a superior teaching that superseded Confucius, that would be analogous to Islam's claim about Muhammad and the Quran.

## Key Difference #2: The Chosen People vs. The Universal Ummah

### Judaism: Particularism

Judaism teaches the concept of *Am Segulah* — the "treasured people" or "chosen people." This does **not** mean Jews are superior. It means God chose the Jewish people to enter a unique covenant at Mount Sinai: to observe the 613 commandments of the Torah and to be a "light unto the nations" (Isaiah 49:6).

Critically, this covenant is **not required for non-Jews**. Gentiles who follow the Seven Noahide Laws (basic universal ethics: no murder, no theft, no idolatry, etc.) are considered righteous and have a place in the World to Come. Judaism does not seek to convert the world.

**Key Point:** Judaism is a *national* religion tied to the Jewish people. You're born into it or choose to join it through conversion, but either way, you become part of the Jewish nation.

### Islam: Universalism

Islam views itself as the final revelation for **all of humanity**. There is no "chosen people" in the particularist sense. Every human being — regardless of ethnicity or geography — is called to submit to Allah and follow the teachings of the Quran and Muhammad.

The *Ummah* (the global Muslim community) is meant to include everyone. Islam actively seeks converts because the message is considered universal and essential for salvation.

**Key Point:** Islam is a *universal* religion. Its message is for everyone, everywhere, always.

### The Chinese Parallel

This difference resembles the distinction between Confucianism (which was primarily for Chinese civilization and governance) and Buddhism (which entered China as a universal teaching meant for all beings). Judaism, like Confucianism, is deeply rooted in a specific people and land. Islam, like Buddhism, presents itself as a truth for all humanity.

## Key Difference #3: The Land of Israel

### Judaism: Sacred Geography

For Jews, the Land of Israel is not just symbolically important — it's *halachically* (legally) essential. Many commandments in the Torah can only be performed in the Land of Israel. The Temple in Jerusalem was the center of Jewish worship. Three times daily, Jews pray facing Jerusalem.

The hope to return to Israel has been the heartbeat of Jewish prayer for 2,000 years. "Next year in Jerusalem" is recited at the conclusion of the Passover Seder and Yom Kippur services. The modern State of Israel, established in 1948, is seen by many religious Jews as the beginning of the fulfillment of ancient prophecies.

**Key Point:** Judaism cannot be separated from the Land of Israel. It's not just where Jews happen to live — it's where God commanded them to build a holy society.

### Islam: Mecca and Jerusalem

For Muslims, Mecca (the birthplace of Muhammad and the site of the Kaaba) is the holiest city. Muslims pray five times daily facing Mecca. Jerusalem is also holy — the third holiest site in Islam — because Muslims believe Muhammad ascended to heaven from the Temple Mount during the Night Journey (Isra and Mi'raj).

However, Islam is not tied to a specific land in the way Judaism is. Islam has thrived in Arabia, North Africa, Persia, Southeast Asia, and beyond. The faith is portable; the *Ummah* can exist anywhere.

**Key Point:** While Jerusalem holds significance in Islam, the faith is not geographically bound. Islam is a religion of global reach.

## Key Difference #4: Law and Interpretation

### Judaism: The Oral Torah and Rabbinic Authority

Judaism teaches that God gave Moses two Torahs at Mount Sinai: the *Written Torah* (the Five Books of Moses) and the *Oral Torah* (explanations, applications, and interpretations). The Oral Torah was eventually written down in the Mishnah (circa 200 CE — during China's late Han Dynasty, 汉朝) and expanded in the Talmud.

Rabbinic scholars continue to interpret and apply Torah law to new situations. When a question arises — "Can I use electricity on Shabbat?" or "Is this food kosher?" — rabbis study precedents, analyze the Talmud, and issue rulings. This process of ongoing interpretation is called *halachic process*.

**Key Point:** Jewish law is *interpretive and evolving* (within strict parameters). Rabbis have the authority to apply ancient principles to modern situations.

### Islam: The Quran, Hadith, and Scholarly Consensus

Islamic law (*sharia*) draws from two primary sources: the Quran (the direct word of Allah) and the Hadith (the recorded sayings and actions of Muhammad). Islamic scholars (*ulama*) interpret these sources using methods like *qiyas* (analogical reasoning) and *ijma* (scholarly consensus).

Different schools of Islamic jurisprudence (*madhhab*) exist — Hanafi, Maliki, Shafi'i, Hanbali, and others — each with slightly different interpretative methodologies. However, the Quran itself is considered the direct, unmediated word of God, and its text is unchangeable.

**Key Point:** Islamic law is also interpretive, but the Quran holds an even more central and untouchable status than the Torah does in Judaism, since Muslims believe it is the literal word of God dictated to Muhammad.

### The Chinese Parallel

Chinese legal and philosophical traditions also evolved through commentary and interpretation. Confucian scholars spent centuries writing commentaries on the classics, much like Jewish and Islamic scholars do with their texts. The difference is that Confucianism never claimed divine authorship of its texts — they were the wisdom of sages, not the direct word of Heaven.

## Key Difference #5: The Concept of the Messiah

### Judaism: The Messiah as Future Restorer

Judaism teaches that the Messiah (*Mashiach*) has not yet come. He will be a human descendant of King David who will:
- Rebuild the Temple in Jerusalem
- Gather all Jews back to the Land of Israel
- Bring world peace
- Restore Torah observance

The Messiah will not be divine. He will be a great leader, but still human. His arrival will mark the beginning of the Messianic Age — an era of universal knowledge of God, justice, and peace.

**Key Point:** The Messiah is a *future* event. Jews are still waiting.

### Islam: Jesus and the End Times

Muslims believe that Jesus (*Isa* in Arabic) was a great prophet, but not divine and not the son of God. Islam teaches that Jesus was not crucified — God saved him and raised him to heaven. Muslims believe Jesus will return near the end of time to defeat the Antichrist (*Dajjal*) and establish justice.

Islam does not have the same concept of a Davidic Messiah. Some traditions speak of the *Mahdi* (a guided one) who will appear before the end times, but this is not equivalent to the Jewish Messiah.

**Key Point:** Islam sees Jesus as a prophet who will return, but the concept of a Messianic Age differs significantly from Jewish eschatology.

## Key Difference #6: Conversion and Identity

### Judaism: A High Bar for Conversion

Judaism has historically not sought converts. In fact, the Talmud teaches that a potential convert should be turned away three times to test their sincerity. Conversion is possible, but it requires:
- Extensive study (often 1-2 years)
- Commitment to observe all 613 commandments
- Circumcision (for men)
- Immersion in a ritual bath (*mikveh*)
- Acceptance by a rabbinic court (*beit din*)

Once someone converts, they are fully Jewish — considered a child of Abraham and Sarah. But Judaism does not evangelize.

**Key Point:** Judaism is a *peoplehood* as much as a religion. Joining it means joining the Jewish nation.

### Islam: Open Invitation

Islam actively welcomes converts. The process is simple: one recites the *Shahada* (declaration of faith) — "There is no god but Allah, and Muhammad is His messenger" — with sincere belief, and one is Muslim.

Islam sees conversion as returning to the natural state (*fitrah*) in which all humans are born. The religion is accessible, universal, and open to all.

**Key Point:** Islam is an *open door*. Anyone can enter through sincere declaration of faith.

## Practical Differences in Daily Life

### Prayer

**Judaism:** Three daily prayer services (morning, afternoon, evening), facing Jerusalem. Prayers are mostly in Hebrew, with specific liturgy. Men traditionally wear a prayer shawl (*tallit*) and phylacteries (*tefillin*) during morning prayers.

**Islam:** Five daily prayers (*Salat*) at specific times, facing Mecca. Prayers include physical movements (standing, bowing, prostrating). The call to prayer (*adhan*) is recited before each prayer time.

### Dietary Laws

**Judaism:** Kosher laws (*kashrut*) include: no pork or shellfish, no mixing meat and dairy, ritual slaughter (*shechitah*), and checking for insects in vegetables.

**Islam:** Halal laws prohibit pork and alcohol, require ritual slaughter (*zabiha*), and bless the animal in the name of Allah at slaughter.

The two systems are similar but not identical. A Jew cannot simply eat halal meat because the slaughter method and blessings differ.

### Sabbath and Holidays

**Judaism:** Shabbat (Saturday) is a day of complete rest. No work, no technology, no commerce. Major holidays include Rosh Hashanah, Yom Kippur, Passover, and Sukkot.

**Islam:** Friday (*Jumu'ah*) is the day for communal prayer, but it is not a full day of rest. The major holidays are Eid al-Fitr (end of Ramadan) and Eid al-Adha (Festival of Sacrifice).

## Why These Differences Matter

Understanding these distinctions helps us appreciate both religions on their own terms. Judaism is a covenant between God and a specific people, tied to a specific land, centered on the Torah given at Sinai, and awaiting the Messiah. Islam is a universal message, portable across cultures, centered on the Quran revealed to Muhammad, and already considering itself the final revelation.

Neither needs to be "better" or "worse." They are different paths, different covenants, different understandings of how God relates to humanity.

## A Note on Respect

In writing this article, I've sought to honor both traditions. If you're a Chinese reader exploring these faiths, know that Jews and Muslims share neighborhoods, trade goods, and sometimes (though sadly not always) live in peace. Both communities have contributed immensely to science, philosophy, medicine, and the arts.

The differences outlined here are theological, not moral. A righteous Muslim and a righteous Jew are both beloved by God. The Talmud teaches, "The righteous of all nations have a share in the World to Come."

## For the Chinese Reader: Building Bridges

If you come from a Confucian, Daoist, or Buddhist background, you might see both Judaism and Islam as "religions of the book" — text-based, law-oriented, monotheistic. Both will seem foreign compared to the fluidity of Chinese folk religion or the non-theistic nature of classical Buddhism.

But here's what you can appreciate: both traditions, like Confucianism, emphasize **law, study, and community**. Both honor ancient texts and scholarly debate. Both value family, charity, and ethical conduct.

If you're considering conversion to either, study carefully. Speak with Jews and Muslims. Visit a synagogue and a mosque. Read the texts. Ask questions. Respect that these are not just philosophies — they are covenants, commitments, and communities.

## Conclusion

Judaism and Islam are sibling faiths — they share a father (Abraham), a belief in one God, and a commitment to living according to divine law. Yet they diverge on prophecy, peoplehood, land, and law.

Judaism says, "We are a people chosen for a mission, waiting for the Messiah, bound to the Torah and the Land of Israel."

Islam says, "We are the final message for all humanity, following the last prophet, called to submit to Allah wherever we are."

Both are beautiful. Both are demanding. Both are true to those who follow them.

And for those of us learning from the outside, the greatest gift is to understand each on its own terms — with respect, curiosity, and humility.

## Key Takeaways

- **Judaism and Islam share common Abrahamic roots but differ profoundly in theology and practice**
- **Prophecy:** Judaism sees Moses as the greatest prophet and revelation as closed; Islam sees Muhammad as the final prophet with the Quran as the ultimate revelation
- **Peoplehood:** Judaism is a national covenant with the Jewish people; Islam is a universal message for all humanity
- **Land:** The Land of Israel is central to Jewish law and theology; Islam is geographically portable
- **Messiah:** Judaism awaits a future Davidic Messiah; Islam teaches Jesus will return in the end times
- **Conversion:** Judaism has a high bar and doesn't proselytize; Islam openly invites all to embrace the faith
- **Respect and Curiosity:** Understanding these differences enriches interfaith dialogue and honors both traditions
