## Introduction

Why do bad things happen to good people? This question has haunted humanity across every culture and era. In Chinese philosophy, the concept of 天理 (tianli, "heavenly principle") attempts to explain cosmic justice, while Confucian thinkers grappled with the apparent randomness of fortune and misfortune. Judaism faces this same ancient puzzle head-on, calling it "theodicy" — the attempt to reconcile God's goodness and power with the existence of suffering and evil in the world.

Unlike philosophical systems that sidestep the question or deny the problem, Judaism insists on wrestling with it honestly. From the biblical figure of Job crying out in anguish to Holocaust survivors asking "Where was God?", the Jewish approach doesn't offer easy answers — but it does offer profound frameworks for understanding, multiple perspectives that have evolved over thousands of years, and ultimately, a path forward even when clarity remains elusive.

## The Question That Won't Go Away

The problem of theodicy can be stated simply: If God is all-powerful, all-knowing, and all-good, why does evil exist? This creates what philosophers call the "trilemma":

1. God is all-powerful and could prevent evil
2. God is all-knowing and aware of all suffering
3. God is all-good and would want to eliminate evil

Yet evil persists. How can all three be true?

### The Talmudic Formulation: צדיק ורע לו

The rabbis of the Talmud (compiled 200-500 CE, during China's Three Kingdoms through Jin Dynasty period — 三国至晋朝) wrestled intensely with this question. They gave it a Hebrew name: **tzaddik v'ra lo** (צדיק ורע לו) — "the righteous person who suffers." The mirror question was **rasha v'tov lo** (רשע וטוב לו) — "the wicked person who prospers."

This wasn't abstract philosophy for them. They lived through Roman persecution, the destruction of the Second Temple in 70 CE, and the failed Bar Kokhba revolt. They knew suffering intimately. Their approach was to collect multiple answers rather than insist on one "correct" explanation.

## Seven Jewish Approaches to Evil and Suffering

### 1. Hidden Justice: We Don't See the Full Picture

**The Talmudic View:** We cannot judge God's actions because we don't have complete information. What appears unjust from our limited perspective may serve a greater purpose we cannot perceive.

The Talmud (Berachot 7a) teaches: "No human can stand in God's place" — meaning we cannot fully understand the divine calculation of justice. This parallels the Chinese proverb 塞翁失马，焉知非福 (*sai weng shi ma, yan zhi fei fu*) — "when the old man lost his horse, how could he have known it wasn't a blessing?" Both traditions recognize that immediate judgment of events may prove premature.

**Application:** When facing suffering, this view counsels humility about our ability to judge. The answer is less "I understand why this happened" and more "I trust there is meaning even when I cannot see it."

### 2. Free Will: The Price of Human Choice

**Rambam's (Maimonides) Rationalist Answer:** God created humans with free will — the ability to choose between good and evil. Much of the suffering in the world results from humans misusing this freedom: murder, theft, oppression, war.

Maimonides (1138-1204 CE, during China's Southern Song Dynasty — 南宋, 1127-1279 CE) argued in his *Guide for the Perplexed* that God cannot simultaneously grant free will and prevent all its negative consequences. A world with moral agents must allow for moral evil.

This doesn't explain natural disasters or diseases, but it does account for a vast amount of human-caused suffering. The Holocaust, for instance, was not an act of God but of human beings choosing radical evil.

**Application:** This view empowers us. We are not puppets. Our choices matter, which means we bear responsibility for reducing suffering in the world.

### 3. Divine Concealment: הסתר פנים (Hester Panim)

**The Kabbalistic and Chassidic View:** Sometimes God "hides His face" (*hester panim*) from us. This concealment allows evil to flourish temporarily, but it serves a purpose — to give humanity space to mature, to choose good freely, and to develop spiritual strength.

The Baal Shem Tov (founder of Chassidism, 1698-1760, during China's Qing Dynasty — 清朝) taught that divine concealment is like a parent stepping back to allow a child to learn. The parent is still present, still loving, but not intervening so the child can grow.

Rabbi Kalonymus Kalman Shapira, who continued teaching Torah in the Warsaw Ghetto during the Holocaust, wrote that even when God seems absent, His essence remains with those who suffer. The hiding itself is a profound mystery — but not abandonment.

**Application:** This view acknowledges the reality of God's seeming absence while maintaining faith in His ultimate presence. It validates the feeling of abandonment while insisting it's not the final truth.

### 4. This World vs. The Next: Olam Hazeh and Olam Haba

**The Traditional Rabbinic Answer:** This world (*olam hazeh*) is not the final reality. The righteous may suffer here but will be rewarded in the World to Come (*olam haba*). The wicked may prosper here but will face judgment in the next world.

The Mishnah (Avot 4:17) states: "This world is like a corridor before the World to Come. Prepare yourself in the corridor so you may enter the banquet hall."

Critics of this view note it can seem like deferral rather than explanation. Yet it reflects a fundamental Jewish belief: our earthly existence is not the totality of existence. Justice may be delayed but is not denied.

**Application:** This perspective provides comfort and hope. Present suffering is real but temporary. Ultimate justice will be rendered.

### 5. Suffering as Refinement: יסורים של אהבה (Yissurim Shel Ahava)

**The Mussar View:** Some suffering is "afflictions of love" — tests or trials that refine and elevate the soul. Just as gold is purified through fire, so too can suffering burn away spiritual dross and reveal inner strength.

The Talmud (Berachot 5a) teaches that when a person suffers, they should examine their actions to see if repentance is needed. If no sin is found, the suffering may be *yissurim shel ahava* — afflictions sent with love to increase reward in the next world or to prompt spiritual growth.

This parallels the Confucian and Daoist teaching that adversity builds character (多难兴邦, *duo nan xing bang* — "much adversity strengthens the nation"). The Chinese concept of 磨练 (*molian*, tempering through hardship) shares this understanding that difficulty can forge excellence.

**Application:** When suffering, ask: What can I learn? How can I grow? This doesn't explain all evil, but it can transform personal trials into opportunities for development.

### 6. The Incomprehensibility of God: The Book of Job's Answer

**The Biblical Response:** Sometimes there is no answer we can grasp. The Book of Job presents a righteous man who loses everything despite his goodness. His friends offer various theodicies — he must have sinned, suffering is educational, etc. All are rejected.

Finally, God speaks "from the whirlwind" (Job 38-41) — not with explanations but with overwhelming questions: "Where were you when I laid the foundations of the earth? Have you comprehended the expanse of the earth? Declare, if you know all this!"

God's response is not a logical argument but a reminder of the vast gulf between human and divine understanding. Job's reply: "I have uttered what I did not understand, things too wonderful for me" (Job 42:3).

**Application:** Some suffering remains mystery. Faith means trusting God even when we cannot understand, while still crying out our pain and confusion honestly.

### 7. Post-Holocaust Theology: The Rebbe's Response

**Modern Chassidic Answer:** After the Holocaust murdered six million Jews — including 1.5 million children — traditional theodicies seemed inadequate to many. Some theologians declared God had died or abandoned His covenant.

The Lubavitcher Rebbe, Rabbi Menachem Mendel Schneerson, offered a different response: We cannot understand the Holocaust. It is beyond human comprehension. But we can *respond* to it — by affirming life, by increasing goodness, by ensuring "never again," and by dedicating ourselves to building a better world.

The Rebbe taught: Don't ask "Why did this happen?" but "What does God want from me *now*?" Transform suffering not by explaining it but by ensuring it produces meaning and positive action.

Elie Wiesel, Holocaust survivor and Nobel laureate, wrote: "I have not lost faith in God. I have moments of anger and protest. Sometimes I've been closer to him for that reason."

**Application:** When suffering overwhelms understanding, respond with action. Increase light. Help others. Create meaning even in the face of meaninglessness.

## Synthesis: Living With the Question

Judaism does not offer one definitive answer to the problem of evil. Instead, it provides a toolkit of perspectives, allowing individuals to find the framework that speaks to their experience and moment.

What unites all these approaches:

1. **Honesty:** Judaism permits — even encourages — questioning and protest. Abraham argues with God over Sodom (Genesis 18), Moses challenges God after the golden calf (Exodus 32), and the Psalms overflow with cries of "How long, O Lord?"

2. **Hope:** Even when understanding fails, Jewish theology maintains that evil is not ultimate. God's goodness will prevail, whether in this world or the next.

3. **Responsibility:** Rather than theological fatalism, Judaism insists we are partners (*shutafim*) with God in repairing the world (*tikkun olam*). We may not control what happens to us, but we control how we respond.

4. **Community:** Suffering is not borne alone. The Jewish practice of visiting the sick (*bikur cholim*), comforting mourners, and communal prayer all express solidarity with those in pain.

## Practical Application

When confronting suffering — your own or others' — the Jewish approach suggests:

**For your own suffering:**
- Cry out honestly to God. Anger is permitted; abandoning faith is not required even when abandonment feels real.
- Examine if any choices led to this situation, and make repairs if possible.
- Look for growth opportunities without forcing premature "lessons."
- Remember this is not the totality of existence.
- Reach out to community; don't suffer in isolation.

**For others' suffering:**
- Avoid easy theodicies. Don't tell someone "God has a plan" or "Everything happens for a reason" — these often bring more pain than comfort.
- **Presence over explanation.** The Hebrew phrase *לשבת ולבכות* (*lashevet v'livkot*) — "to sit and weep" — describes the mitzvah of simply being present with those who mourn.
- Take concrete action to alleviate suffering where possible.
- Validate pain without trying to explain it away.

**For theological wrestling:**
- Study multiple perspectives. Read Job, the Talmudic debates, modern thinkers.
- Accept that certainty may be impossible. Judaism values the struggle, not just the answer.
- Balance intellectual honesty with spiritual trust.

## The Chinese-Jewish Parallel: 天人合一 and Divine Partnership

Chinese philosophy's concept of 天人合一 (*tian ren he yi*, "Heaven and humanity in harmony") suggests that cosmic order and human action are deeply interconnected. This resonates with the Jewish teaching that humans are partners with God in completing creation.

Both traditions resist the idea of passive acceptance of suffering. Confucius taught active moral cultivation and social responsibility; Judaism commands pursuit of justice (*tzedek tzedek tirdof* — "Justice, justice shall you pursue," Deuteronomy 16:20).

The shared wisdom: We may not control the universe, but we are not powerless. Our choices, our compassion, our pursuit of goodness — these matter. They shape the world and perhaps even influence the heavenly realm.

## Key Takeaways

- **Theodicy** is the attempt to reconcile God's goodness with the existence of evil. Judaism offers multiple approaches rather than one answer.

- **Seven major perspectives:** Hidden justice, free will, divine concealment, this world vs. the next, suffering as refinement, divine incomprehensibility, and post-Holocaust action-oriented response.

- **Honest questioning is permitted:** Abraham, Moses, Job, and the Psalms all model crying out to God in pain and confusion.

- **Response matters more than explanation:** The Lubavitcher Rebbe taught that when understanding fails, we should ask "What does God want from me now?" and respond with increased goodness.

- **Suffering is communal:** Jewish tradition emphasizes presence, solidarity, and practical help over theological explanations.

- **Faith and struggle coexist:** Wrestling with these questions is itself an expression of faith, not a betrayal of it.
