# Understanding Gematria: The Numerical Values of Hebrew Letters

---

Every Hebrew letter is more than just a sound or shape—it also carries a numerical value. This ancient system, known as *gematria* (גימטריה), has been used for thousands of years to unlock hidden meanings in Torah texts, explore mystical connections between words, and discover deeper layers of spiritual wisdom. For someone just beginning to explore Judaism, gematria opens a fascinating window into how Jewish tradition sees language as a bridge between the physical and spiritual worlds.

## What Is Gematria?

Gematria is the practice of assigning numerical values to Hebrew letters and using those numbers to find connections between words, phrases, and concepts. In Hebrew, unlike English, there are no separate symbols for numbers—the letters themselves double as numerals. The first ten letters represent 1-10, the next nine represent 20-90, and the final four letters represent 100-400.

Here's the basic chart:

When you add up the values of all the letters in a Hebrew word, you get its gematria. Words with the same numerical value are thought to share a hidden connection or reveal something about each other.

## The Logic Behind Gematria

To understand why Jews take gematria seriously, you need to understand how traditional Judaism views language. The Torah begins with God creating the world through speech: "And God said, 'Let there be light,' and there was light" (Genesis 1:3). Jewish mystical tradition teaches that Hebrew letters are not just symbols—they are spiritual building blocks, the very "DNA" of creation.

If letters have creative power, then their numerical values reflect deeper patterns in the universe. Gematria is not numerology in the superstitious sense; it's a method for uncovering the Torah's hidden layers. Rabbis use it to find connections between biblical stories, explain difficult passages, and teach moral lessons in memorable ways.

## Classic Examples of Gematria

### 1. **Ahavah (Love) = Echad (One)**

One of the most famous examples is the word *ahavah* (אהבה), meaning "love," which has a gematria of 13:

- א (1) + ה (5) + ב (2) + ה (5) = 13

The word *echad* (אחד), meaning "one," also equals 13:

- א (1) + ח (8) + ד (4) = 13

This teaches a beautiful lesson: Love and unity are inseparable. True love brings two people into oneness. This is why the Shema declares "God is One" (Deuteronomy 6:4)—it's a statement about divine unity, but also about the unity love creates.

### 2. **Chai (Life) = 18**

The Hebrew word *chai* (חי), meaning "life," equals 18:

- ח (8) + י (10) = 18

This is why Jewish culture places special significance on the number 18. Gifts of money at weddings, bar mitzvahs, and other celebrations are traditionally given in multiples of 18 ($18, $36, $180, etc.). It's not just a custom—it's a way of saying, "May this gift bring life, blessing, and vitality."

### 3. **Nachash (Snake) = Mashiach (Messiah)**

The word *nachash* (נחש), meaning "snake" or "serpent," equals 358:

- נ (50) + ח (8) + ש (300) = 358

Remarkably, *Mashiach* (משיח), the Hebrew word for "Messiah," also equals 358:

- מ (40) + ש (300) + י (10) + ח (8) = 358

This gematria teaches that the Messiah will reverse the spiritual damage done by the serpent in the Garden of Eden. The force that brought death into the world will be defeated by the force that brings ultimate redemption.

### 4. **Yaakov (Jacob) = 182, Esav (Esau) = 376**

The patriarch Jacob's name (יעקב) equals 182, while his brother Esau's name (עשו) equals 376. Interestingly, 376 is exactly double 188, which is close to Jacob's value. This numerical relationship reflects their lifelong struggle: two brothers, two nations, locked in a cosmic wrestling match that continues through history.

## Gematria and Chinese Numerology: A Cultural Parallel

Chinese culture has its own rich tradition of numbers carrying symbolic meaning. Just as gematria assigns significance to Hebrew letter values, Chinese numerology sees certain numbers as lucky or unlucky based on their sounds and associations.

The number **8 (八, bā)** is considered extremely lucky in Chinese culture because it sounds like *fa* (发), meaning "to prosper" or "to get rich." In Jewish gematria, the letter chet (ח), which equals 8, begins the word *chai* (life), making 18 doubly significant—both in its Hebrew spelling and its echo in Chinese prosperity symbolism.

The number **18** holds parallel meanings in both cultures: In Judaism, it represents life (*chai*). In Chinese culture, 18 can be broken down as 1 + 8 = 9, which sounds like *jiu* (久), meaning "long-lasting" or "eternity." Both traditions see 18 as a number of blessing and continuity.

Similarly, the number **4 (四, sì)** is often avoided in Chinese culture because it sounds like *si* (死), meaning "death." In Jewish mysticism, the number 4 represents the physical world—four directions, four elements, four matriarchs—but when multiplied or combined with other numbers, it takes on transformative power. The connection between numbers and meaning transcends culture.

## How Gematria Is Used in Jewish Study

### 1. **Torah Commentary**

Rabbis use gematria to draw connections between different Torah verses. For example, when the Torah says Abraham took 318 men to rescue Lot (Genesis 14:14), the Talmud notes that 318 is the gematria of *Eliezer* (אליעזר), Abraham's faithful servant. This suggests Abraham didn't actually take hundreds of soldiers—just Eliezer, whose righteousness was worth an army.

### 2. **Explaining Mitzvot**

The Torah commands us to keep 613 mitzvot (commandments). The gematria of *Torah* (תורה) is 611:

- ת (400) + ו (6) + ר (200) + ה (5) = 611

Add the two commandments we heard directly from God at Mount Sinai ("I am the Lord your God" and "You shall have no other gods"), and you get 613. This teaches that all 613 commandments flow from the Torah and the direct encounter with God.

### 3. **Mystical Prayer**

Kabbalists use gematria in prayer and meditation to focus the mind on divine names and attributes. By contemplating the numerical values of God's names, they seek to align their consciousness with spiritual realities.

### 4. **Interpreting Dreams and Events**

Some Jewish communities use gematria to interpret personal experiences. If significant events happen on dates with meaningful gematria, or if names and places involved share numerical values, people see it as a sign of divine providence at work.

## Limitations and Cautions

Jewish tradition warns against overusing gematria. The great medieval scholar Nachmanides wrote that while gematria can illuminate Torah, it should not be used to create new laws or obligations. It's a tool for deepening understanding, not for inventing doctrine.

Rabbi Abraham ibn Ezra famously said, "Gematria is like a servant—when you need it, it's there to help, but you don't let the servant run the house." In other words, gematria should serve Torah study, not replace straightforward interpretation.

Modern scholars also caution that with enough creativity, you can make almost any two words "match" numerically. The key is whether a gematria connection reveals a genuine insight that fits with broader Jewish teachings, or whether it's just clever wordplay.

## Different Systems of Gematria

Over time, Jewish mystics developed variations on basic gematria:

### **Mispar Katan (Small Number)**
This reduces numbers to single digits. For example, *Torah* (611) becomes 6 + 1 + 1 = 8.

### **Mispar Gadol (Large Number)**
This uses the "final forms" of certain letters (final kaf = 500, final mem = 600, etc.) to create larger values.

### **Atbash**
This reverses the alphabet: aleph becomes tav, bet becomes shin, etc. The prophet Jeremiah uses this system when he writes "Sheshach" (ששך) as a code for "Babylon" (בבל).

### **Ordinal Gematria**
Each letter gets its position in the alphabet: aleph = 1, bet = 2, etc., regardless of standard values.

## Learning Gematria as a Beginner

You don't need to memorize hundreds of gematria connections to benefit from this system. Start simple:

1. **Learn the numerical values of the letters.** Once you know the Hebrew alphabet, add the numbers to your knowledge.

2. **Look for famous examples.** As you read Torah commentaries or Jewish books, you'll encounter gematria naturally. Notice when a rabbi points out a meaningful connection.

3. **Use gematria to remember concepts.** The fact that *ahavah* and *echad* both equal 13 makes it easier to remember that love creates unity.

4. **Don't force it.** Gematria is most powerful when it reveals something unexpected that fits perfectly with what you already understand about a text.

5. **Ask a rabbi.** If you find an interesting gematria connection, share it with someone knowledgeable. They can tell you if it's meaningful or just coincidence.

## Why Numbers Matter

At its heart, gematria reflects a deep Jewish belief: The universe is not random. Patterns, connections, and meanings are woven into creation itself. Numbers are one way God speaks to us through the structure of reality.

When you study gematria, you're not just playing with numbers—you're learning to see the world the way Jewish mystics do, as a text written by God, where every detail matters and every connection points toward deeper truth.

Whether you encounter the number 18 at a Jewish wedding, see the word *chai* on a necklace, or discover that two seemingly unrelated Torah words share a gematria, you're touching an ancient tradition that treats language, numbers, and meaning as inseparable.

As you continue your journey into Judaism, let gematria remind you that Hebrew is not just another language—it's a doorway into the mind of creation itself.
