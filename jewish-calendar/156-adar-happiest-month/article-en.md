# Adar: The Happiest Month in the Jewish Calendar

---

When the Hebrew month of Adar arrives, something shifts in the Jewish consciousness. The Talmud teaches us a simple but profound instruction: "Mishenichnas Adar marbim b'simcha" — "When Adar enters, we increase in joy" (Ta'anit 29a). But what makes this particular month so special? Why does Judaism single out these twenty-nine or thirty days as a time for elevated happiness?

## The Calendar Context

The Jewish calendar follows a lunar cycle, with each month beginning at the new moon. Adar is the twelfth month in a regular year, falling roughly between late February and early March. However, in a Jewish leap year (which occurs seven times in every nineteen-year cycle), we actually have two months of Adar — Adar I and Adar II — giving us double the opportunity for joy.

This adjustment keeps the Jewish holidays aligned with their proper seasons. Without it, Passover (which must occur in spring) would drift through all seasons over time. The rabbis determined that when an extra month is needed, Adar is the one to duplicate. This decision itself hints at the month's special character — when the calendar needs balancing, we add more Adar, more joy.

## Purim: The Heart of Adar's Joy

The primary reason for Adar's joyous character is Purim, celebrated on the 14th of Adar (or Adar II in a leap year). The story, recorded in the Book of Esther (Megillat Esther), tells of a dramatic reversal: what began as a decree of destruction against the Jewish people in ancient Persia ended in salvation and celebration.

Haman, the villain of the story, cast lots (purim in Hebrew) to determine the "lucky" day to annihilate the Jews, and the lot fell on the 13th of Adar. He believed this month was inauspicious for the Jewish people because Moses died on the 7th of Adar. But Haman didn't know that Moses was also born on that same date — a detail that transforms the symbolism entirely. In Jewish thought, the completeness of a tzaddik's (righteous person's) life, from birth to death, is itself a form of perfection and blessing.

Through the courage of Queen Esther and the wisdom of Mordechai, the plot was foiled. The Jews defended themselves on the 13th and celebrated their salvation on the 14th (and in walled cities like Shushan, on the 15th). The holiday of Purim commemorates this incredible turnaround — from terror to triumph, from mourning to dancing.

## The Character of Purim's Joy

Purim's joy is unique in the Jewish calendar. While other holidays involve specific rituals and restrictions, Purim's celebration is characterized by exuberance and even playfulness. The Talmud instructs that one should drink on Purim until unable to distinguish between "cursed is Haman" and "blessed is Mordechai" (Megillah 7b) — though many authorities interpret this not as actual inebriation but as reaching a state of heightened spiritual joy.

We dress in costumes, give gifts of food to friends (mishloach manot), donate to the poor (matanot la'evyonim), feast together, and read the Megillah amid noise-making and celebration. Children (and adults!) bring graggers (noisemakers) to drown out Haman's name whenever it appears in the reading.

This joy isn't frivolous — it's profound. Purim celebrates hidden miracles, divine providence working through natural events. Unlike the openly supernatural miracles of Passover or Chanukah, Purim's salvation came through political maneuvering, courage, and seemingly coincidental timing. God's name doesn't even appear in the Book of Esther. Yet Jewish tradition sees profound divine orchestration in every detail of the story.

## Connecting to Chinese Culture: Joy in Reversal

The theme of dramatic reversal that makes Adar joyful resonates across cultures. In Chinese culture, the concept of 否极泰来 (pǐ jí tài lái — "when misfortune reaches its extreme, good fortune follows") expresses a similar understanding of life's cyclical nature. The I Ching (易经) philosophy teaches that situations transform into their opposites when they reach an extreme.

This wisdom appears in the Purim story perfectly: the very day meant for destruction became the day of deliverance. The gallows Haman built for Mordechai became his own execution site. His position of power led to his downfall. Like the eternal dance of yin and yang, darkness gives way to light.

Chinese New Year celebrations, which often overlap with the season of Adar, share some of Purim's festive spirit: special foods, gifts, family gatherings, the wearing of special clothing, and the driving away of evil (firecrackers to scare away the monster Nian parallels the noise we make to blot out Haman's name).

## The Mazal of Adar

Jewish astrology associates each Hebrew month with a constellation (mazal). Adar's constellation is Pisces (Dagim — fish in Hebrew). Fish carry special symbolism in Jewish thought: they live beneath the water's surface, hidden from the evil eye; they multiply prolifically; and they represent blessing and abundance.

This imagery connects to both Purim and Adar's character. Just as fish are hidden beneath the surface, so too was God's hand hidden in the Purim story. Just as fish are fertile and numerous, so too did the Jewish people multiply and thrive despite attempts to destroy them.

In Chinese culture, fish (鱼, yú) also symbolize abundance because the word sounds like "surplus" (余, yú). Fish appear prominently in Lunar New Year celebrations as symbols of prosperity for the coming year. This convergence of symbolism across two ancient cultures points to something universal in how we understand blessing and providence.

## Practical Expressions of Adar's Joy

How do Jews actually practice the commandment to increase in joy during Adar? Here are some traditional customs:

**Begin preparations early**: Many communities start Purim preparations weeks in advance — making costumes, planning the festive meal (se'udah), preparing mishloach manot baskets. The anticipation itself increases joy.

**Learn Torah related to the holiday**: Study groups focus on the Book of Esther, the laws of Purim, and teachings about joy and divine providence. Joy rooted in understanding is deeper than superficial entertainment.

**Increase charity**: The entire month, not just Purim itself, is considered auspicious for giving tzedakah (charity). The joy of giving enhances the giver's happiness.

**Avoid sadness**: Some communities avoid scheduling sad events during Adar. Weddings, which are joyous occasions, are encouraged (though not during the first week of the following month, Nisan, for other reasons).

**Ad-lo-yada celebrations**: In Israel and many Jewish communities, elaborate Purim carnivals and parades (called Ad-lo-yada, from the Talmudic phrase about drinking) fill the streets with music, costumes, and celebration.

## The Deeper Meaning: Joy as Spiritual Work

Judaism doesn't see joy as merely an emotion that happens to us; it's a mitzvah (commandment), something we actively cultivate. This is especially true in Adar. The Chassidic masters taught that joy breaks through barriers — spiritual, emotional, and even physical obstacles dissolve in the presence of genuine simcha.

Rabbi Nachman of Breslov famously taught, "Mitzvah gedolah lihiyot b'simcha tamid" — "It is a great mitzvah to always be joyful." He saw sadness and depression as strategies of the yetzer hara (evil inclination) to distance us from God and from actualizing our potential. Joy, in contrast, opens us to blessing, creativity, and connection.

Adar teaches us that joy isn't dependent on circumstances being perfect. The Jews of Shushan had just faced existential threat. Yet from that very experience came the deepest celebration. The reversal from potential tragedy to triumph created a joy so profound that it echoes through every subsequent Adar for over two millennia.

## Adar in a Leap Year

In a Jewish leap year, we have Adar I and Adar II. Which is the "real" Adar for joy and Purim? Jewish law assigns Purim to Adar II, considering it the primary Adar. However, the 14th of Adar I is called "Purim Katan" (Little Purim), and it's customary to have a slightly more festive day then too — perhaps a special meal, refraining from fasting or eulogies.

This means that in a leap year, we have not just one month of increased joy, but two! The preparation begins in Adar I, builds through the month, continues into Adar II, and culminates in the full Purim celebration. Sixty days of increasing joy.

## A Message for Our Times

Living in an uncertain world, the message of Adar feels particularly relevant. We cannot always control our circumstances, but we can choose our response. We can cultivate joy even when external reasons for sadness exist. We can recognize that apparent coincidences may be divine providence working in hidden ways. We can trust that reversals are possible — that what seems dark can transform into light.

The instruction to increase joy in Adar isn't naive optimism. It's a sophisticated spiritual technology: actively choosing to see blessing, to celebrate survival, to recognize hidden miracles, and to share that joy with others through gifts, feasting, and charity.

## Conclusion: Choosing Joy

Adar reminds us that the Jewish calendar isn't just a system for tracking days — it's a roadmap for the soul's journey through the year. Each month carries its own spiritual energy, its own opportunities and challenges.

When Adar arrives, with its call to increase in joy, we're being invited into something transformative. Not the superficial happiness of entertainment or distraction, but the deep joy that comes from recognizing that we've survived, that good has triumphed, that God's providence continues to guide history even when hidden, and that we are part of an eternal story larger than ourselves.

As the saying goes: "Mishenichnas Adar marbim b'simcha." When Adar enters, we increase in joy. Not because everything is perfect, but because we choose to see the blessing, celebrate the miracle, and share the happiness. In a world that gives us plenty of reasons for sadness, Adar gives us permission — indeed, commands us — to choose joy instead.

May we merit to internalize this lesson not just during Adar, but throughout the entire year, transforming our lives and our world through the power of genuine, profound, and contagious simcha.
