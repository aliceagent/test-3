## Introduction

Have you ever noticed how the moon disappears completely from the sky, only to return a few nights later as a tiny silver crescent? This monthly cycle of renewal lies at the heart of one of Judaism's most meaningful celebrations: Rosh Chodesh (ראש חודש), literally "the head of the month."

Unlike major holidays that everyone knows about, Rosh Chodesh is what we might call a "minor holiday" — a semi-festive day that adds special prayers and traditions to mark the beginning of each new Hebrew month. But what makes Rosh Chodesh particularly fascinating is its deep connection to Jewish women, who have observed this day as their own special holiday for over three thousand years.

In many ways, Rosh Chodesh embodies a uniquely feminine perspective on time, renewal, and faith. Let's explore what makes this monthly celebration so special.

## What Is Rosh Chodesh?

### The Lunar Calendar Connection

The Jewish calendar follows the moon rather than the sun. Each month begins when the new moon appears in the sky — specifically, when the first tiny crescent becomes visible after the moon has completely disappeared.

In ancient times — during what corresponds to China's Western Zhou Dynasty (西周, ~1046-771 BCE) — the beginning of each month was determined by actual observation. Two witnesses would watch the night sky, and when they saw the new crescent moon, they would testify before the *beit din* (rabbinical court) in Jerusalem. The court would then officially declare: "The new month is sanctified!" This announcement would be spread throughout the land by lighting signal fires on mountaintops, creating a chain of communication across the entire country.

Today we use a fixed calculated calendar, but the principle remains the same: each Hebrew month begins with the appearance of the new moon.

### Why the Moon?

The Jewish people are often compared to the moon. Here's why this comparison is so meaningful:

Throughout Jewish history, our nation has experienced incredible ups and downs — periods of great achievement and devastating loss, times of flourishing and times of persecution. Like the moon, which waxes to fullness and then wanes until it disappears completely, the Jewish people have faced cycles of triumph and tragedy.

But here's the crucial point: even when the moon disappears entirely from the sky, it *always* returns. It never burns out like a star. It simply renews itself, month after month, for all eternity.

This is the message of Rosh Chodesh: **No matter how dark things get, renewal is always coming. Disappearance is never permanent.**

## How God Gave the First Mitzvah

The very first commandment God gave to the Jewish people as a nation — even before they left Egypt (during China's Late Shang Dynasty, 商朝晚期, ~1300-1046 BCE) — was the mitzvah of sanctifying the new month.

As it says in the Book of Exodus (12:2): "This month shall be for you the beginning of months; it shall be the first month of the year for you."

God was essentially saying: "You are now free to mark time. You are no longer slaves whose time belongs to their masters. From now on, *you* determine when each month begins. *You* sanctify time itself."

What a profound gift! By giving the Jewish people control over the calendar, God made us partners in the very structure of creation. We're not passive observers of time — we actively participate in defining it.

## Rosh Chodesh in the Holy Temple

When the Temple stood in Jerusalem (from the Western Zhou Dynasty through the Han Dynasty, 西周至汉朝, ~957 BCE - 70 CE), Rosh Chodesh was marked with special sacrifices and celebrations.

According to the Torah (Numbers 28:11-15), special offerings were brought: two bulls, one ram, and seven yearling lambs, along with grain and wine offerings. These sacrifices were "in addition to the regular daily offerings" — showing that Rosh Chodesh was indeed a special day, though not as major as Shabbat or the festivals.

People would travel to Jerusalem to witness these celebrations, and festive meals would be held. It was a day of joy and spiritual renewal.

## How We Celebrate Rosh Chodesh Today

Even though the Temple no longer stands, we continue to mark Rosh Chodesh with several special observances:

### 1. **Special Prayers**

During each of the three daily prayer services on Rosh Chodesh, we add a paragraph called *Ya'aleh V'yavo* ("May [our prayers] ascend and come [before God]"). This insertion reminds us that the day is special and asks God to remember us for good in the new month ahead.

We also recite a shortened version of *Hallel* — joyful psalms of praise to God. This is unusual, as Hallel is normally reserved for major festivals. The fact that we say it on Rosh Chodesh (even in an abbreviated form) shows the day's significance.

### 2. **Torah Reading**

On Rosh Chodesh morning, we read from the Torah — specifically the portion from Numbers (28:1-15) that describes the special sacrifices that were once offered in the Temple. Four people are called up to the Torah, more than on a regular weekday but fewer than on Shabbat.

After the Torah reading, we recite Psalm 104, which contains the verse: "He made the moon to mark the seasons."

### 3. **An Additional Prayer Service**

Following the morning prayers, we add a fourth service called *Musaf* (literally "addition"). This prayer service corresponds to the additional sacrifice that was once offered in the Temple on Rosh Chodesh. Through prayer, we fulfill the words of the prophet Hosea: "We will offer the words of our lips in place of bulls" (Hosea 14:3).

### 4. **A Festive Meal**

Many communities have the custom of making one meal on Rosh Chodesh slightly more elaborate than usual. While we don't have the full festive meals of major holidays, having a special bread and some delicacies is a way to honor the day.

### 5. **Announcing the New Month**

On the Shabbat before Rosh Chodesh (called *Shabbat Mevarchim* — "the Shabbat when we bless the month"), the upcoming new month is formally announced in the synagogue. The community declares together: "May it be God's will that the coming month bring us goodness and blessing!" Then we announce the day(s) of the week when Rosh Chodesh will occur.

This public announcement creates anticipation and helps everyone prepare spiritually for the new beginning ahead.

### 6. **Chodesh Tov!**

On Rosh Chodesh itself, it's customary to greet people with "*Chodesh tov*!" (חודש טוב) — "A good month!" or "*Chodesh tov u'mevorach*!" — "A good and blessed month!" Just as we wish each other "Shabbat Shalom" on Shabbat, this greeting reminds everyone that today is special.

## Why Rosh Chodesh Is Especially Connected to Women

Here's where Rosh Chodesh becomes truly unique: it's traditionally considered a special holiday for Jewish women. Many women have the custom of abstaining from certain types of work on this day — particularly heavy household chores like sewing, laundry, and weaving.

Why? The answer takes us back to one of the most dramatic moments in Jewish history.

### The Story of the Golden Calf

During the forty years that the Jewish people wandered in the desert after leaving Egypt (corresponding to China's Late Shang Dynasty, 商朝晚期, ~1300-1260 BCE), Moses ascended Mount Sinai to receive the Torah. When he didn't return exactly when expected, some of the people panicked.

They came to Aaron, Moses's brother, and demanded: "Make us a god who will go before us!" They wanted to create an idol — a golden calf — to replace Moses and God.

Aaron asked them to bring their gold jewelry to create this idol. The men immediately complied, eager to participate in this tragic mistake.

But the women refused.

According to the Midrash (ancient rabbinic commentary collected during China's Three Kingdoms to Jin Dynasty period, 三国至晋朝时期, ~200-500 CE), the women said: "Do you really think we're going to trade the God who created heaven and earth, who brought us out of Egypt with miracles and wonders, for a statue of a calf? Absolutely not!"

They refused to give their jewelry. They refused to participate in creating an idol. They held firm in their faith when others faltered.

As a reward for this steadfast faith, God gave the women Rosh Chodesh as their special holiday. The *Tur* (a major Jewish legal code from the Song-Yuan Dynasty period in China, 宋元时期, written in the 1300s) records: "It is written in Pirkei D'Rabbi Eliezer that women were given Rosh Chodesh as a holiday because they did not participate in the making of the Golden Calf."

### The Deeper Meaning

But there's an even deeper reason why women are connected to Rosh Chodesh, explained beautifully by the Lubavitcher Rebbe.

Faith — *emunah* in Hebrew — is not merely an abstract intellectual concept. True faith is something you feel in your bones, something that guides your actions even when logic might lead you astray. It's instinctive, deeply rooted, unwavering.

When the men saw that Moses hadn't returned on time, their intellectual side took over. "Something must have happened," they reasoned. "We need a Plan B." Their faith, strong as it may have been, became "fuzzy" under pressure.

But the women's faith didn't waver. Why? Because women, the Rebbe taught, carry within them a more instinctive, deeply rooted faith that isn't dependent on intellectual analysis. Even when things looked hopeless, even when Moses seemed to have disappeared forever, the women *knew* in their essence that God was still there, still protecting them, still guiding them.

This is the kind of faith that has kept the Jewish people alive throughout history. And it's precisely this kind of faith that has been passed down through generations — *by women to their children*.

Just as the moon disappears each month but always returns, the Jewish people have faced countless disappearances throughout history — but we've always renewed ourselves. And it's the faith of Jewish women that has ensured this perpetual renewal.

## A Cultural Parallel: Moon Celebrations in Chinese Tradition

It's worth noting that Chinese culture also has a deep connection to the moon and its cycles. The Mid-Autumn Festival (中秋节) celebrates the full moon, symbolizing family reunion and harmony. Both Jewish and Chinese traditions recognize the moon as a symbol of continuity, renewal, and the passage of time.

However, while the Mid-Autumn Festival celebrates the moon at its fullest and brightest, Rosh Chodesh celebrates the moon at its renewal — the moment when it reappears after complete darkness. This subtle difference reflects Judaism's emphasis on the power of renewal and the message that even after the darkest times, light returns.

## Practical Application: How You Can Observe Rosh Chodesh

If you're interested in beginning to observe Rosh Chodesh, here are some practical ways to do so:

### **For Everyone:**

1. **Check your calendar** to know when Rosh Chodesh occurs. (Most Hebrew months are 29 or 30 days, so Rosh Chodesh happens roughly every 29-30 days. When a month has 30 days, Rosh Chodesh is celebrated for two days: the 30th day of the old month and the 1st day of the new month.)

2. **Add the Ya'aleh V'yavo prayer** to your Amidah (if you pray daily). If you forget, don't worry — unlike Shabbat or holidays, we don't repeat the entire prayer.

3. **Say Hallel** in the morning. Many prayer books indicate which paragraphs to skip for Rosh Chodesh (we skip two paragraphs that are said on major holidays).

4. **Make a slightly special meal** — perhaps bake fresh bread or prepare a favorite dish. Use the meal as an opportunity to discuss the new month ahead and what you hope to accomplish.

5. **Greet people with "Chodesh Tov!"** throughout the day. Spreading joy is a mitzvah in itself.

### **For Women Specifically:**

1. **Consider taking a break from heavy chores.** You don't have to stop all work (Rosh Chodesh is not Shabbat), but traditionally women avoid particularly strenuous household tasks like laundry, sewing, and heavy cleaning. Use this freed-up time for something spiritual or restful.

2. **Join or start a Rosh Chodesh women's group.** In recent decades, beautiful gatherings have emerged where women come together on Rosh Chodesh to study Torah, share stories, sing, and celebrate their unique connection to the day. These gatherings often include a light meal, words of Torah, and meaningful conversation. Check if there's one in your community — or start one yourself!

3. **Use the day for personal renewal.** Just as the moon renews itself each month, use Rosh Chodesh as a time to reflect on the past month and set intentions for the new one. What went well? What would you like to improve? How can you grow spiritually?

4. **Pass it on to your daughters.** Explain to them why Rosh Chodesh is special for women, tell them the story of the Golden Calf, and help them understand the strength of female faith. These traditions are meant to be passed down from mother to daughter, generation after generation.

## The Message of Rosh Chodesh for Today

We live in a world that often values constant forward momentum, endless productivity, and uninterrupted progress. But the moon teaches us something different: that there's wisdom in cycles, beauty in renewal, and power in returning to the beginning.

Every month, the moon disappears. For a few nights, the sky is dark where it once shone. And then, like magic, a tiny sliver appears — and we know that fullness is coming again.

Rosh Chodesh reminds us that disappearance is not the same as destruction. Waning is not the same as dying. Sometimes we need to go back to the beginning, to become "nothing" again, in order to grow anew with fresh strength.

For women in particular, Rosh Chodesh offers a powerful message: Your faith matters. Your steadfastness makes a difference. When others waver, your inner certainty sustains not just yourself but entire generations. The holiday that was given to you as a reward three thousand years ago remains yours today — a monthly reminder of your unique spiritual strength.

## Key Takeaways

- **Rosh Chodesh** means "head of the month" and marks the beginning of each new Hebrew month, timed with the appearance of the new moon.

- The **first commandment** God gave to the Jewish people as a nation was to sanctify the new month — giving us the power to define and sanctify time itself.

- The **moon symbolizes the Jewish people**: just as the moon waxes and wanes but always renews itself, the Jewish people have faced countless difficulties but always survived and flourished again.

- **Rosh Chodesh is particularly special for women**, given as a reward because women refused to participate in creating the Golden Calf. Their steadfast faith represents the deep, instinctive belief that has sustained the Jewish people throughout history.

- We observe Rosh Chodesh with **special prayers** (Ya'aleh V'yavo, abbreviated Hallel, Musaf), **Torah reading**, and customs like making a **slightly festive meal** and greeting others with **"Chodesh Tov!"**

- Women traditionally **refrain from heavy household work** on Rosh Chodesh and gather for special Torah study and celebration.

- The day teaches us that **renewal is always possible** — even after the darkest disappearance, the light returns.
