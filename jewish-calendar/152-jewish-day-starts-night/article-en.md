In the rhythm of Jewish time, something profound emerges that sets it apart from the common understanding of when a day begins. While most of the modern world marks midnight as the start of a new day — a convention established by Roman timekeeping and standardized by medieval European clocks — Jewish tradition begins each day at sunset, when the first stars appear in the evening sky.

This isn't merely an alternative convention, a quirk of ancient record-keeping that persists out of tradition. Rather, it reflects a fundamentally different understanding of time, transformation, and the relationship between darkness and light. It shapes when holidays begin, how Shabbat is experienced, and even how Jews conceptualize spiritual growth and personal change.

## The Biblical Foundation

The source of this practice lies in the very first chapter of the Torah, in the account of Creation that takes place approximately 5,784 years ago according to Jewish tradition (corresponding to approximately 3760 BCE, during what would be China's legendary period before the Xia Dynasty, 夏朝前期). After each day of divine creative work, the text repeats a distinctive phrase:

"*Vayehi erev, vayehi voker, yom echad*" — "And there was evening, and there was morning—one day" (Genesis 1:5).

The pattern continues throughout the Creation narrative: "And there was evening, and there was morning—a second day... a third day... a fourth day..." The Hebrew text consistently places evening (*erev*, ערב) before morning (*boker*, בקר), establishing a fundamental pattern that would shape Jewish understanding of time for millennia.

This sequence is not accidental or merely poetic. The Torah deliberately structures each creative day as beginning with evening and culminating in morning. When God created light on the first day, the text describes it emerging *from* primordial darkness: "And darkness was upon the face of the deep... And God said, 'Let there be light'" (Genesis 1:2-3). Light doesn't replace darkness; it transforms the darkness into something purposeful, creating the rhythm of evening-and-morning.

The Jewish sages noticed that the Torah never uses the word "night" (*lailah*, לילה) during the Creation account when describing the daily cycle. Instead, it uses "evening" (*erev*), which etymologically relates to mixture or blending. Evening represents the transitional zone where day and night intermingle, where endings and beginnings blur—the perfect starting point for understanding each day.

## The Spiritual Significance of Beginning in Darkness

The evening-first calendar carries profound spiritual and philosophical meaning. Beginning the day at night suggests that our highest spiritual work, our deepest growth, and our most significant transformations often happen in darkness — in times of uncertainty, challenge, hiddenness, or the absence of clarity.

Just as seeds germinate in the dark earth before breaking through to sunlight, and just as human life begins in the darkness of the womb before emerging into the world, spiritual growth frequently begins in life's darker moments. The day begins in darkness because that is where possibility lives, where potential gestates, where the unformed slowly takes shape.

This understanding radically transforms how we approach difficulty. Rather than seeing nighttime, struggle, confusion, or the absence of clarity as interruptions to our spiritual journey or obstacles to overcome before "real life" can begin, Jewish wisdom recognizes them as the very foundation from which growth emerges. The day begins in darkness because transformation requires incubation, and incubation requires the protective cover of hiddenness.

Rabbi Nachman of Breslov (1772-1810), one of Hasidism's great mystics living during what corresponds to China's late Qing Dynasty period (清朝后期), taught: "There is no night so dark that it does not lead to morning." But he also taught the inverse: there is no morning so bright that it doesn't emerge from night. The sequence matters. Light gains its meaning precisely because it conquers darkness; hope gains its power precisely because it emerges from despair.

The Kabbalah (Jewish mysticism) further develops this insight. The *Zohar*, the central kabbalistic text, explains that the evening-beginning represents *tzimtzum* — divine contraction or concealment. God withdraws, creating space for creation to exist independently. Only through this initial concealment can revelation eventually occur. Night represents divine hiddenness; day represents divine revelation. But hiddenness must come first, because without space created by withdrawal, there can be no independent existence to experience revelation.

## Ancient Parallel Wisdom: Chinese Philosophical Resonance

This evening-first calendar finds fascinating parallels in ancient Chinese philosophical thinking, particularly in the development of Yin-Yang theory during the Zhou Dynasty (周朝, 1046-256 BCE) — roughly contemporary with the period when the Jewish understanding of the evening-first day was becoming formalized in practice.

Chinese sages understood that apparent opposites were actually complementary forces forming a unified whole. Yin (阴), associated with darkness, receptivity, rest, potential, and inwardness, was not inferior to Yang (阳), associated with light, action, activity, actualization, and outwardness. Both were necessary for cosmic harmony and natural cycles.

The *Tao Te Ching* (道德经), attributed to Laozi (老子, approximately 6th century BCE), teaches: "Being and non-being produce each other; difficult and easy complete each other; long and short contrast each other..." This principle of complementary opposites mirrors the Jewish insight that evening and morning aren't competing states but complementary phases of a unified day.

The ancient Chinese calendrical systems developed during the Han Dynasty (汉朝, 206 BCE - 220 CE) incorporated sophisticated understanding of dark and light cycles, lunar phases, and solar seasons. Their recognition that transformation happens in hidden phases — that the new moon exists in darkness before gradually revealing itself, that winter's dormancy precedes spring's growth — aligns remarkably with Jewish insight about beginning each day in evening's embrace.

The I Ching (易经, *Book of Changes*) contains a hexagram called "Darkness" or "Obscurity" (明夷, Míng Yí), which teaches that in times of difficulty or darkness, the wise person conceals their light, remaining inwardly illuminated while outwardly adapting. This parallels the Jewish idea that the day's beginning in darkness doesn't mean the absence of light, but rather light in potential, waiting to be revealed.

## When Exactly Does the Day Begin?

The practical determination of when evening transitions into the new day has generated detailed halachic (Jewish legal) discussion. The Talmud identifies several possible transition points:

**Shkiah (שקיעה)** — The moment of sunset when the sun's disk disappears below the horizon. Some authorities consider this the day's official beginning.

**Tzait hakochavim (צאת הכוכבים)** — "The emergence of the stars," specifically when three medium-sized stars become visible in the sky. This marks the definitive end of one day and beginning of the next.

**Bein hashmashot (בין השמשות)** — "Between the suns," the twilight period between sunset and nightfall. This in-between time has uncertain status: is it still the previous day, already the next day, or perhaps both/neither?

Different Jewish communities and authorities adopt varying stringencies. Some begin Shabbat and holidays at sunset (*shkiah*); others wait until star-emergence (*tzait*). The most common practice is to add extra time before sunset as a buffer, ensuring the holy day begins before any possible dispute.

This attention to precise transition moments reflects Jewish legal precision, but also contains spiritual wisdom: the most significant transformations happen in liminal spaces, in thresholds, in the uncertain zones between defined states. *Bein hashmashot* represents life's transitional moments—times of change, uncertainty, and possibility.

## Practical Implications for Jewish Life

This timing affects Jewish religious practice in concrete and meaningful ways:

**Shabbat begins on Friday evening**, not Saturday morning. Jewish families light candles at sunset Friday, immediately transforming ordinary time into sacred time. This means Shabbat preparation happens during Friday daylight — cooking, cleaning, setting the table — while the actual beginning occurs as the world settles into evening's quiet. The transition from work to rest, from secular to sacred, happens communally as families gather for Friday night dinner.

**Holidays commence at sunset**, creating seamless transitions from ordinary time into sacred time. Passover Seders begin after nightfall. Yom Kippur's 25-hour fast begins at sunset. Chanukah candles are lit as darkness falls. This synchronizes the emotional experience (moving from light into darkness, then kindling lights against the darkness) with the theological meaning (bringing divine light into the world's darkness).

**Fast days begin and end at specific star-emergence times**, not arbitrary clock hours. This connects observance to natural cycles rather than mechanical time, maintaining awareness of our place within creation's rhythms.

**The daily prayer cycle** reflects this rhythm. *Ma'ariv* (evening prayer) is the first prayer of the new day, followed by *Shacharit* (morning prayer) and *Mincha* (afternoon prayer). We begin our day addressing God in darkness before engaging the world in daylight.

**The counting of ritual days** follows this pattern. The seven days of mourning (*shiva*), the counting of the Omer period between Passover and Shavuot, and the weekly Sabbath all begin at night and continue through the following daylight.

## Light Emerging from Darkness: The Deeper Pattern

The deeper teaching suggests that light is most meaningful when it emerges from darkness. A flame is most visible against the night sky; placed in bright sunlight, the same flame becomes nearly invisible. Hope is most precious when it develops during difficulty; hope that emerges from comfortable circumstances lacks the weight and depth of hope born in crisis.

The Jewish day begins in evening because the most profound transformations often begin in life's darker passages. This principle extends far beyond daily rhythm into life's broader patterns:

**Personal growth** — Many people report that their most significant spiritual insights emerged during difficult periods: illness, loss, failure, confusion. These "dark nights of the soul" often catalyze deeper understanding and more authentic commitment than comfortable times.

**Historical redemption** — Jewish tradition teaches that redemption will emerge from exile, light from darkness, freedom from slavery. The Exodus from Egypt began at midnight, the darkest point. The future redemption, according to prophecy, will emerge from the darkness of exile.

**Creative work** — Artists, writers, and thinkers across cultures report that creative inspiration often comes during darkness, in dreams, in the quiet of night when distractions cease and the unconscious mind can work.

**National resilience** — Many of Judaism's greatest insights, texts, and innovations emerged during periods of exile, persecution, or challenge. The Talmud was compiled during Roman oppression. Kabbalah flourished during medieval expulsions. Modern Jewish thought developed during and after the Holocaust. Darkness became the womb of renewal.

## A Different Relationship to Time

The evening-first day creates a fundamentally different relationship to time than the midnight-start convention. In the modern midnight-based system, we typically:

1. Begin a day (at midnight, while asleep)
2. Wake up and engage in daytime activities
3. End the day by going to sleep

This creates a psychological pattern where sleep is the ending, the conclusion, the period of non-productivity that closes the day.

In the Jewish evening-first system, the pattern shifts:

1. Begin the day with evening prayer and family time
2. Carry the day's intention into sleep
3. Wake up into the day's continuation
4. Engage in daytime activities as the unfolding of what began last night

This makes sleep part of the day's work rather than its conclusion. Sleep becomes incubation time, processing time, renewal time — an active component of the day rather than dead time between days. Dreams, which occur during sleep, become part of the day's spiritual experience rather than interruptions to consciousness.

The Talmud teaches that sleep is "one-sixtieth of death," a taste of the world to come. Placing sleep at the day's beginning rather than ending transforms it from a small death that closes experience into a small death that births renewal.

## Marking Time, Understanding Life

The Jewish day starts at night because wisdom recognizes that every ending prepares the ground for beginning, and every beginning honors what came before. In this rhythm, we find not just a way of marking time, but a way of understanding life itself — as a continuous movement from potential to actualization, from hiddenness to revelation, from evening to morning, always beginning again in hope.

When you light Shabbat candles as the sun sets Friday evening, you're not just marking time. You're participating in the cosmic rhythm established at Creation. You're affirming that sacred time begins in darkness, that rest precedes activity, that divine concealment creates space for divine revelation.

The next time you experience a difficult period — a dark night of uncertainty, confusion, or pain — remember that according to Jewish wisdom, this might not be an interruption to your journey but its very beginning. The day starts at night. Transformation begins in darkness. And the light, when it comes, will be all the more meaningful because it emerged from the dark.

## Key Takeaways

- The **Jewish day begins at sunset** (when stars appear), not at midnight, based on Genesis: "And there was evening, and there was morning"
- Beginning in **darkness reflects the spiritual principle** that growth, transformation, and redemption often emerge from difficult or hidden periods
- This creates a **different rhythm** for Jewish practice: Shabbat begins Friday evening, holidays start at sunset, sleep becomes part of the day rather than its conclusion
- The pattern resonates with **Yin-Yang philosophy** and Chinese calendar wisdom recognizing complementary opposites and transformation in hidden phases
- The evening-first day teaches that **light is most meaningful when it emerges from darkness**, that endings contain new beginnings
