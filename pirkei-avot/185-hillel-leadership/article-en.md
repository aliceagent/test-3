# In a Place Where There Are No Leaders, Strive to Be a Leader

## The Teaching

---

*"Bemakom she'ein anashim, hishtadel lihyot ish."*  
"In a place where there are no leaders, strive to be a leader."  
— Hillel, Pirkei Avot 2:5

This brief statement from Hillel the Elder contains a profound challenge: when you find yourself in a situation where leadership is absent, don't wait for someone else to step forward. You must rise to the occasion.

## Understanding the Hebrew

The word *anashim* literally means "men" or "people," but in this context it carries the connotation of ethical people, leaders, or those who take responsibility. The word *ish* (singular of *anashim*) means more than just "a man" — it means a person of substance, integrity, and action.

Hillel is not talking about power-hungry self-promotion. He's describing a moment when moral leadership is desperately needed but conspicuously absent. In that vacuum, someone must act — and that someone might have to be you.

## When Leadership Is Missing

We've all encountered situations where no one is willing to take charge:

- A community project languishes because everyone assumes someone else will organize it
- An injustice occurs in plain sight, but bystanders hope others will intervene
- A crisis emerges at work or in your neighborhood, and everyone looks around waiting for a hero
- A family faces a difficult decision, but no one wants the burden of making the call

Hillel's teaching addresses precisely these moments. He recognizes that leadership isn't always about formal titles or elected positions. Sometimes it's about recognizing a need and choosing to meet it.

## The Weight of Responsibility

Why don't people step up more often? Usually because leadership is hard:

- **Fear of failure**: What if I do it wrong?
- **Fear of criticism**: People will judge my decisions
- **Impostor syndrome**: I'm not qualified enough
- **Comfort in anonymity**: It's easier to blend in than stand out
- **Hope someone else will**: Surely there's someone better suited

These fears are real, and Hillel doesn't dismiss them. But his teaching suggests that when the alternative is moral vacuum, personal hesitation becomes a luxury we cannot afford.

## Not Just Any Leadership

Hillel's statement comes with an important qualifier embedded in Jewish thought: the leadership he's advocating must align with Torah values. He's not saying "grab power whenever you can." Rather, he's saying: when ethical guidance is needed and absent, provide it.

This means:

- Leading with humility, not ego
- Serving the community's needs, not your own ambitions
- Making decisions based on Jewish values and wisdom
- Being willing to step back when appropriate leadership emerges
- Accepting criticism gracefully and learning from mistakes

The Talmud teaches that true leaders don't seek honor — honor seeks them. Hillel himself embodied this principle, living in poverty while leading the Sanhedrin, always putting service above status.

## A Chinese Parallel: The Mandate of Heaven

There's a fascinating parallel in Chinese philosophy regarding leadership and moral responsibility. The concept of *Tianming* (天命), the Mandate of Heaven, held that legitimate rule required virtue and moral authority. When rulers became corrupt or neglectful, they lost the Mandate, creating a moral imperative for new leadership to emerge.

Similarly, Confucius taught about the *junzi* (君子), the "superior person" or "gentleman," who acts with virtue regardless of formal position. The *junzi* takes moral responsibility not because of rank but because of character. When society lacks ethical guidance, the *junzi* must provide it.

Both traditions recognize that moral leadership is not merely about power structures — it's about individuals choosing to embody and uphold ethical principles when others will not.

## Historical Examples

Jewish history provides powerful examples of ordinary people rising to extraordinary leadership when circumstances demanded it:

**Nachshon ben Aminadav**: When the Israelites stood at the Red Sea with the Egyptian army approaching, everyone froze in fear. According to the Midrash, Nachshon didn't wait for Moses to act — he walked into the water up to his nostrils. Only then did the sea split. His willingness to lead through action saved the nation.

**Esther**: A young Jewish woman in the Persian palace could have remained silent when Haman plotted genocide. She had every reason to protect herself. But when Mordechai reminded her, "Who knows whether you have attained royalty for such a time as this?" she chose to act. "If I perish, I perish," she declared — and she saved her people.

**The Maccabees**: When the majority of Jews were assimilating under Greek rule and the priesthood had become corrupt, a family of rural priests from Modi'in chose to resist. They had no army, no resources, no obvious path to victory. But they saw a spiritual crisis with no leaders addressing it, so they became those leaders.

These weren't people seeking power. They were people recognizing a moral emergency and choosing not to be bystanders.

## Modern Applications

How does this ancient teaching apply to our lives today?

**In Your Community**: Maybe your local Jewish community lacks programming for young families, or elderly members are isolated, or new immigrants need support. You could wait for the synagogue board to address it, or you could start a monthly gathering in your home.

**At Work**: Perhaps you notice unethical practices being normalized, or talented colleagues being overlooked, or a culture of silence around problems. Speaking up feels risky — but Hillel reminds us that silence in the face of wrong is its own kind of failure.

**In Your Family**: When aging parents need care coordination and siblings are overwhelmed or distant, someone has to step into the organizing role. It may not be convenient, but the alternative is that vulnerable people suffer.

**In Society**: When you witness injustice, discrimination, or suffering, the natural human response is to assume someone else will act. Police will handle it. Social services will intervene. Someone more qualified will step up. But sometimes you are the most qualified person present — not because of credentials, but because you're there and you care.

## The Balance: When Not to Lead

Judaism also teaches that there's wisdom in knowing when *not* to lead. The Talmud warns against seeking positions of authority (Avot 1:10): "Love work, hate authority, and do not become familiar with the government."

So how do we balance Hillel's call to leadership with the warning against power-seeking?

The key is intention and context:

- **Lead when there's a vacuum**, not when you're displacing capable leaders
- **Lead to serve**, not to elevate yourself
- **Lead when you have relevant knowledge or skills**, not just to be in charge
- **Lead temporarily**, and gladly hand off responsibility when others can carry it
- **Lead collaboratively**, inviting others to join rather than monopolizing control

Hillel himself modeled this perfectly. He became the head of the Sanhedrin not through ambition but through demonstrated wisdom and humility. He famously said, "If I am not for myself, who will be for me? But if I am only for myself, what am I?" Leadership requires self-confidence balanced with selflessness.

## Building the Courage to Lead

If you recognize a situation that needs leadership but feel inadequate to the task, consider these steps:

**1. Start Small**: You don't need to solve everything at once. Address one specific need. Organize one event. Help one person. Leadership is built through action, not grand declarations.

**2. Seek Wisdom**: Consult with mentors, read relevant texts, pray for guidance. Jewish tradition encourages seeking counsel before major decisions. Leadership doesn't mean going it alone.

**3. Invite Collaboration**: Say, "I'm willing to organize this, but I need help." Often others are waiting for someone to initiate. Your willingness to lead gives them permission to contribute.

**4. Accept Imperfection**: You will make mistakes. That's guaranteed. What matters is whether you learn from them and keep trying. The Talmud teaches that Torah scholars increase peace in the world even as they argue — because they're committed to truth-seeking, not ego-protection.

**5. Remember Your Why**: You're not stepping up for glory or recognition. You're stepping up because something important needs doing and you're in a position to help. Keep that motivation clear.

## The Reward and the Challenge

Hillel's teaching doesn't promise that leadership will be easy or that you'll be thanked. In fact, Jewish leaders throughout history have often faced criticism, exhaustion, and loneliness. Moses himself tried to resign multiple times, overwhelmed by the complaints and demands of the people.

But there's a deeper reward: the knowledge that you showed up when it mattered. That you didn't let fear or comfort keep you on the sidelines while something important hung in the balance. That you became the kind of person who takes responsibility rather than waiting for others to carry the burden.

The Talmud teaches: "Where there is Torah, there are no leaders; where there are no leaders, make yourself into Torah." In other words, in the absence of external guidance, internalize wisdom and become a living example of it.

## A Personal Challenge

As you go through your week, notice the small vacuums of leadership around you:

- The meeting where no one wants to volunteer
- The family chat where a difficult topic needs addressing
- The community problem everyone complains about but no one tackles
- The moment when someone vulnerable needs an advocate

In at least one of those situations, be the person who steps forward. Not because you're perfect or fully qualified, but because you're willing. Because you understand that leadership isn't about having all the answers — it's about caring enough to try.

Hillel's teaching is both a challenge and an encouragement. It says: Yes, leadership is hard. Yes, you might feel inadequate. But if not you, then who? And if you wait for perfect circumstances or perfect confidence, you may wait forever.

The world doesn't need perfect leaders. It needs people who, in the absence of leadership, choose to lead.

## Conclusion

"In a place where there are no leaders, strive to be a leader."

This isn't a teaching about ambition. It's a teaching about responsibility. About recognizing that sometimes you are the answer to the prayer you've been praying. That the leader you've been waiting for might need to be you.

Not because you're better than others, but because you're present, you're aware, and you have the ability to act.

The Hebrew word *hishtadel* — "strive" — is active and effortful. Hillel doesn't say it will come naturally or easily. He says you must try, must work at it, must push through doubt and discomfort.

Because in the end, a community without leadership isn't just lacking direction. It's lacking people who care enough to provide it. And you can be the person who cares.
