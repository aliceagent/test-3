# If I Am Not for Myself, Who Will Be for Me? — Hillel's Three Questions

One of the most famous teachings in all of Jewish wisdom literature comes from Hillel the Elder, a sage who lived around 2,000 years ago during the time of King Herod and the Second Temple. In Pirkei Avot (Ethics of the Fathers) 1:14, Hillel poses three powerful questions that have guided Jewish ethical thinking ever since:

> "If I am not for myself, who will be for me? And when I am for myself, what am I? And if not now, when?"

These three questions appear simple on the surface, but they contain profound wisdom about how to live a balanced, meaningful life. Let's explore each question and discover what Hillel is teaching us.

## Question One: "If I Am Not for Myself, Who Will Be for Me?"

At first glance, this question seems to be about self-interest. Hillel is telling us that we must take responsibility for our own lives, our own growth, and our own spiritual development. No one else can do the work of living your life for you.

### The Principle of Self-Responsibility

In Jewish thought, every person has unique potential and a unique mission in this world. The Talmud teaches that when we die, God will ask us, "Did you fulfill your potential?" Not someone else's potential—*your* potential. This means that self-care, self-development, and self-advocacy are not selfish—they are essential religious responsibilities.

Think of it this way: If you don't take care of your own health, who will exercise for you? If you don't pursue your own education, who will learn on your behalf? If you don't work on your own character flaws, who will do that inner work for you?

This teaching acknowledges a fundamental truth: You are the primary agent in your own life. While we need teachers, friends, and community, ultimately, the work of becoming who you're meant to be rests on your shoulders.

### A Chinese Parallel: Self-Cultivation in Confucian Thought

This concept resonates deeply with traditional Chinese philosophy. Confucius taught that self-cultivation (修身, *xiūshēn*) is the foundation of all moral development. In the *Great Learning* (*Daxue*), one of the Four Books of Confucianism, we read that bringing order to one's family, state, and world begins with cultivating oneself.

Similarly, Mencius emphasized that every person has innate goodness and the responsibility to develop it. Just as Hillel asks, "If I am not for myself, who will be for me?" Confucian teaching asks us to take charge of our own moral development. No sage can do this work for you—you must cultivate virtue within yourself.

Both traditions recognize that self-development is not narcissism; it's the necessary starting point for contributing to society.

## Question Two: "And When I Am for Myself, What Am I?"

Just when we might think Hillel is advocating pure individualism, he immediately balances his first question with a second one. Yes, you must take responsibility for yourself—but if you *only* focus on yourself, what kind of person are you?

### The Danger of Pure Self-Interest

The second question warns against excessive self-centeredness. A life lived only for oneself is ultimately empty and meaningless. Judaism teaches that we are created in God's image not only as individuals but as members of a community. We exist in relationship—to God, to our fellow human beings, to the Jewish people, and to all of humanity.

The Torah is filled with commandments that require us to look beyond ourselves: giving charity (tzedakah), loving our neighbor, welcoming strangers, visiting the sick, comforting mourners, and pursuing justice. These mitzvot remind us that being "for ourselves" cannot be the end of the story.

Rabbi Jonathan Sacks explained this paradox beautifully: "We are called to be both individuals and members of a community. We must have the courage to stand alone when necessary, but also the humility to stand together when needed."

### The Middle Way

Hillel's two questions together create a tension, a balance. You must care for yourself, but not *only* for yourself. You must develop your own potential, but use it to contribute to something greater than yourself. This is not a contradiction—it's a dynamic balance that must be maintained throughout life.

Think of an orchestra: Each musician must practice their instrument alone, perfecting their own technique (question one). But the purpose of all that individual practice is to contribute to the collective beauty of the symphony (question two). Both are necessary; neither alone is sufficient.

### Balance in Chinese Philosophy

This balance between self and society is also central to Chinese thought. The concept of *zhong yong* (中庸), or the "Doctrine of the Mean," teaches that virtue lies in finding the appropriate balance between extremes. Neither pure selfishness nor complete self-negation is the path of wisdom.

The ancient Chinese philosopher Mozi (墨子), who lived around the same time as Hillel, taught "universal love" (*jian ai*, 兼爱), arguing that people should care for others as they care for themselves. While Confucians emphasized graded love (stronger obligations to family, then community, then all humanity), both schools agreed that concern for others is essential to moral life.

Hillel's teaching parallels this: Cultivate yourself, but direct that cultivation outward in service to others.

## Question Three: "And If Not Now, When?"

The third question introduces the element of time. Even if we understand the balance between self-care and service to others, when should we begin? Hillel's answer is clear: Now. Not tomorrow, not next year, not when you're "ready"—now.

### The Urgency of Moral Action

Jewish tradition teaches that procrastination in spiritual matters is dangerous. The Talmud warns, "Do not say, 'When I have free time I will study,' for you may never have free time" (Pirkei Avot 2:4). Life is finite, and opportunities pass. The person you could help today may not be there tomorrow. The character trait you need to fix may cause harm if you delay.

This urgency doesn't mean we should be reckless or hasty. Rather, it means we shouldn't deceive ourselves with excuses: "I'm too busy," "I'm not ready," "I'll start next month." These are often masks for fear, laziness, or complacency.

The Talmud tells us that the Evil Inclination (yetzer hara) has a strategy: It doesn't tell you not to do a mitzvah. Instead, it whispers, "Just wait until tomorrow." And tomorrow becomes next week, next month, next year—and the opportunity is lost.

### Seizing the Present Moment

There's a Chassidic story about a student who asked his Rebbe, "When should I repent?" The Rebbe answered, "On the last day of your life." The student protested, "But I don't know when that will be!" The Rebbe smiled: "Exactly. So you should repent today."

This is Hillel's wisdom: The present moment is all we truly have. Whatever you need to do—whether it's beginning to study Torah, making amends with someone you've hurt, starting to give charity, or taking better care of your health—begin today.

### The Present Moment in Chinese Wisdom

This emphasis on the present moment appears in Chinese philosophy as well. The *I Ching* (易经, *Yijing*, Book of Changes) teaches that every moment has its appropriate action, its *kairos*. Confucius spoke of *shi* (时), the right timing for action, emphasizing that wisdom includes knowing when to act.

The Chan (Zen) Buddhist tradition, which developed in China, places enormous emphasis on present-moment awareness. While this comes from a different spiritual tradition than Judaism, the practical wisdom is similar: Don't postpone what matters. Act when the moment calls for action.

## Putting the Three Questions Together

When we read Hillel's three questions as a unified teaching, we discover a complete philosophy of ethical living:

1. **Take responsibility for your own life and growth.** Don't wait for others to do the work that only you can do.

2. **Use your growth to serve others.** Personal development is not an end in itself—it's preparation for contributing to the world.

3. **Begin now.** Don't postpone what matters. Time is finite, and opportunities don't wait.

### A Practical Framework

These three questions provide a framework for making decisions and evaluating your life:

- **When facing a challenge**: Ask yourself Hillel's first question. Am I taking appropriate responsibility, or am I waiting for someone else to solve this for me?

- **When experiencing success**: Ask the second question. Am I using my talents, resources, and opportunities only for myself, or am I contributing to my community and to God's purposes in the world?

- **When postponing something important**: Ask the third question. Why am I waiting? What am I afraid of? If this matters, why not begin today?

## Living Hillel's Questions Today

How can we apply Hillel's wisdom in our modern lives? Here are some practical examples:

### In Your Jewish Journey

If you're exploring Judaism or considering conversion, Hillel's questions speak directly to you:

- **Question 1**: Take ownership of your Jewish education. Find a teacher, buy books, listen to lectures. No one can learn for you.

- **Question 2**: As you learn, think about how you can contribute to the Jewish community. How will your unique background and talents enrich Jewish life?

- **Question 3**: Don't wait until you "know enough" to start practicing. Begin with one mitzvah—light Shabbat candles, say a blessing, study one page of Torah. Start today.

### In Your Career

- **Question 1**: Take responsibility for your professional development. Acquire skills, seek mentorship, pursue excellence.

- **Question 2**: Use your career to make a positive impact. How does your work serve others? How can you use your position to increase kindness and justice?

- **Question 3**: Don't postpone difficult conversations, necessary changes, or opportunities for growth. Act when the time is right.

### In Your Relationships

- **Question 1**: Take responsibility for your own emotional health and communication patterns. Work on yourself.

- **Question 2**: Invest in others. Be present, listen deeply, offer help without being asked.

- **Question 3**: Don't delay saying "I'm sorry," "I love you," or "I forgive you." Repair relationships while there's still time.

## The Enduring Wisdom of Hillel

Nearly two thousand years after Hillel posed these three questions, they remain as relevant as ever. In a world that often pushes us toward either extreme individualism or self-negating collectivism, Hillel offers a balanced path. In a culture of procrastination and distraction, he reminds us that the present moment is all we truly have.

These aren't questions that can be answered once and forgotten. They're questions we must ask ourselves regularly, perhaps even daily:

- Am I taking appropriate responsibility for my life?
- Am I using my gifts to serve others?
- What important thing am I postponing that I should begin today?

The beauty of Hillel's teaching is that it combines idealism with realism, individual responsibility with communal care, and timeless wisdom with practical urgency. It's a teaching that grows with you—each time you return to it, you'll find new depths and new applications.

May we all find the wisdom to stand up for ourselves while standing together with others, and may we find the courage to begin today.

## Questions for Reflection

1. In what areas of your life are you waiting for someone else to take responsibility that truly belongs to you?

2. When you think about your achievements and ambitions, how much are they directed toward your own benefit versus serving others?

3. What important action have you been postponing? What would it look like to begin today, even in a small way?

4. How do you maintain balance between self-care and service to others? Where do you tend to lean too far in one direction?

5. If you were to write your own three questions for living a meaningful life, what would they be?
