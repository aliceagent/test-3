# Who is Strong? One Who Conquers Their Inclination

## The Teaching

---

In *Pirkei Avot* (Ethics of the Fathers) 4:1, Ben Zoma asks four profound questions about the ideal human being. Among them: **"Who is strong? One who conquers their inclination."** (*Eizehu gibor? HaKovesh et yitzro.*)

At first glance, this seems counterintuitive. Shouldn't strength be measured by physical power, military victories, or the ability to dominate others? Yet Ben Zoma redirects our attention inward, teaching that true strength lies not in conquering external enemies, but in mastering the most formidable opponent we will ever face: ourselves.

## Understanding the Yetzer Hara

In Jewish thought, every person possesses two internal drives:

1. **Yetzer HaTov** — The good inclination, which pulls us toward kindness, truth, and holiness
2. **Yetzer HaRa** — The evil inclination, which tempts us toward selfishness, instant gratification, and wrongdoing

Importantly, the *yetzer hara* is not inherently evil. The Talmud (Yoma 69b) teaches that without it, no one would build a house, marry, or pursue any form of achievement. It's the drive for self-preservation, ambition, and desire. The challenge is not to eliminate it, but to channel it constructively.

Ben Zoma's teaching addresses this struggle. The person who can redirect their impulses, resist temptation when necessary, and choose the harder right over the easier wrong — this is the truly strong individual.

## What Does "Conquering" Mean?

The Hebrew word *kovesh* (conquers) is carefully chosen. It doesn't mean "destroys" or "eliminates." Instead, it suggests subjugation — bringing something powerful under control while allowing it to continue existing.

Think of a river. Unchecked, a river can flood, destroy crops, and devastate communities. But when properly channeled through irrigation systems, that same river becomes the source of life, agriculture, and prosperity. So too with our inclinations: the goal is redirection, not annihilation.

This ongoing struggle requires:

- **Self-awareness**: Recognizing when your impulses are leading you astray
- **Discipline**: Building habits that support your values, even when difficult  
- **Persistence**: Understanding that this battle is lifelong, with daily victories and setbacks

## Chinese Parallels: The Inner Sage and the Inner Beast

This concept resonates deeply with classical Chinese philosophy. Confucius taught about *克己复礼* (*kè jǐ fù lǐ*) — "Restraining the self and returning to propriety." The character *克* (to overcome, to conquer) is the same word Ben Zoma uses for mastering one's inclinations.

Mencius (Mengzi), the great Confucian philosopher, spoke of the *四端* (*sì duān*) — the four sprouts of moral virtue that exist within everyone. He taught that cultivating these sprouts while restraining base desires is the path to becoming a *君子* (*jūnzǐ*), a person of noble character. This mirrors the Jewish concept of strengthening the *yetzer hatov* to govern the *yetzer hara*.

In the *Daodejing*, Laozi writes: *"胜人者有力,胜己者强"* — "Those who conquer others have force; those who conquer themselves have strength." This is nearly identical to Ben Zoma's teaching, showing how universal this wisdom truly is.

## The Challenge of Modern Life

If anything, Ben Zoma's words are more relevant today than ever. Modern society presents unprecedented temptations:

- **Instant gratification**: Everything from food delivery to entertainment is available at the touch of a button
- **Digital distraction**: Social media and smartphones constantly pull our attention away from meaningful work
- **Consumerism**: Advertising relentlessly tells us we need more, we deserve more, we must have more
- **Moral compromises**: Situations where the "easy" path involves cutting corners, bending rules, or looking the other way

Every day, we face countless micro-decisions: Will I hit snooze or get up? Will I scroll mindlessly or study? Will I speak words of kindness or gossip? Will I tell an uncomfortable truth or a convenient lie?

Each choice is a battle. Each victory strengthens our character. Each defeat offers a chance to learn.

## Practical Strategies for Conquering Your Inclination

Jewish tradition offers concrete tools for this lifelong work:

### 1. **Small, Consistent Victories**

The Talmud (Kiddushin 30b) states: "If that disgusting creature (*yetzer hara*) encounters you, drag it to the study hall." When temptation strikes, immediately redirect your attention to something holy — Torah study, prayer, or an act of kindness.

Start with manageable challenges. If you struggle with anger, begin by pausing for three seconds before responding. If you're prone to gossip, practice one day a week of especially careful speech. Small wins compound over time.

### 2. **Environmental Design**

Make good choices easier and bad choices harder. Joseph the Righteous, when tempted by Potiphar's wife, literally ran from the house (Genesis 39:12). He didn't rely on willpower alone — he removed himself from the situation entirely.

In practice: Delete apps that waste your time. Keep unhealthy foods out of your home. Surround yourself with people who elevate you rather than drag you down.

### 3. **Accountability and Community**

*Pirkei Avot* itself advises: "Make for yourself a teacher, acquire for yourself a friend" (1:6). Share your struggles with a trusted mentor or study partner. External accountability transforms private battles into supported growth.

### 4. **Daily Reflection**

Many Jewish traditions include a *cheshbon hanefesh* — an accounting of the soul. Before sleep, review your day: Where did you succeed in mastering your inclinations? Where did you stumble? What will you work on tomorrow?

This practice, done with honesty and self-compassion (not self-flagellation), builds increasing self-awareness and moral strength.

### 5. **Celebrate Progress, Not Perfection**

The Talmud (Berachot 34b) teaches that "in the place where penitents stand, even the completely righteous cannot stand." Your struggles, when met with sincere effort, are themselves holy. Don't aim for perfection; aim for consistent growth.

## The Paradox: Strength Through Vulnerability

There's a beautiful irony here. Admitting you have inclinations that need conquering requires humility. It means acknowledging: "I am not naturally perfect. I have impulses that pull me toward wrong. I must actively work to be good."

This admission — that you need to struggle — is itself an act of strength. The person who denies their *yetzer hara* is not strong; they're self-deceived. True power comes from honest self-assessment followed by determined action.

## Who is Weak?

Implicitly, Ben Zoma also answers the opposite question: Who is weak? One who is enslaved to their impulses. The person who cannot say "no" to their appetites. The individual who reacts rather than responds, who is tossed about by every desire and emotion like a boat without a rudder.

External strength without internal mastery is hollow. History is filled with powerful people — emperors, generals, billionaires — who were ultimately destroyed by their inability to control themselves: their lust, their rage, their greed, their pride.

## The Lifelong Journey

It's important to understand: this is not a battle you win once and then coast. It's a daily, sometimes hourly, effort. The Talmud (Sukkah 52b) describes the *yetzer hara* as growing stronger throughout life, not weaker. As we age, new challenges emerge: new temptations, new forms of pride, new opportunities to compromise.

But this is not discouraging — it's liberating. You're not expected to achieve perfection. You're called to strive, today, to be slightly better than you were yesterday. As Rabbi Tarfon teaches in the very next chapter of *Pirkei Avot*: "It is not your duty to complete the work, but neither are you free to desist from it" (2:16).

## A Word of Encouragement

If you're reading this and feeling discouraged by your failures, take heart. The very fact that you're engaged in this struggle means you're already practicing Ben Zoma's definition of strength. Every person who has achieved greatness in Torah and character has faced the same battles you face.

Moses, the greatest prophet, had to conquer his anger. Abraham had to overcome his inclination toward comfort and stay in the land of Canaan. David had to battle his yetzer for power and desire. The righteous are not those without struggle — they are those who struggle and persist.

## Conclusion: Redefining Strength

Ben Zoma's teaching invites us to completely reimagine what strength means. In a world that glorifies domination, accumulation, and control over others, Judaism points inward. The greatest victories are not won on battlefields but in the quiet moments when you choose discipline over indulgence, truth over comfort, kindness over cruelty.

This is available to everyone, regardless of physical ability, social status, or natural temperament. An elderly person choosing patience, a poor person resisting envy, a powerful person exercising restraint — each is demonstrating the highest form of strength.

The next time you face temptation — to anger, to laziness, to dishonesty, to despair — remember Ben Zoma's question. In that moment, you have the opportunity to be truly strong.

*Who is strong? One who conquers their inclination.*

The battlefield is within. The victory is yours to claim.
