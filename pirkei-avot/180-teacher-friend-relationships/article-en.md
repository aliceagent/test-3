# Make for Yourself a Teacher, Acquire for Yourself a Friend — The Power of Learning Relationships

## Introduction

---

In Pirkei Avot (Ethics of the Fathers) 1:6, Rabbi Yehoshua ben Perachya teaches: "Make for yourself a teacher, acquire for yourself a friend, and judge every person favorably." This seemingly simple instruction contains profound wisdom about how we grow as human beings and how we navigate the journey of learning Torah and living a meaningful Jewish life.

The teaching appears in the opening chapter of Pirkei Avot, a tractate of the Mishnah devoted entirely to ethical wisdom and character development. Unlike other parts of the Talmud that deal with ritual law or legal disputes, Pirkei Avot focuses on how we should conduct ourselves, how we should think, and how we should relate to others. This particular teaching addresses a fundamental truth: we cannot grow alone.

## Making Yourself a Teacher: The Active Pursuit of Guidance

The first part of the teaching instructs us to "make for yourself a teacher" (*aseh lecha rav*). Notice the language: not "find" a teacher, but "make" one. This subtle distinction reveals something important about the teacher-student relationship in Jewish tradition.

### The Student's Responsibility

The Hebrew word *aseh* means "make" or "do" — it's an active verb. You are not passively waiting for the perfect teacher to appear. You are actively taking responsibility for your own learning by seeking out someone who can guide you. This might mean:

- Approaching a rabbi or teacher and asking if they will study with you
- Committing to a regular learning schedule with someone more knowledgeable
- Being willing to travel to study with a particular teacher
- Making financial sacrifices to support your learning
- Opening yourself to receive correction and guidance, even when it's uncomfortable

In traditional Jewish communities, having a *rav* (rabbi or teacher) meant more than attending their lectures. It meant developing a personal relationship where you could ask questions about Jewish law, seek advice about life decisions, and receive guidance in your spiritual growth.

### What Makes Someone Your Teacher?

Your teacher doesn't have to be the most famous scholar or the most charismatic speaker. Your teacher is someone who:

- Knows more than you do in areas where you want to grow
- Cares about your development as a person
- Will tell you the truth, even when it's difficult to hear
- Lives according to the values they teach
- Makes time for your questions

In the Talmud, we see that even the greatest sages had teachers. Rabbi Akiva, one of the most celebrated scholars in Jewish history, didn't even begin learning until he was 40 years old. Yet he "made for himself" a teacher — Rabbi Eliezer — and through that relationship, transformed himself and ultimately influenced all of Jewish learning for generations.

### The Humility to Be Taught

Making yourself a teacher requires humility. It means admitting: "I don't know everything. I need guidance. I can learn from someone else." This is especially challenging in modern cultures that emphasize independence and self-sufficiency.

But Jewish wisdom recognizes that trying to figure everything out on your own is not independence — it's isolation. Real growth happens in relationship. Your teacher sees things you cannot see about yourself. They've traveled the path you're beginning to walk. They can warn you about pitfalls, encourage you when you're discouraged, and help you understand complex ideas.

## Acquiring a Friend: The Mutual Journey

The second instruction is to "acquire for yourself a friend" (*u'kneh lecha chaver*). Again, the language is striking. The word *kneh* means "acquire" or "purchase" — suggesting that gaining a true learning partner requires investment and effort.

### What Is a Learning Friend?

A *chaver* (friend) in this context isn't just someone you enjoy spending time with. It's someone who:

- Studies Torah with you regularly
- Challenges your thinking and helps you understand ideas more deeply
- Keeps you accountable to your commitments
- Grows alongside you
- Shares your values and goals

The Talmud is filled with pairs of learning partners: Hillel and Shammai, Rabbi Yochanan and Resh Lakish, Abaye and Rava. These pairs didn't always agree — in fact, disagreement was part of their friendship. They pushed each other to think more carefully, argue more rigorously, and understand more deeply.

### Why "Acquire" a Friend?

The word "acquire" suggests that friendship requires investment. This might mean:

- Initiating the relationship, even if it feels awkward
- Setting aside regular time to study together
- Being patient when your learning partner doesn't understand something immediately
- Accepting criticism and offering it gently
- Forgiving mistakes and misunderstandings
- Celebrating each other's growth and achievements

In Chinese tradition, there's a similar concept in the relationship between 學友 (*xuéyǒu*, study companions) — people who commit to learning together. Just as classical Chinese scholars would form study groups to prepare for imperial examinations, Jewish learning has always emphasized *chavruta* (study partnership), where two people learn together, questioning and explaining to one another.

### The Benefits of Learning with a Friend

Learning with a friend offers benefits that solitary study cannot provide:

1. **Clarity**: Explaining an idea to someone else forces you to understand it more clearly yourself.
2. **Correction**: Your friend catches mistakes you would miss on your own.
3. **Motivation**: It's harder to skip study sessions when someone is counting on you.
4. **Depth**: Debate and discussion reveal layers of meaning you wouldn't discover alone.
5. **Joy**: Shared learning creates shared memories and bonds that last a lifetime.

The Talmud states: "Either study with a partner or die" (Ta'anit 7a). This isn't literal — it's emphasizing that learning alone is a kind of intellectual death. Without the friction and spark of another mind engaging with yours, your thinking remains stagnant.

## The Difference Between Teacher and Friend

Why do we need both a teacher and a friend? Why isn't one enough?

### Your Teacher's Role

Your teacher provides:
- **Authority**: They can tell you definitively what Jewish law requires
- **Experience**: They've wrestled with questions you're just beginning to ask
- **Perspective**: They see patterns across many students and situations
- **Structure**: They know what you should learn and in what order

Your relationship with your teacher involves hierarchy. You listen more than you speak. You ask more than you answer. You receive more than you give. This isn't inequality — it's recognizing that wisdom flows from those who have it to those who seek it.

### Your Friend's Role

Your learning friend provides:
- **Equality**: You are partners, working together toward understanding
- **Freedom**: You can question, experiment, and explore without fear of judgment
- **Mutual growth**: You teach each other, learn from each other, and grow together
- **Accountability**: You keep each other honest and committed

With your friend, the relationship is balanced. You speak as much as you listen. You teach as much as you learn. You give as much as you receive.

### The Complementary Nature

Together, your teacher and your friend create a complete learning ecosystem:

- Your teacher tells you where to go; your friend walks beside you on the journey
- Your teacher provides the map; your friend helps you navigate the terrain
- Your teacher corrects your course; your friend encourages you to keep going
- Your teacher challenges you from above; your friend challenges you from alongside

## Judging Every Person Favorably

The third part of Rabbi Yehoshua's teaching — "judge every person favorably" (*hevei dan et kol ha'adam le'kaf zechut*) — might seem unrelated at first. But it's actually the foundation that makes the first two instructions possible.

### The Connection

If you cannot judge others favorably, you cannot have genuine learning relationships. Here's why:

- **With your teacher**: If you're always suspicious of their motives or dismissive of their knowledge, you won't be able to receive what they offer. You must give them the benefit of the doubt — trust that they care about your growth, that their correction comes from love, that their guidance is worth following.

- **With your friend**: If you interpret every challenge as an attack, every question as criticism, every disagreement as rejection, you cannot maintain a study partnership. You must assume your friend is operating in good faith — that their difficult questions are meant to help you think more clearly, not to embarrass you.

- **With everyone**: Even people who aren't your teacher or your designated study partner have wisdom to offer. If you judge everyone favorably, you can learn from anyone — from the person who challenges you, from the person who disagrees with you, even from the person who seems to have nothing to teach you.

The Chinese philosopher Confucius taught something similar: "When I walk along with two others, they serve as my teachers. I pick out the good points of one and imitate them, and the bad points of the other and correct them in myself." This requires judging favorably — seeing the good in others and being willing to learn from it.

## Practical Steps: Building Your Learning Community

How do you actually implement this teaching in your life?

### Finding Your Teacher

1. **Identify your needs**: What do you want to learn? What areas of your life need guidance?
2. **Research options**: Who in your community has knowledge and experience in these areas?
3. **Reach out**: Send an email, make a phone call, or approach someone after services. Be specific about what you're looking for.
4. **Start small**: Ask for a monthly meeting or a short study session, not a lifetime commitment.
5. **Be a good student**: Prepare for meetings, follow through on suggestions, and show appreciation.

### Finding Your Learning Friend

1. **Look in your current circles**: Is there someone at your synagogue, in your conversion class, or in your study group who seems to share your goals?
2. **Be the initiator**: Suggest studying together once a week. Propose a specific text or topic.
3. **Commit to consistency**: Pick a regular time and protect it. Consistency builds the relationship.
4. **Accept imperfection**: Your learning partner won't be perfect. Neither will you. That's okay.
5. **Celebrate progress**: Acknowledge growth — both your partner's and your own.

### Creating a Culture of Favorable Judgment

1. **Pause before reacting**: When someone says or does something that bothers you, take a breath before responding.
2. **Ask questions**: "What did you mean by that?" often reveals that you misunderstood.
3. **Assume good intentions**: Most people are trying their best most of the time.
4. **Look for the gift**: Even criticism or disagreement can contain something valuable if you're willing to look for it.

## The Lifelong Journey

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Perachya's teaching isn't a one-time action item. It's a lifelong practice. You will have different teachers at different stages of your life. You will have different learning partners as you move through different communities and different life circumstances. You will need to practice judging favorably every single day.

But if you commit to this practice — making teachers, acquiring friends, and judging favorably — you will never stop growing. You will always have someone to learn from, someone to learn with, and the wisdom to receive what others have to offer.

This is the path of Jewish learning: not solitary, not self-sufficient, but relational, communal, and endlessly generative. It's a path that has sustained the Jewish people for thousands of years, and it will sustain you too.

## Conclusion

"Make for yourself a teacher, acquire for yourself a friend, and judge every person favorably." These three instructions create a framework for lifelong growth:

- The teacher who guides you
- The friend who grows with you  
- The openness of heart that allows you to learn from everyone

In a world that often prizes independence and self-reliance, Jewish wisdom offers a different vision: we become ourselves through relationship. We learn who we are by learning with others. We grow not by climbing alone, but by building a community of learning that lifts us all.

Whether you're just beginning your Jewish journey or you've been learning for decades, these principles remain true. Find your teacher. Find your friend. Open your heart to what everyone has to teach you. And watch as your life becomes richer, deeper, and more meaningful than you imagined possible.
