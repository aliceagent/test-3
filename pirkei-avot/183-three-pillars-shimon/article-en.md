# The World Stands on Three Things: Torah, Worship, and Deeds of Kindness

## Introduction: A Three-Thousand-Year Blueprint

Imagine being asked to reduce the entire purpose of civilization into one sentence. What would you say?

Nearly 2,300 years ago, a sage named Shimon HaTzaddik (Shimon the Righteous) did exactly that. In just eleven Hebrew words, he captured what sustains the world:

> **"The world stands on three things: on Torah, on service [worship], and on acts of loving-kindness."**  
> *(Pirkei Avot 1:2)*

This teaching opens the second Mishnah of Pirkei Avot, immediately following the chain of tradition from Moses to the Men of the Great Assembly. Shimon was one of the last surviving members of that illustrious body, bridging the era of prophecy and the age of rabbinic Judaism. His words aren't merely philosophical—they're a practical framework for living a meaningful life and building a society that endures.

## Who Was Shimon HaTzaddik?

Shimon the Righteous lived during the Second Temple period, approximately 300 BCE. According to Jewish tradition, he served as the High Priest for forty years and was renowned for his piety, wisdom, and humility. He witnessed the visit of Alexander the Great to Jerusalem and is credited with preserving the Temple through his spiritual leadership.

When the Talmud speaks of "Shimon HaTzaddik," it uses a title reserved for the greatest spiritual giants. He earned this name not through political power but through embodying the very principles he taught. He didn't just *teach* about the three pillars—he *was* them.

## The First Pillar: Torah (תורה)

### What Is Torah?

Torah means "teaching" or "instruction." At its narrowest, it refers to the Five Books of Moses. More broadly, it encompasses all Jewish learning: the written Torah, the oral tradition, the Talmud, Jewish law, ethics, philosophy, and mysticism.

But even more fundamentally, Torah represents the pursuit of truth and wisdom. It's the divine blueprint that preceded creation itself. The Midrash teaches that God "looked into the Torah and created the world"—meaning reality itself is structured according to divine wisdom.

### Why Torah Sustains the World

Without shared values, knowledge, and truth, societies crumble. Torah provides the moral and intellectual foundation for everything else. It answers the questions: What is good? What is just? How should we live?

Consider this parallel: In Chinese philosophy, the concept of 道 (Dào, "the Way") in Daoism similarly represents the underlying principle that orders reality. Confucianism emphasizes 礼 (lǐ, ritual propriety) and 仁 (rén, benevolence) as foundational to social harmony. These parallel the Torah's dual emphasis on law (halacha) and narrative wisdom.

Just as a building needs architectural plans before construction begins, the world needs Torah—divine wisdom—to exist with purpose and meaning rather than chaos.

### Torah in Daily Life

Torah study isn't just for scholars. It's a daily practice accessible to everyone:

- **Beginners**: Start with five minutes reading Psalms, or studying one verse with commentary
- **Intermediate**: Study a page of Chumash (the Five Books) with Rashi's explanations
- **Advanced**: Engage with Talmud, halacha, or Jewish philosophy

The mitzvah (commandment) of Torah study applies equally to every Jewish person. As the Talmud says: "Talmud Torah k'neged kulam"—Torah study is equal in weight to all other commandments combined, because it leads to all of them.

## The Second Pillar: Avodah (עבודה) — Worship/Service

### Two Meanings, One Pillar

The Hebrew word *avodah* has a dual meaning:
1. **Temple service**: The sacrifices and rituals performed by the priests in the Holy Temple
2. **Prayer**: After the Temple's destruction, prayer became "the service of the heart"

Both meanings point to the same idea: connecting with the Divine through intentional, structured practice.

### The Heart of Worship

In the Temple era, *avodah* meant bringing offerings—not because God needs them, but because humans need the act of giving, dedicating, and elevating the physical world. After the Temple was destroyed in 70 CE, the Rabbis transformed these rituals into the thrice-daily prayer services we have today.

Prayer (*tefillah*) literally means "self-judgment" or "introspection." It's not about asking for favors—it's about aligning yourself with reality, acknowledging your place in the universe, and connecting to something greater than yourself.

### Cultural Parallels: The Universal Need for Ritual

Every civilization recognizes the importance of sacred time and structured devotion:

- **Chinese tradition**: The ancient practice of 祭祀 (jìsì, ancestral worship) and temple ceremonies honored heaven and ancestors through ritual offerings, much like the Temple service.
- **Daily practice**: Just as many Chinese families burn incense daily or visit temples on auspicious days, Jewish prayer creates a rhythm of spiritual connection three times daily (Shacharit, Mincha, Maariv).

Both systems recognize that human beings need *regular, structured encounters with the sacred*—not just when we feel inspired, but as a discipline that shapes us over time.

### Avodah in Daily Life

You don't need to be a High Priest to fulfill this pillar:

- **Pray**: Even if you don't know Hebrew yet, pray in your own language. Speak to God honestly.
- **Create sacred time**: Set aside moments each day—morning, afternoon, evening—to pause and reflect.
- **Intentional action**: Approach routine tasks (eating, working, relationships) as opportunities to serve something higher.

## The Third Pillar: Gemilut Chasadim (גמילות חסדים) — Acts of Loving-Kindness

### Beyond Charity

The third pillar is often translated as "acts of loving-kindness," but it's more expansive than simple charity (*tzedakah*). While tzedakah specifically refers to giving money to those in need, gemilut chasadim includes all acts of kindness:

- Visiting the sick
- Comforting mourners
- Helping someone find work
- Offering good advice
- Welcoming guests
- Attending weddings and funerals
- Simply listening when someone needs to talk

The Talmud (Sukkah 49b) explains that gemilut chasadim is *greater* than tzedakah in three ways:
1. Tzedakah is performed with money; chesed can be done with your person
2. Tzedakah is given to the poor; chesed is given to anyone
3. Tzedakah is for the living; chesed extends to the deceased (proper burial)

### The Chinese Virtue of Rén (仁)

The Confucian virtue of 仁 (rén)—often translated as "benevolence," "humaneness," or "kindness"—bears a striking resemblance to gemilut chasadim. Both emphasize:

- **Empathy**: Feeling for others as you feel for yourself
- **Active compassion**: Not just feeling, but doing
- **Social harmony**: Building a society where people care for one another

Confucius taught: "Do not impose on others what you do not wish for yourself" (Analects 15:23)—the "Silver Rule" that closely parallels Hillel's famous teaching: "What is hateful to you, do not do to your fellow" (Shabbat 31a).

Both traditions recognize that a civilization is measured not by its wealth or military power, but by how it treats its most vulnerable members.

### Gemilut Chasadim in Daily Life

This pillar is perhaps the most immediately accessible:

- **Start small**: Hold a door, give a sincere compliment, help carry someone's groceries
- **Notice needs**: Train yourself to see when someone needs help (even if they don't ask)
- **Give your time**: Sometimes presence matters more than money
- **Anonymous kindness**: Do good without seeking recognition or reward

Rabbi Moshe Chaim Luzzatto (the Ramchal) wrote: "The purpose of man is to bring pleasure to his Creator by perfecting himself and the world around him." Acts of kindness directly fulfill this purpose.

## Why Three? The Perfect Balance

Why did Shimon HaTzaddik choose these three—and not two, or four, or ten?

### The Intellectual, Spiritual, and Social

The three pillars form a complete system:

1. **Torah** addresses the *intellectual* dimension: knowledge, wisdom, truth
2. **Avodah** addresses the *spiritual* dimension: connection, transcendence, meaning
3. **Gemilut chasadim** addresses the *social* dimension: relationships, community, compassion

A person who studies Torah but never prays becomes dry and academic. A person who prays but never studies becomes superstitious. A person who studies and prays but never helps others becomes self-absorbed. All three are necessary.

### The Three Legs of a Stool

Imagine a three-legged stool. Remove any leg, and it collapses. The same applies to society—and to your own life.

- **Without Torah**: Society loses its moral compass and descends into relativism or chaos
- **Without Avodah**: Life becomes purely material and loses transcendent meaning
- **Without Gemilut Chasadim**: Society becomes cruel, selfish, and fragmented

Ancient Chinese philosophy similarly valued balance. The harmony of Heaven (天, tiān), Earth (地, dì), and Humanity (人, rén) formed a cosmic triad. Shimon's teaching offers a practical, actionable version of this balance.

## Applying the Three Pillars Today

How do you integrate these pillars into modern life?

### A Daily Framework

- **Morning (Torah)**: Spend 10-15 minutes studying—read a Psalm, study a verse, listen to a Torah podcast
- **Midday (Avodah)**: Pause for Mincha (afternoon prayer), or simply take three minutes to reflect and express gratitude
- **Evening (Gemilut Chasadim)**: Do one act of kindness before bed—call a lonely friend, help your spouse with dishes, donate to a cause

### A Weekly Framework

- **Shabbat (Torah)**: Dedicate time at the Shabbat table for discussion and study
- **Shabbat (Avodah)**: Attend synagogue services, sing zemirot (Shabbat songs), elevate the day
- **Shabbat (Gemilut Chasadim)**: Invite guests for meals, visit someone who's alone

### A Life Framework

As you grow:
- **Build a learning habit**: Join a weekly class, find a study partner (chavruta)
- **Deepen prayer**: Learn the structure of the siddur, understand what you're saying
- **Institutionalize kindness**: Volunteer regularly, mentor someone, become known for generosity

## The World Stands—Because of You

The language Shimon uses is striking: the world *stands* (עומד, omed) on these three things. Not "is built on" or "depends on"—but *stands*.

This implies active, continuous support. Like Atlas holding up the sky in Greek mythology, these three pillars don't just create the world—they sustain it moment by moment.

And here's the empowering part: *you* are part of that support system. Every time you:
- Learn one Torah concept
- Offer one sincere prayer
- Do one act of kindness

…you are literally holding up the world.

## Conclusion: Begin Where You Are

You don't need to be a Torah scholar, a mystic, or a saint to live by Shimon's teaching. You just need to start.

Choose one pillar to focus on this week:
- **Torah**: Read one chapter of Psalms daily
- **Avodah**: Say the Shema morning and night
- **Gemilut Chasadim**: Do one anonymous act of kindness each day

The world stands on three things—but it begins with one person, taking one step, in one moment.

And that person can be you.
