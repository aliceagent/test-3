# Do Not Judge Your Fellow Until You Have Reached Their Place

## Introduction: A Teaching That Transforms How We See Others

"Al tadin et chaveircha ad she'tagia limkomo" — "Do not judge your fellow until you have reached their place." These words from Hillel the Elder in Pirkei Avot (Ethics of the Fathers) 2:5 contain one of Judaism's most profound lessons about empathy, humility, and human relationships.

In a world where snap judgments fill our social media feeds and instant opinions dominate conversations, this ancient wisdom challenges us to pause, reflect, and recognize the complexity of every human story. But what does it really mean to "reach someone's place"? And how can we possibly apply this teaching when we're constantly asked to form opinions and make decisions?

## The Source: Hillel's Wisdom in Context

The teaching appears in the Mishnah, part of a chain of advice from Hillel the Elder, one of the most important sages of the Second Temple period. Hillel lived during the first century BCE and was known for his patience, humility, and emphasis on loving-kindness. 

The full context of this teaching includes two other famous statements from Hillel:

1. "Do not separate yourself from the community."
2. "Do not judge your fellow until you have reached their place."
3. "Do not say something that cannot be understood in the belief that it will be understood eventually."

Each teaching flows into the next, painting a picture of how we should conduct ourselves in relation to others. But the second teaching — our focus — strikes at the heart of human interaction: judgment.

## What Does "Their Place" Mean?

The Hebrew word "mekom" (מקום) literally means "place," but in Jewish thought, it carries deeper meanings. It can refer to:

**Physical Location**: The actual circumstances someone finds themselves in — their environment, their home, their community.

**Situational Context**: The specific challenges, pressures, and constraints they face at a given moment.

**Life Experience**: The accumulated trials, traumas, joys, and lessons that shape who someone has become.

**Spiritual State**: The level of knowledge, faith, and emotional capacity a person possesses at that point in their journey.

The Rabbis understood that we can never fully stand in another person's shoes. We haven't lived their childhood, experienced their struggles, or faced their specific set of temptations and challenges. Therefore, how can we presume to judge their actions with certainty?

## The Two Levels of Interpretation

### The Practical Level: Withhold Harsh Judgment

On the most straightforward level, this teaching tells us: before you criticize someone's behavior, ask yourself if you truly understand what they're going through.

Imagine seeing someone snap at a cashier or cut you off in traffic. Your immediate reaction might be anger or judgment: "What a rude person!" But what if that person just received devastating news? What if they're rushing to the hospital? What if they haven't slept in days caring for a sick child?

The Talmud (Shabbat 127b) teaches us about Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai's student, Rabbi Yehoshua, who once saw an ugly person and remarked on their appearance. The person responded: "Go tell the craftsman who made me, 'How ugly is this vessel you have made!'" Rabbi Yehoshua immediately realized his error — we don't judge God's creations so flippantly.

Similarly, we shouldn't judge people's choices so quickly. We don't know their "place."

### The Deeper Level: Recognize Your Own Limitations

On a more profound level, Hillel is saying something even more challenging: **You will never reach their place.** Each person's combination of genetics, upbringing, trauma, blessing, temperament, and circumstance is unique. 

Rabbi Yisrael Salanter, founder of the Mussar movement, explained that if we had exactly the same nature and experiences as another person, we would make exactly the same choices. Our certainty about what we "would have done" in someone else's situation is therefore an illusion.

This doesn't mean there's no such thing as right and wrong, or that we can't have legal and moral standards. Judaism absolutely has clear ethical boundaries. Rather, it means that while we can judge *actions* as objectively right or wrong, we should be very cautious about judging the *person* who performed them, because we don't understand the full context of their struggle.

## Chinese Cultural Parallels: "Never Know Another's Heart"

This Jewish teaching finds remarkable echoes in Chinese philosophy and literature. The Chinese proverb "知人知面不知心" (zhī rén zhī miàn bù zhī xīn) — "You may know someone's face but not their heart" — expresses similar wisdom about the limits of our understanding of others.

Confucius taught: "己所不欲,勿施于人" (jǐ suǒ bù yù, wù shī yú rén) — "Do not impose on others what you yourself do not desire." This Golden Rule appears in Jewish tradition as Hillel's famous teaching: "What is hateful to you, do not do to your fellow." Both traditions recognize that empathy — imagining ourselves in another's position — is foundational to ethics.

The concept of "将心比心" (jiāng xīn bǐ xīn) — "compare heart to heart" — encourages us to use our own feelings as a measure for understanding others. Before judging someone, ask: "If I were in their heart, how would I feel? What would I do?"

Buddhist thought, which deeply influenced Chinese culture, also emphasizes compassion arising from recognition that we cannot know another's karma or the full chain of causes that led to their present circumstances.

## Practical Applications: How to Live This Teaching

### 1. Pause Before Judging

When you feel judgment rising toward someone — whether a family member, colleague, or stranger — train yourself to pause. Ask:
- What don't I know about their situation?
- What pressures might they be facing?
- What pain might be driving this behavior?

This doesn't mean excusing harmful actions. It means approaching correction with humility rather than condemnation.

### 2. Judge Actions, Not Souls

Judaism makes a crucial distinction: we are required to evaluate actions according to Torah standards, but we must be careful about judging the person's essential worth or spiritual state.

A person who violates Shabbat might be doing so out of ignorance, rebellion, desperation, or a hundred other reasons. We can say "violating Shabbat is wrong" while recognizing we don't fully understand why this particular person made that choice at this moment.

### 3. Give the Benefit of the Doubt (Dan L'Kaf Zechut)

This teaching connects directly to another principle in Pirkei Avot (1:6): "Dan et kol ha'adam l'kaf zechut" — "Judge every person favorably." 

When we encounter ambiguous behavior — someone doesn't return our call, seems cold, makes a questionable choice — we should actively search for innocent explanations rather than assuming the worst.

### 4. Reflect on Your Own Journey

The teaching also contains an implicit humility check: "until you have reached their place" reminds us that we ourselves are on a journey. We haven't "arrived" anywhere. We're still growing, still struggling, still learning.

This awareness should make us gentler with others. As the saying goes: "Be kind, for everyone you meet is fighting a hard battle."

### 5. Apply It to Yourself

Paradoxically, while we should judge others favorably, we should judge *ourselves* strictly. The Chofetz Chaim taught that we often do the reverse — we're lenient with our own faults ("I had no choice!") and harsh on others' mistakes ("How could they?").

## When We Must Judge: The Role of Jewish Law

Does this teaching mean we can never evaluate others' behavior? Of course not. Jewish courts must judge. Parents must correct children. Communities must uphold standards.

The key is the *spirit* in which we judge:

**With Humility**: Recognizing our own flaws and that "there but for the grace of God go I."

**With Compassion**: Seeking to understand before condemning.

**With Justice Tempered by Mercy**: Following both God's attribute of strict justice (Elokim) and His attribute of mercy (Hashem).

The great Rabbi Akiva taught: "Beloved is man, for he was created in the image of God" (Pirkei Avot 3:14). Every person we judge bears the divine image. That should give us pause.

## The Teaching in Our Time: Social Media and Cancel Culture

Perhaps no generation has needed this teaching more than ours. Social media has created a culture of instant, public judgment. A person's worst moment can be captured, shared, and condemned by millions within hours. Careers end. Reputations are destroyed. Complex human beings are reduced to a single mistake.

Hillel's wisdom offers an antidote: "Do not judge your fellow until you have reached their place."

This doesn't mean we can't call out genuine wrongdoing. It means we should approach such situations with nuance, recognizing that:
- We see only a fragment of someone's story
- Public shaming often does more harm than good
- People can grow and change
- Mistakes don't define a person's entire worth

## Conclusion: The Path to a More Compassionate World

"Do not judge your fellow until you have reached their place" is more than good advice — it's a transformative practice. When we truly internalize this teaching, several things happen:

**We become humbler**, recognizing how much we don't know about others' inner lives.

**We become more compassionate**, extending grace to those who are struggling.

**We become better listeners**, genuinely curious about others' experiences rather than rushing to conclusions.

**We become more honest with ourselves**, acknowledging our own failings and the grace we need.

This teaching doesn't ask us to be naive or to abandon discernment. It asks us to approach judgment with the awareness that every human being is infinitely complex, shaped by factors we cannot see, facing battles we know nothing about.

In the words of the Talmud (Berakhot 58a): Just as their faces are different, so too are their minds different. Each person is a unique world, deserving of the respect and humility that comes from recognizing: I have not reached their place. I cannot fully know their struggle. Therefore, I will judge carefully, compassionately, and with profound awareness of my own limitations.

May we merit to see others through eyes of understanding, and may our judgments — when we must make them — be tempered with the humility that comes from knowing we walk a path of growth ourselves.
