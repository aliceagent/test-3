## Introduction

Imagine you're walking through a park after a summer storm. The clouds part, and suddenly a brilliant rainbow stretches across the sky. Or you're standing on a beach, watching waves crash endlessly against the shore. Or late at night, lightning suddenly illuminates the darkness with breathtaking power.

Most people pause in these moments, feeling something beyond words — wonder, smallness before nature's grandeur, an almost spiritual awe. Judaism recognizes this impulse and gives it a voice: special blessings (*berachot*) recited when encountering natural wonders.

These blessings aren't just formulaic words. They transform passive amazement into active gratitude. They remind us that the universe didn't create itself — that behind the rainbow, the ocean, and the lightning stands the Creator who sustains it all. Let's explore when and how Jews thank God for nature's most stunning displays.

## What Are Nature Blessings?

Judaism has different blessings for different experiences. You say one blessing before eating bread, another before fruit, another after a meal. Similarly, there are specific blessings designated for extraordinary natural phenomena.

These blessings fall into two main categories:

1. **Blessings recited upon witnessing God's power in nature** — lightning, thunder, mountains, deserts
2. **Blessings recited upon seeing beautiful or rare natural phenomena** — rainbows, the ocean (after not seeing it for 30 days), fruit trees blossoming in spring

The tradition of blessing God for nature goes back millennia. The Talmud (compiled around 200-500 CE — during China's Three Kingdoms through Jin Dynasty period, 三国时期至晋朝) records that the sages instituted these blessings to ensure we don't take the world for granted. When we see something extraordinary, we shouldn't just think "Wow, that's beautiful" and move on. We should stop, acknowledge the Creator, and express gratitude.

## The Blessing for Lightning and Thunder

### When Do You Say It?

When you see lightning or hear thunder, you recite the blessing:

**Hebrew:**  
בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה יְיָ אֱלֹהֵינוּ מֶלֶךְ הָעוֹלָם שֶׁכֹּחוֹ וּגְבוּרָתוֹ מָלֵא עוֹלָם

**Transliteration:**  
*Baruch atah Adonai, Eloheinu melech ha-olam, she-kocho u-gevurato malei olam.*

**Translation:**  
"Blessed are You, Lord our God, King of the universe, whose strength and power fill the world."

### How It Works

- You say this blessing **once per storm** — not for every single lightning flash or thunder clap. Once you've blessed the storm, you don't repeat the blessing even if it continues for hours.
- You can say it for **either lightning or thunder**. You don't need both.
- Some communities have the custom to say it specifically for thunder (the sound), but both are valid.

### The Message

This blessing doesn't just acknowledge power; it acknowledges *controlled* power. Lightning contains millions of volts and can split trees, yet it follows natural laws set by God. Thunder can shake buildings, yet it's precisely calibrated to the physics of sound waves. The blessing reminds us: this isn't chaos — it's divine power carefully channeled through creation.

## The Blessing for a Rainbow

### When Do You Say It?

When you see a rainbow, you recite:

**Hebrew:**  
בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה יְיָ אֱלֹהֵינוּ מֶלֶךְ הָעוֹלָם זוֹכֵר הַבְּרִית וְנֶאֱמָן בִּבְרִיתוֹ וְקַיָּם בְּמַאֲמָרוֹ

**Transliteration:**  
*Baruch atah Adonai, Eloheinu melech ha-olam, zocher ha-brit, v'ne'eman bi-vrito, v'kayam b'ma'amaro.*

**Translation:**  
"Blessed are You, Lord our God, King of the universe, who remembers the covenant, is faithful to His covenant, and keeps His promise."

### The Story Behind the Blessing

This blessing references the covenant God made with Noah after the flood. In Genesis 9:13-15, God says:

> "I have set My rainbow in the cloud, and it shall be a sign of the covenant between Me and the earth… Never again shall the waters become a flood to destroy all flesh."

Every time you see a rainbow, Judaism asks you to remember this promise: God will never again destroy the world through a global flood. The rainbow is a visible reminder of God's mercy and His commitment to sustaining creation.

### Special Notes

- You say this blessing **only if you haven't seen a rainbow in at least 30 days**. If you saw one yesterday and see another today, you don't repeat the blessing.
- Some have the custom **not to point** at a rainbow, out of respect for this divine sign.
- According to Jewish mysticism (Kabbalah), a rainbow appears when the world deserves punishment but God shows mercy instead. It's simultaneously a sign of danger averted and compassion granted.

## The Blessing for the Ocean

### When Do You Say It?

When you see the ocean after not seeing it for **at least 30 days**, you recite:

**Hebrew:**  
בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה יְיָ אֱלֹהֵינוּ מֶלֶךְ הָעוֹלָם עֹשֶׂה אֶת הַיָּם הַגָּדוֹל

**Transliteration:**  
*Baruch atah Adonai, Eloheinu melech ha-olam, oseh et ha-yam ha-gadol.*

**Translation:**  
"Blessed are You, Lord our God, King of the universe, who made the great sea."

### Why the Ocean?

The ocean represents one of creation's most overwhelming displays of God's power. The Talmud describes it as one of the wonders that never loses its majesty — you can see it a thousand times, and it remains awe-inspiring.

### Practical Details

- This blessing applies to any **major ocean** (Pacific, Atlantic, Indian, Arctic). Some authorities say it also applies to very large seas like the Mediterranean.
- You must not have seen this body of water for **30 consecutive days** to say the blessing again.
- You say the blessing **upon seeing it**, not upon touching the water or going in.

## Other Nature Blessings

Judaism has blessings for many other natural wonders:

### Mountains, Deserts, and Extraordinary Landscapes

When you see strikingly beautiful or unusual natural formations — towering mountains, vast deserts, dramatic canyons — you recite:

**Hebrew:**  
בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה יְיָ אֱלֹהֵינוּ מֶלֶךְ הָעוֹלָם עֹשֶׂה מַעֲשֵׂה בְרֵאשִׁית

**Transliteration:**  
*Baruch atah Adonai, Eloheinu melech ha-olam, oseh ma'aseh bereishit.*

**Translation:**  
"Blessed are You, Lord our God, King of the universe, who makes the work of creation."

This blessing can be said for any natural sight that inspires wonder — the Grand Canyon, the Himalayas, the Sahara, the Yellow Mountains (黄山).

### Trees Blossoming in Spring

The first time each year you see fruit trees blossoming (typically in spring), you say:

**Hebrew:**  
בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה יְיָ אֱלֹהֵינוּ מֶלֶךְ הָעוֹלָם שֶׁלֹּא חִסַּר בְּעוֹלָמוֹ כְּלוּם וּבָרָא בוֹ בְּרִיּוֹת טוֹבוֹת וְאִילָנוֹת טוֹבוֹת לֵהָנוֹת בָּהֶם בְּנֵי אָדָם

**Transliteration:**  
*Baruch atah Adonai, Eloheinu melech ha-olam, she-lo chisar b'olamo klum, u-vara vo briyot tovot v'ilanot tovot l'hanot bahem b'nei adam.*

**Translation:**  
"Blessed are You, Lord our God, King of the universe, who has made nothing lacking in His world, and created in it good creatures and good trees for people to enjoy."

This is called **Birkat Ha'Ilanot** (the blessing on the trees) and is traditionally said during the Hebrew month of Nissan (usually March or April). It's a celebration of renewal and the cycle of seasons.

## Cultural Connections: Nature Reverence in Chinese and Jewish Thought

The Jewish practice of blessing natural phenomena resonates deeply with traditional Chinese reverence for nature.

In classical Chinese philosophy, particularly in Daoism (道家), nature is seen as the ultimate expression of harmony and the Dao. Mountains and rivers (*shanshui*, 山水) aren't just scenery — they're manifestations of cosmic principles. Chinese landscape painting (*shanshui hua*, 山水画) doesn't merely depict nature; it invites contemplation of the infinite.

Similarly, Judaism sees nature not as mere backdrop but as a continuous revelation of the Creator. The Psalms (written during what corresponds to China's Spring and Autumn period, 春秋时期) repeatedly call on creation itself to praise God:

> "The heavens declare the glory of God, the sky proclaims His handiwork" (Psalm 19:2)

Both traditions teach that encountering nature properly requires more than just looking — it requires mindfulness, gratitude, and recognition of something greater than ourselves.

In Confucian thought (儒家), ritual (*li*, 礼) structures human life and connects people to the cosmic order. Jewish blessings function similarly: they're micro-rituals that turn spontaneous awe into conscious thanksgiving, transforming a moment of wonder into an encounter with the divine.

## Practical Application: Living With Nature Blessings

### Start Small

You don't need to memorize all these blessings at once. Start with one:

- If you live near the ocean, learn the ocean blessing.
- If thunderstorms are common where you are, learn the lightning/thunder blessing.
- If you rarely see these, start with the simpler "oseh ma'aseh bereishit" for beautiful scenery.

### Keep a Card or Note

Many Jews carry a small card with these blessings written out in Hebrew, transliteration, and translation. Keep it in your wallet or save it as a photo on your phone. When the moment comes, you'll have it ready.

### Say It Out Loud (When Appropriate)

If you're alone, say the blessing out loud. If you're with others who might not understand, you can say it quietly or even in your heart. The key is intentionality — acknowledging God as the source of what you're witnessing.

### Make It a Teaching Moment

If you're with family or friends (especially children), explain what you're doing: "In Judaism, when we see lightning, we say a blessing thanking God for His power." It turns a private moment into a shared lesson.

## Why These Blessings Matter

In our modern world, we can live almost entirely disconnected from nature. We wake up in buildings, work in buildings, sleep in buildings. We control temperature with air conditioning, light with electricity, weather with roofs and umbrellas.

But occasionally, nature breaks through:

- A thunderstorm powerful enough to shake the windows
- A rainbow vivid enough to stop traffic
- An ocean vast enough to make you feel tiny

These moments remind us we're not the center of the universe. Judaism's nature blessings don't just acknowledge this truth — they sanctify it. They turn humility before creation into gratitude toward the Creator.

The medieval Jewish philosopher Judah Halevi (1075-1141 CE, during China's Song Dynasty, 宋朝) wrote in his book *The Kuzari*:

> "The religious person differs from the non-religious not in what they see, but in what they see *through* what they see."

When you recite a blessing over lightning, you're not denying the physics of electrical discharge. You're seeing *through* the physics to the Divine will that established those laws in the first place.

## Key Takeaways

- **Lightning/Thunder blessing**: Acknowledges God's power filling the world — said once per storm.
- **Rainbow blessing**: Remembers God's covenant with Noah never to flood the earth again — said once per 30 days.
- **Ocean blessing**: Praises God for creating the great sea — said when seeing the ocean after 30+ days.
- **Beautiful landscapes blessing**: Can be said for extraordinary natural sights like mountains, deserts, canyons.
- **Spring blossoms blessing**: Said annually (in Nissan) upon seeing fruit trees blooming.
- These blessings transform passive wonder into active gratitude and God-consciousness.

## Closing Thought

Next time you see lightning split the sky, a rainbow curve across the clouds, or waves roll endlessly onto the shore, pause. You're witnessing something that has inspired awe for thousands of years — since long before China's first dynasties, since before the Torah was given at Sinai.

Judaism gives you words to match that feeling. Use them. Let the natural world become not just a spectacle, but a doorway to gratitude.
