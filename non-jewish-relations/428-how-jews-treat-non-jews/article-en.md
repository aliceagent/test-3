One of the most important questions in any religious tradition is: how should believers treat those outside their faith? In an age of globalization and multiculturalism, understanding how Judaism views relations with non-Jews is crucial for both Jews and those seeking to understand Jewish ethics.

Jewish law (halacha) and ethical teachings address this question extensively. The Torah, Talmud, and centuries of rabbinical writings establish clear guidelines: Jews must treat non-Jews with respect, fairness, honesty, and compassion. While historical contexts complicated these ideals, the core principle remains: every human being is created in God's image (tzelem Elokim) and deserves dignity.

For Chinese readers accustomed to Confucian ethics emphasizing proper treatment of all people or Buddhist compassion extending to all sentient beings, Jewish teachings on non-Jewish relations offer interesting parallels and contrasts.

## Biblical Foundations

### Created in God's Image

The Torah establishes the fundamental principle in its opening chapter:

**Genesis 1:27**: "So God created man in His own image, in the image of God He created him; male and female He created them."

This applies to all humanity, not only Jews. Every person — regardless of religion, ethnicity, or status — bears the divine image. This theological principle grounds all Jewish ethics toward non-Jews.

### The Stranger Among You

The Torah repeatedly commands compassionate treatment of strangers (gerim):

**Leviticus 19:33-34**: "When a stranger resides with you in your land, you shall not wrong him. The stranger who resides with you shall be to you as one of your citizens; you shall love him as yourself, for you were strangers in the land of Egypt."

**Exodus 22:20**: "You shall not wrong a stranger or oppress him, for you were strangers in the land of Egypt."

**Exodus 23:9**: "You shall not oppress a stranger, for you know the feelings of the stranger, having yourselves been strangers in the land of Egypt."

The Torah mentions the obligation to treat strangers well **36 times** — more than almost any other commandment. This reflects its centrality to Jewish ethics.

**Note on Translation**: The Hebrew word "ger" is complex. In biblical context, it often means "resident alien" — a non-Israelite living among Israelites. In later rabbinical usage, "ger" came to mean "convert." The ethical principles of fair treatment apply in both cases.

### Justice for All

**Leviticus 24:22**: "You shall have one standard for stranger and citizen alike: for I the Lord am your God."

This verse establishes equal justice — courts must apply the same laws to Jews and non-Jews. No separate legal standards based on religion or ethnicity.

**Deuteronomy 16:20**: "Justice, justice shall you pursue."

The repetition emphasizes justice as absolute requirement, not selective based on the recipient's identity.

## Talmudic Development

The Talmud expands and applies biblical principles:

### The Ways of Peace (Darkhei Shalom)

A key Talmudic principle governing Jewish-gentile relations is "darkhei shalom" — "ways of peace":

**Mishnah Gittin 5:8-9** teaches:
- Non-Jewish poor should be supported alongside Jewish poor
- Non-Jewish sick should be visited alongside Jewish sick
- Non-Jewish dead should be buried with dignity alongside Jewish dead

**Reason**: "Mipnei darkhei shalom" — "for the sake of peaceful relations" or "for the ways of peace."

Some scholars interpret this as merely pragmatic — avoiding conflict with non-Jewish neighbors. Others see it as genuinely ethical — pursuing peace and harmony is inherent good, and compassion should extend to all.

### Desecration of God's Name (Chillul Hashem)

**Talmud Bava Kama 113b**: Rabbi Shimon ben Shetach refused to keep a valuable jewel accidentally left in a garment he purchased from a non-Jew. He returned it, saying, "Did you think I'm a barbarian? I bought a garment, not a jewel."

The non-Jew responded: "Blessed be the God of the Jews!"

**Principle**: Unethical treatment of non-Jews causes chillul Hashem (desecration of God's name) — making God and Judaism look bad. Conversely, ethical behavior creates kiddush Hashem (sanctification of God's name).

### Honesty in Business

**Leviticus 25:14**: "When you sell property to your neighbor or buy from your neighbor, you shall not wrong one another."

Talmudic interpretation: "Your neighbor" includes all people, not only fellow Jews. Fraud, deception, and dishonesty in business dealings with anyone is prohibited.

**Tosefta Bava Kama 10:15**: "Theft from a non-Jew is forbidden, and more stringent than theft from a Jew, because of desecration of God's Name."

This remarkable statement inverts what one might expect. It argues that wronging non-Jews is *worse* because it reflects poorly on Judaism and God.

### Saving Lives

**Talmud Sanhedrin 74a** establishes that one must violate almost any commandment to save a life. This principle, "pikuach nefesh," applies to Jewish and non-Jewish lives equally.

**Modern Application**: Jewish medical ethics requires doctors to treat all patients equally regardless of religion. Israeli hospitals treat wounded enemies alongside Israeli citizens without distinction.

## Maimonides' Formulation

The great medieval codifier Maimonides (Rambam, 1138-1204) systematized Jewish law's approach to non-Jews:

### Support and Compassion

**Mishneh Torah, Hilchot Melachim 10:12**: "Our Sages commanded us to visit the sick of the gentiles, bury their dead along with the dead of Israel, and support their poor along with the poor of Israel, for the sake of the ways of peace."

Maimonides establishes these as normative Jewish law, not optional charity.

### Honest Business

**Mishneh Torah, Hilchot Gezeilah 1:2**: "It is forbidden to steal from or defraud anyone, Jew or gentile."

**Mishneh Torah, Hilchot De'ot 5:13**: "One should speak truth and maintain integrity with all people — Jews and non-Jews alike."

### Lost Property

**Mishneh Torah, Hilchot Gezeilah 11:3**: If a Jew finds lost property belonging to a non-Jew, he must return it if the non-Jew would return lost Jewish property, or if not returning it would cause desecration of God's name.

This establishes reciprocity and concern for reputation as factors, but the baseline is fairness.

## Medieval Complexity: Historical Context

Honesty requires acknowledging that some Talmudic and medieval texts contain troubling passages that seem to distinguish between Jews and gentiles in problematic ways:

**Historical Context**: 
- Medieval Jews lived as persecuted minorities in Christian and Muslim lands
- Legal restrictions, forced conversions, expulsions, and violence shaped Jewish-gentile relations
- Some Talmudic passages use "goy" (gentile) to refer specifically to hostile pagans or oppressors, not all non-Jews
- Medieval commentators sometimes narrowed earlier universal principles, restricting them to specific contexts

**Example**: Some medieval texts debate whether the prohibition against "wronging" applies equally to gentiles, or whether there are distinctions in financial dealings.

**Modern Scholarship**: Contemporary Orthodox scholars largely interpret these texts as:
1. Context-specific (addressing particular hostile situations)
2. Not normative halacha (not the final legal position)
3. Superseded by broader ethical principles

**Chief Rabbi Jonathan Sacks** wrote: "The test of our religious commitment is not whether we are loving toward our own. That is relatively easy. It is whether we extend our sense of humanity to those not like us."

Modern Orthodox Judaism overwhelmingly affirms universal ethical obligations toward all people.

## Modern Orthodox Positions

Contemporary Orthodox authorities have clarified Jewish obligations toward non-Jews:

### Rabbi Joseph B. Soloveitchik

The leading Modern Orthodox thinker of the 20th century taught that the image of God in every person creates absolute moral duties:

**"Confrontation" (1964)**: "We must relate to all humans with respect and dignity because they bear the divine image, regardless of their faith."

### Rabbi Eliezer Waldenberg

The renowned Israeli posek (legal decisor) ruled that modern medical ethics requires treating all patients equally, regardless of religion, and that this flows from fundamental Torah values.

### Rabbi Aharon Lichtenstein

Rosh Yeshiva of Yeshivat Har Etzion, he wrote extensively on Jewish ethics and emphasized that "bein adam l'chavero" (interpersonal ethics) applies universally:

**"Does Jewish Tradition Recognize an Ethic Independent of Halakha?" (1975)**: Jewish ethics demands more than technical legal compliance. Compassion, honesty, and fairness toward all people are Torah values, not just pragmatic accommodations.

### Rabbi Lord Jonathan Sacks

Former Chief Rabbi of the UK, he championed interfaith dialogue and universal ethics:

**"The Dignity of Difference" (2002)**: "God has spoken to mankind in many languages through Judaism to Jews, Christianity to Christians, Islam to Muslims."

He argued that respecting religious diversity is a Torah value, grounded in creation of all humans in God's image.

## Practical Halacha Today

What do these principles mean practically?

### Business Ethics

- **Honesty**: Jews must be scrupulously honest in business with everyone, Jew or non-Jew. Fraud, deception, and exploitation are forbidden.
- **Contracts**: Agreements with non-Jews are fully binding. Breaking contracts is theft.
- **Fair Pricing**: Overcharging or taking advantage of another's ignorance is prohibited universally.

### Social Relations

- **Respect**: Jews should treat non-Jewish neighbors, colleagues, and acquaintances with respect and courtesy.
- **Charity**: While Jewish poor have priority in allocation of Jewish communal funds, helping non-Jewish poor is obligatory, particularly in mixed societies.
- **Medical Care**: Jewish medical professionals must treat all patients equally.

### Government and Society

- **Law of the Land**: "Dina d'malchuta dina" — the law of the land is binding. Jews must obey civil law in democratic societies (barring laws forcing violation of core Torah principles).
- **Civic Duty**: Jews should be good citizens — paying taxes, voting, serving in military when appropriate, contributing to societal welfare.
- **Interfaith Cooperation**: Jews can and should cooperate with non-Jews on shared ethical goals (fighting poverty, promoting justice, environmental protection).

### Religious Dialogue

**Permissibility**: Jews may engage in respectful dialogue with Christians, Muslims, Buddhists, and others, learning about their traditions and finding common ground.

**Boundaries**: Jews shouldn't participate in non-Jewish worship services or rituals that involve idolatry, but attending interfaith events, educational programs, and civic ceremonies is generally acceptable.

**Torah Study with Non-Jews**: Teaching Torah to non-Jews is permissible and even encouraged, particularly regarding the Seven Noahide Laws and universal ethical teachings.

## Comparing with Confucian Ethics

Chinese readers familiar with Confucian thought will notice parallels:

### Ren (仁 - Benevolence)

Confucius taught extending kindness to all people. Mencius emphasized that compassion is innate in humans. Similarly, Judaism teaches universal compassion grounded in shared divine image.

### Shu (恕 - Reciprocity)

The Confucian principle "Do not impose on others what you do not wish for yourself" echoes Hillel's famous teaching: "What is hateful to you, do not do to your neighbor. This is the whole Torah; the rest is commentary" (Talmud Shabbat 31a). Both apply universally.

### Zhong (忠 - Loyalty/Faithfulness)

Confucian emphasis on faithfulness in relationships parallels Jewish emphasis on keeping contracts and maintaining honesty with all people.

**Differences**: 
- Confucianism is more focused on social harmony and virtue cultivation; Judaism emphasizes divine commandment
- Confucianism centers on family and hierarchical relationships; Judaism emphasizes equal dignity of all before God
- Confucianism is largely secular/philosophical; Judaism is theistic

## Why This Matters

Understanding how Jews should treat non-Jews matters for several reasons:

**For Jews**: Clarifying ethical obligations in increasingly multicultural societies. Most Jews today live, work, and socialize primarily with non-Jews. These relationships must embody Torah values.

**For Non-Jews**: Addressing misconceptions. Some associate Judaism with insularity or exclusivism. While Jews maintain distinct identity, authentic Jewish ethics demands universal respect and compassion.

**For Interfaith Relations**: Providing a basis for cooperation. Jews and non-Jews can work together on shared ethical goals, grounded in mutual respect.

**For Moral Philosophy**: Offering a religious ethical framework that combines particular identity with universal obligations — a model for other traditions navigating similar tensions.

## Conclusion: The Image of God

The teaching that every human bears the divine image remains the cornerstone of Jewish ethics toward non-Jews. This isn't mere theory; it has practical implications:

- When a Jewish doctor treats a non-Jewish patient, she sees the image of God
- When a Jewish business owner deals with a non-Jewish customer, he must reflect divine honesty
- When Jews interact with non-Jewish neighbors, they represent God and Torah in the world

Rabbi Abraham Joshua Heschel, who marched alongside Martin Luther King Jr., embodied this principle. When asked why a rabbi participated in civil rights protests, he said: "I felt my legs were praying."

Jewish ethics toward non-Jews isn't about tolerance or political correctness. It's about recognizing the sacred in every person and treating them accordingly — not despite their being non-Jewish, but because they, like all humans, are created in God's image.

This is the essence of Jewish universalism: particular identity and universal ethics, distinct covenant and shared humanity, chosen people and equal dignity — held together in creative tension, reflecting the complexity and richness of the Torah's vision for humanity.
