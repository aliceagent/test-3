# Judaism and Christianity: Key Theological Differences Explained Respectfully

---

Judaism and Christianity share profound connections: the same biblical patriarchs, many of the same sacred texts, belief in the God of Israel, and intertwined historical development. Yet they diverge dramatically on fundamental theological questions. For nearly two millennia, these two faiths have existed side by side — sometimes in peaceful coexistence, sometimes in tragic conflict — each claiming to represent authentic relationship with God.

Understanding where Judaism and Christianity differ — respectfully and clearly — matters for interfaith dialogue, mutual understanding, and reducing prejudice. This article explains the key theological differences without claiming one is "right" and the other "wrong," but rather clarifying what each tradition believes and why.

For Chinese readers, this comparison might resonate with how Buddhism and Taoism share Chinese cultural roots yet diverge theologically, or how Confucianism and Buddhism coexist in Chinese society despite different metaphysical views.

## 1. The Nature of God: Unity vs. Trinity

Perhaps the most fundamental difference concerns the nature of God Himself.

### Jewish View: Absolute Divine Unity (Tawhid/Yichud)

Judaism teaches **strict monotheism** — God is absolutely, indivisibly One. This is expressed in the Shema, Judaism's central prayer:

**Deuteronomy 6:4**: "Hear O Israel, the Lord our God, the Lord is One."

The Hebrew word "echad" (one) means singular, unified, indivisible. Jewish theology insists:
- God has no parts, no divisions, no plurality
- God is not composed of persons or aspects
- God cannot become human or take physical form (God is incorporeal)
- Worship of anything or anyone besides the One God is idolatry

**Maimonides' Principles** (13 Principles of Faith, #2): "God is One. There is no unity like His. He is our God. He was, He is, He will be."

This uncompromising monotheism shapes all Jewish theology and practice.

### Christian View: Trinity

Christianity teaches that God exists as **Trinity** — one God in three "persons" or "hypostases":
- God the Father
- God the Son (Jesus Christ)
- God the Holy Spirit

Classical Christian theology (as formulated in the Nicene Creed, 325 CE, and refined at subsequent councils) insists:
- There is only one God (not three gods — that would be polytheism)
- Yet this one God exists eternally as three distinct persons
- The Father is fully God, the Son is fully God, the Spirit is fully God
- Yet they are not three separate gods but one divine essence

**The Mystery**: Christians acknowledge Trinity as mystery — beyond full human comprehension but revealed in Scripture and essential to Christian faith.

**The Incarnation**: Central to Christianity is the belief that God the Son **became human** in Jesus Christ — fully divine and fully human simultaneously. In Jesus, divinity and humanity united in one person.

### Why This Difference Matters

From a **Jewish perspective**:
- Trinity appears to compromise pure monotheism
- The idea of God becoming human seems to violate God's transcendence and incorporeality
- Worshipping Jesus as God looks like idolatry — worshipping a created being (a human) as divine

From a **Christian perspective**:
- Trinity doesn't compromise monotheism but reveals its depth
- The Incarnation shows God's love — willing to become human to redeem humanity
- Worshipping Jesus isn't idolatry because Jesus is God, not a mere human

**Jewish authorities' views on Christian monotheism**: 
- **Maimonides** considered Christianity idolatrous because of Trinity
- **Tosafists** (medieval French scholars) were more lenient, viewing Christians as "Noahides in error" — trying to worship the true God but with mistaken theology
- **Modern Orthodox view**: Most see Christianity as not idolatry for Christians (though forbidden for Jews), acknowledging Christians worship the God of Israel even if theology differs

## 2. Jesus: Prophet, Teacher, or Divine Messiah?

### Jewish View: Jesus as Historical Figure, Not Messiah

Judaism acknowledges Jesus (Hebrew: Yeshua) as a historical Jewish figure who lived in 1st-century Judea, but rejects Christian claims about him:

**Not the Messiah**: 
- The Hebrew Bible prophesies a future Messiah (anointed king) who will:
  - Rebuild the Temple in Jerusalem
  - Gather all Jews back to the Land of Israel
  - Bring world peace and end war
  - Spread knowledge of God to all nations
  - Establish God's kingdom on earth

- Jesus didn't accomplish these tasks in his lifetime
- Christians' explanation that Jesus will fulfill these in a "Second Coming" doesn't satisfy Jewish criteria — the Messiah accomplishes the mission in one coming, not two

**Not Divine**: 
- Judaism categorically rejects the idea that any human is God or part of God
- Calling a human "son of God" in divine sense is blasphemy in Jewish theology
- The messiah will be human — a righteous, wise king descended from David, but fully human nonetheless

**Not a Prophet**:
- While some Jews view Jesus as a teacher or even a rabbi with interesting ideas, Judaism doesn't accept him as a prophet
- True prophets don't contradict Torah or claim to be divine

**Not the Fulfillment of Scripture**:
- Christianity sees Jesus as fulfilling Hebrew Bible prophecies
- Judaism argues these interpretations are wrong — the prophecies refer to other events or remain unfulfilled

**Jesus' Jewishness**: Modern Jewish scholars acknowledge Jesus was an observant Jew who kept Torah, prayed in synagogues, and observed Jewish festivals. The break between Judaism and Christianity came after Jesus' death, as his followers developed Christology and gentile Christianity diverged from Jewish practice.

### Christian View: Jesus as Divine Savior

Christianity centers entirely on Jesus as:

**The Messiah (Christ)**: 
- Jesus is the long-awaited Jewish Messiah predicted in Hebrew Scriptures
- His first coming accomplished spiritual redemption; his second coming will accomplish physical redemption (world peace, resurrection, judgment)
- The Hebrew Bible contains hundreds of prophecies Christians see fulfilled in Jesus (virgin birth, birthplace, suffering, resurrection)

**Fully Divine and Fully Human**:
- Jesus is God incarnate — God the Son who became human
- Conceived by the Holy Spirit, born of the Virgin Mary
- Lived as fully human while remaining fully divine
- Performed miracles demonstrating divine authority

**The Savior**: 
- Jesus died on the cross as a sacrifice for humanity's sins
- His death atoned for sin, making salvation possible
- He rose from the dead on the third day, conquering death
- Through faith in Jesus, believers receive forgiveness and eternal life

**Son of God**: 
- This title means more than prophet or righteous person — it denotes unique divine sonship
- Jesus shares the divine nature; worshipping him is worshipping God

**Central to Salvation**: 
- **John 14:6**: "I am the way, the truth, and the life. No one comes to the Father except through me."
- Christianity teaches Jesus is the only path to salvation

### Irreconcilable Difference

This difference is irreducible: Christians believe Jesus is God; Jews believe he isn't. Christians believe Jesus is the Messiah; Jews believe he isn't. There's no middle ground that satisfies both positions.

However, respectful dialogue doesn't require agreement — only mutual understanding and respect for the other's sincerity.

## 3. Salvation: Law vs. Grace?

### Jewish View: Covenant, Commandments, and Repentance

Judaism doesn't have a concept of "salvation" identical to Christianity's, but addresses similar questions (how does one achieve righteousness and closeness to God?):

**The Covenant**: 
- God made a covenant with the Jewish people at Mount Sinai
- Jews are obligated to follow Torah — 613 commandments covering ritual, ethical, and civil life
- Keeping these commandments is how Jews fulfill their covenant with God

**Righteousness Through Action**:
- Judaism emphasizes **deeds** (ma'asim tovim) — righteous actions, observance of commandments, ethical behavior
- There's no concept of "original sin" inherited from Adam
- Humans are born morally neutral with both good and evil inclinations (yetzer hatov and yetzer hara)
- Through free will and effort, people can choose righteousness

**Repentance (Teshuvah)**:
- Sin isn't permanent stain requiring divine sacrifice
- Repentance is always available: sincere regret, ceasing the sin, confessing to God, and resolving not to repeat it
- God forgives sins directly when people genuinely repent
- No mediator is needed; each person has direct access to God

**World to Come**:
- Righteous people of all nations have a share in the World to Come (afterlife)
- Non-Jews don't need to convert to Judaism; keeping the Seven Noahide Laws is sufficient for righteousness
- Judaism focuses more on this world than the next — sanctifying daily life through mitzvot (commandments)

**Not "Salvation by Works"**:
- Christianity sometimes characterizes Judaism as "salvation by works" — earning salvation through deeds
- Jews respond: it's not about earning but about responding to covenant relationship. Like a child honoring parents not to "earn" love but to express relationship, Jews keep commandments to maintain covenant with God

### Christian View: Salvation by Grace Through Faith

Christianity fundamentally differs:

**Original Sin**:
- Humanity inherited sinful nature from Adam's fall
- All humans are born sinful, incapable of perfect righteousness
- No amount of good deeds can overcome this sinful nature or earn salvation

**The Law Cannot Save**:
- The Torah (Law) reveals God's standards but cannot make people righteous
- Paul writes (Romans 3:20): "For by works of the law no human being will be justified in his sight, since through the law comes knowledge of sin."
- The Law shows us we're sinners, but can't save us

**Grace Through Faith**:
- Salvation is a **gift** from God, not earned by human effort
- God's grace (unmerited favor) offers salvation through Jesus' sacrifice
- **Ephesians 2:8-9**: "For by grace you have been saved through faith. And this is not your own doing; it is the gift of God, not a result of works, so that no one may boast."
- Faith in Jesus — trusting his atoning death and resurrection — brings salvation

**Jesus as Mediator**:
- Jesus is the necessary mediator between God and humanity
- His sacrifice on the cross paid the penalty for sin
- Through him alone can humans be reconciled to God

**Faith and Works**:
- Different Christian traditions debate the role of works:
  - **Protestant**: Faith alone saves (sola fide), though genuine faith produces good works
  - **Catholic**: Faith and works cooperate; faith without works is dead
  - **Orthodox**: Salvation is lifelong process (theosis) involving faith, sacraments, and transformation

### Why This Matters

These differences lead to very different religious experiences:

**Judaism**: Emphasizes daily practice, study, community observance, ethical living, and direct relationship with God.

**Christianity**: Emphasizes personal relationship with Jesus, gratitude for grace, transformation through the Holy Spirit, and evangelism (sharing the gospel).

## 4. Scripture: Tanakh vs. Old and New Testaments

### Jewish View: The Tanakh and Oral Torah

**Tanakh** (Hebrew Bible):
- Torah (Five Books of Moses) — most sacred
- Nevi'im (Prophets)
- Ketuvim (Writings)

**Oral Torah**:
- Mishnah, Talmud, and centuries of rabbinical commentary
- Judaism sees these as part of divine revelation given at Sinai and transmitted orally before being written down
- Interpretation and application of Torah through rabbinical tradition

**New Testament**: 
- Not accepted as Scripture
- Seen as Christian texts about Christian beliefs, not divinely inspired Jewish texts

**Interpretation**:
- Jewish interpretation focuses on Hebrew text, rabbinical commentaries (like Rashi, Maimonides), and legal discussions
- Emphasis on understanding commandments and their application

### Christian View: Old and New Testaments

**Old Testament**:
- Christians adopt the Hebrew Bible (though usually in a different order and sometimes with additional books called Deuterocanonical/Apocrypha)
- Called "Old Testament" to distinguish from "New Testament"
- Christians read it through Christological lens — seeing Jesus prophesied throughout

**New Testament**:
- Four Gospels (Matthew, Mark, Luke, John) — accounts of Jesus' life and teachings
- Acts of the Apostles — early church history
- Epistles (letters by Paul and others) — theology and instruction to early Christians
- Revelation — apocalyptic prophecy

**Authority**:
- Both testaments are divinely inspired Scripture
- The New Testament represents a new covenant, fulfilling and superseding the old covenant in some ways

**Interpretation**:
- Christians read Hebrew Bible through New Testament lens
- Prophecies are seen as pointing to Jesus
- Some laws (dietary, ritual) are viewed as no longer binding; moral laws remain

### Example of Different Interpretation

**Isaiah 53** (Suffering Servant):

**Jewish interpretation**: 
- Refers to the Jewish people collectively, suffering through exile and persecution
- Or refers to a righteous prophet or future messianic figure

**Christian interpretation**: 
- Clear prophecy of Jesus' suffering and death
- Verse 5: "He was wounded for our transgressions" — points directly to Jesus' crucifixion

Both read the same text; both see completely different meanings.

## 5. Law (Torah): Eternal vs. Fulfilled

### Jewish View: Torah is Eternal

**Immutability**:
- Torah's commandments are eternal and unchanging
- Deuteronomy 13:1: "You shall not add to it or subtract from it"
- The 613 commandments remain binding on Jews for all time
- No prophet, teacher, or messiah can change Torah

**Continuous Obligation**:
- Observant Jews keep dietary laws (kashrut), Sabbath, holidays, ritual purity laws (where applicable), and ethical commandments
- Reform and other liberal movements debate which laws are binding, but Orthodox Judaism maintains all remain obligatory

**Centrality of Practice**:
- Judaism is orthoprax (emphasizing correct practice) as much as orthodox (correct belief)
- How you live matters as much as what you believe

### Christian View: Law Fulfilled in Christ

**Fulfillment**:
- **Matthew 5:17**: Jesus said, "Do not think that I have come to abolish the Law or the Prophets; I have not come to abolish them but to fulfill them."
- Christians interpret "fulfill" to mean Jesus completed the Law's purpose
- The ceremonial and ritual laws (dietary laws, sacrifices, circumcision) are no longer required for Christians

**New Covenant**:
- Jesus inaugurated a new covenant, written on hearts rather than stone tablets
- Christians are saved by grace, not by keeping Torah's ritual laws
- Moral laws (Ten Commandments, ethical teachings) remain binding

**Gentile Christians**:
- Early Christianity debated whether Gentile converts must keep Torah (circumcision, dietary laws, etc.)
- The Council of Jerusalem (Acts 15) ruled Gentile Christians are not obligated to keep ritual Torah
- This opened Christianity to the non-Jewish world

### The Great Divide

For Jews, a religion that says Torah laws are no longer binding is false by definition. Torah is eternal; any teaching claiming otherwise contradicts God's explicit word.

For Christians, Jesus' sacrifice and resurrection fundamentally changed the covenant relationship. The ceremonial law pointed forward to Christ; once he came, those shadows gave way to reality.

## 6. The Messiah and Messianic Age

### Jewish View: Messiah Yet to Come

**Future Redemption**:
- The Messiah (mashiach, "anointed one") is a future Jewish king descended from David
- He will be fully human (not divine), exceptionally wise and righteous
- He will accomplish:
  - Rebuild the Temple in Jerusalem
  - Gather Jewish exiles back to Israel
  - Establish world peace (nations will no longer make war)
  - Spread knowledge of God to all nations
  - Restore Davidic kingdom

**One Coming, Complete Mission**:
- The Messiah will accomplish all these in his lifetime
- No concept of "second coming" to finish the job

**Current State**:
- The Messianic Age has not yet arrived (there is no world peace, Temple isn't rebuilt, etc.)
- Jews still await the Messiah's coming

### Christian View: Jesus the Messiah, Second Coming Awaited

**Two Comings**:
- Jesus' first coming accomplished spiritual redemption (salvation from sin)
- His second coming will accomplish physical redemption (resurrection, judgment, new heaven and earth, end of evil)

**Already and Not Yet**:
- The Kingdom of God has already begun (through Jesus' first coming) but is not yet consummated
- Christians live in the "in-between" time, awaiting Christ's return

**Signs and Timing**:
- Jesus' return is imminent (could happen any time)
- Various signs predicted: gospel preached to all nations, tribulation, appearance of Antichrist, etc.
- Timing is unknown ("No one knows the day or the hour")

**Ultimate Victory**:
- Christ will defeat evil, judge the living and the dead, resurrect believers, and establish eternal kingdom

## 7. People of God: Who Is Included?

### Jewish View: Covenant with Israel

**Chosen People**:
- God made a covenant specifically with the Jewish people at Mount Sinai
- This is not racial superiority but particular mission — to be "a kingdom of priests and a holy nation" (Exodus 19:6)
- Torah and its commandments apply to Jews

**Non-Jews**:
- Non-Jews are not obligated to keep Torah
- Righteous non-Jews (those keeping the Seven Noahide Laws) have full share in the World to Come
- Conversion to Judaism is possible but not necessary for salvation/righteousness

**Ethnic and Religious Identity**:
- Jewishness is both religious and ethnic
- A person born to a Jewish mother is Jewish, even if not observant
- Converts are fully Jewish, equal in all respects

### Christian View: Universal Church

**New Israel**:
- The Church (body of all believers in Jesus) is the "new Israel" or "true Israel"
- Membership isn't by birth but by faith in Christ
- **Galatians 3:28**: "There is neither Jew nor Greek, slave nor free, male nor female, for you are all one in Christ Jesus."

**Supersessionism**:
- Traditional Christian theology taught **supersessionism** (replacement theology): the Church replaced Israel as God's chosen people because Israel rejected Jesus
- Jews who don't accept Jesus are outside the covenant

**Modern Revisions**:
- Many modern Christians, especially post-Holocaust, reject supersessionism
- They affirm God's ongoing covenant with the Jewish people
- Some believe Jews have their own valid covenant path to God; others still believe Jesus is necessary even for Jews

**Universality**:
- Christianity is for all people regardless of ethnicity
- The Great Commission: "Go and make disciples of all nations" (Matthew 28:19)
- Evangelism (spreading the gospel) is central to Christian mission

## Conclusion: Respect Amidst Difference

These differences — God's nature, Jesus' identity, path to salvation, scripture, law, and covenant — are profound and irreducible. They cannot be minimized or harmonized away.

Yet both faiths worship the God of Abraham, revere many of the same texts, share ethical commitments (justice, compassion, love of neighbor), and have enriched human civilization.

**For Jews**: Christianity is a separate religion that emerged from Judaism, diverged dramatically, and is not compatible with Jewish belief. A Jew who accepts Christian theology (especially Jesus as divine) is no longer practicing Judaism.

**For Christians**: Judaism is the parent faith, honored but incomplete without Jesus. Christians hope Jews will recognize Jesus as Messiah but (in modern times) increasingly respect Jewish commitment to their covenant.

**For Both**: Respectful dialogue is possible. Understanding differences doesn't require erasing them. We can learn from each other, work together on shared ethical goals, and build mutual respect while maintaining our distinct beliefs.

As Rabbi Jonathan Sacks wrote: "God has spoken to mankind in many languages: through Judaism to Jews, Christianity to Christians, Islam to Muslims." Each tradition sees itself as having truth; respecting others doesn't require abandoning that conviction — only coupling it with humility and love.

For Chinese readers, these differences might seem puzzling: why can't these closely related traditions find common ground? But consider: philosophical Confucianism and religious Taoism both emerged in ancient China, share textual references, and coexist in Chinese culture — yet their metaphysical views differ fundamentally. Similarly, Judaism and Christianity share roots but diverge on ultimate questions.

The challenge — and opportunity — is to honor truth as each tradition sees it while respecting the other's sincerity, avoiding violence and hatred, and recognizing our common humanity as creatures made in the image of God.
