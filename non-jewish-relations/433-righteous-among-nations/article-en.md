# Righteous Among the Nations: Non-Jews Who Saved Jewish Lives

In humanity's darkest hour, when Nazi Germany systematically murdered six million Jews, thousands of non-Jews risked everything to save their neighbors. They weren't soldiers or politicians—they were farmers, teachers, diplomats, and factory workers who chose conscience over safety. Israel honors these heroes with the title "Righteous Among the Nations" (Chasidei Umot Ha'Olam), recognizing that even in the darkest evil, human goodness persists.

## A Recognition Born from Gratitude

In 1953, the Israeli Parliament (Knesset) established Yad Vashem—the World Holocaust Remembrance Center in Jerusalem. Among its missions was identifying and honoring non-Jews who risked their lives to save Jews during the Holocaust.

The title comes from the Talmud, which teaches that "the righteous among the nations have a share in the World to Come" (Tosefta Sanhedrin 13:2). This recognizes that righteousness isn't limited to Jews—moral courage transcends religion and nationality.

The criteria are strict:
- Active involvement in saving Jews from death or deportation
- Risk to the rescuer's own life, safety, or freedom
- No expectation of financial reward or conversion
- Testimony from survivors or reliable witnesses

To date, Yad Vashem has recognized over 27,000 Righteous Among the Nations from 51 countries. The true number of rescuers is certainly higher—many acted in secret, leaving no records, or survivors didn't know their rescuers' names.

## Motivations: Why They Risked Everything

Psychologists and historians have studied what motivated these rescuers. Unlike bystanders who looked away or perpetrators who participated, rescuers shared certain traits:

**Deep moral conviction**: Many cited simple principles—"It was the right thing to do" or "How could I not help?" Some were religious (Christian, Muslim, or secular humanist); all felt answerable to conscience above law or convenience.

**Personal connection**: Many rescuers knew Jewish neighbors personally. They couldn't abstract Jews into statistics; they saw Sarah, the baker's daughter, or David, who went to school with their son. Hatred requires dehumanization; rescue requires seeing humanity.

**Family values**: Rescuers often grew up in families that emphasized helping others. They watched their parents welcome strangers, share food with the poor, or stand up to injustice. Heroism was often learned at home.

**Sense of agency**: Unlike those who felt powerless, rescuers believed their actions mattered. Even when they couldn't save everyone, they could save someone.

The remarkable fact is how ordinary most rescuers were. They weren't saints or superheroes—they were regular people who faced an extraordinary moral choice and chose correctly.

## Stories of Courage

### Oskar Schindler: The Industrialist Who Saved 1,200

Oskar Schindler was an unlikely hero—a German industrialist, Nazi Party member, and opportunist who initially profited from Jewish slave labor in his factory in Kraków, Poland. But witnessing the brutal liquidation of the Kraków ghetto transformed him.

Schindler began protecting his Jewish workers, claiming they were "essential" to war production. He bribed Nazi officials, spent his entire fortune on protection money, and eventually compiled "Schindler's List"—1,200 Jews designated as necessary workers, thus saved from Auschwitz.

By war's end, Schindler was penniless. The Jews he saved collected money to help him, and he's buried in Jerusalem's Catholic cemetery on Mount Zion, his grave always covered with stones left by grateful visitors—a Jewish tradition honoring the dead.

His story, popularized by Steven Spielberg's film, illustrates a profound truth: people can change. Schindler began motivated by profit and ended motivated by conscience. Redemption is possible.

### Irena Sendler: Smuggling Children from the Warsaw Ghetto

Irena Sendler, a Polish social worker, witnessed children starving and dying in the Warsaw Ghetto. She obtained a pass to enter, ostensibly checking for typhus. Instead, she began smuggling children out—in ambulances, toolboxes, suitcases, through sewers and secret passages.

She placed children with Polish families or in convents, carefully recording their true names and hiding the lists in glass jars buried in a garden. She hoped to reunite them with families after the war—though most families had perished.

The Gestapo eventually arrested and tortured her, breaking her legs and feet. Fellow rescuers bribed guards for her release. She survived the war and lived until 2008, having saved approximately 2,500 children.

When asked about her heroism, she said, "Every child saved with my help is the justification of my existence on this earth, not a title to glory." She was troubled that she couldn't save more.

### Chiune Sugihara: The Japanese Schindler and the Shanghai Connection

For Chinese readers, Chiune Sugihara's story holds special significance, as his actions intersected with Shanghai's wartime Jewish community.

In 1940, Sugihara served as Japanese vice-consul in Kaunas, Lithuania. Thousands of Jewish refugees fleeing Nazi-occupied Poland begged for transit visas to escape through Japan. Japanese policy forbade issuing visas without proper documentation—which the refugees lacked—and required proof of final destination and financial means.

Sugihara cabled Tokyo three times requesting permission to issue visas. Three times he was refused. He faced an impossible choice: obey orders or save lives.

For 29 days, Sugihara worked 18-hour days, hand-writing visas. His wife Yukiko massaged his hands when they cramped. He issued visas even as his train departed Lithuania, throwing signed papers through the window. He saved approximately 6,000 Jews.

These visa holders traveled the Trans-Siberian Railway to Japan, then to their final destinations—many to Shanghai, which during 1938-1941 was one of the few places accepting Jewish refugees without visa requirements. Shanghai's Jewish community, though struggling itself, welcomed these refugees.

Sugihara's disobedience cost him his diplomatic career. Japan recalled him and he lived modestly until 1985, when Israeli survivors found him and honored him as Righteous Among the Nations. He died in 1986.

His wife Yukiko later said, "He told me that we might have to someday face difficulty because of these visas, but that he could not stand by and watch these people die. He said that it would be alright, as long as we were alive."

The Sugihara story is particularly important in the Chinese context because many of the Jews he saved ended up in Shanghai's International Settlement and the Hongkou ghetto (established by Japanese authorities in 1943). Today, the Shanghai Jewish Refugees Museum preserves this history, commemorating both Sugihara's courage and Shanghai's role as a haven.

### The Village That Said "No": Le Chambon-sur-Lignon

Sometimes rescue was collective. The French village of Le Chambon-sur-Lignon, led by Protestant pastor André Trocmé, refused to cooperate with Nazi deportation orders. The entire village—about 5,000 people—conspired to hide and protect Jews, eventually saving approximately 3,000-5,000 people.

When police came searching, children were hidden in the countryside. The village maintained this conspiracy for years, never producing a single Jew for deportation. When asked why, residents said simply, "It was the right thing to do." Their Huguenot ancestors had faced persecution, so they understood.

This story demonstrates that evil requires cooperation. When a community collectively refuses to participate, oppression becomes difficult.

### Raoul Wallenberg: The Diplomat Who Disappeared

Swedish diplomat Raoul Wallenberg arrived in Budapest in July 1944 as the Nazis began deporting Hungarian Jews to Auschwitz. He issued protective passes claiming the holder was under Swedish protection, rented buildings as "Swedish territory," and personally pulled Jews off deportation trains.

He saved tens of thousands before Soviet forces "liberated" Budapest. Suspecting him of espionage, the Soviets arrested him. He disappeared into the gulag system and was never seen again. His fate remains unknown, though he likely died in Soviet custody around 1947.

Wallenberg demonstrates the tragic reality that sometimes rescuers paid the ultimate price—not from those they opposed, but from unexpected quarters.

## The Question of Why So Few

If 27,000 were recognized as Righteous, millions were bystanders or collaborators. Why so few rescuers?

**Fear**: The penalty for helping Jews was death—not just for the rescuer but often for their entire family. This wasn't theoretical; Nazis executed many helpers they discovered.

**Antisemitism**: Centuries of Christian antisemitism had created deep prejudices. Nazi propaganda portrayed Jews as enemies. Many believed the lies or simply didn't care.

**Social pressure**: Helping Jews made one a traitor or troublemaker. Most people conform to social norms rather than resist them.

**Psychological distance**: It's easier to ignore suffering than to confront it. Many claimed they "didn't know," though that willful ignorance was itself a moral failure.

The Holocaust exposed an uncomfortable truth: most people, facing an extreme moral test, will follow orders, conform to the crowd, or protect their own interests. The righteous were exceptional precisely because they were rare.

## Honoring Memory: The Garden of the Righteous

At Yad Vashem, the Garden of the Righteous Among the Nations honors these heroes. Each recognized rescuer receives a medal and certificate, and in many cases, a tree was planted in their name (the practice ended when space ran out, but names are now inscribed on walls).

The recognition extends beyond the individual. In 1963, Yad Vashem recognized "the people of Denmark" collectively for their organized rescue of nearly all Danish Jews, smuggling them to Sweden in fishing boats. This remains one of the Holocaust's few bright moments—an entire nation refusing to cooperate with genocide.

## Lessons for Today

The Righteous Among the Nations offer lessons for every generation:

**Ordinary people can do extraordinary things**: You don't need superpowers to be a hero. You need conscience and courage.

**Small actions matter**: Many rescuers saved one or two people. The Talmud teaches, "Whoever saves one life, it is as if they saved an entire world" (Sanhedrin 37a).

**Moral choices are individual**: "Everyone else was doing it" is never an excuse. Each person faces their own moral choices and bears responsibility for them.

**Goodness transcends identity**: The rescuers were Christian, Muslim, atheist, from dozens of countries. Righteousness isn't limited by religion, nationality, or ethnicity.

**Indifference is a choice**: The opposite of love isn't hate—it's indifference. Bystanders who "did nothing" made a choice with consequences. Rescue requires choosing to act.

In a world still facing genocide, ethnic cleansing, and mass suffering, the Righteous Among the Nations remain relevant. They prove that even when surrounded by evil, individuals can choose good. They demonstrate that conscience can overcome fear, and that one person's courage can save worlds.

As Jews, we remember not only the six million who died but also the thousands who risked everything to help. We remember that darkness is never complete, that humanity persists even in inhumane times, and that righteousness exists among all nations.

The Hebrew word for "righteous"—tzaddik—also means "balanced" or "straight." The Righteous Among the Nations walked a straight path when the world had gone crooked. They stood upright when others bowed to evil. They remained human when humanity seemed lost.

Their memory is a blessing. Their example is a challenge.
