# The Ten Plagues at the Seder: Ritual, Memory, and Empathy

The recitation of the Ten Plagues is one of the Seder's most dramatic moments. As each plague is named — blood, frogs, lice, wild beasts, pestilence, boils, hail, locusts, darkness, death of the firstborn — participants remove a drop of wine from their cups, either with a finger or by pouring. This ancient custom, rooted in rabbinic tradition, transforms the plagues from distant biblical narrative into embodied ritual. More remarkably, it teaches one of Judaism's most profound ethical lessons: we diminish our joy when recalling our enemies' suffering.

## The Biblical Account

The Torah (Exodus 7-12) narrates how God sent ten plagues upon Egypt after Pharaoh repeatedly refused to free the enslaved Israelites. These plagues escalated in severity:

1. **Blood (Dam)** - Water turned to blood
2. **Frogs (Tzfarde'a)** - Frogs swarmed the land
3. **Lice (Kinim)** - Dust became lice covering people and animals
4. **Wild Beasts (Arov)** - Swarms of dangerous animals
5. **Pestilence (Dever)** - Disease killed Egyptian livestock
6. **Boils (Shechin)** - Painful skin eruptions on humans and animals
7. **Hail (Barad)** - Devastating hailstorm with fire
8. **Locusts (Arbeh)** - Locusts devoured all remaining vegetation
9. **Darkness (Choshech)** - Thick darkness covered Egypt for three days
10. **Death of the Firstborn (Makat Bechorot)** - Every Egyptian firstborn died at midnight

Each plague demonstrated God's power over nature and Egyptian deities, progressively breaking Pharaoh's resistance until he finally released the Israelites.

## The Custom: Removing Wine Drops

The Talmud doesn't explicitly mention removing wine at the plagues, but the custom appears in medieval texts and has become universal. The *Kol Bo* (13th-14th century) and *Maharil* (14th-15th century) describe Jews removing wine as plagues are mentioned, though the rationale isn't specified.

**The Method:** Most participants dip a finger into the wine cup and remove a drop, placing it on a plate or napkin, once for each plague. Some use a spoon instead of a finger. Sephardi Jews may pour a bit from the cup. Either way, the cup's contents decrease — we literally diminish our wine.

**Why Wine?** Wine symbolizes joy and celebration. At the Seder, we drink four cups of wine representing the four expressions of redemption (Exodus 6:6-7): "I will take you out," "I will deliver you," "I will redeem you," "I will take you to Me." Wine is our festive drink, making it the perfect symbol to diminish when remembering suffering.

## The Ethical Teaching: Compassion for Enemies

The most beautiful explanation comes from rabbinic tradition: We reduce our joy because the Egyptians — our oppressors — suffered.

The Talmud (Megillah 10b) relates that when the Israelites crossed the Red Sea and the Egyptian army drowned, the angels in heaven began singing praises to God. God rebuked them: "My handiwork is drowning in the sea, and you sing songs?" Even though the Egyptians had enslaved and murdered Israelite children, God did not rejoice in their destruction.

This is remarkable moral teaching: Your enemies' suffering should not bring you joy. Removing wine drops enacts this principle. We don't pour full glasses and toast the plagues. We diminish our celebration, acknowledging that even justified punishment involves tragedy.

## Sources of the Tradition

**Proverbs 24:17-18:** "Do not rejoice when your enemy falls, and let your heart not be glad when he stumbles, lest the Lord see it and it displease Him."

This biblical verse captures the ethical principle underlying the custom. Victory over evil is necessary, but celebration of others' suffering — even deserved suffering — is morally problematic.

**Midrash Tehillim (Psalm 136):** This rabbinic text notes that when reading Hallel (psalms of praise) during Passover week, we recite an abbreviated version (except on the first night) because of the Egyptians' drowning. Our joy is incomplete when it came at such cost.

**The Mechilta (ancient midrash on Exodus)** teaches that God saved Israel by dividing the sea, but took no pleasure in Egypt's destruction. This divine example models how humans should respond to enemies' downfall.

## Ten Drops, Many Interpretations

While removing drops for each plague is standard, various customs add more drops:

**Some add three drops** for the mnemonic *D'tzach Adash B'achav* (דצ״ך עד״ש באח״ב), which Rabbi Yehuda (Talmud, Pesachim 116a) used to remember the plagues by their Hebrew initials. These three acronyms make 13 drops total.

**Some traditions remove 16 drops**, adding one each for "wrath," "fury," and "trouble" when reciting the verse: "He sent upon them His fierce anger, wrath, fury, and trouble, a delegation of evil angels" (Psalm 78:49).

**The 16 drops (or 13, or 10)** symbolize incomplete celebration. Numerologically, 16 is one less than 17, which equals *tov* (טוב, "good") in Hebrew numerology. We're one drop short of complete goodness, because our redemption came through others' suffering.

## Empathy Without Moral Equivalence

An important nuance: diminishing our wine doesn't equate Israelite suffering with Egyptian suffering, nor does it suggest the plagues were unjust. The Egyptians enslaved a people for generations, murdered their male infants, and forced brutal labor. The plagues were divine justice responding to horrific evil.

Yet even so, Judaism teaches: Don't celebrate the mechanism of justice. The oppressed becoming free is good; oppressors suffering is tragic, even if necessary. This distinction prevents righteous vengeance from becoming cruelty.

Rabbi Jonathan Sacks wrote: "Judaism is a religion of love and compassion, but it is also a religion of justice. These are not contradictory values but complementary ones. Justice without compassion is cruelty. Compassion without justice is sentimentality."

The wine-drop custom embodies this balance: We remember the plagues (justice), but we diminish our celebration (compassion).

## Teaching Children

The plague recitation is vivid and engaging for children, who often enjoy naming each plague dramatically. Many Haggadahs illustrate the plagues with cartoonish images. Some families use props — plastic frogs, toy animals, flashlights to extinguish for darkness — making the storytelling multisensory.

But alongside the drama, the wine removal teaches empathy. Parents can explain: "We're happy we were freed, but sad that people had to suffer for us to go free. Even when someone does bad things, we don't want them to be hurt."

This is sophisticated moral education for young children: distinguishing between necessary consequences and gleeful revenge, between justice and cruelty, between celebration of freedom and celebration of enemies' suffering.

## Historical Memory and Contemporary Ethics

The lesson extends beyond ancient Egypt. Throughout history, Jews have faced oppression and sometimes, violently overthrown their oppressors. The wine-drop custom provides ethical guardrails: You may fight for freedom, but don't delight in bloodshed. You may defeat enemies, but don't dehumanize them.

This teaching took on new resonance after the Holocaust. When Israel gained independence in 1948, fighting wars for survival against hostile neighbors, Jewish leaders often invoked this principle: Defend yourself fiercely, but don't lose your humanity.

Prime Minister Golda Meir famously said: "We can forgive the Arabs for killing our children. We cannot forgive them for forcing us to kill their children. We will only have peace with the Arabs when they love their children more than they hate us."

This quote reflects the tension: military defense is necessary, but killing — even in justified war — leaves moral wounds. The wine drops acknowledge this.

## Connecting to Chinese Ethical Thought

Chinese philosophy similarly emphasizes tempering victory with humility. The *Daodejing* (Chapter 31) states: "Weapons are instruments of ill omen... When you have won a war, observe the rites of mourning."

Confucius taught that the superior person does not rejoice in others' misfortune. The concept of *ren* (仁, humanity/benevolence) requires treating even opponents with fundamental respect.

The difference is that Judaism weaves this teaching into ritual. It's not just philosophical — you physically remove wine drops, making the ethics embodied and memorable.

## The Sequence: Building Understanding

The Seder's structure places the plagues carefully:

**Before the plagues**: We tell the story of slavery, building empathy for the Israelites' suffering.

**During the plagues**: We recount divine justice, but remove wine, teaching empathy for the Egyptians.

**After the plagues**: We sing Dayenu (gratitude) and Hallel (praise), but with awareness that our freedom cost others dearly.

This sequence creates emotional complexity: gratitude mixed with sobriety, celebration tempered with memory, joy incomplete because of necessary violence.

## Modern Additions: Expanding Empathy

Some contemporary Seders add extra drops for modern suffering:
- Drops for refugees fleeing oppression today
- Drops for victims of famine and disease
- Drops for those still enslaved (human trafficking affects millions)
- Drops for environmental destruction

These additions extend the principle: If we diminish our joy for ancient Egyptians, how much more so should we acknowledge contemporary suffering?

This can be controversial. Some argue it dilutes the specific Exodus story. Others see it as natural evolution — applying timeless ethics to current realities.

## The Theological Dimension

The plagues raise difficult theological questions: Why did God harden Pharaoh's heart? Why punish Egyptians who didn't personally enslave Israelites? Why kill firstborn children?

Rabbis have grappled with these for millennia. Some interpretations:

**Pharaoh's hardened heart**: After repeatedly choosing cruelty, Pharaoh lost his capacity to repent. This teaches that persistent evil eventually becomes character, limiting future choice.

**Collective responsibility**: Egypt as a society benefited from slavery. Passive citizens who didn't actively oppose injustice shared moral culpability.

**The firstborn**: In ancient Near Eastern culture, the firstborn represented continuity and future. Their death symbolized Egypt's future being cut off as Israel's future had been threatened.

**Proportionate justice**: The plagues mirrored Egyptian crimes. They drowned Israelite boys; the sea drowned Egyptian soldiers. Measure for measure (*midah keneged midah*).

These explanations don't fully resolve the moral discomfort — and that's partly the point. The wine removal acknowledges that discomfort. We don't have to explain away every aspect of the story to still learn its ethical lessons.

## Non-Literal Readings

Some modern Jews read the plagues metaphorically:
- Each plague represents an internal "narrowness" (*mitzrayim*, the Hebrew word for Egypt, means "narrow places")
- The plagues symbolize stages of liberation from psychological bondage
- They represent natural consequences of oppressive systems, which ultimately destroy societies

These readings shift focus from historical events to timeless patterns, making the text relevant without requiring literal belief in the biblical narrative.

## The Ritual's Power

Whether one reads the plagues literally or metaphorically, the wine-drop ritual works on multiple levels:

**Cognitive**: It makes us think about the ethics of liberation narratives.

**Emotional**: It complicates our feelings, adding sobriety to celebration.

**Physical**: The bodily act — finger in cup, drop removed — creates lasting memory.

**Communal**: Doing this together reinforces shared values.

**Educational**: It sparks conversation, especially when children ask, "Why are we spilling our wine?"

This is ritual at its best — simple, memorable, meaningful, generative of ongoing discussion.

## Conclusion: The Wine We Don't Drink

At the Seder's end, our wine cups are full again — we drink the fourth cup entirely. We don't stay in diminishment. The removed drops teach empathy, not despair.

This is Judaism's both/and approach: Celebrate freedom *and* acknowledge its costs. Tell victory stories *and* remember the defeated. Drink deeply of joy *and* remove drops for suffering.

The drops we remove don't disappear — they stain the napkin or pool on the plate, visible reminders throughout the meal. We don't hide from the uncomfortable parts of our story. We integrate them.

For Chinese readers, this might resonate with the concept of holding contradictions — yin and yang, hardness and softness, celebration and mourning. The mature person doesn't collapse into simplicity but holds complexity with grace.

The ten drops of wine removed at the Seder are tiny — barely noticeable in the cup's volume. But their symbolic weight is enormous. They say: Even in our greatest celebration, we remember that freedom is rarely free, victory is rarely clean, and the divine image exists even in our enemies.

This is the genius of Jewish ritual — transforming abstract ethics into physical practice, making ancient stories speak to contemporary hearts, and ensuring that when we say "Never again" about oppression, we remember the full humanity of everyone involved. Ten drops, ten lessons, one profound hope: that we can celebrate liberation without losing our capacity for compassion.
