The Four Questions — *Ma Nishtana* in Hebrew — form one of the most recognized moments of the Passover Seder. Traditionally asked by the youngest person capable of reading them, these questions launch the telling of the Exodus story. But they are far more than a script for children; they embody a revolutionary educational philosophy that has sustained Jewish learning for millennia.

## The Full Text

### Hebrew (transliterated)

**Ma nishtana halaila hazeh mikol haleilot?**

1. **Sheb'chol haleilot anu ochlin chametz u'matzah, halaila hazeh kulo matzah.**

2. **Sheb'chol haleilot anu ochlin sh'ar y'rakot, halaila hazeh maror.**

3. **Sheb'chol haleilot ein anu matbilin afilu pa'am echat, halaila hazeh sh'tei f'amim.**

4. **Sheb'chol haleilot anu ochlin bein yoshvin u'vein m'subin, halaila hazeh kulanu m'subin.**

### English Translation

**Why is this night different from all other nights?**

1. **On all other nights we eat chametz (leavened bread) and matzah; on this night, only matzah.**

2. **On all other nights we eat all kinds of vegetables; on this night, maror (bitter herbs).**

3. **On all other nights we do not dip even once; on this night, we dip twice.**

4. **On all other nights we eat either sitting upright or reclining; on this night, we all recline.**

### Chinese Translation (中文翻译)

**今夜为何与其他所有夜晚不同?**

1. **在其他所有夜晚,我们吃发酵面包和无酵饼;但在今夜,只吃无酵饼。**

2. **在其他所有夜晚,我们吃各种蔬菜;但在今夜,我们吃苦菜。**

3. **在其他所有夜晚,我们一次也不蘸;但在今夜,我们蘸两次。**

4. **在其他所有夜晚,我们或坐直或斜倚进食;但在今夜,我们都斜倚。**

## The Structure: Questions Before Answers

Notice something remarkable: the Seder begins with questions, not answers. The Haggadah doesn't say "Tonight we will tell you about the Exodus." Instead, it creates curiosity. Why?

The Talmud (Pesachim 116a) explains that the entire Seder is designed to provoke children to ask questions. The word *Haggadah* means "the telling," but Jewish pedagogy understands that true learning begins with wondering, not with passive listening. These four questions are formal, but they model a broader principle: Judaism values the question as much as the answer.

Confucius taught, "Learning without thought is labor lost; thought without learning is perilous." The Ma Nishtana embodies this balance — we notice what's different (thought), then learn the story that explains it (learning).

## Question One: Why Only Matzah?

**The Surface Answer:** During Passover week, we eat no leavened bread (*chametz*), only matzah — unleavened flatbread. This commemorates the Exodus, when the Israelites fled Egypt so quickly that their bread dough had no time to rise (Exodus 12:39).

**The Deeper Meaning:** Matzah is called *lechem oni* — "bread of affliction" (Deuteronomy 16:3). It represents both poverty (the simple bread of slaves) and freedom (the bread of those who escaped). This dual symbolism teaches that the line between slavery and freedom is thin; we must remember our origins even in prosperity.

The Talmud (Berachot 17a) notes that leaven (*se'or*) represents arrogance and the "evil inclination" because it puffs up. Matzah, flat and humble, symbolizes humility and spiritual readiness. Removing chametz from our homes is physical; removing arrogance from our hearts is spiritual. The same act operates on multiple levels.

Matzah is also called "the bread over which many words are spoken" — the entire Haggadah is recited while matzah sits before us, making it not just food but a teaching tool.

## Question Two: Why Maror (Bitter Herbs)?

**The Surface Answer:** We eat bitter herbs to remember the bitterness of slavery. The Torah commands eating the Passover offering "with matzah and bitter herbs" (Numbers 9:11).

**The Deeper Meaning:** The Hebrew word *maror* comes from the root *mar* (bitter), the same root as *Miriam* (Moses' sister) and *mar'ah* (vision). Bitterness, paradoxically, can lead to insight. The Egyptian slavery was bitter, but it forged the Jewish people's identity.

The Talmud (Pesachim 39a) specifies vegetables like horseradish or romaine lettuce. Interestingly, romaine starts sweet and becomes bitter as it matures — mirroring the Israelites' experience in Egypt. Joseph's family arrived welcomed and honored; generations later, their descendants were enslaved. Historical memory must account for transformation over time.

Rabbi Samson Raphael Hirsch (19th century) taught that we eat maror not to punish ourselves but to develop empathy. Having tasted bitterness, we become sensitive to others' suffering. The Torah repeatedly commands kindness to strangers "because you were strangers in Egypt" (Exodus 22:20, 23:9, Deuteronomy 10:19). The maror is experiential ethics training.

## Question Three: Why Dip Twice?

**The Surface Answer:** During the Seder, we dip twice — first karpas (a green vegetable) into salt water, later maror into charoset (the sweet paste). On regular nights, we don't necessarily dip vegetables at all.

**The Deeper Meaning:** The Talmud (Pesachim 116a) notes that dipping was a sign of luxury in ancient times (like sauce with appetizers at a feast). The double dipping signals that this is a special, festive night — yet what we dip includes reminders of tears (salt water) and slavery (bitter herbs). Even our celebration incorporates memory of suffering.

Some commentators see deeper symbolism: the two dippings parallel two descents and two ascents in Jewish history. The first dipping (karpas in salt water) recalls going down to Egypt and the tears of slavery. The second dipping (maror in charoset) represents going into exile after the Temple's destruction, but the sweetness of charoset hints at future redemption.

Another interpretation: dipping transforms the thing dipped. Karpas (life) touched by salt water (tears) teaches that suffering shapes us. Maror (bitterness) softened by charoset (sweetness) teaches that even bitterness can be integrated into meaning. Transformation is the theme.

## Question Four: Why Recline?

**The Surface Answer:** In ancient times, free people reclined on cushions while eating, leaning on their left side. Slaves stood or sat upright. By reclining at the Seder, we enact freedom.

**The Deeper Meaning:** The Hebrew word for reclining is *heseibah*, related to *mesibah* (party/feast). Freedom isn't just political or legal — it's psychological and social. Free people can relax, take their time, enjoy their meal. The reclining posture is a physical manifestation of inner liberation.

Interestingly, the Talmud (Pesachim 108a) specifies reclining on the *left* side, leaving the right hand free for eating (most people being right-handed). This detail shows Judaism's attention to embodied practice — spiritual truths are expressed through physical actions.

The Shulchan Aruch (Code of Jewish Law) rules that women should also recline, because they too were redeemed from Egypt. This was progressive for the 16th century and remains a statement about equality in freedom.

Some modern commentators note that in many cultures today, people sit upright at formal dinners, while reclining might seem casual. The principle remains: adopt the posture of free people in your context. If sitting upright is how free people eat in your culture, then sit with dignity. The symbolism is flexible; the meaning is constant.

## Why These Four Questions?

The Mishnah (Pesachim 10:4) actually lists different questions than we ask today, including why we eat roasted meat. After the Temple's destruction made Passover sacrifice impossible, the questions evolved. This demonstrates that Jewish tradition is living, not fossilized — it adapts while preserving core meanings.

These four questions focus on *differences* because noticing difference is the beginning of learning. Children naturally ask "Why?" when something breaks routine. The Seder deliberately creates anomalies — we eat strange bread, bitter herbs, dip twice, recline — to trigger curiosity. The entire night becomes a teaching moment.

The questions progress from food (matzah, maror) to actions (dipping, reclining), from what we consume to how we behave. This movement suggests that freedom encompasses both material conditions and behavioral choices.

## The Educational Philosophy

In traditional Chinese education, the teacher transmits knowledge; the student receives. In the Ma Nishtana, we see a different model: the child questions; the adult responds with a story; everyone participates in discussion. The Talmud states, "The one who asks is wise" (Pesachim 116a).

This approach treats even young children as thinking participants, not passive recipients. It assumes curiosity is innate and should be cultivated, not suppressed. It makes learning a dialogue, not a monologue.

The Ma Nishtana teaches children that their questions matter, that tradition welcomes inquiry, and that observant practice should be thoughtful, not rote. These lessons extend beyond Passover. A child who learns to ask "Why is tonight different?" will grow into an adult who asks deeper questions about ethics, justice, and meaning.

## When There Is No Child

What if no children are present at the Seder? The Talmud (Pesachim 116a) rules that adults ask the questions themselves, even if dining alone. Why? Because we must always cultivate our own curiosity. Even if we've attended 80 Seders, we ask again, because this year we are different people in a different world, and the same questions may yield new answers.

The Hasidic master Rebbe Nachman of Breslov taught that a person should always see themselves as beginning again, with fresh eyes. The Ma Nishtana ritualizes this renewal — every year, we return to wonder.

## Conclusion: Questions as Sacred Practice

The Four Questions transform a meal into an educational experience, a historical commemoration into a living encounter. They democratize knowledge by empowering even the youngest to initiate the central ritual. They model intellectual humility — even experts return to foundational questions.

For Chinese readers, this practice might resonate with the Confucian emphasis on filial piety and generational transmission — but with a twist. Here, the child isn't just receiving tradition; the child activates it. The youngest becomes the catalyst for the entire gathering's purpose.

In asking *Ma Nishtana*, we acknowledge that not all nights are the same, not all moments are equal, and some stories must be told again and again because their truth is inexhaustible. We ask why tonight is different because remembering difference prevents complacency. We ask even when we know the answers because questions keep us awake, alert, and alive.

As Elie Wiesel wrote, "The questions remain questions." And perhaps that's the deepest truth of all — some questions aren't meant to be resolved but to be lived with, returned to, and passed down. The Ma Nishtana ensures that every generation inherits not just answers but the sacred practice of asking.
