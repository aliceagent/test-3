## Introduction

As the sun sets on Saturday evening and three stars appear in the sky, Jews around the world prepare for a ceremony as beautiful as it is meaningful. With wine, spices, and braided candlelight, we bid farewell to Shabbat and step back into the ordinary week ahead.

This ceremony is called **Havdalah** (הַבְדָּלָה), which means "separation" or "distinction." It marks the boundary between the sacred and the mundane, between the holy day of rest and the six days of work. Unlike the solemn tone of many religious rituals, Havdalah carries a sense of both sweet sadness and hopeful anticipation — we are reluctant to let go of Shabbat's peace, yet eager to carry its light into the week ahead.

For someone new to Judaism, Havdalah offers a gentle introduction to Jewish ritual life: it engages all five senses, takes only a few minutes, and can be performed at home with simple items. The ceremony originated in ancient times — during what corresponds to China's Han Dynasty (汉朝, 206 BCE - 220 CE) — when the Talmudic sages established the blessings and customs we still follow today.

In this article, we'll explore what Havdalah is, why we do it, and how to perform the ceremony step by step — whether you're observing it alone in Beijing or with friends in Hong Kong.

## What is Havdalah?

### The Meaning of Separation

Just as we usher in Shabbat with the Kiddush blessing over wine on Friday night, we mark its departure with Havdalah on Saturday night. The core idea is **havdalah** itself — the act of distinguishing, separating, creating boundaries.

This concept appears at the very beginning of the Torah: "God separated between the light and the darkness" (Genesis 1:4). Throughout creation, God established distinctions: between day and night, land and sea, the sacred and the ordinary. By performing Havdalah, we align ourselves with this divine pattern of making meaningful distinctions in our lives.

The Talmud (Pesachim 103b) records the words of the Havdalah blessing:

> "Blessed are You, Lord our God, King of the universe, **who distinguishes between the sacred and the mundane, between light and darkness, between Israel and the nations, between the seventh day and the six days of work.**"

These four distinctions remind us that boundaries are not walls — they are frameworks that give life structure and meaning.

### A Multi-Sensory Experience

What makes Havdalah unique among Jewish rituals is how it engages all five senses:

- **Taste**: We drink wine (or grape juice)
- **Smell**: We inhale the fragrance of sweet spices
- **Sight**: We gaze at the braided candle's flame
- **Touch**: We feel the warmth of the candle and the cup in our hands
- **Hearing**: We listen to the blessings being recited

This full-body involvement reflects a deep Jewish truth: spiritual life is not abstract or detached from the physical world. We serve God with our entire being — body, mind, and soul together.

## When Do We Perform Havdalah?

Shabbat ends when **three medium-sized stars are visible in the night sky** (Shabbat 35b). This varies by location and season, but in most parts of the world, it occurs approximately 40-72 minutes after sunset.

A simple rule of thumb used in North America: **add one hour and 10 minutes to Friday's candle-lighting time**. For example, if you lit candles at 6:00 PM Friday, Shabbat ends around 7:10 PM Saturday night.

You can find exact times for your city using online tools like [Chabad.org's Candle Lighting Times](https://www.chabad.org/calendar/candlelighting_cdo/aid/6226/jewish/Accurate-Shabbat-Candle-Lighting-Times.htm), [MyZmanim](https://www.myzmanim.com/), or [Hebcal](https://www.hebcal.com/shabbat/).

### What If You Don't Have Wine or a Candle?

If the proper time has passed and you don't have the items needed for a full Havdalah ceremony, you can still begin weekday activities by reciting these words in Hebrew:

**בָּרוּךְ הַמַּבְדִּיל בֵּין קֹדֶשׁ לְחוֹל**  
*Baruch ha-mavdil bein kodesh le-chol.*  
"Blessed is the One who distinguishes between the sacred and the mundane."

This short declaration serves as a "mini-Havdalah" and allows you to begin your weekday tasks. However, you are still obligated to perform the full Havdalah ceremony over wine as soon as possible — even if it's Sunday or Monday.

## What You'll Need

To perform Havdalah, gather these four items:

### 1. A Cup of Wine (or Grape Juice)

Use a **kiddush cup** — a special goblet, ideally made of silver or glass. Fill it until the wine overflows slightly onto a plate below. This symbolic overflow represents our hope that blessings will spill abundantly into the coming week.

Red wine is traditionally preferred, though white wine or grape juice is also acceptable.

### 2. Sweet-Smelling Spices (בְּשָׂמִים, *Besamim*)

Whole cloves are the most common choice because they stay fragrant for a long time. Other options include cinnamon sticks, myrtle leaves (הֲדַס, *hadas*), or a mixture of aromatic spices.

These are often kept in a decorative **spice box** (*besamim box*), a beautiful Jewish ritual object that can be passed down through generations.

**Why spices?** The Talmud teaches that on Shabbat, every Jew receives an **extra soul** (*neshamah yeteirah*) — a heightened spiritual capacity. When Shabbat ends, this additional soul departs, and we feel a subtle sense of loss. The sweet fragrance revives and comforts us during this transition.

### 3. A Braided Candle

Use a **Havdalah candle** — a candle with at least two wicks braided together. The intertwined flames symbolize the unity of Shabbat and the merging of spiritual insight we gained during the holy day.

If you don't have a braided candle, you can hold two regular candles together with their flames touching.

**Why fire?** Fire was one of the first discoveries Adam made after the first Shabbat ended. According to tradition (Genesis Rabbah 11:2), when darkness fell on Saturday night after the world's first Shabbat, Adam was afraid. God then taught him to strike two stones together to create fire — humanity's first act of creative work after the day of rest.

### 4. A Plate or Piece of Foil

You'll need something to catch drips from the overflowing wine and to extinguish the candle at the end.

## How to Perform Havdalah: Step-by-Step

Havdalah consists of **five sections**:

### Step 1: Introductory Verses

Hold the full cup of wine in your right hand and recite or sing these joyous verses from Isaiah and Psalms:

> "Behold, God is my salvation; I will trust and not be afraid, for God is my strength and song, and He has become my salvation. You shall draw water with joy from the springs of salvation. Salvation is the Lord's; may Your blessing be upon Your people. The Lord of hosts is with us; the God of Jacob is our stronghold. Lord of hosts, happy is the one who trusts in You. Lord, save us; may the King answer us on the day we call."

These verses establish a hopeful, confident tone as we step into the new week.

### Step 2: Blessing Over Wine

Recite the blessing over the wine:

**בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה ה׳ אֱלֹהֵינוּ מֶלֶךְ הָעוֹלָם, בּוֹרֵא פְּרִי הַגָּפֶן.**

*Baruch atah Adonai, Eloheinu melech ha'olam, borei p'ri ha-gafen.*

"Blessed are You, Lord our God, King of the universe, who creates the fruit of the vine."

**Do not drink yet** — we will drink at the very end of the ceremony.

### Step 3: Blessing Over Spices

Pick up the spice box or container and recite:

**בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה ה׳ אֱלֹהֵינוּ מֶלֶךְ הָעוֹלָם, בּוֹרֵא מִינֵי בְשָׂמִים.**

*Baruch atah Adonai, Eloheinu melech ha'olam, borei minei v'samim.*

"Blessed are You, Lord our God, King of the universe, who creates various kinds of spices."

**Now smell the spices.** Pass the spice box around so everyone present can inhale the fragrance. This is a moment to pause, close your eyes, and let the aroma comfort your soul.

### Step 4: Blessing Over Fire

Someone lights the braided Havdalah candle. Hold your hands near the flame with fingers slightly spread, and look at your fingernails in the candlelight. Observe how the light creates shadows on your hands and illuminates the contours of your fingers.

Recite:

**בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה ה׳ אֱלֹהֵינוּ מֶלֶךְ הָעוֹלָם, בּוֹרֵא מְאוֹרֵי הָאֵשׁ.**

*Baruch atah Adonai, Eloheinu melech ha'olam, borei m'orei ha'esh.*

"Blessed are You, Lord our God, King of the universe, who creates the lights of fire."

Notice the plural — "lights" (*m'orei*), not "light." This refers to the multiple wicks of the braided candle.

**Why look at our fingernails?** To make use of the firelight immediately after reciting the blessing. We demonstrate that the fire is useful, practical — not just symbolic. Some also say that looking at the fingernails, which grow constantly, reminds us of renewal and the week of productivity ahead.

### Step 5: The Havdalah Blessing

Place the cup of wine back in your right hand and recite the central blessing:

**בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה ה׳ אֱלֹהֵינוּ מֶלֶךְ הָעוֹלָם, הַמַּבְדִּיל בֵּין קֹדֶשׁ לְחוֹל, בֵּין אוֹר לְחֹשֶׁךְ, בֵּין יִשְׂרָאֵל לָעַמִּים, בֵּין יוֹם הַשְּׁבִיעִי לְשֵׁשֶׁת יְמֵי הַמַּעֲשֶׂה. בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה ה׳, הַמַּבְדִּיל בֵּין קֹדֶשׁ לְחוֹל.**

*Baruch atah Adonai, Eloheinu melech ha'olam, ha-mavdil bein kodesh le-chol, bein or le-choshech, bein Yisrael la-amim, bein yom ha-shvi'i le-sheishet yemei ha-ma'aseh. Baruch atah Adonai, ha-mavdil bein kodesh le-chol.*

"Blessed are You, Lord our God, King of the universe, who distinguishes between the sacred and the mundane, between light and darkness, between Israel and the nations, between the seventh day and the six days of work. Blessed are You, Lord, who distinguishes between the sacred and the mundane."

Now **drink most of the wine** (at least a cheekful — about 1.5 fluid ounces). It is customary to sit down while drinking.

### Step 6: Extinguish the Candle

Pour a bit of the remaining wine from the cup onto a plate to extinguish the flame. As the flame goes out with a hissing sound, the ceremony is complete.

Some people dip their fingers in the spilled wine and touch their eyes or pockets — a symbolic gesture wishing for spiritual insight and prosperity in the week ahead.

### Traditional Song: *Eliyahu HaNavi*

After extinguishing the candle, it is customary to sing:

> **Eliyahu HaNavi, Eliyahu HaTishbi,**  
> **Eliyahu, Eliyahu, Eliyahu HaGiladi.**  
> **Bimheirah v'yameinu yavo eleinu,**  
> **Im Mashiach ben David, im Mashiach ben David.**

"Elijah the Prophet, Elijah the Tishbite, Elijah the Gileadite — may he come speedily in our days, with the Messiah son of David."

This song expresses hope that the prophet Elijah, herald of the Messiah, will arrive soon, perhaps even during the coming week.

## The Deeper Meaning: Light and Darkness, Sacred and Mundane

Havdalah is far more than a ritual checklist. Each element carries profound symbolism that speaks to how we navigate life.

### The Dance of Light and Shadow

When we hold our fingers before the Havdalah candle, we see both **light and shadow** playing across our hands. This mirrors the human experience: life is not purely light or purely dark. We live in the interplay between the two.

The Chinese philosophical tradition understands this well. The concept of **yin and yang (阴阳)** teaches that opposites are not enemies but complementary forces. Light needs darkness to be recognized; rest gives meaning to work; the sacred is defined against the backdrop of the ordinary.

Havdalah teaches us to embrace this duality without confusion. We don't blur the boundaries — we celebrate them. Shabbat is holy; the weekdays are for creative work. Both have their place, their purpose, their dignity.

### Fire: Humanity's First Creative Act

According to Jewish tradition, when Adam awoke after the first Shabbat, darkness fell for the first time. He had never experienced night before. Frightened, he cried out to God. God responded by granting him the wisdom to strike two stones together and create fire.

This story teaches that **human creativity begins where Shabbat ends.** During Shabbat, we refrain from creative work and receive divine rest. But when Shabbat departs, we become partners with God in improving the world. Fire — the symbol of technology, progress, and human ingenuity — is our first tool.

In this sense, Havdalah is not just a farewell to rest, but a **commissioning for the week ahead.** We are being sent back into the world, carrying the spiritual clarity we gained on Shabbat, ready to transform the mundane into the meaningful.

### The Comfort of Spices: Holding On to Holiness

The Talmud (Beitzah 16a) teaches that every Jew receives an *neshamah yeteirah* — an "additional soul" — during Shabbat. This heightened spiritual sensitivity allows us to experience deeper joy, greater peace, and clearer insight.

When Shabbat ends, this extra soul departs. We feel its loss, even if we can't articulate it. The sweet fragrance of the spices serves as a gentle comfort, reviving our spirits and reminding us that even as Shabbat departs, its influence remains within us.

In traditional Chinese culture, incense plays a similar role during important ceremonies and transitions. The lingering fragrance marks the passage of time and sanctifies moments of change. Havdalah's spices perform the same function: they sanctify the transition from sacred rest to productive work.

## Havdalah Alone: Observing the Ceremony in Isolation

What if you live in a city without a Jewish community? What if you're the only person in your office tower who knows what Havdalah is?

**You can — and should — still perform Havdalah.**

Judaism is a communal religion, but it does not require a minyan (prayer quorum) for Havdalah. You can do it alone in your apartment, in a hotel room while traveling, or even outdoors under the stars.

Here are some tips for making Havdalah meaningful when you're on your own:

1. **Create a dedicated space.** Clear a small table. Set out your wine cup, spices, and candle. Even if it's just a corner of your desk, make it beautiful.

2. **Recite the blessings slowly.** When you're alone, there's no rush. Say each word with intention. Let the Hebrew syllables fill the room.

3. **Linger with the spices.** Close your eyes. Breathe deeply. Let the fragrance remind you that you are not alone — you are part of an ancient people who have been doing this for over two thousand years.

4. **Use technology to connect.** Play a recording of Havdalah songs. Listen to a rabbi recite the blessings and follow along. Join a live Zoom Havdalah hosted by Chabad or another community.

5. **Journal afterward.** Spend five minutes writing down one insight from Shabbat that you want to carry into the week. This transforms Havdalah from a ritual ending into a bridge between rest and action.

## Cultural Parallels: Transitions in Chinese Tradition

While Havdalah is uniquely Jewish, the human need to mark transitions is universal.

In traditional Chinese culture, **tea ceremonies** serve a similar function: they create a pause, a moment of mindfulness that separates one activity from another. The careful preparation of tea, the silence, the attention to aroma and taste — all echo the multi-sensory nature of Havdalah.

Similarly, the Chinese practice of **burning incense at ancestral altars** or during festivals sanctifies the boundary between the everyday and the sacred. The rising smoke carries prayers upward, just as the fragrance of Havdalah spices lifts our spirits.

The Confucian concept of **ritual propriety (礼, lǐ)** also resonates with Havdalah. Confucius taught that rituals are not empty formalities but the structure through which human beings elevate ordinary life into something meaningful. By performing Havdalah with care and intention, we transform a simple cup of wine into a vessel of blessing, a spice box into a source of comfort, a candle into a symbol of human creativity.

## Common Questions About Havdalah

### 1. What if I missed Havdalah on Saturday night?

You can still recite Havdalah at any point until **Tuesday at sunset.** The blessing over fire is omitted if you're doing Havdalah after Sunday, but the other blessings remain.

### 2. Can I use grape juice instead of wine?

Yes. Grape juice is completely acceptable, especially if you don't drink alcohol or if wine is unavailable.

### 3. Can women perform Havdalah?

Absolutely. While some traditional communities have customs where men lead, halachically (according to Jewish law), women are fully obligated in Havdalah and may recite all the blessings.

### 4. What if I don't have a braided candle?

Hold two regular candles together with their flames touching. This creates the same effect of intertwined light.

### 5. Do I have to stand during Havdalah?

Most people sit during Havdalah, especially when drinking the wine at the end. There is no requirement to stand.

### 6. Can I perform Havdalah if I didn't observe Shabbat?

Halachic opinions vary. However, from a spiritual perspective, Havdalah can serve as a meaningful practice even for someone who is not yet fully observant. It's a step toward greater Shabbat observance.

## Key Takeaways

- **Havdalah marks the end of Shabbat** — the transition from the sacred to the ordinary, performed after three stars appear in the night sky.

- **The ceremony engages all five senses**: taste (wine), smell (spices), sight (candle), touch (warmth of flame), and hearing (blessings).

- **Four blessings are recited**: over wine, over spices, over fire, and the Havdalah blessing that distinguishes between sacred and mundane.

- **Havdalah can be performed alone** — no minyan (prayer quorum) is required, making it accessible even in isolated locations.

- **The spices comfort us** as the "extra soul" of Shabbat departs, and the braided candle recalls Adam's first creative act after the first Shabbat.

- **Fire symbolizes human creativity** — the work of the week ahead, where we become God's partners in improving the world.

- **Havdalah is not just an ending, but a beginning** — a commissioning to carry Shabbat's peace and clarity into six days of purposeful work.
