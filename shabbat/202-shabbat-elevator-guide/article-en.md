# The Shabbat Elevator: How It Works and When You Can Use It

If you've ever stayed in a hotel in Israel or visited an Orthodox Jewish community, you may have noticed something peculiar: elevators that stop automatically at every floor on Shabbat. No buttons pressed, no manual control—just a slow, methodical ascent and descent, pausing dutifully at each level. This is the "Shabbat elevator," and for many observant Jews, it's a technological solution to a halachic challenge.

But what exactly makes a regular elevator unusable on Shabbat? How does the Shabbat mode address these concerns? And are there situations where even this accommodation doesn't work? Let's explore the fascinating intersection of ancient law and modern technology.

## The Core Problem: Electricity on Shabbat

To understand Shabbat elevators, we first need to grasp why using a regular elevator on Shabbat is problematic. The Torah prohibits thirty-nine categories of creative work (*melachot*) on Shabbat—activities that were used in building the Mishkan (Tabernacle) in the wilderness. These include lighting fire, extinguishing fire, and completing an object.

When you press a button in a modern elevator, several electrical actions occur:
- You complete an electrical circuit
- A signal is sent to the elevator's control system
- Motors activate to move the cab
- Lights illuminate to indicate the floor
- In some systems, a digital display changes

Many halachic authorities consider pressing an elevator button to be similar to lighting a fire (because electricity flows) or building/completing a structure (because you're activating a dormant system). While there's debate among poskim (rabbinic authorities) about the exact nature of the prohibition, most Orthodox authorities agree that directly activating an elevator violates Shabbat.

## The Shabbat Elevator Solution

The Shabbat elevator operates on a pre-programmed timer. Before Shabbat begins, the elevator is set to "Shabbat mode," which typically works like this:

1. **Automatic operation**: The elevator runs continuously, stopping at every floor in a predetermined pattern (usually ascending to the top floor, then descending to the bottom, then repeating).

2. **No manual control**: All buttons are disabled—pressing them has no effect. Passengers cannot select their destination floor.

3. **Extended door time**: Doors remain open longer at each floor to allow passengers to enter or exit without rushing.

4. **Disabled sensors**: Many elevators disable door sensors that would normally prevent closing if something is in the doorway. This is because triggering such a sensor might cause electrical changes.

By removing all human agency from the elevator's operation, the Shabbat elevator avoids the halachic issues of directly causing electrical circuits to activate. You're simply entering a moving vehicle that operates independently of your actions—similar to boarding a continuously running escalator or moving walkway.

## When Can You Use a Shabbat Elevator?

The permissibility of using a Shabbat elevator depends on several factors, and different communities have different customs (minhagim).

### Generally Permitted:
- **Ascending floors**: Most authorities permit using a Shabbat elevator to go up, especially in apartment buildings where climbing many flights of stairs would be difficult.
- **Elderly and health needs**: Those with mobility issues, heart conditions, or significant difficulty climbing stairs may use Shabbat elevators with even more leniency.
- **Young children**: Families with small children who cannot manage many flights of stairs have more flexibility.

### Areas of Concern:
- **Descending floors**: Some authorities are stricter about descending, arguing that your added weight makes the elevator motor work harder (called *pesik reisha d'lo nicha lei*—an inevitable but undesired consequence). Others permit it, noting that modern elevator systems compensate automatically and your individual weight doesn't directly cause additional work.
- **Weight sensors**: If the elevator uses weight sensors to determine when to close doors or whether to move, entering or exiting could trigger electrical changes. Most modern Shabbat-mode elevators disable these sensors.
- **Emergency situations**: In cases of genuine danger (pikuach nefesh), all Shabbat prohibitions are suspended, and you should use a regular elevator immediately.

### Different Standards in Different Places:
- **Israel**: Shabbat elevators are ubiquitous in apartment buildings, hospitals, and hotels. Most Israeli Orthodox Jews use them freely, especially for ascending.
- **Haredi (Ultra-Orthodox) communities**: Some strictly observant communities avoid Shabbat elevators entirely, preferring to climb stairs regardless of difficulty, viewing this as the safest halachic approach.
- **Sephardic vs. Ashkenazic traditions**: Sephardic authorities (following the rulings of Rav Ovadia Yosef zt"l) tend to be more lenient, while some Ashkenazic authorities are more stringent.

## The Engineering Challenge

Creating a truly kosher Shabbat elevator is more complex than simply programming a timer. Modern elevators contain numerous computerized systems that can create unexpected halachic issues:

- **Digital displays**: These change when the elevator moves, potentially violating prohibitions against writing or erasing.
- **Weight sensors**: As mentioned, these can cause electrical responses to human presence.
- **Energy-saving features**: Some systems slow down or adjust motor speed based on load, meaning passenger weight affects electrical consumption.
- **Security cameras**: If the elevator records or processes video, this raises questions about creating images on Shabbat.
- **Internet-connected systems**: Modern "smart" elevators that report status to maintenance systems may transmit data when passengers board.

The Institute for Science and Halacha in Israel has developed detailed guidelines for Shabbat-mode elevators, working with manufacturers to ensure these systems truly operate independently of passenger actions. Certified Shabbat elevators receive halachic approval from recognized rabbinic authorities.

## A Cultural Parallel: Respecting Rest Days

The concept of modifying technology to preserve a day of rest has parallels in Chinese cultural history. During the Tang Dynasty (similar to the period of the Geonim in Jewish history, roughly 618-907 CE), government officials observed rest days called *xiūmù* (休沐), literally "rest and wash." On these days, officials were expected to refrain from official duties and avoid unnecessary activity.

While xiūmù wasn't as comprehensive as Shabbat, both traditions recognize something profound: true rest requires intention. Just as a Shabbat elevator preserves the distinction between workday functionality and Shabbat rest, the xiūmù days acknowledged that constant work erodes human dignity and spiritual health.

In modern terms, think of it this way: Shabbat is like putting your entire life in "airplane mode"—not because you're flying somewhere, but because you need to disconnect from the ceaseless demands of creation and control. The Shabbat elevator is one small mechanism that makes this possible in our technologically saturated world.

## Practical Advice

If you're visiting a community or location with Shabbat elevators, here's what you should know:

1. **Plan ahead**: Shabbat elevators move slowly and stop at every floor. Allow extra time for travel.

2. **Ask your host or rabbi**: Different communities have different customs. When in doubt, ask what the local practice is.

3. **Consider the climb**: If you're able-bodied and visiting someone only a few floors up, climbing stairs avoids all halachic concerns and provides exercise.

4. **Health comes first**: If climbing stairs poses any health risk, using the Shabbat elevator is absolutely permitted. Don't endanger yourself out of stringency.

5. **Non-Jews traveling with you**: A non-Jew can use a regular elevator on Shabbat, but asking them to press buttons specifically for your benefit raises complex questions of *amira l'akum* (asking a non-Jew to do prohibited work). Consult a rabbi for specific situations.

6. **Emergencies**: Again, if there's any danger, use whatever means necessary. Life overrides all Shabbat restrictions.

## The Deeper Question: Is This What Shabbat Is About?

Some critics, both within and outside the Orthodox community, view Shabbat elevators as the epitome of legalistic hairsplitting—finding technical loopholes to "get around" Shabbat restrictions. Why not just climb the stairs?

Defenders of Shabbat elevators respond on several levels:

**Practical mercy**: Judaism isn't asceticism. The goal of Shabbat isn't to make life difficult, but to create sacred rest. For someone elderly, disabled, or living on the 15th floor of an apartment building, climbing stairs could make Shabbat unbearable rather than restful.

**Precedent in Jewish law**: The principle of *shinui* (changing the normal manner of doing something) appears throughout halacha. The Shabbat elevator operates on a similar logic—changing the normal human-initiated operation to a pre-programmed, automatic one transforms the nature of the action.

**Consistency**: If we permit carrying items within an eruv (an enclosed area created by symbolic boundaries), using hot water from a tank heated before Shabbat, or eating food kept warm on a hotplate, why would we categorically reject all accommodation for modern life? Judaism has always distinguished between the spirit and letter of law, seeking ways to observe both.

**Preserving observance**: In practical terms, if the choice is between allowing Shabbat elevators or watching people abandon Shabbat observance entirely because it's too difficult, the former preserves Jewish practice while the latter loses it.

That said, the controversy is real and reflects a deeper tension in Orthodox Judaism: how much should technology solve halachic problems, and when should we accept inconvenience as part of the practice?

## Conclusion

The Shabbat elevator is a small but significant example of how Jewish law adapts to technological change. It represents neither blind adherence to ancient rules nor casual abandonment of tradition, but rather a careful negotiation between two values: preserving the sacred cessation from creative work that defines Shabbat, and ensuring that observance remains livable in a modern world.

Whether you personally use a Shabbat elevator is a question to discuss with your rabbi, taking into account your health, your community's customs, and your own understanding of halacha. What's most important is understanding the principles at stake: Shabbat isn't about arbitrary rules, but about creating a weekly sanctuary in time—a day when we step back from being creators and simply exist as creations, resting in gratitude for the world God made.

And if that rest requires a slowly ascending elevator to get you home to your family, well—that's what the halacha is there for.
