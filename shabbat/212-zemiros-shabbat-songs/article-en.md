# Zemiros Lyrics and Melodies: Learn the Songs of the Shabbat Table

## Introduction

---

Walk into any Orthodox Jewish home on Friday night, and you'll likely hear something magical: families gathered around the Shabbat table singing together. These aren't modern pop songs or religious hymns from a prayer book—they're *zemiros* (זְמִירוֹת), traditional songs that have been sung at Shabbat meals for centuries.

Zemiros transform the Shabbat table into a spiritual celebration. They elevate the meal beyond physical nourishment into an experience of joy, connection, and holiness. Each song carries layers of meaning—praising God, celebrating Shabbat's sanctity, and expressing the Jewish people's deep longing for spiritual elevation.

For newcomers to Jewish observance, learning zemiros can feel overwhelming. The melodies are unfamiliar, the Hebrew lyrics challenging, and different communities have their own customs. But don't let that discourage you! This guide will introduce you to the most popular zemiros, provide lyrics with transliterations, and help you understand what makes these songs so central to the Shabbat experience.

## What Are Zemiros?

The word *zemer* (singular) means "song" or "melody" in Hebrew. Zemiros are specifically songs sung at the Shabbat table—during Friday night dinner, Shabbat lunch, and the third meal (*seudah shlishit*) on Saturday afternoon.

Unlike the formal prayers recited in synagogue, zemiros are more informal and joyous. They're meant to enhance the festive atmosphere of Shabbat meals and inspire participants to reflect on the day's spiritual significance.

Most zemiros were composed by medieval Jewish poets and rabbis, particularly from Spain, North Africa, and the Middle East during the Golden Age of Jewish poetry (roughly 900-1200 CE). Many are written in acrostic form, with each verse beginning with successive letters of the Hebrew alphabet or spelling out the author's name.

## When Are Zemiros Sung?

**Friday Night (Shabbat Evening):** After *Kiddush* (sanctification over wine) and washing hands for the meal, families sing zemiros between courses. This is typically the longest session of singing.

**Shabbat Day Lunch:** After the morning synagogue service and *Kiddush*, more zemiros are sung during the midday meal.

**Seudah Shlishit (Third Meal):** On Saturday afternoon before sundown, the final Shabbat meal includes contemplative zemiros, often with deeper, more spiritual themes.

Different communities emphasize different meals—some sing more at night, others at the third meal. There's no single "correct" way.

## Popular Zemiros: Lyrics, Transliterations, and Meanings

### 1. Shalom Aleichem (שָׁלוֹם עֲלֵיכֶם)

**When Sung:** Friday night, immediately upon returning from synagogue

**Theme:** Welcoming the Shabbat angels

According to tradition, two angels accompany each person home from synagogue on Friday night—one good and one whose mission is to examine the home's Shabbat preparations.

**Lyrics (first verse):**

שָׁלוֹם עֲלֵיכֶם, מַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת, מַלְאֲכֵי עֶלְיוֹן  
מִמֶּלֶךְ מַלְכֵי הַמְּלָכִים, הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא

**Transliteration:**
*Shalom aleichem, mal'achei ha-shareit, mal'achei elyon*  
*Mi-melech mal'chei ha-m'lachim, ha-kadosh baruch hu*

**Translation:**
"Peace unto you, ministering angels, angels of the Most High  
From the King of kings of kings, the Holy One, blessed be He"

The song has four verses: welcoming the angels, asking for their blessing, requesting peaceful departure, and repeating the welcome. It sets a tone of peace and holiness for the entire Shabbat.

---

### 2. Yom Zeh L'Yisrael (יוֹם זֶה לְיִשְׂרָאֵל)

**When Sung:** Friday night

**Theme:** Shabbat as Israel's day of light and joy

**Lyrics (chorus):**

יוֹם זֶה לְיִשְׂרָאֵל אוֹרָה וְשִׂמְחָה  
שַׁבָּת מְנוּחָה

**Transliteration:**
*Yom zeh l'Yisrael orah v'simchah*  
*Shabbat menucha*

**Translation:**
"This day for Israel is light and joy  
Shabbat of rest"

This zemer emphasizes Shabbat's unique gifts to the Jewish people: light (spiritual illumination), joy (celebration), and rest (peace from weekday pressures). It reminds us that Shabbat isn't merely about prohibitions—it's about receiving special blessings unavailable during the week.

---

### 3. Tzur Mishelo (צוּר מִשֶּׁלוֹ)

**When Sung:** Friday night, after the meal before *Birkat HaMazon* (Grace After Meals)

**Theme:** Gratitude for God's sustenance

**Lyrics (first verse):**

צוּר מִשֶּׁלוֹ אָכַלְנוּ, בָּרְכוּ אֱמוּנַי  
שָׂבַעְנוּ וְהוֹתַרְנוּ, כִּדְבַר ה׳

**Transliteration:**
*Tzur mishelo achalnu, bar'chu emunai*  
*Sava'nu v'hotarnu, kid'var Adonai*

**Translation:**
"Rock from Whose [table] we have eaten, bless, my faithful ones  
We have been satisfied and have left over, according to God's word"

This song thanks God for providing food with abundance. It's particularly appropriate before the Grace After Meals, as it transitions from eating to formally thanking the Provider.

---

### 4. Dror Yikra (דְּרוֹר יִקְרָא)

**When Sung:** Friday night or Shabbat day

**Theme:** Freedom and spiritual elevation through Shabbat

**Lyrics (first verse):**

דְּרוֹר יִקְרָא לְבֵן עִם בַּת  
וְיִנְצָרְכֶם כְּמוֹ בָבַת

**Transliteration:**
*D'ror yikra l'vein im bat*  
*V'yintzarchem k'mo vavat*

**Translation:**
"He will proclaim freedom for son and daughter  
And will protect you like the pupil of the eye"

Written by the 10th-century poet Dunash ben Labrat, this zemer uses the language of Jubilee freedom (Leviticus 25:10) to describe Shabbat's liberation from mundane concerns. It's one of the most beloved zemiros, with numerous melodies.

---

### 5. Menucha V'Simcha (מְנוּחָה וְשִׂמְחָה)

**When Sung:** Shabbat day

**Theme:** Rest and joy as Shabbat's gifts

**Lyrics (chorus):**

מְנוּחָה וְשִׂמְחָה אוֹר לַיְּהוּדִים  
יוֹם שַׁבָּתוֹן, יוֹם מַחֲמַדִּים  
שׁוֹמְרָיו וְזוֹכְרָיו הֵמָּה מְעִידִים  
כִּי לְשִׁשָּׁה כָּל בְּרוּאִים עוֹמְדִים

**Transliteration:**
*Menucha v'simcha or la-yehudim*  
*Yom Shabbaton, yom machamadim*  
*Shom'rav v'zochrav heima me'idim*  
*Ki l'shisha kol bruim om'dim*

**Translation:**
"Rest and joy, a light for the Jews  
Day of Shabbat rest, day of delights  
Those who observe it and remember it bear witness  
That in six days all was created"

This zemer beautifully captures Shabbat's dual nature: spiritual rest (*menucha*) and joyful celebration (*simcha*).

---

### 6. Kol M'kadesh (כָּל מְקַדֵּשׁ)

**When Sung:** Friday night or Shabbat day

**Theme:** Rewards for honoring Shabbat

**Lyrics (first verse):**

כָּל מְקַדֵּשׁ שְׁבִיעִי כָּרָאוּי לוֹ  
כָּל שׁוֹמֵר שַׁבָּת כַּדָּת מֵחַלְּלוֹ  
שְׂכָרוֹ הַרְבֵּה מְאֹד עַל פִּי פָעֳלוֹ  
אִישׁ עַל מַחֲנֵהוּ וְאִישׁ עַל דִּגְלוֹ

**Transliteration:**
*Kol m'kadesh shvi'i ka-ra'ui lo*  
*Kol shomer Shabbat ka-dat mei-chal'lo*  
*S'charo harbei m'od al pi fo'olo*  
*Ish al machaneihu v'ish al diglo*

**Translation:**
"Whoever sanctifies the seventh day as is proper  
Whoever observes the Shabbat according to law, not desecrating it  
His reward is very great according to his effort  
Each person at his camp and each at his banner"

This song promises abundant rewards for those who honor Shabbat properly, comparing their merits to the organized Israelite encampment in the wilderness.

---

### 7. Yom Shabbaton (יוֹם שַׁבָּתוֹן)

**When Sung:** Seudah shlishit (third meal)

**Theme:** Shabbat as a taste of the World to Come

**Lyrics (first verse):**

יוֹם שַׁבָּתוֹן אֵין לִשְׁכּוֹחַ  
זִכְרוֹ כְּרֵיחַ הַנִּיחוֹחַ

**Transliteration:**
*Yom Shabbaton ein lishko'ach*  
*Zichro k'rei'ach ha-nicho'ach*

**Translation:**
"The day of Shabbat rest is not to be forgotten  
Its remembrance is like a pleasant fragrance"

Often sung as twilight approaches and Shabbat draws to a close, this zemer expresses the bittersweet longing to hold onto the day's holiness.

## Learning Tips for Beginners

**1. Start with one or two songs:** Don't try to learn all zemiros at once. Master "Shalom Aleichem" and "Tzur Mishelo" first—they're sung in most communities.

**2. Find recordings online:** YouTube and Jewish music websites offer countless versions. Listen to different melodies and choose ones that resonate with you.

**3. Use transliteration:** Don't worry if you can't read Hebrew yet. Many *benchers* (booklets with Grace After Meals and zemiros) include transliterations.

**4. Focus on meaning:** Understanding what you're singing makes it more meaningful. Review translations before Shabbat.

**5. Sing with confidence:** Zemiros aren't performances—they're communal expressions of joy. Even if you don't know the tune perfectly, join in!

**6. Different tunes are okay:** Many zemiros have multiple melodies. There's no single "correct" version. Communities preserve different musical traditions.

## Cultural Context: Why Singing Matters

In traditional Jewish thought, music elevates the soul in ways words alone cannot. The Kabbalistic tradition teaches that melodies can reach spiritual heights unreachable through speech. Singing zemiros together creates unity among family members and guests, regardless of their Hebrew proficiency or musical ability.

In Chinese culture, shared meals carry deep significance for family bonding and respect. Similarly, the Shabbat table with zemiros strengthens family ties and passes traditions from generation to generation. Just as Chinese families might share stories and toasts during festive meals, Jewish families use zemiros to celebrate together.

## Conclusion

Zemiros are more than just songs—they're a living tradition connecting Jews across time and geography. When you sing "Dror Yikra," you're joining your voice with Jews from medieval Spain, modern Jerusalem, and everywhere in between.

You don't need a beautiful voice or perfect Hebrew pronunciation. You just need willingness to participate in this ancient, joyful practice. Start with one song this Shabbat. Listen to a melody during the week. Invite others to sing along. 

Before you know it, you'll find yourself looking forward to Shabbat not just for the rest and the food, but for the chance to lift your voice in songs that have comforted, inspired, and united the Jewish people for centuries.

**Shabbat shalom v'mevorach** — a peaceful and blessed Shabbat to you!
