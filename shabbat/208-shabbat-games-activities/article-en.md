# Shabbat Games and Activities: Fun Without Screens

When Shabbat arrives each Friday evening, Orthodox Jews unplug from technology for 25 hours. No phones, no computers, no television, no video games. For families accustomed to digital entertainment, this can seem daunting. But Shabbat without screens isn't about deprivation—it's about rediscovering a different kind of fun, the kind that brings families together through laughter, conversation, and creativity.

The prohibition against using electricity on Shabbat has roots in the Torah's command to rest and refrain from creative work (*melachah*). While our ancestors didn't have screens to worry about, the principle remains: Shabbat is about human connection, not digital stimulation. In a world where children grow up with tablets and parents check phones compulsively, Shabbat offers a weekly reset—a chance to remember what play looked like before pixels.

## The Philosophy Behind Screen-Free Shabbat

The Talmud teaches that Shabbat is *me'ein olam haba*—a taste of the World to Come (Berachot 57b). In that ideal future, the Sages envisioned a world of deep connection, peace, and joy. How does staring at a screen fit into that vision?

Rabbi Abraham Joshua Heschel described Shabbat as a "palace in time," a sanctuary we enter not by walking through a door but by stepping out of our weekday mentality. Screens keep us tethered to the outside world—notifications, news, work emails disguised as leisure. When we put them down, we're not just following a rule; we're making space for something better.

For children, this weekly unplugging is a gift. Studies show that excessive screen time correlates with attention problems, sleep disruption, and reduced social skills. Shabbat gives kids a chance to develop imagination, patience, and interpersonal skills—tools they'll need far more than another high score on a mobile game.

## Games for Young Children (Ages 3-7)

### Shabbat Scavenger Hunt
Hide small toys, treats, or Shabbat-themed items (plastic kiddush cups, toy challah, stuffed animals) around the house before candle lighting. On Shabbat afternoon, give children clues or a simple picture list to find them. This burns energy and keeps little ones engaged.

**Variation:** Hide items in a specific order that tells the story of the Exodus from Egypt. When they find each item (frog, staff, matzah, sea, mountain), tell that part of the story.

### Simon Says (Shabbat Edition)
Classic Simon Says, but with Shabbat-appropriate commands: "Simon says walk like you're carrying a tray of grape juice to the table," "Simon says pretend to smell the spices for Havdalah," "Simon says wave like you're waving goodbye to Shabbat." Silly, active, and surprisingly educational.

### Building Blocks and Puzzles
While building new structures is forbidden as *boneh* (construction), playing with already-assembled blocks or completing puzzles you've done before is fine according to most opinions. Set up elaborate block cities on Friday afternoon, then play with them throughout Shabbat.

### Story Time with Props
Read books aloud, but add props and costumes. When reading about Noah's Ark, gather stuffed animals. For Queen Esther stories, provide dress-up clothes. Children remember stories they've acted out far better than those they've only heard.

## Games for Elementary Age (Ages 8-12)

### Shabbat Charades
Write actions, Torah stories, or Jewish concepts on slips of paper before Shabbat. Players act them out silently while others guess. Topics can include mitzvot (putting on tefillin, lighting Shabbat candles), biblical figures (Moses parting the sea, David fighting Goliath), or everyday actions (making challah, setting the table).

**Advanced version:** Hebrew charades, where the answer must be a Hebrew word, increasing vocabulary in a playful way.

### 20 Questions (Jewish Edition)
One person thinks of something Jewish—a holiday, a mitzvah, a biblical character, a Hebrew word. Others have 20 yes/no questions to figure it out. This game sharpens deductive reasoning and Jewish knowledge simultaneously.

### Board Games and Card Games
Many board games work perfectly for Shabbat. Classics like Monopoly, Scrabble (use Hebrew letters for an extra challenge), Uno, and chess require no electricity. Strategy games like Settlers of Catan or Ticket to Ride keep older children engaged for hours.

**Important note:** Avoid games that involve gambling, betting, or writing (unless you use a pre-existing board). Games with dice are permissible since the prohibition on *goral* (lots) applies to serious decision-making, not recreation.

### Jewish Geography
One player names a Jewish place, person, or thing starting with the letter A. The next player names one starting with B, and so on through the Hebrew or English alphabet. For example: A—Abraham, B—Bereshit, C—Challah, D—David. This game can go on for ages and teaches a remarkable amount of Jewish literacy.

### Parsha Pictionary
Before Shabbat, prepare slips of paper with scenes from that week's Torah portion. Players draw the scene (without letters or numbers) while others guess what it depicts. Discussing why certain scenes were easier or harder to draw leads to deeper Torah conversations.

## Games for Teens and Adults

### Two Truths and a Lie (Shabbat Version)
Each person shares three statements about their week, their life, or Jewish topics—two true, one false. Others vote on which is the lie. This simple game leads to fascinating conversations and helps family members learn surprising things about each other.

### Debates and Discussions
Pose a Jewish ethical dilemma and divide into teams to argue both sides. Examples: "Should wealthy Jews be required to give 20% charity or just 10%?" "Is it better to pray with deep intention alone or with a community but less focus?" There are no winners, just the joy of wrestling with ideas—a very Jewish activity.

The Talmud itself is essentially a 2,500-year-old record of such debates. Rabbi Hillel and Rabbi Shammai disagreed on hundreds of issues, yet the Talmud records both opinions with respect. Teaching children to argue respectfully and think critically honors this tradition.

### Memory Lane
Go around the table and share memories: "Tell us about a time you laughed so hard you cried," or "What's your earliest Shabbat memory?" or "If you could relive one day from this past year, which would it be?" These conversations create connection and give children insight into their parents' and grandparents' lives.

### Singing Zemiros
Traditional Shabbat table songs (*zemiros*) are participatory entertainment that has survived for centuries. Learn one new zemer each Shabbat. Many have simple melodies and repetitive Hebrew, making them accessible even for beginners. Singing together releases endorphins, creates harmony (literally and figuratively), and makes Shabbat feel special.

## Creative Activities

### Arts and Crafts (Within Halachic Limits)
While many creative activities are forbidden on Shabbat, some are permissible. Playing with Play-Doh that was already opened before Shabbat is generally allowed. Arranging flowers that are already cut and in water is fine. Coloring in coloring books with crayons (since you're not creating permanent writing or images) is acceptable according to some opinions, though consult your rabbi on specifics.

### Shabbat Walks
Walking is one of the best Shabbat activities—and it's free. Explore your neighborhood, visit a park, or hike a nearby trail. Bring a guidebook (prepared before Shabbat) and play nature bingo: spot five different birds, three types of trees, two insects. Discuss the week's parsha while walking, or simply enjoy companionable silence.

In urban areas within an *eruv* (a halachic boundary that permits carrying), you can push strollers or bring toys to a playground. Check your local eruv status and boundaries.

### Cooking and Baking (Before Shabbat)
While you can't cook on Shabbat itself, preparing Shabbat food together on Friday afternoon is bonding time. Children who helped bake the challah or roll the gefilte fish balls take pride in serving "their" dish at the table. Make it fun with music, conversation, and the understanding that a messy kitchen is a sign of good times.

## Chinese Cultural Parallels: The Joy of Unplugged Play

Chinese culture has deep traditions of family activities that require no electricity. The game of *围棋* (wéiqí, Go) has been played for thousands of years, developing strategic thinking through simple stones on a board. Chinese chess (*象棋, xiàngqí*) entertains across generations in parks and homes. During traditional Chinese holidays like Mid-Autumn Festival and Spring Festival, families gather for storytelling, riddles, and lantern games that build memory and connection.

The Confucian emphasis on filial piety and multi-generational respect mirrors the Shabbat table's power to bring grandparents, parents, and children together in conversation. Both traditions understand that the best inheritance isn't money or property—it's time, stories, and shared joy.

## Practical Tips for Success

**Start small.** If your family is used to screens all weekend, don't expect a perfect first Shabbat. Try one new game or activity. Build gradually.

**Prepare ahead.** Friday afternoon is the time to set up board games, put out puzzles, prepare craft materials, and hide scavenger hunt items. A little planning makes Shabbat smoother.

**Lower your standards.** If the house gets messy, that's okay. Shabbat isn't about perfection; it's about people. Better a chaotic but connected Shabbat than a pristine but lonely one.

**Involve older children in planning.** Ask teens what games they'd enjoy. Give them ownership. When they help plan, they're invested in participating.

**Model enthusiasm.** If parents complain about "having nothing to do" without screens, children will too. If parents approach Shabbat activities with joy, children will follow.

**Create traditions.** Maybe every Shabbat afternoon is puzzle time, or every Friday night after dinner is charades. Traditions create anticipation and structure.

## When Screens Are Permitted

It's important to note that halacha permits screen use in medical emergencies or for people whose mental health requires some level of connectivity. If complete digital disconnection causes severe anxiety or depression, consult a rabbi about possible accommodations. Judaism values *pikuach nefesh* (preservation of life) and mental health above all other concerns.

## The Long-Term Payoff

In a world increasingly mediated by screens, Shabbat is counter-cultural. It says: real life is better than virtual life. Presence is more valuable than productivity. Connection matters more than consumption.

Children raised with screen-free Shabbats develop skills that serve them forever: the ability to entertain themselves, to hold conversations, to sit with boredom until creativity emerges. They learn that fun doesn't require a charger.

Years later, when those children are adults, they'll remember not the TV shows they missed but the Shabbat afternoon when the whole family played Monopoly for three hours, or the time they acted out the entire book of Esther with costumes, or the walk when Dad pointed out constellations and told stories about when he was young.

The Talmud asks: What is the measure of a person's wealth? The Sages answer: one who is happy with their portion (*Pirkei Avot* 4:1). Shabbat without screens teaches this lesson weekly. Happiness isn't downloaded; it's discovered in the space between people, in laughter around a table, in the simple pleasure of play.

## Conclusion

Shabbat games and activities aren't substitutes for screens—they're something far better. They're the recovery of an older, deeper kind of fun, the kind that builds relationships and memories instead of screen time statistics.

So this Shabbat, put the devices away without fear. Pull out the board games, the colored pencils, the building blocks. Tell stories, sing songs, go for a walk. Rediscover the revolutionary idea that the best entertainment is each other.

*Shabbat shalom*—a peaceful Shabbat, not because nothing is happening, but because everything that matters is.
