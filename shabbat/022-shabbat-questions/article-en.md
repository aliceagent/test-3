## Introduction

"Can I turn on a light? Can I use my phone? What about opening the refrigerator?"

If you're new to Shabbat observance, you've probably found yourself with a hundred questions about what you can and cannot do on the seventh day. You're not alone. Even people who grew up observing Shabbat sometimes encounter new situations that require careful thought.

This article provides clear, practical answers to the most common Shabbat questions. Think of it as your reference guide — something to review before your first Shabbat, or to consult when you're not sure whether something is permitted.

The laws of Shabbat can seem complex at first, but they're built on a logical framework. Once you understand the underlying principles, many answers become intuitive. And remember: every person who keeps Shabbat today started as a beginner. It's okay to learn gradually and to ask questions.

## The Framework: Understanding the 39 Melachot

During what corresponds to China's Western Zhou Dynasty (西周, ~1046-771 BCE) — while Chinese scholars were codifying the rituals of the Zhou Li (周禮) — Jewish sages were systematically organizing the laws of Shabbat based on the work involved in building the Tabernacle in the wilderness.

The Torah identifies 39 categories of creative work (melachot) that are forbidden on Shabbat. These aren't random restrictions — they're forms of creative activity that assert human mastery over the world. On Shabbat, we step back from being creators and acknowledge God as the ultimate Creator.

The 39 melachot include activities like:
- Planting and harvesting
- Building and demolishing  
- Writing and erasing
- Cooking and baking
- Lighting and extinguishing fire
- Carrying between domains
- And many others

Modern technology has created new questions, but the principles remain the same. Rabbis analyze whether a modern action fits into one of these 39 categories or their derivatives.

Now let's address your specific questions.

## Electricity and Appliances

### Can I turn lights on or off?

**No.** Flipping a light switch completes an electrical circuit, which most authorities consider a form of "building" (boneh) or creating fire (since early electrical applications produced sparks). Additionally, an incandescent bulb heats a filament, which involves the prohibition against kindling (hav'arah).

**Solution:** Use timers set before Shabbat to turn lights on and off automatically at preset times.

### Can I use appliances that were left on before Shabbat?

**It depends on the appliance:**
- **Oven/hotplate:** Yes, you can use it to warm pre-cooked food (more on that below)
- **Fan or air conditioner:** Yes, if it was running before Shabbat
- **Radio or computer:** No, even if left on — using them involves other prohibitions

### Can I open a refrigerator?

**Generally yes, with preparation:**
- The light inside must be disabled (unscrew the bulb, cover it, or disconnect it) before Shabbat
- Modern refrigerators have motors that cycle on/off based on temperature — opening the door may trigger the motor sooner than it would otherwise start
- Some authorities permit this, others prefer refrigerators with "Shabbat mode" that disables this sensor

### Can I use an elevator?

**Only a Shabbat elevator** — one programmed to stop automatically at every floor without requiring you to press any buttons. Standard elevators are prohibited because pressing the button completes an electrical circuit.

### What about a timer (Shabbat clock)?

**Yes, timers are permitted and encouraged.** You can use them for lights, hot water urns, slow cookers, and other appliances. The key is that you set the timer before Shabbat — the device then operates automatically without your intervention.

## Phones and Electronics

### Can I use my phone?

**Absolutely not.** Using a smartphone violates multiple prohibitions:
- Turning on the screen (completing circuit, creating light)
- Typing (writing - kotev)
- Deleting text (erasing - mochek)
- Creating digital information (boneh - building)
- Potential carrying violations if you take it outside an eruv

### Can I answer the doorbell?

**No** — pressing a button completes an electrical circuit.

**Solution:** Leave a note on your door on Shabbat explaining you don't answer the bell, and guests should knock instead.

### Can I use a computer or tablet?

**No**, for the same reasons as a phone (writing, creating, electricity).

### Can I take photographs?

**No** — creating an image violates the prohibition against fixing/creating (fixing ink to parchment or, in digital form, creating digital data).

### What about medical devices?

**Medical necessities permitted by rabbinic authorities include:**
- Hearing aids (if turned on before Shabbat)
- CPAP machines for sleep apnea
- Medical alert devices for elderly or ill persons
- Any device needed for pikuach nefesh (saving a life)

When health is at stake, consult a rabbi familiar with both Jewish law and medical needs.

## Cooking and Food Preparation

### Can I cook on Shabbat?

**No.** Cooking (bishul) is one of the 39 melachot. This includes:
- Lighting a stove or oven
- Boiling water
- Frying, baking, or cooking raw food
- Even cooking something that's edible raw but improved by cooking (like warming certain vegetables)

### Can I reheat food?

**Yes, under specific conditions:**

Food that is **fully cooked** and **solid** (like chicken, kugel, or cholent) can be warmed on a heat source that was on before Shabbat (like a hotplate or blech — a metal sheet placed over stovetop burners).

**Rules:**
- The food must be completely cooked before Shabbat
- It should still be warm (at least slightly) when you reheat it — ideally kept warm since before Shabbat
- You cannot place it directly on a flame; it must be on a covered heat source
- Some authorities require the food to be at least partially warm when placed on the heat

### Can I make coffee or tea?

**Partially permitted:**

- **Instant coffee:** Pour water from an urn that was heated before Shabbat over instant coffee grounds
- **Regular coffee/tea:** More complicated — cannot use a coffee maker; cannot pour boiling water directly over tea leaves or coffee grounds (this would constitute cooking)

**The common practice:** 
- Use instant coffee with water from a Shabbat urn (electric urn that maintains temperature)
- Or prepare cold brew coffee concentrate before Shabbat and mix with hot water on Shabbat

### Can I use a slow cooker (crock pot)?

**Yes — one of the best Shabbat tools!**

Turn it on before Shabbat with raw ingredients, and let it cook overnight. By Shabbat lunch, you'll have hot cholent, stew, or other slow-cooked dishes. The key: you're not initiating cooking on Shabbat; it started before Shabbat and continues automatically.

### Can I open a can or bottle?

**Generally no** — creating an opening is prohibited (making a utensil, kli).

**Solutions:**
- Open cans and bottles before Shabbat
- Tear or completely remove bottle caps in a destructive manner (not creating a usable opening for future use)
- Some authorities permit opening soda cans in an unusual way (from the bottom) or by fully destroying the tab

This is a complex area — consult your rabbi for practical guidance.

### Can I cut vegetables or prepare a salad?

**Yes, but with conditions:**

You can cut vegetables and prepare food **shortly before the meal** at which it will be eaten. You cannot prepare a salad on Shabbat morning for Saturday night dinner — this violates the prohibition against "selecting" (borer) and preparation for after Shabbat.

**The principle:** Food preparation is permitted when done for immediate use (within 1-2 hours), not as advance preparation.

## Writing, Creating, and Destroying

### Can I write?

**No.** Writing (kotev) and erasing (mochek) are among the 39 melachot. This includes:
- Writing with pen or pencil
- Typing on a keyboard
- Texting or emailing
- Drawing or coloring
- Signing a document

### Can I tear toilet paper or paper towels?

**No.** Tearing along a perforated line creates a finished edge, which is a form of making a utensil.

**Solutions:**
- Pre-tear toilet paper and paper towels before Shabbat
- Use tissues (which aren't perforated)
- Some families keep a box of pre-cut tissues in the bathroom

### Can I open mail or packages?

**No** — tearing is prohibited, and you may also be reading writing not intended for Shabbat, or creating an opening in an envelope.

## Carrying and Muktzeh

### Can I carry items outside my home?

**Only within an eruv** — a halachic boundary that encloses an area, turning it into a unified private domain for carrying purposes.

**Without an eruv:** You cannot carry anything outside, including:
- House keys
- Tissues
- Prayer books
- Tallit bags

**Solutions:**
- Wear jewelry-style keys
- Attach items to your clothing (becomes part of your garment)
- Leave items at your destination before Shabbat
- Walk only within an eruv

### What is muktzeh?

**Muktzeh** (מוקצה) means "set aside" — objects that are off-limits to move on Shabbat because they're associated with prohibited activities.

**Examples of muktzeh items:**
- Money (associated with commerce, which is Shabbat-inappropriate)
- Pens and pencils (used for writing)
- Phones and electronics (prohibited to use)
- Tools (associated with prohibited work)
- Raw food that isn't edible as-is

**Not muktzeh:**
- Books
- Clothing
- Food that's ready to eat
- Dishes and utensils
- Furniture (unless it holds muktzeh items)

**The principle:** If you wouldn't use it on Shabbat, you generally shouldn't move it either (with some exceptions).

## Transportation

### Can I drive a car?

**No.** Driving violates multiple prohibitions:
- Igniting fuel (combustion engine)
- Completing electrical circuits (modern cars)
- Carrying items from private domain to public domain (getting in and out of car)
- Traveling beyond the techum Shabbat (Shabbat boundary, approximately 1 km beyond your city)

**The deeper reason:** Shabbat is about creating a sanctuary in time. Driving fragments your day and scatters your focus. Walking keeps you local, present, connected to your community.

### Can I ride a bicycle?

**No** — carrying the bike outside an eruv is prohibited, and there's also concern you might repair it if it breaks.

### Can I ride in a car driven by a non-Jew?

**Generally no**, except in cases of severe need (serious medical issue, elderly person who cannot walk, etc.). Consult a rabbi for specific situations.

## Personal Care

### Can I shower or bathe?

**Complex area with different customs:**

**Cold shower:** Generally permitted

**Hot water:** 
- Water heated before Shabbat (by a timer, for example): Many authorities permit
- Heating water on Shabbat: Prohibited
- Using a solar water heater: Disputed; many permit

**Practical approach many follow:** Take a warm (not hot) shower using water from a tank heated before Shabbat.

### Can I brush my teeth?

**Disputed among authorities:**

**Stricter view:** No — may cause bleeding (gums), involves creating lather

**Lenient view:** Permitted with water only (no toothpaste), using soft brush gently

**Common practice:** Many use a soft brush with water but no toothpaste, or rinse vigorously with mouthwash

### Can I wash my hands or face?

**Yes**, for comfort, cleanliness, or before eating bread.

You can use:
- Cold water freely
- Warm water that was heated before Shabbat
- Liquid soap (more lenient than bar soap, which raises concerns about smoothing)

### Can I apply cosmetics?

**Generally no:**
- Lipstick, mascara, foundation: Prohibited (dyeing, creating a coating)
- Light powder: Disputed
- Creams and oils: Generally permitted if very light, prohibited if creates a coating or color

**Practical approach:** Many women apply whatever cosmetics they want before Shabbat and avoid applying on Shabbat.

### Can I comb or brush my hair?

**Yes, but carefully:**
- Use a soft brush
- Brush gently to avoid pulling out hair (which is prohibited)
- Some use a wide-tooth comb instead of a brush

## Emergency and Medical Situations

### What if there's a medical emergency?

**Pikuach nefesh (saving a life) overrides ALL Shabbat laws.**

If someone's life is in danger:
- Call an ambulance (even on a cell phone)
- Drive to the hospital
- Turn on lights
- Do whatever is necessary to save the life

**No questions asked. No hesitation.**

The sanctity of human life is greater than the sanctity of Shabbat. In fact, violating Shabbat to save a life is not just permitted — it's obligatory.

### What about non-emergency medical issues?

**Grey areas that depend on severity:**

- **Fever, pain, illness that doesn't threaten life:** May involve rabbinic leniencies; consult a rabbi
- **Taking medication:** Generally permitted for chronic conditions; disputed for minor ailments
- **Calling a doctor:** If potentially serious, permitted

**General principle:** When in doubt about a medical situation, be lenient. Better to violate Shabbat unnecessarily than to risk a life by hesitating.

## The Spirit Behind the Law

You might be thinking: "This is a lot of restrictions. Where's the joy?"

Here's the secret that people who love Shabbat understand:

**Boundaries create freedom.**

Think about it: During the Zhou Dynasty (周朝), when Chinese ritual texts were being codified, scholars understood that elaborate rituals weren't burdensome — they created sacred space. The detailed choreography of proper conduct (禮, li) didn't restrict life; it elevated ordinary moments into meaningful acts.

The same is true for Shabbat.

By saying "no" to electricity, you say "yes" to candlelight.  
By saying "no" to driving, you say "yes" to walking with your family.  
By saying "no" to your phone, you say "yes" to presence, to conversation, to being truly *here*.

Shabbat's restrictions aren't about what you *can't* do — they're about what you *can* experience when the noise stops.

## Practical Tips for Beginners

**1. Start Small**

You don't have to do everything at once. Pick one category and master it:
- Week 1: No phone
- Week 2: No phone + no driving
- Week 3: Add cooking restrictions
- Week 4: Add carrying/eruv awareness

**2. Prepare on Friday**

The better your preparation, the easier Shabbat becomes:
- Pre-tear toilet paper
- Set timers for lights
- Cook food in advance
- Open any packages you'll need
- Charge phone (then turn it off)

**3. Keep a List**

When you think "Can I do X on Shabbat?" and you're not sure — write it down (before or after Shabbat) and look it up later or ask a rabbi.

**4. Find a Community**

Connect with others who keep Shabbat. They can:
- Answer practical questions
- Share tips and shortcuts
- Invite you for Shabbat meals
- Encourage you when it's difficult

**5. Remember Why**

On Friday night, when you light the candles and close your eyes, remember: you're doing something that Jews have done for 3,000 years. From China's Shang Dynasty (商朝) to today, through empires and exiles, this day has remained sacred. You're part of that unbroken chain.

## Conclusion

Shabbat observance is like learning a new language — at first, you're constantly looking things up and asking questions. But gradually, it becomes second nature. You develop an instinct for what fits the spirit of the day and what doesn't.

Don't be overwhelmed by the details. Every person who keeps Shabbat today started exactly where you are now, with the same questions and uncertainties.

Start somewhere. Ask questions. Be patient with yourself.

And most importantly: discover the gift that Shabbat truly is — a day when you can step off the treadmill of productivity and simply *be*.

The questions you asked in this article will soon become muscle memory. And then you'll discover what Shabbat is really about: not what you can't do, but who you become when you stop doing.

## Key Takeaways

- Shabbat restrictions are based on 39 categories of creative work from the Torah
- Electricity and electronics are generally prohibited (use timers set before Shabbat)
- Cooking is forbidden; reheating fully-cooked food on pre-existing heat is permitted
- Writing, creating, and most technology use are prohibited
- Carrying outside requires an eruv
- Medical emergencies override ALL Shabbat laws — saving a life is paramount
- Start with what you can manage and grow gradually
- Shabbat's boundaries create freedom and sacred space
