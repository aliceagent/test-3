# Sephardi Halachic Authorities: From the Shulchan Aruch to Rav Ovadia Yosef

When an observant Jew faces a halachic question—Can I use this medicine on Shabbat? Is this business practice permitted? How do I observe this holiday?—they consult a *posek* (פוסק), a rabbi qualified to render halachic decisions.

But halachic authorities are not uniform. A Sephardi Jew and an Ashkenazi Jew asking the same question may receive different answers because they follow different *poskim* (plural), who represent distinct methodologies, precedents, and traditions.

This article introduces the major Sephardi halachic authorities—the giants whose rulings have shaped Sephardi practice from the medieval period to today—and explains how their approach differs from Ashkenazi decisors.

## The Foundations: Rambam and the Rationalist Tradition

### Rambam (Maimonides, 1138-1204)

Rabbi Moshe ben Maimon, known as Rambam or Maimonides, is the single most influential figure in Sephardi halachic tradition.

**Life:** Born in Córdoba, Spain; fled persecution to Morocco, then Egypt, where he became the leader of Egyptian Jewry and physician to the Sultan.

**Major Works:**
1. **Mishneh Torah**: A comprehensive code of all Jewish law, organized systematically. Unlike the Talmud's discursive style, the Mishneh Torah presents clear rulings without lengthy debate. It covers all 613 mitzvot, even those not applicable in the absence of the Temple.

2. **Guide for the Perplexed**: A philosophical work reconciling Torah with Aristotelian philosophy, profoundly influencing Jewish thought.

3. **Commentary on the Mishnah**: Including his formulation of the Thirteen Principles of Faith, still recited in many prayer books.

**Halachic methodology:**
- **Rationalist:** Rambam sought logical consistency, often deriving rulings from first principles rather than relying solely on precedent.
- **Lenient when justified:** If the Talmud was unclear or multiple opinions existed, Rambam often chose the more lenient position, unless there was strong reason for stringency.
- **Universalizing:** He aimed to create a unified code applicable to all Jews everywhere.

**Influence on Sephardim:** Rambam became the foundational authority for Sephardi Jews, especially in the Middle East and North Africa. Yemenite Jews, in particular, follow Rambam almost exclusively.

**Example ruling:** Rambam ruled that one may rely on non-Jewish supervision for certain kashrut matters if the Jew has confidence in the non-Jew's honesty (Mishneh Torah, Hilchot Ma'achalot Assurot 11:25). This is more lenient than some Ashkenazi positions requiring direct Jewish supervision in all cases.

### Why Ashkenazim Don't Always Follow Rambam

While Rambam is universally respected, Ashkenazi Jews often follow the **Rosh** (Rabbi Asher ben Yechiel, 1250-1327), a German rabbi who fled to Spain. The Rosh represented the Tosafist (French-German) tradition of Talmud study, which sometimes reached different conclusions than Rambam.

The Shulchan Aruch (see below) tried to balance Rambam, the Rosh, and the Rif (Rabbi Isaac Alfasi, 1013-1103), another North African authority.

## The Codifier: Rabbi Yosef Karo and the Shulchan Aruch

### Rabbi Yosef Karo (1488-1575)

Born in Spain, exiled in 1492, Rabbi Karo eventually settled in Safed, Israel, where he led a mystical fellowship and produced the most influential Jewish legal code.

**Major Works:**

1. **Beit Yosef**: A massive commentary on the *Tur* (an earlier code by Rabbi Jacob ben Asher), analyzing every law by tracing it back through the Talmud and later authorities. The Beit Yosef is a work of monumental scholarship, weighing opinions and rendering decisions.

2. **Shulchan Aruch** ("The Set Table"): A condensed, practical code derived from the Beit Yosef, organized into four sections:
   - **Orach Chayim**: Daily life, Shabbat, holidays
   - **Yoreh De'ah**: Kashrut, ritual purity, mourning
   - **Even HaEzer**: Marriage and divorce
   - **Choshen Mishpat**: Monetary law and ethics

**Methodology:**
Rabbi Karo generally followed the majority opinion among the "three pillars"—Rambam, Rif, and Rosh. When all three agreed, that was the ruling. When they disagreed, he sided with the majority.

This created a Sephardi-leaning code, since two of the three (Rambam and Rif) were Sephardi.

**The Ashkenazi Response: Rema**

Rabbi Moses Isserles (the Rema, 1530-1572) of Kraków, Poland, added glosses to the Shulchan Aruch noting Ashkenazi customs and disagreements. Printed editions include both Rabbi Karo's text and the Rema's notes, making the Shulchan Aruch a dual-tradition work.

**Result:** Sephardim follow the Shulchan Aruch as written. Ashkenazim follow the Rema's glosses. Both use the same book, but with different conclusions!

**Example:** The Shulchan Aruch (Orach Chayim 453) permits eating kitniyot (rice, legumes) on Passover. The Rema adds: "But in all Ashkenazi lands, the custom is to forbid kitniyot." Thus, same book, opposite practices.

### Why Rabbi Karo Is the Supreme Sephardi Authority

Rabbi Karo's *Shulchan Aruch* became the baseline for Sephardi halacha. Later Sephardi poskim almost always begin with "The Shulchan Aruch says..." and build from there.

His influence is so great that "the Shulchan Aruch" is sometimes synonymous with "Sephardi halacha," even though the book itself encompasses both traditions.

## The Mystic-Legalist: The Arizal and Kabbalistic Influence

### Rabbi Isaac Luria (the Arizal, 1534-1572)

The Arizal (an acronym for "the divine Rabbi Isaac of blessed memory") was not primarily a halachic authority but a Kabbalist who revolutionized Jewish mysticism in Safed.

**Influence on Sephardi halacha:**

The Arizal taught specific intentions (*kavvanot*) and customs for mitzvot, based on Kabbalistic understanding of the spiritual realms. These were transmitted by his student Rabbi Chaim Vital.

**Sephardi poskim integrated Kabbalah into halacha:**
- Certain prayers recited at specific times based on Kabbalistic significance
- Customs around Shabbat (like not eating fish and meat together, avoiding garlic on Shabbat—practices rooted in Kabbalistic teachings)
- The *tikkun chatzot* (midnight prayer lamenting the Temple's destruction)

**Ashkenazi-Sephardi difference:** While Hasidic Ashkenazim later embraced Kabbalah enthusiastically, Mitnagdic (Lithuanian) Ashkenazim remained more rationalist. Sephardim mainstreamed Kabbalah earlier and more universally.

**Example:** Many Sephardim recite *Leshem Yichud* (a Kabbalistic formula expressing intention to unify God's name) before performing mitzvot. This is standard in Sephardi prayer books but absent from most Ashkenazi ones (except Hasidic).

## The Iraqi Giant: The Ben Ish Chai

### Rabbi Yosef Chaim of Baghdad (1835-1909)

Known universally as the **Ben Ish Chai** (after his most famous work), Rabbi Yosef Chaim was the leading Sephardi authority for Iraqi and broader Middle Eastern Jewry.

**Background:** Born into a distinguished rabbinic family in Baghdad, he became the chief rabbi and spiritual leader of Iraqi Jewry, delivering weekly sermons that attracted thousands.

**Major Work: Ben Ish Chai**

A two-volume work organized according to the weekly Torah portions, covering halacha, Kabbalah, ethics, and customs. Written in accessible Hebrew, it became immensely popular.

**Methodology:**
- Deep integration of Kabbalah and halacha
- Followed the Shulchan Aruch and later Sephardi authorities
- Emphasized stringency in certain areas (modesty, Shabbat observance) while following traditional Sephardi leniencies in others (like kitniyot)

**Influence:** The Ben Ish Chai's rulings are authoritative for Iraqi, Syrian, and many other Middle Eastern Sephardi communities. His customs are followed meticulously, often even over later authorities.

**Example ruling:** The Ben Ish Chai ruled that one should stand while reciting *Vayevarech David* (a prayer in the morning service), based on Kabbalistic reasoning. Iraqi Jews follow this; other Sephardim may not.

**Modern impact:** In Israel, many Sephardim from Iraq, Syria, and Iran continue to identify as "following the Ben Ish Chai," distinguishing their practice from other Sephardi sub-traditions.

## The 20th Century Giant: Rav Ovadia Yosef

### Rabbi Ovadia Yosef (1920-2013)

Perhaps the most influential Sephardi posek of the modern era, Rav Ovadia reshaped Sephardi halacha and played a major role in Israeli religious and political life.

**Life:**
Born in Baghdad, moved to Jerusalem as a child. Became a prodigy, mastering the entire Talmud and codes by his teens. Served as Chief Rabbi of Egypt (1947-1950), Chief Sephardi Rabbi of Tel Aviv, and ultimately the Sephardi Chief Rabbi of Israel (1973-1983).

**Major Works:**

1. **Yabia Omer**: Multi-volume responsa (halachic questions and answers) covering all areas of Jewish law. Known for encyclopedic knowledge and exhaustive citations.

2. **Yechave Da'at**: More accessible responsa on practical questions.

3. **Chazon Ovadia**: Halachic guides to Shabbat, holidays, and other topics.

4. **Weekly halachic television and radio programs**: Rav Ovadia answered live questions, making halacha accessible to the masses.

**Methodology:**
- **Return to the Shulchan Aruch**: Rav Ovadia argued that Sephardim should follow Rabbi Yosef Karo's Shulchan Aruch, not later stringencies that crept in from Ashkenazi influence or overly strict local customs.
- **Lenient when halachically justified**: He famously ruled leniently on many issues—not out of laxity, but because the original Sephardi sources supported leniency. He fought against what he saw as unnecessary stringencies (*chumrot*) that burdened people without halachic basis.
- **Unifying Sephardi practice**: Rav Ovadia sought to create a unified Sephardi halachic standard, overriding conflicting local customs by returning to foundational sources.

**Controversial rulings (considered lenient by some, authentically Sephardi by others):**

1. **Kitniyot on Pesach**: Firmly ruled that Sephardim may eat rice, beans, corn, etc., on Passover, and should not adopt Ashkenazi stringencies.

2. **Electricity on Shabbat for medical needs**: More lenient than many Ashkenazi authorities in permitting certain uses.

3. **Conversion standards**: More lenient in accepting conversions performed by certain rabbis whom stricter authorities questioned.

4. **Women's hair covering**: Ruled that married women need not cover all their hair in public, based on classical Sephardi sources—contrary to stricter Ashkenazi and some Sephardi stringencies.

**Impact:**
- Sephardi Jews worldwide look to Rav Ovadia's rulings as authoritative
- His political party, Shas, became a major force in Israeli politics, advocating for Sephardi interests
- His funeral in 2013 drew an estimated 800,000 mourners—one of the largest in Israeli history

**Example ruling:** Rav Ovadia ruled that Sephardim may use a thermos (which keeps water hot without continued heating) on Shabbat, following the lenient position in the Shulchan Aruch, whereas many Ashkenazi authorities forbid this.

## Other Important Sephardi Authorities

### Rabbi Benzion Abba Shaul (1924-1998)

A leading Israeli Sephardi posek, known for his piety and accessibility. His rulings, collected in *Or LeTzion*, are widely followed.

### Rabbi Mordechai Eliyahu (1929-2010)

Sephardi Chief Rabbi of Israel (1983-1993), known for his Kabbalistic piety and moderate halachic positions.

### Rabbi Shalom Messas (1909-2003)

Chief Rabbi of Morocco and later Chief Sephardi Rabbi of Jerusalem. Known for lenient rulings rooted in North African Sephardi tradition.

### Rabbi Haim David Halevi (1924-1998)

Sephardi Chief Rabbi of Tel Aviv, author of *Aseh Lecha Rav* responsa. Known for creative, modern halachic solutions.

### Contemporary: Rabbi Yitzchak Yosef

Son of Rav Ovadia Yosef, current Sephardi Chief Rabbi of Israel, continuing his father's methodology and influence.

## Key Differences: Sephardi vs. Ashkenazi Halachic Approach

### 1. Methodology

**Sephardi (following Rambam and Shulchan Aruch):**
- Rationalist: Seek logical consistency
- Prefer leniency when the Talmud or early authorities are unclear
- Return to foundational sources (Talmud, Rambam, Shulchan Aruch) over later customs

**Ashkenazi (following Tosafot, Rema, later authorities):**
- Dialectical: Explore multiple opinions through pilpul
- Prefer stringency (*lechumra*) when in doubt
- Greater weight to later customs and authorities (*acharonim*)

### 2. Specific Areas of Difference

**Kitniyot on Passover:**
- Sephardi: Permitted (following Shulchan Aruch)
- Ashkenazi: Forbidden (following Rema)

**Waiting Between Meat and Dairy:**
- Sephardi: Typically 6 hours (following Rambam), but some communities 1-3 hours
- Ashkenazi: 6 hours (following Tosafot), though some 3 hours

**Mixing Fish and Meat:**
- Sephardi: Generally avoid, based on Kabbalistic/health concerns
- Ashkenazi: Permitted (though custom varies)

**Women's Hair Covering:**
- Sephardi (following Rav Ovadia): Front hair may show; full covering not required
- Ashkenazi (strict): All hair covered with wig or scarf

**Shabbat Restrictions:**
- Both strict, but details differ (e.g., using timers, warming food, etc.)

### 3. Kabbalah

**Sephardi:** Integrated into mainstream practice
**Ashkenazi:** Mitnagdim resist; Hasidim embrace

## Practical Implications: Who Should Follow Which?

**Traditional rule:**
- Follow your family's tradition (*minhag avot*)
- Sephardi by lineage → follow Sephardi poskim
- Ashkenazi by lineage → follow Ashkenazi poskim

**Mixed families (Ashkenazi-Sephardi marriage):**
- Traditional: Follow the husband's tradition
- Modern: Choose one tradition for consistency, or consult a rabbi

**Converts:**
- Often follow the community or rabbi who converted them
- Can choose Sephardi or Ashkenazi tradition based on affinity

**Returning to observance:**
- Research your ancestry if possible
- If unknown or mixed, choose the tradition of your community or teacher

**The key principle:** Don't pick and choose leniencies from both. Follow one tradition consistently. (Though in cases of genuine hardship, a rabbi may permit cross-tradition leniencies.)

## Conclusion: The Richness of Sephardi Halachic Tradition

The Sephardi halachic tradition, from Rambam through Rav Ovadia Yosef, represents a sophisticated, rationalist, and compassionate approach to Jewish law. Rooted in the Shulchan Aruch, enriched by Kabbalah, and refined by centuries of scholarship, it offers a comprehensive path for living a Torah life.

Understanding this tradition is essential for:
- Sephardi Jews seeking to follow their heritage authentically
- Ashkenazi Jews who want to appreciate the diversity within halacha
- Anyone navigating the modern Jewish world, where both traditions coexist

The Talmud (Eruvin 13b) records a dispute between the schools of Hillel and Shammai. A voice from heaven declared: "Both are the words of the living God, but the halacha follows Hillel." The same could be said of Sephardi and Ashkenazi traditions—both are authentic, both are precious, and both lead to the same ultimate goal: serving God and living a holy life.

*May the merit of these great sages—from Rambam to Rav Ovadia—inspire us to study Torah, observe mitzvot, and walk humbly before the Holy One, blessed be He.*
