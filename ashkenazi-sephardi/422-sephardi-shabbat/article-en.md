Walk into a Sephardi synagogue before dawn on Shabbat morning, and you'll encounter something remarkable: a gathering of worshippers singing elaborate poetic hymns by candlelight, their melodies echoing centuries of Jewish life in Spain, Morocco, Syria, and Turkey. This is Baqashot — just one of many distinctive Sephardi Shabbat customs that have enriched Jewish life for generations.

While all Jews observe the same Shabbat — resting on the seventh day, lighting candles, sharing festive meals — the specific ways communities celebrate this holy day vary beautifully. Sephardi Shabbat traditions, shaped by Mediterranean and Middle Eastern cultures, offer a fascinating contrast to the more widely known Ashkenazi practices.

## Friday Night: Welcoming the Sabbath Queen

The Sephardi Friday evening service differs in both liturgy and atmosphere from its Ashkenazi counterpart. While both traditions sing "Lecha Dodi" — the mystical poem welcoming Shabbat as a bride — Sephardi communities often use distinctive melodies passed down through generations, some dating back to medieval Spain.

**Kiddush Differences**: The Sephardi Kiddush (blessing over wine) includes a slightly different text. Sephardim say "mekadesh ha-Shabbat" (who sanctifies the Shabbat) while Ashkenazim say "mekadesh ha-Shabbat" in a different grammatical form. These minute variations reflect different textual traditions going back over a thousand years.

**The Friday Night Table**: A Sephardi Shabbat table gleams with distinct culinary traditions. Instead of challah braided in the familiar Ashkenazi style, Sephardi communities often serve round or flat breads. Moroccan Jews make a sweet, orange-flower-water-scented challah called "mouna." Syrian Jews serve a sesame-covered round bread.

The meal itself reflects Mediterranean cuisine: slow-cooked stews like Moroccan "dafina" (similar to but distinct from Ashkenazi cholent), Iraqi "t'bit" (chicken and rice cooked overnight), or Yemenite "jachnun" (rolled dough slow-baked until caramelized). These dishes, like Ashkenazi cholent, solve the problem of hot food on Shabbat when cooking is forbidden — they're prepared before Shabbat and left on a low flame overnight.

**Table Songs**: Sephardi communities sing "zemirot" (table songs) with distinctive melodies. Many are in Ladino (Judeo-Spanish), Arabic, or use Middle Eastern musical modes called "maqamat." The Syrian Jewish tradition, for example, adapts these table songs to different maqamat depending on the season and the Torah reading of that week — a sophisticated musical tradition connecting the dinner table to the synagogue.

## Shabbat Morning: Baqashot and Prayer

Perhaps no Sephardi custom is more distinctive than Baqashot (literally "requests" or "petitions") — a pre-dawn service of poetic hymns held before the regular Shabbat morning prayers, particularly in winter months when nights are long.

**The Origin of Baqashot**: This tradition began in 16th-century Safed (Tzfat), the mystical city in northern Israel where Kabbalistic Judaism flourished. The great mystics — Rabbi Isaac Luria (the Arizal), Rabbi Moses Cordovero, and others — gathered before dawn to sing hymns preparing their souls for Shabbat morning prayer. The practice spread throughout the Sephardi world, particularly to communities in Syria, Turkey, and North Africa.

**The Experience**: Participants gather in the synagogue at 3 or 4 AM. By candlelight, they sing dozens of piyyutim (liturgical poems) composed over centuries — works by Spanish golden age poets like Rabbi Yehuda Halevi and Solomon ibn Gabirol, alongside mystical compositions by later Kabbalists. The melodies are hauntingly beautiful, using Middle Eastern scales and ornamentation. The service can last two to three hours, culminating in the regular morning prayers at sunrise.

This tradition remains strong in Syrian Jewish communities, particularly in Brooklyn, New York, and in Deal, New Jersey, where Baqashot gatherings draw hundreds every Shabbat in winter.

**Prayer Nusach**: The Sephardi prayer liturgy (nusach) differs textually from Ashkenazi prayers. Sephardim follow the order and text established by Rabbi Isaac Luria (the Arizal) and codified by the Yemenite sage Rabbi Yosef Chaim (the Ben Ish Chai) and others. The prayers include different poems (piyyutim), slightly different wording in key blessings, and a different order for certain sections.

**Torah Reading Customs**: When the Torah is removed from the ark, Sephardi and Ashkenazi customs diverge. Sephardim sing distinctive verses before the reading, and the Torah is typically held upright by the person who will read from it, rather than placed flat on the reading desk. Some Sephardi communities read the Aramaic Targum (translation) verse by verse after the Hebrew, maintaining an ancient Talmudic practice that Ashkenazim discontinued.

## Shabbat Afternoon: Rest and Study

Sephardi communities observe the traditional Shabbat afternoon routine: a light meal (seudah shlishit), rest, Torah study, and Mincha (afternoon prayers). However, specific customs vary:

**Pizmonim**: Syrian Jews have a rich tradition of singing "pizmonim" (religious songs) after the afternoon meal. These songs, many in Hebrew or Ladino, combine religious themes with beautiful melodies adapted from Arab musical traditions. Families gather, sing together, and study Torah, creating a warm communal atmosphere.

**Moroccan Mimouna**: Moroccan Jews observe a unique celebration called "Mimouna" immediately after Passover ends — though not a Shabbat custom, it illustrates Sephardi festive traditions. After Shabbat ends, Sephardi communities may have distinctive customs: Moroccan Jews sing specific songs, Iraqi Jews have particular melodies, and so forth.

## Foods: A Mediterranean Palette

Sephardi Shabbat cuisine reflects the Mediterranean, North African, and Middle Eastern regions where these communities flourished:

**Fish**: While Ashkenazi Jews serve gefilte fish (ground fish patties), Sephardim prepare whole fish or fish in sauce. Moroccan Jews make spicy fish tagines. Syrian Jews prepare "kibbet samak" (fish kibbeh). Greek Jews serve fish with lemon and herbs.

**Salads**: Multiple colorful salads start the meal — roasted peppers, eggplant, tomatoes, beets — reflecting Middle Eastern mezze traditions.

**Meat**: Dishes like Moroccan meatballs in tomato sauce (kefta), Iraqi stuffed vegetables (mahshi), or Turkish stuffed eggplant differ dramatically from Ashkenazi brisket or roast chicken.

**Sabbath Bread**: As mentioned, Sephardi breads vary by community: Moroccan mouna (sweet bread), Iraqi samoon (diamond-shaped flatbread), Yemenite kubaneh (pull-apart bread), or Syrian challah with sesame.

**Desserts**: Honey-soaked pastries like baklava, sesame cookies, date-filled ma'amoul, or almond sweets replace Ashkenazi honey cake or mandelbrot.

## Saturday Night: Havdalah and Beyond

The ceremony ending Shabbat (Havdalah) is largely similar across communities, though with musical variations. Some Sephardi communities have distinctive post-Havdalah customs:

**Melaveh Malka**: Both Ashkenazi and Sephardi Jews observe the custom of eating a fourth meal after Shabbat ends (escorting the "Sabbath Queen" out). Sephardi versions may include specific songs or dishes — Moroccan Jews might prepare couscous, while Iraqi Jews serve special pastries.

## Unity Through Diversity

These differences might seem vast, yet they reflect a deeper unity. Sephardi and Ashkenazi Jews are observing the exact same biblical commandment, sanctifying the same day, resting from the same categories of forbidden work. The variations lie in the cultural expression — the melodies, foods, and specific customs — not in the fundamental observance.

For Chinese readers, this might resonate: just as regional Chinese cuisines vary dramatically while remaining authentically Chinese, Jewish regional customs vary while remaining authentically Jewish. The thread binding them together is the shared text: Torah, Talmud, and the rhythm of the weekly Shabbat cycle.

In modern Israel and diaspora communities where Ashkenazi and Sephardi Jews live side by side, these traditions enrich one another. A Sephardi family might appreciate Ashkenazi gefilte fish; an Ashkenazi family might discover the joy of Moroccan dafina. Young people in mixed communities learn each other's melodies, creating new fusion traditions.

The beauty of Sephardi Shabbat lies not in its superiority to Ashkenazi practice, but in its particularity — the way centuries of Jewish life in the Mediterranean world shaped and colored the observance of the eternal Sabbath. Each community's customs are a gift to the whole Jewish people, enriching the tapestry of tradition.
