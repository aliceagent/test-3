# Ashkenazi vs. Sephardi: Understanding the Two Great Traditions

Judaism is one religion with one Torah, given to one people at one mountain. Yet walk into two different synagogues—one Ashkenazi, one Sephardi—and you might think you're witnessing two different faiths. The melodies are different, the pronunciation of Hebrew is different, some prayers are in different orders, holiday customs vary, and even the food served afterward is entirely distinct.

How did one people develop two such distinct traditions? The answer lies in geography, history, and the interplay between unchanging Torah law and the diverse cultures where Jews lived for two millennia.

Understanding Ashkenazi and Sephardi Judaism is essential for appreciating the richness of Jewish tradition and navigating the modern Jewish world, where both traditions coexist, sometimes blend, and occasionally clash.

## The Names: What They Mean

**Ashkenaz** is the Hebrew word used in the Bible and Talmud that medieval Jews applied to the Germanic lands—roughly modern Germany, northern France, and eventually Eastern Europe (Poland, Lithuania, Russia, Ukraine, etc.).

**Sepharad** is the Hebrew word medieval Jews used for the Iberian Peninsula—Spain and Portugal.

Thus:
- **Ashkenazi Jews** (plural: Ashkenazim) are Jews whose ancestors lived in Ashkenaz—the Germanic/Eastern European tradition
- **Sephardi Jews** (plural: Sephardim) are Jews whose ancestors lived in Sepharad—the Spanish/Portuguese tradition, and by extension, the broader Mediterranean and Middle Eastern Jewish world

**Important nuance:** In modern usage, "Sephardi" sometimes refers specifically to Jews of Spanish/Portuguese origin (including their descendants in Turkey, the Balkans, the Netherlands, and the Americas) and sometimes broadly to all Jews from the Middle East and North Africa (including those who never lived in Spain)—Moroccan, Yemeni, Iraqi, Syrian, Persian, etc.

The term **Mizrachi** (מזרחי, "Eastern") is increasingly used for Middle Eastern/North African Jews to distinguish them from Iberian Sephardim, but the lines are often blurred, and "Sephardi" remains a catch-all for non-Ashkenazi traditions.

## Historical Divergence: How Did This Happen?

### The Early Centuries (70-1000 CE)

After the destruction of the Second Temple in 70 CE and the subsequent Roman persecutions, Jews scattered throughout the Roman Empire and beyond—into Babylonia (modern Iraq), North Africa, the Arabian Peninsula, and Europe.

Initially, Jewish practice remained fairly unified, centered on the Babylonian and Jerusalem Talmuds and the teachings of the Geonim (rabbinic leaders of the academies in Babylonia).

But as Jewish communities became geographically separated—with limited communication across vast distances—they began developing local customs (*minhagim*).

### The Golden Age of Spain and the Rise of Ashkenaz (1000-1492)

**Sephardic development:**

In Muslim-ruled Spain (Al-Andalus, 8th-15th centuries), Jews experienced a "Golden Age"—a period of relative prosperity, cultural flourishing, and scholarly achievement.

Great Sephardic figures:
- **Rabbi Shlomo ibn Gabirol** (1021-1070): Philosopher and poet
- **Rabbi Yehuda Halevi** (1075-1141): Poet and author of the *Kuzari*
- **Rambam (Maimonides, 1138-1204)**: The greatest medieval Jewish philosopher and codifier of Jewish law, whose *Mishneh Torah* and *Guide for the Perplexed* shaped all of Judaism

Sephardic Jews developed a culture blending Jewish learning with philosophy, science, poetry, and engagement with broader Islamic and later Christian cultures.

**Ashkenazi development:**

In Northern France and the Rhineland (Germany), Ashkenazi Jewry developed under Christian rule, often facing persecution but creating centers of intense Talmudic study.

Great Ashkenazi figures:
- **Rashi (Rabbi Shlomo Yitzchaki, 1040-1105)**: The foremost commentator on the Torah and Talmud, whose works are foundational for all Jews, Ashkenazi and Sephardi alike
- **Rabbeinu Tam** (1100-1171): Rashi's grandson, a leading Tosafist (commentators on the Talmud)
- **Rosh (Rabbi Asher ben Yechiel, 1250-1327)**: A German rabbi who fled to Spain and whose halachic works bridge Ashkenazi and Sephardic traditions

Ashkenazi culture emphasized pilpul (sharp dialectical analysis of Talmud), stringency in halachic observance, and a more insular communal structure in response to external hostility.

### The Expulsion from Spain (1492) and Its Aftermath

In 1492, the Spanish Inquisition expelled all Jews from Spain (Portugal followed in 1497). Sephardic Jews scattered:
- **To the Ottoman Empire** (Turkey, the Balkans, the Land of Israel): The Sultans welcomed Jewish refugees, and Sephardic communities flourished in Istanbul, Salonika, Safed, and Jerusalem
- **To North Africa** (Morocco, Tunisia, Algeria)
- **To the Americas** (the earliest Jewish communities in the New World were Sephardi)
- **To the Netherlands and Italy**

Meanwhile, Ashkenazi Jewry expanded eastward into Poland and Lithuania, which became the heartland of Ashkenazi life until the Holocaust.

### The Result: Two Major Streams

By the 16th century, two distinct traditions had crystallized:

**Ashkenazi Judaism:**
- Centered in Eastern Europe
- Yiddish-speaking (a Germanic-Jewish language)
- Stringent halachic tendencies
- Distinct liturgy, melodies, and customs
- Later, gave rise to Hasidic and Mitnagdic (Lithuanian yeshiva) movements

**Sephardi Judaism:**
- Centered in the Ottoman Empire, North Africa, and small communities in Western Europe and the Americas
- Ladino-speaking (Judeo-Spanish) among Spanish exiles; Arabic among Middle Eastern Jews
- More lenient halachic tendencies in some areas
- Distinct liturgy, melodies, and customs
- Greater integration of Kabbalah (Jewish mysticism) into mainstream practice

## Key Differences Between Ashkenazi and Sephardi Traditions

### 1. Hebrew Pronunciation

**Ashkenazi:** Pronounced "Ashkenazis" versus "Ashkenazit" by Sephardim
- "T" at the end of words (Shabbos, Talis)
- Kamatz pronounced "aw" (*Baruch ataw Adonai*)
- Different stress patterns

**Sephardi:** Closer to modern Israeli Hebrew
- "T" is "t" (Shabbat, Talit)
- Kamatz pronounced "ah" (*Baruch atah Adonai*)
- Stress often on the last syllable

**Modern Israeli Hebrew** is based largely on Sephardi pronunciation.

### 2. Liturgy and Prayer

Both use the same core prayers, but:

**Order and additions differ:**
- Ashkenazim add certain piyyutim (liturgical poems) on holidays that Sephardim don't
- Sephardim include Kabbalistic additions (like *Leshem Yichud*) more frequently
- The High Holy Day liturgy has significant variations

**Melodies:**
- Ashkenazi nusach (melodic prayer modes) are completely different from Sephardi
- Sephardic melodies are often influenced by Middle Eastern and Spanish musical traditions
- Ashkenazi melodies influenced by Eastern European folk music

**Nusach (prayer rite):**
- Ashkenazim use Nusach Ashkenaz or Nusach Sefard (confusingly, this is a Hasidic variant blending Ashkenazi and Kabbalistic Sephardi elements—it's still Ashkenazi!)
- Sephardim use various nuscha'ot: Spanish-Portuguese, Edot HaMizrach (Eastern communities), Moroccan, Yemenite, etc.

### 3. Passover Customs

The most famous difference:

**Kitniyot (legumes, rice, corn):**
- **Ashkenazim:** Forbidden on Passover (a medieval custom, not biblical law)
- **Sephardim:** Permitted

This stems from Ashkenazi stringency—fearing that grains like rice might be confused with forbidden chametz (leavened wheat, barley, oats, rye, spelt), Ashkenazi rabbis forbade kitniyot. Sephardi rabbis saw no need for this extra prohibition.

**Modern impact:** Ashkenazi families on Passover eat only matzah, potatoes, vegetables, meat, eggs—no rice, beans, or corn. Sephardim enjoy rice-based dishes, making Passover culinarily easier.

**Recent development:** Some contemporary Ashkenazi rabbis permit kitniyot for those in financial hardship or living where alternatives are unavailable.

### 4. Halachic Authorities and Decision-Making

**Ashkenazi halachic decisors (*poskim*):**
- Rosh (Rabbi Asher ben Yechiel)
- Rema (Rabbi Moses Isserles, 1530-1572)—whose glosses on the Shulchan Aruch represent Ashkenazi practice
- Vilna Gaon (1720-1797)
- Chofetz Chaim (1839-1933)
- Modern poskim: Rabbi Moshe Feinstein, Rabbi Yosef Eliyahu Henkin

**Sephardi halachic decisors:**
- Rambam (Maimonides)
- Rabbi Yosef Karo (1488-1575)—author of the *Shulchan Aruch*, the code of Jewish law
- Ben Ish Chai (1835-1909)—Iraqi rabbi
- Rav Ovadia Yosef (1920-2013)—modern Sephardi chief rabbi of Israel

**Key difference:** Ashkenazi tradition tends toward stringency (*chumra*); Sephardi tradition often follows Rambam's rationalist approach, preferring leniency when halachically defensible.

### 5. Kabbalah and Mysticism

**Sephardim:** Kabbalah is mainstream. The teachings of the Arizal (Rabbi Isaac Luria, 1534-1572) deeply influenced Sephardi practice. Kabbalistic intentions (*kavvanot*) are incorporated into prayers.

**Ashkenazim:** Kabbalah was more esoteric until the Hasidic movement (18th century) brought it to the masses. Mitnagdim (opponents of Hasidism) remained more rationalist.

**Practical impact:** Sephardic liturgy includes more mystical elements. For example, *Kabbalat Shabbat* (the Friday evening service welcoming Shabbat) is Kabbalistic in origin and universal now, but Sephardim were quicker to adopt it.

### 6. Lifecycle Events

**Brit Milah (circumcision):**
- Both perform on the 8th day, but certain blessings and customs differ

**Bar/Bat Mitzvah:**
- Ashkenazim typically celebrate Bar Mitzvah at 13 with a Torah reading in synagogue
- Some Sephardic communities have different customs (for example, Moroccan Jews celebrate with a *seudah* but not necessarily an aliyah)

**Weddings:**
- Both use a *chuppah* (wedding canopy), but details vary—Sephardic weddings often include elaborate piyyutim and blessings; Ashkenazi weddings feature the *badchan* (jester) in traditional communities
- Ashkenazim break a glass at the end; many Sephardim do too, but some have different customs

**Mourning:**
- Both sit shiva (seven days of mourning), but specific customs differ
- Sephardim often recite different memorial prayers

### 7. Food

Oh, the food!

**Ashkenazi cuisine:**
- Gefilte fish (ground fish patties)
- Cholent (slow-cooked Shabbat stew with meat, beans, potatoes, barley)
- Kugel (noodle or potato pudding)
- Latkes (potato pancakes for Chanukah)
- Brisket, matzah ball soup, bagels, lox
- Influences: Polish, Russian, German, Hungarian

**Sephardic cuisine:**
- Fish in spicy tomato sauce
- Hamin (similar to cholent but with chickpeas, rice, eggs)
- Couscous, falafel, hummus
- Burekas (savory pastries)
- Sufganiyot (jelly donuts for Chanukah—originally Sephardi)
- Influences: Spanish, Moroccan, Turkish, Iraqi, Yemeni

**Humor:** Ashkenazim joke that their food is bland (boiled everything). Sephardim joke that Ashkenazi food has no flavor. Both are delicious in their own right!

### 8. Rabbinical Leadership

**Ashkenazi:**
- The rabbi (rav) is a scholar-authority figure
- Yeshivot (Talmudic academies) are central
- Stringency in halacha is respected

**Sephardi:**
- The *chacham* (wise one) or *mori* (my teacher) serves as spiritual guide and halachic authority
- Emphasis on respect for elders and tradition
- Balance between strictness and compassion

## Are They Really That Different?

Yes and no.

**The core is identical:**
- Same Torah
- Same Talmud
- Same 613 mitzvot
- Same belief in one God, the chosenness of the Jewish people, the binding nature of halacha

**The differences are real but secondary:**
- Customs, not laws (mostly)
- Melodies, not theology
- Cultural flavors, not fundamental beliefs

An Ashkenazi Jew can pray in a Sephardi synagogue and vice versa. The prayers are recognizable, the rituals familiar. It's like two dialects of the same language.

## Modern Developments: Blending and Tension

### In Israel

Israel has brought Ashkenazi and Sephardi Jews together in unprecedented ways.

**Challenges:**
- Early Israeli society was dominated by Ashkenazi elites; Sephardim faced discrimination
- Political and social tensions emerged (Sephardim often support right-wing parties; Ashkenazim historically dominated left-wing movements)

**Blending:**
- Intermarriage between Ashkenazim and Sephardim is common
- Israeli Hebrew pronunciation is Sephardi
- Israeli food culture blends both traditions (hummus and gefilte fish coexist)
- Chief Rabbinate has both Ashkenazi and Sephardi chief rabbis

### In the Diaspora

In the United States and elsewhere, most synagogues are Ashkenazi, but Sephardic communities exist and thrive.

**Increasing dialogue:** Modern Orthodox and Haredi communities increasingly respect both traditions. Some Ashkenazim adopt Sephardi leniencies (like kitniyot on Passover); some Sephardim adopt Ashkenazi stringencies.

### Mixed Families

When Ashkenazi and Sephardi Jews marry:
- Often adopt the father's tradition (traditional halachic rule) or choose one tradition for consistency
- Some blend: Ashkenazi liturgy but Sephardi Passover customs, for example
- Children might feel pulled between two heritages

## Which Is "Right"?

Neither. Both.

The Talmud (Pesachim 50b) teaches: "A person should never deviate from local custom." Both Ashkenazi and Sephardi traditions are authentic, halachically valid expressions of Torah.

Rav Ovadia Yosef (the preeminent Sephardi halachic authority of the 20th century) ruled that Sephardim should not adopt Ashkenazi stringencies unless halachically necessary, and vice versa. Each tradition has its integrity.

**For newcomers to Judaism:** Choose based on community, teacher, or personal connection. There's no inherently superior tradition.

## Conclusion: One People, Many Colors

The existence of Ashkenazi and Sephardi traditions is not a weakness but a strength. It shows Judaism's adaptability—how the unchanging Torah can be lived faithfully in vastly different cultural contexts.

A Yemenite Jew and a Lithuanian Jew might sound different when they pray, eat different foods, and cite different authorities—but they're brothers, united by the same covenant, the same history, and the same destiny.

The Talmud (Sanhedrin 37a) teaches that God created humanity from one person to show that no one can say, "My ancestor was greater than yours." Similarly, no Jew can claim their tradition is superior. We are all children of Abraham, inheritors of Sinai, bearers of the same sacred mission.

In the modern world, where Ashkenazi and Sephardi Jews live side by side, study together, and marry each other, the distinctions are softening—but the richness remains. Learning both traditions, respecting both, and drawing from the wisdom of both makes us fuller Jews.

As the saying goes: *Klal Yisrael*—the whole of Israel—is like a tapestry woven from many colored threads. Ashkenazi and Sephardi are two of the most prominent colors, each beautiful, each essential to the whole.
