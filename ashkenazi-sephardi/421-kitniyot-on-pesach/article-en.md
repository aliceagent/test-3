# Kitniyot on Pesach: Why Ashkenazim and Sephardim Eat Differently

One of the most visible differences between Ashkenazi and Sephardi Jewish practice appears on the Passover dinner table. While both communities meticulously avoid chametz (leavened bread products made from wheat, barley, rye, oats, or spelt), Ashkenazi Jews add another layer of restriction: kitniyot — rice, beans, corn, lentils, peas, and similar legumes and grains.

For Chinese readers familiar with rice as a dietary staple, the idea that some Jews avoid rice during Passover while others eat it freely might seem puzzling. This divergence offers a fascinating window into how Jewish law develops through custom, and how different communities can maintain distinct practices while remaining part of the same tradition.

## What is Kitniyot?

The Hebrew word "kitniyot" (קִטְנִיּוֹת) originally referred to legumes — beans, lentils, peas, and similar foods. Over time, especially in medieval Europe, the category expanded to include rice, corn, millet, and even foods made from these ingredients. Some authorities today even include peanuts, soy, and sesame in this category.

Importantly, kitniyot is not chametz. Chametz refers specifically to the five grains (wheat, barley, rye, oats, spelt) that can ferment and leaven when mixed with water. Eating chametz on Passover violates a direct biblical prohibition carrying severe consequences. Kitniyot, by contrast, is a custom (minhag) adopted by Ashkenazi communities — a "fence around the Torah" meant to prevent accidental violations of the actual prohibition.

## How Did This Custom Begin?

The kitniyot prohibition emerged in medieval France and Germany, roughly in the 13th century. The Talmud itself makes no mention of avoiding kitniyot on Passover, and the great Babylonian academies never established such a practice. This was a European innovation.

Several reasons are given for the origin of this custom:

**Confusion with Actual Grains**: In medieval times, legumes and grains were often stored in the same sacks, ground in the same mills, and sold in the same markets. Flour from kitniyot could easily become mixed with wheat flour. Since identifying which flour was which might be difficult, especially after milling, avoiding all kitniyot prevented accidental consumption of chametz.

**Similar Preparation Methods**: Rice and legumes can be cooked into porridge-like dishes that resemble chametz preparations. Bread could even be made from rice or corn flour. The concern was that people might become confused, thinking that if rice bread is permitted, perhaps wheat bread is too — a dangerous misconception.

**The "Swelling" Factor**: Some medieval authorities noted that kitniyot swells when cooked, somewhat resembling the rising of leavened dough. Though this isn't actual fermentation, the visual similarity raised concerns.

The great Ashkenazi authority Rabbi Moses Isserles (the Rema, 1530-1572) codified this custom in his glosses to the Shulchan Aruch, the authoritative code of Jewish law. He acknowledged that kitniyot is not actually chametz, but ruled that Ashkenazim should maintain their ancestral custom of avoiding it. Once established, the custom took on the force of communal law.

## Why Sephardim Never Adopted This Custom

Meanwhile, Sephardi Jews in Spain, North Africa, the Middle East, and the Mediterranean never adopted this stringency. Why not?

**Different Agricultural Environments**: In Mediterranean and Middle Eastern climates, rice, legumes, and wheat were distinct crops grown in different conditions. The risk of mixture was much lower. Rice paddies don't resemble wheat fields.

**Established Talmudic Tradition**: Sephardi communities maintained closer ties to the Babylonian Talmudic tradition, where rice and legumes were commonly eaten year-round without confusion with chametz. For them, adding new restrictions without clear Talmudic basis seemed unnecessary.

**Different Legal Philosophy**: Sephardi authorities, following scholars like Maimonides (the Rambam), generally preferred not to add stringencies beyond what the Torah and Talmud explicitly require. The approach emphasized clarity in law: chametz is forbidden; everything else is permitted unless clearly problematic.

The Spanish rabbi Joseph Caro (1488-1575), author of the Shulchan Aruch, explicitly permitted kitniyot. Living in Safed (in modern-day Israel), he followed Sephardi tradition. Though Ashkenazi communities accepted his code of law, they maintained their own customs where they differed — the kitniyot prohibition being one of the most significant.

## Modern Developments and Debates

In contemporary times, this division has generated considerable discussion:

**Israeli Society**: Modern Israel is a melting pot where Ashkenazi and Sephardi Jews live side by side. Intermarriage between communities is common. Supermarkets stock both "kitniyot-free" and regular Passover products. Many families have one spouse who grew up avoiding kitniyot and another who grew up eating rice on Passover. Halachic authorities have had to navigate these mixed households, generally ruling that each spouse may follow their ancestral custom.

**The Conservative Movement's 2015 Decision**: In 2015, the Rabbinical Assembly of Conservative Judaism (primarily serving Ashkenazi communities) issued a ruling permitting kitniyot. They argued that the original reasons for the prohibition no longer apply in modern food production, where contamination risks are minimal and labeling is clear. This decision was controversial but reflects how customs can adapt to changed circumstances.

**Practical Challenges**: For Ashkenazi Jews keeping kosher, the kitniyot prohibition significantly restricts diet during Passover's eight days. It eliminates rice, beans, lentils, corn, peas, soy, and products containing these ingredients — making nutrition more challenging and expensive. Many rely heavily on potatoes, which are permitted.

**Baby Formula and Medicine**: Modern authorities generally permit kitniyot-derived baby formula and essential medications for Ashkenazim, recognizing that health takes precedence and that the prohibition was only ever a custom, not biblical law.

## What This Teaches Us About Jewish Law

The kitniyot custom illustrates several important principles:

**Minhag Matters**: Custom can be as binding as law within a community. "Minhag Yisrael Torah hi" — "the custom of Israel is Torah." Even practices not found in the Talmud can become authoritative through communal acceptance.

**Unity in Diversity**: Judaism maintains remarkable unity despite significant differences in practice. Ashkenazi and Sephardi Jews recognize each other's practices as legitimate, even when different. Neither claims the other is "doing it wrong."

**Dynamic Tradition**: Jewish law isn't static. Communities respond to local conditions, creating practices that make sense in their context. What's stringent in one place may be unnecessary in another.

**The Fence Around the Torah**: Many Jewish customs exist not because something is inherently forbidden, but as protective measures. The kitniyot prohibition aimed to prevent accidental violation of actual biblical law — a fence around the Torah.

## Bridging the Divide

For Chinese readers learning about Judaism, this difference might initially seem confusing. Why can't Jews simply agree? But consider: China too has regional variations in custom, cuisine, and practice that don't undermine Chinese identity. Northern and southern Chinese food traditions differ dramatically, yet both are authentically Chinese.

Similarly, Ashkenazi avoidance of kitniyot and Sephardi permission to eat it are both authentically Jewish. The tradition is large enough to contain both practices, bound together by shared commitment to the core laws of Passover and the meaning of the festival itself.

Today, when an Ashkenazi Jew visits a Sephardi home for Passover and sees rice on the table, or vice versa, it's not a moment of judgment but of recognition: here is another branch of the same tree, nourished by the same roots, reaching toward the same heaven.
