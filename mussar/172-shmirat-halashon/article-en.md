# Guarding Your Speech (Shmirat HaLashon): The Laws of Lashon Hara

## Introduction: The Power of Words

In the Jewish tradition, words hold extraordinary power. The Talmud teaches that "death and life are in the power of the tongue" (Proverbs 18:21). While physical wounds may heal, the damage caused by harmful speech can last a lifetime. The concept of *Shmirat HaLashon* — literally "guarding your tongue" — represents one of Judaism's most important ethical teachings, addressing how we speak about others when they're not present to defend themselves.

The Hebrew term *lashon hara* (literally "evil tongue") refers to true but derogatory speech about another person. It's distinct from slander, which is false. Remarkably, Judaism considers speaking truthful but damaging information about someone to be even more serious than lying, because the victim has no recourse — they cannot defend themselves by claiming the statement is untrue.

## Why Is Speech So Important in Judaism?

Judaism teaches that God created the world through speech: "And God said, 'Let there be light,' and there was light" (Genesis 1:3). Human beings, created in God's image, share this creative power. Our words can build or destroy, heal or wound, unite or divide communities.

The sages teach that *lashon hara* is equivalent to the three cardinal sins combined: idolatry, forbidden sexual relations, and murder (Talmud, Arachin 15b). Why murder? Because harmful speech can "kill" three people: the speaker, the listener, and the person being spoken about. It destroys the speaker's character, corrupts the listener's perception, and damages the subject's reputation — a form of social death.

## What Qualifies as Lashon Hara?

Understanding what constitutes *lashon hara* is nuanced. The definitive work on this topic is the *Chafetz Chaim* (literally "Desires Life"), written by Rabbi Yisrael Meir Kagan in the late 19th century. Here are the key principles:

### 1. True but Harmful Information

If sharing information will cause someone harm — financially, emotionally, or socially — it's generally considered *lashon hara*, even if true. For example:

- "Did you hear that Sarah lost her job? She wasn't performing well."
- "David's business is struggling — I heard he can't pay his suppliers."
- "That family's son dropped out of university."

Even if these statements are accurate, sharing them serves no constructive purpose and damages reputations.

### 2. It Includes Negative Implications

*Lashon hara* isn't limited to explicit criticism. It includes:

- **Damning with faint praise**: "For someone with no training, she did surprisingly well."
- **Body language and tone**: Rolling your eyes or saying "We won't discuss what happened at the meeting..."
- **Revealing private information**: Sharing details someone wished to keep confidential, even if not explicitly negative.

### 3. It Applies to Groups Too

Speaking negatively about communities, organizations, or groups of people falls under these laws. Stereotyping or disparaging entire communities causes collective harm.

## What Lashon Hara Is NOT

There are legitimate exceptions when sharing negative information is permitted or even required:

### 1. Constructive Purpose (To'elet)

When sharing information serves a genuine constructive purpose and meets strict conditions:

- **Business references**: Warning someone about a dishonest business partner (but only with firsthand knowledge and proper intent).
- **Matchmaking**: Sharing relevant concerns about a potential spouse (if asked directly and the information is verifiable).
- **Protecting others from harm**: Alerting someone to danger or preventing injustice.

However, these exceptions require meeting seven conditions outlined by the Chafetz Chaim, including: direct knowledge, no exaggeration, proper intent, no alternative solution, and that revealing the information won't cause disproportionate harm.

### 2. Public Information

Once information is genuinely public knowledge (published in credible news sources, public court records), the prohibition may be lessened, though the ethical obligation to avoid gossip remains.

### 3. In the Presence of Three

If information was shared openly in front of three people, the assumption is that it will become public. However, actively spreading it further is still problematic.

## The Subtle Dangers: Rechilut and Avak Lashon Hara

Jewish law identifies related prohibitions:

### Rechilut (Tale-Bearing)

This means reporting to someone what others said about them, even if the statements weren't necessarily negative. "Did you know that Michael said you should have handled that situation differently?" This creates discord and suspicion between people, even if the original comment was constructive criticism.

### Avak Lashon Hara (The "Dust" of Evil Speech)

This includes speech that *leads to* lashon hara or creates the conditions for it:

- Praising someone excessively in front of their enemies (inviting contradiction)
- Saying "I don't want to talk about what happened..." (planting suspicion)
- Asking "Where did you hear that?" (encouraging gossip)

## Listening Is Also Forbidden

The prohibition extends to the listener. Accepting *lashon hara* is considered as serious as speaking it. The Talmud states: "For *lashon hara*, four are punished: the speaker, the acceptor, the subject, and the one who gave credence to it."

What should you do when someone begins sharing *lashon hara*?

1. **Don't believe it**: Remind yourself that you're hearing one perspective.
2. **Gently redirect**: "I'd prefer not to discuss this" or "Can we talk about something else?"
3. **If you must listen**: Sometimes social circumstances make it difficult to leave. Don't validate the speech, don't ask follow-up questions, and don't believe or repeat what you heard.
4. **Judge favorably**: If you already know the information, try to interpret it charitably.

## Practical Guidelines for Daily Life

### Before Speaking, Ask Three Questions:

1. **Is it true?** (If not, it's slander, not just *lashon hara*)
2. **Is it necessary?** (Does sharing this serve any constructive purpose?)
3. **Is it kind?** (Will this harm someone's reputation or relationships?)

### Common Situations:

**At work**: Discussing colleagues' performance issues with people who have no need to know is *lashon hara*. However, addressing performance with a supervisor in good faith and seeking solutions is permitted.

**Among friends**: Sharing personal struggles someone confided in you violates trust. Even saying "Please pray for my friend who's going through something difficult" requires permission.

**Online**: Social media amplifies *lashon hara*. Public complaints, negative reviews written in anger, and critical comments about public figures all require careful consideration.

## The Positive Commandment: Speak Well

*Shmirat HaLashon* isn't just about avoiding harm — it's about actively speaking positively:

- **Judge favorably** (*dan l'kaf zechut*): When you hear something questionable, interpret it charitably.
- **Speak words of encouragement**: Build people up rather than tearing them down.
- **Give honest, constructive feedback**: When criticism is necessary (to the person directly, with love), it should be specific, kind, and helpful.

## Chinese Parallels: The Universal Wisdom of Guarded Speech

The importance of careful speech resonates across cultures. Consider these Chinese proverbs that echo Jewish wisdom:

**"病从口入,祸从口出" (Bìng cóng kǒu rù, huò cóng kǒu chū)**: "Illness enters through the mouth; disaster comes out of it." This mirrors the Jewish understanding that our words can bring harm upon ourselves and others.

**"言多必失" (Yán duō bì shī)**: "Speaking much leads to mistakes." The Talmud similarly teaches: "I have found nothing better for a person than silence" (Pirkei Avot 1:17).

**"谨言慎行" (Jǐn yán shèn xíng)**: "Be cautious in speech and prudent in action." This aligns with the Jewish concept of *shmirat ha'lashon*, recognizing that words and deeds are equally important.

In Confucian thought, *ren* (仁, benevolence) includes treating others with kindness in both action and speech. Similarly, Jewish ethics emphasizes that speech is a form of action with real consequences.

## The Challenge and the Reward

Guarding your speech is one of Judaism's most difficult commandments. The Chafetz Chaim noted that most people violate prohibitions against *lashon hara* daily, often without realizing it. We live in a culture where gossip is entertainment, "venting" is considered healthy, and sharing opinions about others is normalized.

Yet the rewards are profound:

- **Stronger relationships**: Trust grows when people know you won't speak about them behind their backs.
- **Inner peace**: The Chafetz Chaim promises that one who guards their tongue merits seeing the goodness of God and living a long, good life.
- **Spiritual growth**: Controlling the tongue requires mastering our impulses, developing empathy, and cultivating humility.

The Talmud teaches that when the Temple was destroyed, prophecy was taken away from prophets and given to children and fools — meaning that truth can come from unexpected sources. But regarding *lashon hara*, the Talmud states: "Anyone who speaks *lashon hara* is as if they deny the fundamental principle" — denying God's watchfulness over human speech.

## Starting Your Journey

Begin small:

1. **Awareness**: Notice when you're about to speak about someone not present. Pause.
2. **The 24-hour rule**: Before sharing negative information, wait 24 hours. Often the impulse passes.
3. **Study**: Read a daily lesson from the Chafetz Chaim's works (available in many languages).
4. **Find an accountability partner**: Work on this mitzvah with a friend.
5. **Self-forgiveness**: You will slip. Learn from it and try again.

## Conclusion

The power to speak is one of God's greatest gifts to humanity. With it, we teach, comfort, inspire, and connect. The laws of *lashon hara* aren't meant to silence us, but to elevate us — to transform our speech into a force for good.

As the Psalms (34:13-14) teach: "Who is the person who desires life and loves days to see good? Guard your tongue from evil, and your lips from speaking deceit." The path to a good life, Judaism teaches, runs directly through our mouths. Every word is a choice between building and destroying, between life and death.

Choose wisely. Your words create your world.
