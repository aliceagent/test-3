# Anger Management the Jewish Way: What the Rambam Taught

Anger is universal. Whether you live in Jerusalem, Beijing, or New York, everyone knows what it feels like when fury rises in your chest—when someone cuts you off in traffic, when a colleague takes credit for your work, when your child refuses to listen for the tenth time.

But while anger is universal, the ways we handle it differ dramatically across cultures. In Jewish tradition, one of the most profound guides to managing anger comes from the Rambam (Rabbi Moses Maimonides, 1138-1204), the great medieval philosopher and Torah scholar whose teachings continue to shape Jewish life today.

## The Rambam's Radical Position on Anger

In his Mishneh Torah, the Rambam makes a striking claim: anger is not just unpleasant or socially destructive—it's spiritually catastrophic. He writes:

> "Anger is an extremely bad trait, and it is proper for a person to distance themselves from it to the opposite extreme. One should train oneself not to become angry even over something that would justify anger." (*Hilchot De'ot* 2:3)

Notice the Rambam doesn't say "avoid excessive anger" or "try to be less angry." He says to train yourself not to become angry at all, even when anger would be justified. This is radical advice—especially in a world that often celebrates "righteous indignation" and sees anger as a sign of caring deeply about justice.

But the Rambam goes further. He teaches that chronic anger is so destructive that it's actually a form of idolatry. When a person is controlled by rage, they've essentially replaced God with their own ego. Instead of trusting in divine justice, they demand immediate vindication. Instead of seeing other people as created in God's image, they see them as obstacles to be destroyed.

## The Middle Path—and Why Anger Is the Exception

The Rambam is famous for teaching the "golden mean"—the idea that virtue lies in the middle between two extremes. Be generous, but not to the point of bankrupting yourself. Be humble, but not to the point of having no self-respect.

But anger is the great exception to this rule.

The Rambam teaches that for most traits, the middle path is best. But for anger, even a little is too much. He compares it to a deadly poison: a small dose is still dangerous. He advises training yourself to remain calm even when insulted, even when wronged, even when you have every "right" to be furious.

This doesn't mean you can't recognize injustice or work to correct it. What it means is that you do so from a place of clarity and strength, not from the chaos of rage.

## Why Is Anger So Destructive?

The Rambam understood something modern psychology has confirmed: anger doesn't help you think more clearly—it actively impairs your judgment. When you're angry:

- You say things you don't mean and can't take back
- You make decisions you'll regret later
- You damage relationships that took years to build
- You lose sight of your long-term goals
- You become a slave to your emotions rather than master of yourself

Moreover, anger is contagious. When you lash out at someone, they lash out at someone else. The cycle of hurt perpetuates itself. The Talmud teaches that when a person is in a rage, even the Divine Presence is not important to them (Nedarim 22b). You literally push God out of the room when you let anger take over.

## Practical Tools from the Rambam

So how do you actually implement the Rambam's teaching? How do you train yourself not to be angry in a world that gives you a thousand reasons to be furious every day?

### 1. **Recognize the Triggers Early**

The Rambam advises becoming aware of what situations trigger your anger. Is it when you feel disrespected? When plans change unexpectedly? When you're hungry or tired? Once you know your patterns, you can prepare yourself mentally before entering high-risk situations.

### 2. **Practice Deliberate Calm**

Act as if you're not angry, even when you feel the rage building. Speak softly. Move slowly. The Rambam teaches that changing your external behavior can gradually reshape your internal emotional state. This isn't "faking it"—it's training yourself through repetition, the same way an athlete trains their body.

### 3. **Reframe the Situation**

Ask yourself: Will this matter in a year? In five years? Most things that enrage us in the moment turn out to be trivial with time. The Rambam encourages us to take the long view, to see our lives from the perspective of eternity rather than the heat of the moment.

### 4. **Remember You're Being Watched**

Not by human eyes, but by God. The awareness that you stand before the Creator at every moment has a sobering effect. Would you scream and curse in the presence of someone you deeply respect? Then why do it in God's presence?

### 5. **Seek to Understand, Not to Win**

Often we get angry because we feel we're "right" and the other person is "wrong." The Rambam teaches us to seek understanding instead. What circumstances led this person to act this way? What pain might they be carrying? Compassion is the antidote to rage.

## A Chinese Parallel: Confucius and the Analects

Interestingly, while the Rambam was writing in 12th-century Egypt, similar ideas about anger control were being taught in China centuries earlier. Confucius (551-479 BCE) taught in the *Analects*:

> "When anger rises, think of the consequences." (*Analects* 12:21)

And:

> "The superior man is modest in his speech but exceeds in his actions."

Both traditions recognize that anger clouds judgment and that self-mastery—not self-expression—is the mark of a truly wise person. In Daoist thought, too, there's an emphasis on *wu wei* (effortless action)—responding to situations with calm flexibility rather than force. This aligns remarkably well with the Rambam's teaching that true strength lies in remaining unmoved by provocation.

Chinese culture has long emphasized harmony (*he*) as a supreme value, both within oneself and in society. The Jewish concept of *shalom* (peace/wholeness) similarly suggests that inner tranquility is foundational to all other virtues. Both traditions see emotional self-regulation not as weakness, but as the highest form of strength.

## The Exception That Proves the Rule

Now, you might ask: are there times when anger is appropriate? The Torah itself describes God as becoming "angry" at injustice. And there are moments in Jewish history when righteous figures expressed fierce opposition to evil.

The Rambam acknowledges this. He says that sometimes a teacher or parent may need to *appear* angry to discipline effectively—but even then, they should remain calm inside. The display of anger is a teaching tool, not a genuine loss of control. It's like an actor playing a role, fully aware they're performing.

And yes, there's a place for passionate opposition to injustice. But the Rambam distinguishes between *measured indignation* directed at correcting evil and *uncontrolled rage* that seeks only to vent or destroy. The former comes from a place of love for what's right; the latter comes from wounded ego.

## Living It Out

The Rambam's teaching on anger is both inspiring and daunting. How do you go from where you are now—perhaps someone who loses their temper regularly—to someone who remains calm even under provocation?

The answer is the same as with any spiritual practice: slowly, imperfectly, and with compassion for yourself.

Start small. The next time someone cuts you off in traffic, instead of honking and cursing, take a deep breath and say a blessing. When your spouse says something that stings, pause for three seconds before responding. When your child spills juice on your laptop, remember that this too shall pass.

Each small victory over anger is a victory over the ego. Each moment of calm is a moment of clarity. And over time, these moments add up. You become the kind of person who doesn't need to raise their voice to be heard, who doesn't need to rage to feel powerful, who doesn't need to lash out to protect their sense of self.

This is the path the Rambam lays out: not the elimination of anger through suppression, but the transformation of anger through awareness, discipline, and faith.

## Conclusion: The Long Game

In our instant-gratification culture, we want quick fixes. We want a meditation app or a breathing technique that will solve our anger problem in ten days.

But the Rambam is offering something deeper and more enduring. He's offering a lifelong practice of character development, a gradual reshaping of who you are at your core. It's not easy. But it's the work that actually transforms you from the inside out.

And here's the beautiful thing: you don't have to be perfect. The Rambam himself wasn't born calm—he trained himself to become that way. Judaism doesn't expect you to be a saint overnight. It just asks you to take one step, then another, in the direction of who you want to become.

So the next time you feel anger rising, remember the Rambam. Remember that you have a choice. You can let the fire consume you, or you can let it refine you.

Choose wisely.
