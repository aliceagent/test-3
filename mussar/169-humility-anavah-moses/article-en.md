# Humility (Anavah): The Trait Moses Was Known For

## The Greatest Compliment in the Torah

In the entire Torah, only one person receives a superlative description of their character traits. Not Abraham, known for his kindness. Not Sarah, praised for her beauty and wisdom. Not Jacob, who wrestled with angels. The Torah reserves its most extraordinary compliment for Moses, and it's surprising: "Now Moses was very humble (*anav me'od*), more than any other person on the face of the earth" (Numbers 12:3).

This is remarkable for several reasons. First, Moses was the greatest prophet who ever lived, the one who spoke to God "face to face." Second, he led millions of people out of slavery and through the wilderness. Third, he received and transmitted the entire Torah. Yet the Torah doesn't emphasize his wisdom, courage, or leadership—it emphasizes his humility.

What is this quality that defined the greatest leader in Jewish history? And how can we, ordinary people living thousands of years later, cultivate it in our own lives?

## What Humility Is (And Isn't)

Before we can understand Moses's humility, we need to clarify what humility actually means in Jewish thought. The Hebrew word *anavah* (humility) is often misunderstood, especially when translated into other languages.

**Humility is NOT:**
- Thinking you're worthless or incompetent
- Denying your talents and accomplishments
- Being passive or refusing to take credit where it's due
- False modesty or self-deprecation for show
- Allowing others to walk all over you

**True humility IS:**
- Accurate self-assessment—knowing both your strengths and limitations
- Recognizing that your talents are gifts from God, not self-generated
- Being open to learning from anyone, regardless of their status
- Focusing on the task rather than your ego
- Feeling gratitude rather than entitlement

The Rambam (Maimonides) explains that humility is the middle path between arrogance and self-abasement. A humble person doesn't think they're superior to others, nor do they think they're inferior. They simply see themselves accurately, in context.

## Moses's Humility in Action

How did Moses demonstrate this quality? The Torah gives us several revealing examples:

### 1. At the Burning Bush: "Who Am I?"

When God first called Moses to lead the Jewish people out of Egypt, Moses's response was telling: "Who am I that I should go to Pharaoh and bring the children of Israel out of Egypt?" (Exodus 3:11).

This wasn't false modesty. Moses had real concerns: he had a speech impediment, he'd been away from Egypt for decades, and he genuinely wondered if he was the right person for this monumental task. His humility allowed him to question whether he truly had what it took—not out of cowardice, but out of honest self-reflection.

Yet when God assured him and gave him the tools he needed, Moses accepted the mission. True humility doesn't mean refusing responsibility; it means taking it on with awareness of your limitations and dependence on God.

### 2. Sharing Authority: The Appointment of the Elders

In Numbers 11, we see Moses overwhelmed by the burden of leading the people alone. Rather than insisting on controlling everything himself, he accepted God's suggestion to appoint seventy elders to help him lead.

When two men, Eldad and Medad, began prophesying in the camp without Moses's permission, Joshua urged Moses to stop them. But Moses replied, "Are you jealous for my sake? Would that all God's people were prophets!" (Numbers 11:29).

This is humility in leadership: Moses didn't feel threatened when others rose to positions of spiritual authority. He didn't guard his status or worry about being replaced. His focus was on what was best for the people, not on maintaining his position.

### 3. Accepting Criticism: Miriam and Aaron's Challenge

Perhaps the most striking example comes in Numbers 12, immediately before the Torah describes Moses's humility. His own siblings, Miriam and Aaron, criticized him—questioning his unique prophetic status and his choice of wife.

How did Moses respond? He didn't defend himself, argue back, or pull rank as leader. The Torah tells us he said nothing. When God punished Miriam with leprosy, Moses immediately prayed for her healing with the shortest prayer in the Torah: "God, please heal her!" (*El na refa na lah*).

A person of lesser character might have let their sister suffer a bit longer, thinking she deserved it. Moses's humility allowed him to put aside any hurt feelings and immediately seek her well-being.

## The Paradox of Humble Leadership

Here's what seems contradictory: Moses was the most humble person on earth, yet he was also the most powerful leader in Jewish history. He stood before Pharaoh and demanded the freedom of an entire nation. He confronted the people when they sinned with the Golden Calf. He didn't hesitate to transmit God's laws, even the difficult ones.

How can someone be both humble and such a forceful leader?

The answer lies in understanding that Moses's humility freed him from ego concerns. When you're not worried about protecting your image or status, you can speak truth fearlessly. When you know your authority comes from God rather than yourself, you can use it without arrogance.

Humble leaders are often the most effective because they:
- Listen to others and accept good advice
- Admit mistakes and correct course quickly
- Focus on the mission rather than personal glory
- Inspire loyalty because people trust their motives
- Make decisions based on what's right, not what makes them look good

Moses could be strong precisely because he was humble. His strength came from God, not from his ego.

## Humility in Chinese Wisdom: Parallel Paths

Interestingly, the Jewish emphasis on humility finds strong parallels in classical Chinese philosophy. Both traditions, developed thousands of miles apart, arrived at similar insights about this crucial character trait.

In Confucian thought, the concept of *qian* (谦, humility/modesty) is one of the fundamental virtues. Confucius taught: "The superior person is modest in speech but exceeds in actions." This mirrors the Jewish teaching that actions speak louder than words, and that true greatness doesn't need to announce itself.

The *Analects* record Confucius saying, "When you know a thing, to hold that you know it; and when you do not know a thing, to allow that you do not know it—this is knowledge." This is remarkably similar to the Jewish concept of intellectual humility: being honest about the limits of your understanding.

In Daoist philosophy, humility is even more central. The *Tao Te Ching* repeatedly emphasizes that water, the humblest element that always seeks the lowest place, is actually the strongest force in nature. Chapter 8 states: "The highest good is like water. Water gives life to all things and does not compete with them. It flows in places people reject and so is like the Tao."

Similarly, Jewish tradition teaches that Torah is compared to water—it sustains life, and just as water naturally flows downward, Torah only "stays" with those who make themselves humble and receptive.

Both traditions warn against arrogance. The ancient Chinese proverb teaches, "A full vessel makes no sound; a half-empty vessel makes the most noise." The Talmud similarly states, "If you have studied much Torah, do not claim credit for yourself, for this is the purpose for which you were created" (Pirkei Avot 2:8).

## How to Cultivate Humility: Practical Steps

Understanding humility intellectually is one thing; developing it is another. The Mussar movement, a Jewish ethical development tradition founded in 19th-century Lithuania, offers practical exercises for cultivating humility:

### 1. Daily Gratitude Practice

Each morning, before your feet hit the floor, acknowledge: "I am alive today not by my own merit, but by God's kindness." This simple recognition recalibrates your perspective. Everything you have—your talents, opportunities, even your next breath—is a gift.

### 2. Learn from Everyone

The Mishnah teaches, "Who is wise? One who learns from every person" (Pirkei Avot 4:1). Make it a practice to find something to learn from each person you encounter, regardless of their age, education, or social status. The janitor may know something the professor doesn't. The child may see something the adult missed.

### 3. Focus on Contribution, Not Recognition

Before starting a task, ask yourself: "How can I contribute here?" rather than "How will this make me look?" This shifts your focus from ego to impact. Do good work for its own sake, not for the applause it might bring.

### 4. Acknowledge Your Limitations

When you don't know something, say so. When you make a mistake, admit it quickly and learn from it. The Talmud praises someone who says "I don't know" when they truly don't know—it's a sign of intellectual honesty, not weakness.

### 5. Practice Active Listening

In conversation, resist the urge to interrupt or mentally rehearse your response while others are speaking. Genuinely listen to understand, not just to respond. This communicates that you value others' perspectives and don't assume yours is automatically superior.

### 6. Study the Lives of the Humble

Regularly read about people who embodied humility—Moses, Rabbi Akiva, the Baal Shem Tov. Let their examples inspire and challenge you. Notice how their humility enhanced rather than diminished their impact.

### 7. Serve Others Anonymously

Do acts of kindness where no one will know it was you. Donate anonymously, help without taking credit, clean up messes you didn't make. This trains you to value the good deed itself rather than the recognition.

## The Humble Path Forward

Moses's greatest legacy isn't the miracles he performed or the laws he transmitted—it's the model he provided of what a human being can become. He showed us that true greatness and deep humility aren't opposites; they're partners.

In a world that constantly tells us to promote ourselves, build our personal brands, and never show weakness, the Jewish path offers something radically different. It tells us that the most exalted position a person can reach is not above others, but alongside them. The greatest power comes not from dominating others, but from serving something greater than yourself.

As you navigate your own journey toward Jewish observance and character development, remember that you don't need to become someone other than who you are. Humility isn't about erasing yourself—it's about seeing yourself clearly in the context of something larger. Your talents are real; they're just not solely your own creation. Your accomplishments matter; they're just not the measure of your worth.

Moses stood before Pharaoh, split the Red Sea, and spoke with God—yet remained the humblest person on earth. What would it look like for you to bring your own unique gifts to the world with that same combination of strength and humility? That's the question worth pondering as you walk this ancient path, adding your own footsteps to those of Moses and all who have followed his humble way.
