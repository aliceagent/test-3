## Introduction

Nearly four thousand years ago—during what corresponds to China's Shang Dynasty (商朝, ~1600-1046 BCE)—a man in ancient Mesopotamia (modern-day Iraq) made a discovery that would change human history. His name was Abraham (אַבְרָהָם, *Avraham*), and he is considered the first Jew, the founding father of the Jewish people, and a spiritual ancestor to billions of people across Judaism, Christianity, and Islam.

But Abraham wasn't born Jewish. There was no Judaism yet. There were no synagogues, no Torah scrolls, no rabbis, no Shabbat candles. So what made him the "first Jew"? What did he discover? And why does his story still matter today—especially for someone in modern China exploring Judaism for the first time?

Abraham's journey is the story of a radical spiritual breakthrough: the discovery of monotheism—the idea that there is one God who created the universe, who cares about human beings, and who asks us to live with justice and compassion. This was a revolutionary idea in a world dominated by idol worship, where gods were seen as capricious forces of nature that needed to be appeased through sacrifice and ritual.

Abraham didn't just reject the idols of his time. He established a new way of living—one based on faith, ethical action, and a covenant relationship with the Divine. His life became the template for what it means to be Jewish: to question, to seek truth, to stand up for justice, and to welcome strangers with radical hospitality.

## The Midrash: Abraham Smashes His Father's Idols

The Torah itself doesn't give us many details about Abraham's early life. It simply tells us that God called to him when he was 75 years old and said: *"Go forth from your land, from your birthplace, and from your father's house, to the land that I will show you"* (Genesis 12:1).

But the rabbis of the Talmudic era—roughly 200-500 CE, during China's Three Kingdoms and Jin Dynasty period (三国至晋朝)—filled in the backstory through *midrash* (interpretive stories that expand on biblical narratives). The most famous of these stories involves young Abraham and his father Terach's idol shop.

According to the midrash, Terach was a manufacturer and seller of idols in the city of Ur. One day, he left young Abraham in charge of the shop. A woman came in with a bowl of flour as an offering to the idols. Abraham took a stick, smashed all the idols except the largest one, and placed the stick in the hand of the remaining idol.

When Terach returned and saw the destruction, he demanded to know what happened. Abraham replied innocently: "A woman brought flour as an offering. Each idol claimed it should be theirs, and they started fighting. Then the biggest one took the stick and destroyed all the others."

Terach shouted, "What are you talking about? These idols have no power! They can't move or think or act!"

Abraham replied calmly: "Let your ears hear what your mouth is saying."

This brilliant story captures the essence of Abraham's breakthrough: **he reasoned his way to monotheism**. He looked at the world and recognized that idols—statues made by human hands—couldn't possibly be divine. They couldn't see, hear, speak, or act. How could lifeless objects created by humans be gods worthy of worship?

## Abraham's Philosophical Discovery

Chinese readers will recognize a parallel here to Confucius's teaching about "rectification of names" (正名, *zhèng míng*)—the idea that things should be called what they truly are, and that clear thinking depends on accurate language. Abraham's idol-smashing story is fundamentally about honest intellectual inquiry: refusing to pretend that something is what it clearly is not.

But Abraham went further. He didn't just reject false gods—he discovered the true God. The Talmud (Tractate Bava Batra 15b) describes Abraham as the one who "recognized his Creator" through observation of the natural world. He looked at the heavens, the earth, the cycles of day and night, the seasons, the laws of nature—and concluded that there must be one intelligent Designer behind it all.

This is what the Jewish philosophical tradition would later call "the argument from design." Just as a beautiful palace implies a builder, the intricate order of the universe implies a Creator. Abraham reasoned that random chance couldn't produce the complexity and regularity he observed. There must be an Author of existence itself.

In this sense, Abraham was the world's first monotheistic philosopher—someone who arrived at belief in one God not through revelation alone, but through rational investigation of the world around him.

## The Ten Tests of Abraham

The Torah doesn't present Abraham as a perfect saint who believed without struggle. Instead, Jewish tradition teaches that Abraham underwent ten major tests (*nisyonot*, נסיונות) throughout his life—challenges that refined his faith and proved his commitment to God's covenant.

These tests include:

1. **Being thrown into the fiery furnace** by King Nimrod for refusing to worship idols (midrashic tradition)
2. **Leaving his homeland** at age 75 to travel to an unknown land (Genesis 12:1)
3. **Famine in Canaan** immediately after arriving in the Promised Land (Genesis 12:10)
4. **Sarah taken by Pharaoh** while Abraham is powerless to protect her (Genesis 12:14-20)
5. **War with the four kings** to rescue his nephew Lot (Genesis 14)
6. **The covenant between the pieces** and God's prophecy of exile (Genesis 15)
7. **Taking Hagar as a concubine** at Sarah's request when she was unable to conceive (Genesis 16)
8. **Circumcision** at age 99, establishing the eternal sign of the covenant (Genesis 17)
9. **Sarah taken again**, this time by King Avimelech (Genesis 20)
10. **The binding of Isaac (Akeidah)**, the ultimate test of faith (Genesis 22)

Each test challenged Abraham in a different way—his physical safety, his family relationships, his economic security, his trust in God's promises. But through each trial, Abraham remained faithful. He didn't understand everything. He sometimes struggled and questioned. But he continued to trust and obey.

The final test—the Akeidah, where God commands Abraham to sacrifice his beloved son Isaac—is discussed in detail in Article #3 of this series. It remains one of the most profound and difficult stories in all of religious literature.

## The Three Pillars: Hesed, Emunah, and Brit

What specifically did Abraham teach the world? Jewish tradition identifies three foundational principles that Abraham embodied and passed down:

### 1. Hesed (חסד): Loving-Kindness and Hospitality

Abraham's tent was famous for being open on all four sides so that travelers approaching from any direction could see they were welcome. The Torah tells us that even when Abraham was recovering from his circumcision at age 99—in great pain on a hot day—he sat at his tent entrance looking for guests to invite in (Genesis 18:1).

When three strangers approached, Abraham ran to greet them, bowed down before them, and personally prepared an elaborate meal. He didn't wait for them to ask. He didn't check their credentials. He saw human beings in need of rest and refreshment, and he acted with generosity and urgency.

This practice of *hachnasat orchim* (welcoming guests) became a cornerstone of Jewish life. The Talmud states that "welcoming guests is greater than receiving the Divine Presence" (Shabbat 127a)—meaning that caring for human beings in need takes precedence even over spiritual devotions.

Chinese readers may recognize a parallel in Confucian *ren* (仁)—benevolence or humaneness—and the ideal of treating all people with warmth and respect, especially strangers and those vulnerable.

### 2. Emunah (אמונה): Faith and Trust in God

Abraham's defining characteristic was his faith. When God told him to leave everything familiar and travel to an unknown destination, he went. When God promised that his descendants would be as numerous as the stars despite Abraham and Sarah being elderly and childless, he believed.

The Torah specifically praises this quality: *"And he believed in the LORD, and He counted it to him as righteousness"* (Genesis 15:6). This verse became foundational to the Jewish understanding of faith—not as blind acceptance, but as trust in God's promises even when circumstances seem impossible.

Abraham's faith wasn't passive. It was active, dynamic, and involved ongoing conversation with God. Abraham argued with God over the fate of Sodom and Gomorrah, challenging: *"Will the Judge of all the earth not do justice?"* (Genesis 18:25). Jewish tradition celebrates this as the model of faith—engaging with God honestly, even in disagreement, rather than silent submission.

### 3. Brit (ברית): Covenant

Abraham established the concept of *brit*—covenant—a binding mutual relationship between God and the Jewish people. This wasn't a contract based on negotiation or equal exchange. It was a sacred bond initiated by God and accepted by Abraham, with commitments on both sides.

God promised:
- To make Abraham's descendants into a great nation
- To give them the Land of Israel
- To bless those who bless them
- To make them a blessing to all families of the earth

Abraham's responsibilities:
- To "walk before God and be wholehearted" (Genesis 17:1)
- To keep God's commandments
- To teach his children the ways of justice and righteousness (Genesis 18:19)
- To perform circumcision as an eternal sign of the covenant

This covenantal relationship became the foundation of Jewish identity. Jews don't just believe in God—they understand themselves to be in a relationship with God, with mutual obligations and promises that span generations.

## Abraham and Sarah: Partners in the Mission

It's crucial to note that Abraham's mission wasn't a solo endeavor. His wife Sarah (שָׂרָה, *Sarah*) was equally important to the founding of the Jewish people. The Torah calls her a *neviah* (prophetess), and the rabbis teach that in some matters, her prophecy exceeded Abraham's (Talmud, Megillah 14a).

Sarah was the one who insisted that Isaac, not Ishmael, would be the heir of the covenant—a decision that God confirmed and instructed Abraham to obey (Genesis 21:12). She was an active partner in welcoming guests and spreading knowledge of the one God. Her hospitality was so legendary that clouds of glory hovered over her tent, her Shabbat candles remained lit all week, and her dough was blessed with abundance (midrashic tradition).

When Sarah died, the Torah dedicates an entire chapter to Abraham's efforts to secure a proper burial place for her—the Cave of Machpelah in Hebron, which remains a holy site to this day. Abraham's grief and his insistence on purchasing (not accepting as a gift) the burial plot demonstrate the deep respect and partnership that defined their relationship.

## The Legacy: What Abraham Means for Jews Today

Abraham is called *Avraham Avinu* (אברהם אבינו)—"Abraham our Father"—in Jewish prayer and tradition. Every Jewish person, whether born Jewish or converted, is considered a spiritual descendant of Abraham and Sarah.

For converts to Judaism, this isn't just metaphorical. Upon conversion, a person takes on the Hebrew name "ben Avraham Avinu v'Sarah Imeinu" (son of Abraham our father and Sarah our mother) or "bat Avraham v'Sarah" (daughter of Abraham and Sarah). This signifies that converts are following in Abraham's footsteps—leaving behind the beliefs and practices of their birth culture to embrace the covenant of the one God.

Abraham's legacy includes:

- **Monotheism**: The foundational belief in one God who is the Creator of all
- **Ethical monotheism**: The idea that God cares about justice, compassion, and human behavior
- **Covenant**: The special relationship between God and the Jewish people
- **Welcoming the stranger**: Radical hospitality as a core religious value
- **Questioning faith**: The tradition of arguing with God and wrestling with difficult questions
- **Transmission to the next generation**: Teaching children the ways of righteousness (Genesis 18:19)

## Practical Application: What Can We Learn from Abraham Today?

For someone in modern China exploring Judaism, Abraham's story offers several timeless lessons:

### 1. Question Everything—Including What Everyone Else Believes

Abraham lived in a world where idol worship was universal. Everyone—his family, his neighbors, his entire civilization—believed in the gods. But Abraham looked at the evidence and concluded they were wrong.

This teaches that truth-seeking sometimes requires intellectual courage and independence. Just because "everyone believes it" doesn't make something true. Abraham teaches us to think for ourselves, follow the evidence where it leads, and be willing to stand alone if necessary.

### 2. Seeking Truth May Require Leaving Comfort Behind

Abraham had to leave his homeland, his family, and everything familiar to pursue his newfound understanding of God. This was an enormous sacrifice—especially in a culture where family and homeland meant everything.

Today, someone exploring Judaism in China may face similar choices: leaving behind familiar customs, potentially disappointing family expectations, standing out as different in a homogeneous society. Abraham's example shows that authentic spiritual seeking sometimes requires courage and sacrifice.

### 3. Faith is Compatible with Questions

Abraham didn't just blindly obey. He argued with God. He asked questions. He expressed doubts. And far from being punished, his honest engagement with God was praised and recorded in the Torah for all generations.

This is the Jewish model of faith: *emunah* doesn't mean "shut up and believe." It means trust combined with honest wrestling, dialogue, and even protest when justice seems absent.

### 4. Be Radically Hospitable

Abraham's tent with four open sides is a powerful image: be open to people from all directions, all backgrounds. Welcome the stranger. Feed the hungry. Offer rest to the weary.

In a world that increasingly builds walls and closes doors, Abraham's hospitality is a countercultural value worth recovering.

### 5. The Journey is Long; Think in Generations

Abraham received many promises from God, but most of them wouldn't be fulfilled in his lifetime. He died without seeing his descendants become a great nation. He owned only one small plot of land in Canaan—a burial cave.

But Abraham lived with a long-term perspective. He planted seeds for the future. He taught his children. He established practices that would endure.

For someone beginning a Jewish journey today, this is important to remember: you're not just learning for yourself. You're potentially starting something that will affect your children, grandchildren, and generations to come.

## Historical Context: Abraham's World

When did Abraham live, and what was the world like then?

According to traditional Jewish chronology, Abraham was born in the year 1948 from Creation, which corresponds to approximately 1812 BCE. This places him firmly in the Middle Bronze Age, during the time of:

- **Shang Dynasty in China** (商朝, ~1600-1046 BCE)
- **Middle Kingdom Egypt** (12th-13th Dynasties, ~1991-1650 BCE)
- **Old Babylonian Period** in Mesopotamia (~1894-1595 BCE)

This was a world of city-states, temple complexes, and established polytheistic religions. Each city had its patron deity, and the gods were understood as powerful but limited beings who controlled specific natural forces: storm gods, fertility goddesses, sun gods, river gods.

The idea that there was one supreme God who created everything, who had no physical form, who couldn't be manipulated by sacrifices, and who cared about human morality—this was genuinely revolutionary. It's hard to overstate how radical Abraham's monotheism was in this context.

Archaeological evidence from this period includes:

- Cylinder seals and tablets mentioning the city of Ur (Abraham's birthplace)
- Temple ziggurats (stepped pyramids) like the one at Ur, dedicated to the moon god Nanna
- Legal codes (like the Code of Hammurabi, ~1750 BCE) that give us a window into the society and values of the time
- Evidence of extensive trade networks connecting Mesopotamia, Egypt, and the Levant

Abraham's journey from Ur to Haran to Canaan follows known trade routes of the time. The places and customs mentioned in Genesis align remarkably well with what we know about the period from archaeology.

## Abraham in Jewish Prayer and Ritual

Abraham's influence permeates Jewish life:

- **Daily prayers begin**: "Blessed are You, LORD our God and God of our fathers, God of Abraham, God of Isaac, and God of Jacob..."
- **Friday night Kiddush**: "And Abraham was old, advanced in days, and the LORD had blessed Abraham with everything" (Genesis 24:1)
- **Circumcision ceremony**: Every Jewish boy is welcomed into "the covenant of Abraham our father"
- **High Holiday liturgy**: References to Abraham's merit and the binding of Isaac
- **Passover Seder**: The entire story of the Exodus is understood as the fulfillment of God's promises to Abraham

Abraham isn't just a historical figure to be studied—he's a living presence in Jewish consciousness, invoked daily, remembered weekly, and celebrated as the spiritual forefather whose merit continues to benefit his descendants.

## Conclusion: Abraham's Universal Message

Abraham's story is ultimately universal. While he's the father of the Jewish people specifically, his message applies to all humanity:

- There is one God, Creator of all
- All human beings are created in God's image and possess inherent dignity
- We are called to live with justice and compassion
- Truth is worth seeking, even when it requires sacrifice
- Faith involves relationship, dialogue, and sometimes struggle
- Hospitality to strangers is a sacred duty
- Our actions echo across generations

For someone in modern China encountering Abraham's story for the first time, the invitation is the same as it was four thousand years ago: to question inherited assumptions, to seek ultimate truth, to stand for justice, to welcome the stranger, and to enter into a covenantal relationship with the Source of all being.

Abraham wasn't perfect. He made mistakes. He struggled. He had moments of doubt. But he kept moving forward, kept trusting, kept seeking. And in doing so, he became the father of faith for billions of people across three great religious traditions.

The Talmud teaches that Abraham "recognized his Creator" through independent investigation. This is the Jewish invitation to all seekers: Look at the world. Reason. Question. Seek. And when you find truth, have the courage to follow it—even if it means leaving everything familiar behind.

That's what Abraham did. And four millennia later, his legacy endures.

## Key Takeaways

- Abraham was the first monotheist, discovering through reason and observation that there must be one Creator
- He established the core Jewish values of faith (*emunah*), loving-kindness (*hesed*), and covenant (*brit*)
- Abraham's hospitality—his tent open on all four sides—became the model for welcoming strangers
- His story demonstrates that authentic faith involves questioning, dialogue, and even arguing with God
- Abraham and Sarah together founded the Jewish people, as equal partners in the mission
- Converts to Judaism today are considered spiritual descendants of Abraham and Sarah
- Abraham's courage to leave behind his entire world for truth continues to inspire seekers across all cultures
