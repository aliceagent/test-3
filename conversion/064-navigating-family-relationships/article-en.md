## Introduction

When you decide to become Jewish, you're not just adopting new beliefs or practices — you're joining a new family. The Jewish people become your people, their history becomes your history, and you take on a new spiritual identity. But what happens to your relationship with your biological family, the parents who raised you and loved you? This is one of the most emotionally complex questions potential converts face, and Jewish tradition has been wrestling with it for thousands of years.

The answer, like many things in Judaism, is nuanced. Yes, you are becoming someone new. Yes, you are leaving behind aspects of your former life. But no, you don't sever ties with your family — and in fact, Jewish law requires you to continue honoring them. This article will guide you through this delicate balance, drawing on ancient wisdom and practical guidance for modern life.

## The "Newborn Child" Paradox

The Talmud teaches: "One who converts is like a newborn child" (*Yevamot* 22a). This beautiful metaphor captures the transformative power of conversion. When you emerge from the mikveh (ritual bath), you are spiritually reborn into the Jewish people. Your sins are forgiven, your slate wiped clean, and you become a child of Abraham and Sarah, the first Jews who chose to follow God.

Historically — during what corresponds to the period from China's Han Dynasty through the early medieval period (around 200-500 CE), when this Talmudic principle was formulated — some rabbis took this metaphor literally. They argued that conversion severs all prior family ties. If you're "newborn," the reasoning went, then your biological parents are no longer your parents in any legal sense. Some even ruled that a convert who had children before converting must have more children afterward to fulfill the commandment to "be fruitful and multiply," since their prior children were from their "previous life."

This sounds harsh to modern ears — and it was debated even in ancient times. But there's profound truth in the metaphor too. Conversion is not a minor lifestyle change; it's a complete transformation of identity. And for that transformation to be real and complete, you can't hold back. You must be willing to release your old self and embrace the new.

## The Physical Reality: You Still Have Parents

Yet here's the tension: While the spiritual transformation is total, the physical facts remain unchanged. You still have biological parents. They gave you life, raised you, fed you, taught you to walk and talk. The person you are today — including the person capable of choosing Judaism — exists because of them.

Rabbi Joseph Caro, writing in the 16th century (during what corresponds to China's Ming Dynasty, 明朝, 1368-1644 CE), addressed this directly in his monumental legal code, the *Shulchan Aruch*. Someone asked him: "Must a convert honor their non-Jewish parents or not?"

Caro's answer was brilliant. He ruled that yes, Jews-by-choice are **forbidden from mistreating their non-Jewish parents**. Notice his careful wording. He doesn't say converts are "commanded to honor" their parents in exactly the same way as born Jews (that might undermine their status as a "newborn" Jew). Instead, he says they may not mistreat them.

Why? Because it makes no sense for conversion to allow someone to treat their parents *worse* than before they became Jewish. Being Jewish should come with *higher* expectations for treating others with respect and honor, not lower ones. Judaism elevates you; it doesn't give you permission to behave badly.

This ruling has become the standard halachic (Jewish legal) position: **Converts must continue to honor and respect their biological parents**, even though those parents are not Jewish.

## Ruth: The Model Convert

The most famous convert in Jewish history is Ruth, the great-grandmother of King David. Her story, told in the Book of Ruth (read on the holiday of Shavuot), takes place approximately three thousand years ago — during what corresponds to China's Western Zhou Dynasty (西周, c. 1046-771 BCE), when ancient Chinese bronze inscriptions were being created and the *I Ching* was taking shape.

Ruth was a Moabite princess — not just a non-Jew, but from a nation historically hostile to Israel. She married an Israelite man who had moved to Moab during a famine. When her husband and father-in-law died, leaving her widowed, her Jewish mother-in-law Naomi prepared to return to Israel and urged Ruth and her sister-in-law to stay in Moab with their own families.

Ruth's sister-in-law Orpah reluctantly agreed and stayed behind. But Ruth refused. In one of the most beautiful passages in all of Scripture, she declared:

> *"Wherever you go, I will go; wherever you lodge, I will lodge. Your people shall be my people, and your God shall be my God. Where you die, I will die, and there I will be buried. May the Lord do thus to me and more if anything but death parts me from you."* (Ruth 1:16-17)

This wasn't just a conversion; it was a total pledge of loyalty. Ruth chose to leave behind her royal status, her homeland, her birth family, and her gods to follow Naomi into an uncertain future in a foreign land.

Notice what she says: "Your people shall be my people." She's joining the Jewish family. But she's also saying this *to Naomi* — maintaining her relationship with her Jewish mother-in-law. Ruth didn't cut ties with everyone she knew; she deepened her bond with the family she'd married into, even though her husband was gone.

Ruth went on to marry Boaz, a righteous Israelite, and become the great-grandmother of King David — which means she's also an ancestor of the future Messiah. The Torah is teaching us that converts aren't second-class Jews. They can become the greatest of the Jewish people. And part of what made Ruth great was her loyalty to family.

## Practical Guidance: Walking the Tightrope

So how do you actually live this in practice? Here are some guidelines:

### 1. **Continue to Honor Your Parents**

- Visit them regularly if possible
- Call or video chat to stay in touch
- Remember their birthdays and important occasions
- Help them when they need assistance
- Speak to them respectfully
- Don't mock their beliefs or criticize their way of life

This is true even if they don't understand your conversion, even if they're hurt by it, and even if they actively oppose it.

### 2. **Set Boundaries When Necessary**

Honoring your parents doesn't mean obeying them in everything, especially when it conflicts with Jewish law. For example:

- You don't have to eat non-kosher food at their home to make them happy
- You don't have to participate in religious ceremonies of another faith
- You don't have to skip Shabbat observance because they want you to visit on Saturday

You can honor them while still maintaining your Jewish practice. Explain your choices gently and respectfully. Offer alternatives (e.g., "I can't come for Saturday lunch, but I'd love to come Friday evening or Sunday").

### 3. **Be Patient and Understanding**

Your conversion may be difficult for your parents to accept. They may:

- Feel you're rejecting them or their values
- Worry about your soul or afterlife
- Fear losing you to a community they don't understand
- Grieve the loss of the future they imagined for you (certain marriage, grandchildren raised a certain way, etc.)
- Be confused or hurt that you've chosen a path so different from theirs

These feelings are valid, even if they're based on misunderstandings. Give them time. Answer their questions patiently. Show them through your actions that you still love them and that becoming Jewish has made you a better person, not alienated you from them.

### 4. **Find the Right Words**

How you talk about your conversion matters. Consider these approaches:

❌ **Avoid:** "I've found the truth, and what you believe is false."  
✅ **Better:** "I've found a spiritual home that feels right for me."

❌ **Avoid:** "I'm a completely different person now."  
✅ **Better:** "I'm still me, but I'm growing and changing in ways that feel meaningful."

❌ **Avoid:** "Judaism forbids me from spending time with you."  
✅ **Better:** "I'd love to spend time together; let me share how we can do that while respecting my new observances."

### 5. **Share Joy, Not Just Rules**

Invite your parents into your Jewish life in ways that feel comfortable:

- Teach them about Shabbat and invite them for a Friday night dinner (prepare everything in advance so you're not cooking on Shabbat)
- Explain the meaning of Jewish holidays you're celebrating
- Show them your mezuzah, tallit, or other ritual objects and what they mean to you
- Share stories from your Torah study or synagogue community
- Let them see how Judaism brings you peace, purpose, and community

The more they see Judaism enriching your life rather than separating you from them, the easier it may become.

### 6. **Consult Your Rabbi**

Every family is different, and some situations are particularly complex. If your parents:

- Are actively hostile to your conversion
- Demand you violate Jewish law
- Cause you emotional or spiritual harm
- Refuse to accept any boundaries

...then you need personalized guidance. Work closely with your converting rabbi to find the right balance between honoring your parents and protecting your Jewish growth.

## The Chinese Cultural Connection

If you come from a Chinese background, you may already deeply understand the importance of family ties. The Confucian principle of filial piety (孝, *xiào*) — honoring and caring for one's parents — is central to Chinese culture and has been for over two thousand years.

In this way, Chinese tradition and Jewish tradition are remarkably aligned. Both see the parent-child relationship as sacred. Both expect children to respect their parents throughout life, not just in childhood. Both teach that honoring parents is a religious duty, not just a nice thing to do.

The *Analects* of Confucius teach: "While [your parents] are alive, serve them according to ritual propriety. When they die, bury them according to ritual propriety." Similarly, Jewish law is filled with detailed guidance about how to honor parents — what to say, what to do, even how to stand in their presence.

Here's where Judaism adds a unique layer: Even after a spiritual rebirth, even after becoming "like a newborn child," the biological reality and moral obligation remain. You honor your parents not because they are Jewish, but because they are *your parents*. Honor transcends religious boundaries.

This may actually make conversion easier for someone from a Chinese background to explain: "I'm not abandoning the family values you taught me. I'm deepening them. Judaism teaches me to honor you even more."

## The Winding Path

Rabbi Paula Rose, writing for *My Jewish Learning*, describes the convert's experience beautifully:

> "Jewish tradition offers us a path for honoring all of our family ties. It is not a simple path, but a winding trail that requires a bit of wandering and stumbling. Sometimes the convert may feel like — and be seen as — a full member of the Jewish family. Other times they may question whether they really belong. Sometimes they will feel lovingly connected to their families of origin; sometimes they will feel like newborn children, freed from prior family ties. Jewish tradition has room for all those twists and turns."

This is the reality: You will sometimes feel fully Jewish, embraced by your new community. Other times you may feel like an outsider. You will sometimes feel deep love for your birth family and gratitude for all they've given you. Other times you may feel distant from them, as if you're living in different worlds.

**All of these feelings are normal.** They're not a sign that you've done something wrong or that your conversion is incomplete. They're part of the journey.

## Conclusion: Two Families, One Heart

When you become Jewish, you don't lose a family — you gain one. You become a child of Abraham and Sarah. You join the Jewish people across time and space. You inherit the stories, the holidays, the Torah, and the mission.

But you also remain a child of your biological parents. You carry their love, their teachings, and the life they gave you. Judaism doesn't ask you to erase that. It asks you to honor it while stepping into your new identity.

This is the delicate dance of the convert: honoring the past while embracing the future, respecting where you came from while choosing where you're going.

Walk this path with patience, compassion, and honesty. Consult with wise rabbis and mentors. Give your family time to adjust. Give yourself time to grow.

And remember: Ruth the convert became the great-grandmother of King David and an ancestor of the Messiah. She didn't do this by cutting off her past, but by choosing her future while bringing the best of who she was into the Jewish people.

You can do the same.

## Key Takeaways

- Converts are "like a newborn child" spiritually, but biological family ties remain
- Jewish law requires converts to honor and respect their non-Jewish parents
- You can maintain your Jewish observance while still showing love to your family
- Ruth the convert is the model: complete commitment to Judaism while honoring family loyalty
- Patience, boundaries, and open communication are key to navigating this transition
- Chinese filial piety (孝) and Jewish honor for parents are remarkably aligned
- Consult your rabbi for guidance on specific family situations
