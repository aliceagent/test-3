# Onkelos, Aquila, and Other Famous Converts Who Shaped Judaism

When you begin your journey toward conversion, you join a remarkable lineage of individuals who came from outside the Jewish people yet became some of its most influential voices. From the translator who made Torah accessible to millions, to the scholar who challenged the sages, to a medieval poet whose words still echo in synagogues today—converts have shaped Judaism in profound and lasting ways.

Let's meet some of these extraordinary individuals whose stories remind us that Judaism has always welcomed those who choose to join the covenant.

## Onkelos: The Voice That Made Torah Speak Aramaic

In the second century CE, a Roman nobleman named Onkelos made a decision that would echo through Jewish history for two thousand years. Born into a patrician family—some sources say he was the nephew of the Roman Emperor Hadrian—Onkelos converted to Judaism and dedicated his life to a monumental project: translating the Torah into Aramaic, the everyday language of Jews throughout the Middle East.

Why was this so important? Imagine living in a time when Hebrew was becoming a sacred language used primarily in synagogues, while Aramaic was the language people spoke at home, in the marketplace, and with their families. Onkelos's translation, known as *Targum Onkelos*, became the bridge that allowed ordinary Jews to understand their most sacred text.

But Onkelos didn't just translate word-for-word. He was a brilliant scholar who studied under Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua, two of the greatest sages of his generation. His translation carefully interprets difficult passages, clarifies ambiguous phrases, and ensures that the text could not be misunderstood to suggest that God has a physical form. The Talmud tells us that Onkelos's translation was so authoritative that a heavenly voice (*bat kol*) confirmed its accuracy.

To this day, many Orthodox Jews read Targum Onkelos alongside the Hebrew text during their weekly Torah study. When you convert, you're joining a tradition that was made accessible partly through the work of a convert.

## Aquila: The Convert Who Challenged His Teachers

Around the same time as Onkelos, another convert named Aquila (also called Akylas) took a different path. Like Onkelos, Aquila was a Roman convert, and some scholars believe they may have even been the same person, though most view them as distinct individuals.

Aquila created a Greek translation of the Torah so literal that it sometimes sacrificed readability for precision. His translation was aimed at Jews in the Greek-speaking world and became so influential that it was used to refute Christian interpretations of Scripture. The Talmud records debates between Aquila and the rabbis, showing that this convert wasn't shy about challenging even his teachers when he thought truth demanded it.

There's a powerful lesson here: converts bring fresh perspectives. They ask questions that those born Jewish might never think to ask. They see connections that others miss. Judaism has always valued this—the Talmud itself is built on vigorous debate and multiple viewpoints.

## Shemaya and Avtalyon: From Converts' Grandchildren to Leaders of Israel

Going back even further, to the first century BCE, we encounter two of the most beloved leaders in Jewish history: Shemaya and Avtalyon. The Talmud tells us they were descendants of Sennacherib, the Assyrian king who besieged Jerusalem. Their ancestors had converted, and they rose to become the heads of the Sanhedrin—the supreme Jewish court.

The Talmud records that when the High Priest would leave the Temple after Yom Kippur, thousands would accompany him home—but they would leave him to follow Shemaya and Avtalyon instead. Why? Because these scholars embodied the true spirit of Torah. The rabbis said of them, "Let Shemaya and Avtalyon enter, who are descendants of converts but whose deeds resemble those of Aaron, the lover of peace and pursuer of peace."

Think about that for a moment. The descendants of the king who tried to destroy Jerusalem became the spiritual leaders who guided the Jewish people. This is the transformative power of conversion—not just changing who you are, but changing the direction of history itself.

## Rabbi Akiva's Teacher: The Convert Whose Question Changed Everything

Rabbi Akiva, one of the greatest sages in Jewish history, had a teacher named Nahum Ish Gamzu. While Nahum himself wasn't a convert, he learned from someone who was: the convert Yochanan ben Torta, who, according to some traditions, came from a family of converts.

But perhaps even more intriguing is the wife of Rabbi Akiva—Rachel—who encouraged and supported him when he was an ignorant shepherd with no education. Some commentaries suggest she may have had connections to convert families, though this is debated. What's clear is that Rabbi Akiva's own journey from complete ignorance to becoming the foundation of rabbinic Judaism parallels the conversion journey: choosing to embrace a new identity through study, commitment, and transformation.

## Ruth: The Original Convert Who Became King David's Grandmother

We can't discuss famous converts without mentioning Ruth, whose story has its own book in the Bible. Ruth was a Moabite princess who chose to follow her mother-in-law Naomi back to Israel after being widowed, speaking the words that have become the ultimate statement of conversion: "Your people shall be my people, and your God shall be my God."

Ruth's declaration wasn't just poetic—it was revolutionary. Moabites were traditional enemies of Israel. Yet Ruth's loyalty, kindness, and faith made her not only accepted but celebrated. She married Boaz, a leader of the Jewish people, and became the great-grandmother of King David—which means she's an ancestor of the Messiah.

During the holiday of Shavuot, when we celebrate receiving the Torah, we read the Book of Ruth. Why? Because conversion and receiving Torah are both about choosing to accept a covenant. You weren't born into it, but you chose it—and that choice is sacred.

## Abraham: The First Convert

In a profound sense, Abraham himself was the first convert. The Talmud calls him *Avraham Avinu*—Abraham our Father—and *Av Hamon Goyim*—the father of many nations. He was born into an idolatrous society, rejected those beliefs, discovered God on his own, and then made the choice to follow God's command to leave everything behind.

The Midrash tells us that Abraham stood on one side, and the entire world stood on the other side—*Ever Ha'Nahar*, the one from the other side of the river. Every convert joins Abraham in that spiritual space, standing apart from their former world to embrace something eternal.

## Warqa ibn Nawfal: The Pre-Islamic Arabian Convert

Jewish history isn't limited to Israel and Europe. In the Arabian Peninsula before the rise of Islam, there was a significant Jewish presence, and many Arab tribes converted to Judaism. One fascinating figure is Warqa ibn Nawfal, a relative of Muhammad's first wife Khadijah. Some scholars believe he converted to Judaism before later becoming a Christian.

Arabia's Jewish communities, including the famous Jewish tribes of Medina like the Banu Qurayza and Banu Nadir, included many descendants of converts. These communities preserved Judaism in the heart of the Arabian Peninsula for centuries, showing that the Jewish story has always been global.

## Obadiah the Proselyte: The Medieval Composer

In the 12th century, a Norman knight named Johannes converted to Judaism, taking the name Obadiah. He became a scholar and composer, and remarkably, some of his musical notations survive to this day—the oldest known written Jewish music.

Obadiah's conversion was dangerous. He lived in an age when converting to Judaism could mean death. He wrote about his struggles, his doubts, and his ultimate conviction. Maimonides himself wrote a famous letter to Obadiah, assuring him that converts are full Jews in every sense and may say "God of our fathers" in prayers—because Abraham is now his father too.

Maimonides wrote: "You should not consider your origin as inferior. While we descend from Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, you descend from the One who created the world."

## What These Stories Teach Us

These converts—and countless others throughout history—teach us several profound lessons:

**1. Converts Often Become Teachers**: Onkelos, Aquila, Shemaya, and Avtalyon didn't just learn Judaism—they taught it, interpreted it, and transmitted it to future generations. Your fresh perspective is valuable.

**2. Judaism Welcomes the Sincere Seeker**: From Ruth to Obadiah, Judaism has consistently welcomed those who genuinely seek to join the covenant, even when it was dangerous or countercultural to do so.

**3. Background Doesn't Determine Destiny**: Whether you come from royalty like Onkelos or from enemy nations like Ruth, your past doesn't define your Jewish future. What matters is your choice and commitment.

**4. Converts Shape Judaism**: These weren't people who passively joined an existing tradition. They actively shaped it, challenged it, enriched it, and helped define it for generations to come.

**5. You're Part of an Ancient Tradition**: When you convert, you're not inventing something new. You're joining a conversation that started with Abraham and continued through Ruth, Onkelos, Aquila, and countless others who heard the call and answered.

## The Chinese Parallel: *Ru Xiang Sui Su* (入乡随俗)

There's a beautiful parallel between the Jewish view of conversion and the Chinese concept of *ru xiang sui su*—"when entering a village, follow its customs." But Judaism takes this a step further. It's not just about outward conformity but about inner transformation.

In Chinese history, there have been various examples of people adopting new cultural identities—ethnic minorities integrating into Han Chinese culture, traders settling in new regions and adopting local customs. Similarly, Jews who convert don't just adopt external practices; they embrace a new covenant, a new family, and a new destiny.

The Kaifeng Jews of China, whose community dates back to the Song Dynasty (960-1279 CE), show how Judaism took root in Chinese soil. While most were descendants of Jewish merchants, the community certainly included Chinese converts over the centuries, blending Jewish tradition with Chinese cultural sensibilities.

## Your Place in This Story

When you stand before the *beit din* (rabbinical court) for your conversion, you'll be asked about your commitment to Torah and mitzvot. You'll immerse in the mikvah, emerging as a new creation. At that moment, you join the company of Onkelos, Ruth, Aquila, and Abraham himself.

Your children, if you have them, will be as Jewish as the descendants of those who stood at Sinai. Your contributions to Jewish thought, practice, and community will be as valid and valuable as any born Jew's. And perhaps—who knows?—your story will one day inspire another seeker, just as these stories inspire you now.

The sages teach that converts are beloved by God because they chose to seek Him. Those born Jewish inherited their connection; you *chose* yours. And there's something uniquely powerful about a choice made freely, with full knowledge and complete commitment.

Welcome to an ancient family that has always included those brave enough to step across the river, leave the familiar behind, and embrace an eternal covenant. You walk in the footsteps of giants.
