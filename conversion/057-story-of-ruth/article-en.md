## Introduction

Imagine leaving everything you've ever known — your country, your family, your gods — to follow an elderly mother-in-law into poverty and uncertainty. No promise of a better life. No guarantee of acceptance. Just faith, loyalty, and love.

This is the story of Ruth.

Nearly three thousand years ago — during what corresponds to China's Western Zhou Dynasty (西周, ~1046-771 BCE), when the Book of Songs (詩經) was being compiled — a young Moabite widow made a choice that would echo through all of Jewish history. Her words, "Your people will be my people, and your God my God," became the eternal declaration of every convert to Judaism.

Ruth's story isn't just ancient history. It's the blueprint for conversion itself — a journey of choice, commitment, and transformation. And remarkably, this foreign woman who chose to become Jewish became the great-grandmother of King David, and thus an ancestor of the Messiah.

Let's explore why Ruth's story matters to every Jewish learner, especially those considering conversion themselves.

## The Story: From Moab to Bethlehem

### A Family in Exile

The Book of Ruth begins with a crisis. During a time of famine in the Land of Israel, a man named Elimelech takes his wife Naomi and their two sons and leaves Bethlehem for the neighboring land of Moab. This might seem like a practical decision, but the rabbis criticize Elimelech for abandoning his community in difficult times. He was a wealthy, influential man who should have stayed to help his people.

In Moab, tragedy strikes. Elimelech dies. His two sons marry Moabite women — Orpah and Ruth — but then, ten years later, both sons die as well. Naomi is left with nothing: no husband, no sons, no grandchildren. Just two foreign daughters-in-law in a land that isn't her own.

### The Journey Home

When Naomi hears that the famine in Israel has ended, she decides to return to Bethlehem. Her two daughters-in-law begin the journey with her, but Naomi urges them to turn back: "Go back, each of you, to your mother's house. May the Lord show kindness to you, as you have shown to the dead and to me."

Orpah, after much weeping, kisses Naomi goodbye and returns to her people. This is not presented as a moral failure — she acted reasonably and kindly. But Ruth refuses to leave.

And here, Ruth speaks the words that define conversion forever:

**"Do not urge me to leave you or to turn back from you. Where you go I will go, and where you stay I will stay. Your people will be my people and your God my God. Where you die I will die, and there I will be buried. May the Lord deal with me, be it ever so severely, if even death separates you and me."** (Ruth 1:16-17)

The rabbis see in this passage the three pillars of conversion:
1. **"Your people will be my people"** — joining the Jewish community
2. **"Your God my God"** — accepting the One God and His commandments
3. **"Where you die I will die, and there I will be buried"** — commitment for this life and the next

Ruth wasn't just following Naomi out of family loyalty. She was choosing Judaism, fully and irrevocably.

### Arriving in Bethlehem: A Stranger in a New Land

When Ruth and Naomi arrive in Bethlehem at the beginning of the barley harvest, they are destitute. The women of the town barely recognize Naomi, now aged and worn by suffering. And Ruth? She is "Ruth the Moabite" — a label that follows her throughout the book. She is the outsider, the convert, the stranger.

To survive, Ruth goes to glean in the fields — a form of charity permitted by Jewish law. The Torah commands landowners to leave the corners of their fields unharvested and not to gather every stalk, so that the poor can collect the leftovers. This is called *leket, shikcha, and pe'ah* (gleaning, forgotten sheaves, and field corners).

Ruth works from morning until evening, humble and industrious. And "by chance" — though nothing in this story is really chance — she ends up gleaning in a field belonging to Boaz, a wealthy relative of Naomi's late husband.

### Boaz: The Redeemer

Boaz notices Ruth immediately. He's heard about her kindness to Naomi, her decision to leave her homeland, and her willingness to "take refuge under the wings of the God of Israel" (Ruth 2:12). This phrase — "under the wings" (*tachat kanfei HaShechinah*) — becomes the classic expression for conversion in Jewish tradition.

Boaz goes out of his way to protect and provide for Ruth. He tells his workers to let her glean even among the standing sheaves (not just the corners), to deliberately leave extra grain for her, and to make sure no one harms or harasses her. He invites her to eat with his workers and ensures she has plenty to take home to Naomi.

Why is Boaz so generous? The Talmud explains that Boaz was a judge and Torah scholar. He recognized Ruth's spiritual greatness. Here was a woman who had given up everything — status, family, the gods of her people — to cling to the God of Israel. She embodied *chesed* (lovingkindness), humility, and faith.

### The Threshing Floor: A Proposal of Redemption

Guided by Naomi, Ruth approaches Boaz on the threshing floor after he has finished winnowing barley and lies down to sleep. In a deeply symbolic act, she uncovers his feet and lies down. When Boaz wakes at midnight, startled, Ruth says: "I am Ruth, your maidservant. Spread your garment over your maidservant, for you are a *go'el* (redeemer)."

This is a proposal, but not just a personal one. Under the laws of *yibum* (levirate marriage) and *ge'ulah* (redemption), a close relative of a deceased man has the responsibility to marry the widow and continue the family line. Boaz is such a relative, though there is one closer relative who has first rights.

Boaz praises Ruth: "You have shown more kindness now than before, because you have not gone after the younger men, whether rich or poor" (Ruth 3:10). Boaz himself was no longer young — the rabbis say he was eighty years old. Yet Ruth sought him out, not for physical attraction or material gain, but because he represented Torah values and spiritual nobility.

Boaz promises to redeem her, but first he must give the closer relative a chance to fulfill the obligation.

### The Gate: Public Redemption

The next morning, Boaz goes to the city gate — the place where legal matters are decided in ancient Israel — and assembles ten elders as witnesses. When the closer relative (unnamed in the text, called only "So-and-so") arrives, Boaz presents the matter: Naomi is selling a piece of land that belonged to Elimelech, and the buyer must also marry Ruth to preserve the family name.

The closer relative is willing to buy the land, but when he learns he must marry Ruth the Moabite and that any children will be credited to the deceased's name (not his own), he refuses. "I cannot redeem it, lest I ruin my own inheritance. You redeem it yourself" (Ruth 4:6).

Why does he refuse? The rabbis suggest he feared that marrying a Moabite would taint his lineage. The Torah forbids Moabites from entering "the assembly of the Lord" (Deuteronomy 23:4), and though Jewish law clarifies this applies only to Moabite *men* (not women like Ruth), this man was either ignorant of the law or simply didn't want the perceived stigma.

Boaz, by contrast, was a scholar and a man of principle. He knew that a female Moabite convert was fully welcome. He redeems Ruth publicly, marrying her before the assembled elders.

The people and elders bless the union: "May the Lord make the woman who is coming into your home like Rachel and Leah, who together built up the house of Israel" (Ruth 4:11). They compare Ruth to the founding mothers of the Jewish people. The convert is welcomed, embraced, and honored.

### The Birth of Obed: A Legacy for All Time

Ruth and Boaz marry, and God grants them a son named Obed. The women of Bethlehem say to Naomi: "Blessed be the Lord, who has not left you this day without a redeemer! May his name be famous in Israel. He will renew your life and sustain you in your old age. For your daughter-in-law, who loves you and who is better to you than seven sons, has borne him" (Ruth 4:14-15).

Better than seven sons! In a culture where sons were everything, Ruth — a foreign woman, a convert — is declared more valuable than the highest blessing of children.

And the text continues with a genealogy that changes everything:

**Obed was the father of Jesse, and Jesse was the father of David.**

Ruth the Moabite, the woman who chose to join the Jewish people, became the great-grandmother of King David — Israel's greatest king and the ancestor of the future Messiah. The Talmud teaches that the Messiah himself will descend from Ruth, a convert.

This is the ultimate statement: Converts are not second-class Jews. They are essential to the Jewish story. They can give birth to royalty. They can give birth to redemption itself.

## What Ruth's Story Teaches Every Convert

### 1. Conversion is About Commitment, Not Convenience

Ruth's choice was hard. She faced poverty, social stigma, and an uncertain future. She could have returned to Moab, remarried, and lived comfortably. But she chose Judaism anyway — not because it was easy, but because it was true.

The rabbis emphasize that a conversion must be *for the sake of Heaven* (*lishmah*), not for marriage, social status, or material gain. Ruth is the model: she converted for pure reasons, out of love for God and the Jewish people.

If you are considering conversion, ask yourself Ruth's question: Am I willing to say, "Your people will be my people, and your God my God" — even if it costs me everything?

### 2. Converts Are Fully Jewish — Period

When Ruth married Boaz, the people blessed her by comparing her to Rachel and Leah. Not as "almost like" the matriarchs — but as their equal. 

The Talmud goes further: "A convert who converts is like a newborn child" (Yevamot 22a). Spiritually, you are reborn into the Jewish people. Your past is left behind. Your future is as a full member of the nation of Israel.

There is no asterisk next to a convert's name. Ruth wasn't "Ruth the convert" in the sense of being lesser. She was Ruth, daughter of Abraham and Sarah (the spiritual father and mother of all converts), and great-grandmother of King David.

If anyone ever makes you feel like a second-class Jew because you converted, remember: Ruth the convert is in the direct lineage of the Messiah.

### 3. God Orchestrates the Journey

Look at the "coincidences" in Ruth's story:
- She "happens" to glean in Boaz's field
- Boaz "happens" to be a close relative and a righteous man
- The closer relative "happens" to refuse, clearing the way for Boaz
- Ruth and Boaz "happen" to have a son who becomes King David's grandfather

None of this is random. The rabbis teach that God is directing every step. When you choose to walk the path toward Judaism, you are not alone. The same Providence that guided Ruth will guide you.

### 4. Loyalty and Kindness Are at the Heart of Judaism

Ruth's defining trait is *chesed* — lovingkindness. She was kind to Naomi when she had nothing to gain. She was humble in the fields. She was modest and dignified in her approach to Boaz. She honored her deceased husband's memory by seeking to continue his family line.

Judaism is not just about rituals and laws. It's about becoming a person of *chesed*, like Ruth. That's why her story is read on Shavuot, the holiday when we celebrate receiving the Torah. The Torah's purpose is to make us into people of kindness, justice, and holiness.

### 5. The Journey is Hard, But the Destination is Worth It

Ruth's journey was long and painful. She lost her husband. She left her homeland. She labored in the fields as a stranger. But in the end, she became part of the greatest dynasty in Jewish history and a model for all time.

Conversion is not a quick or easy process. It takes time, study, commitment, and often sacrifice. There will be moments of doubt and difficulty. But the reward — becoming part of the eternal covenant between God and the Jewish people — is beyond measure.

## Cultural Connection: Ruth and the Confucian Virtue of Filial Piety

Ruth's devotion to Naomi resonates deeply with Chinese values, particularly the Confucian principle of *xiao* (孝, filial piety). Just as Confucius taught the importance of loyalty to one's parents and elders, Ruth demonstrated extraordinary loyalty to her mother-in-law, even when she had no legal or social obligation to do so.

In Chinese tradition, stories like that of Mulan (木蘭), who took her father's place in the army, or the Twenty-Four Filial Exemplars (二十四孝), celebrate similar acts of selfless devotion to family. Ruth's story fits naturally into this tradition — she is the ultimate example of a daughter-in-law's *xiao*, taken to the spiritual level by her simultaneous commitment to God.

The Talmud emphasizes that honoring parents (or in-laws) is one of the commandments for which "a person enjoys the fruit in this world and the principal remains in the World to Come" (Shabbat 127a). Ruth's *chesed* toward Naomi earned her a place in history and eternity.

## Practical Application: How to Embody Ruth's Qualities on Your Own Journey

Whether you are converting or already Jewish, Ruth's story offers a roadmap:

**1. Make a clear, verbal commitment**
Ruth didn't just passively follow Naomi. She declared her choice out loud: "Your people will be my people." If you're serious about conversion, find a rabbi and say it clearly: "I want to become Jewish." Don't wait until you "feel ready." Ruth wasn't ready for poverty and uncertainty, but she committed anyway.

**2. Join the community**
"Your people will be my people" means integration, not isolation. Attend synagogue. Celebrate Shabbat with other Jews. Join a learning group. Conversion is not a solo journey. You are joining a family.

**3. Study with humility**
Ruth was a "gleaner" — she didn't demand or expect. She worked quietly and learned from those around her. Approach your studies the same way. Ask questions. Listen to your rabbi and teachers. Don't rush.

**4. Expect challenges**
Ruth was called "Ruth the Moabite" throughout her story — a constant reminder that she was different. You may face questions, curiosity, or even skepticism from born Jews or from your own family. This is part of the process. Stay focused on your goal.

**5. Trust in Divine Providence**
The same God who guided Ruth to Boaz's field is guiding you. When doors open (finding a rabbi, a community, a learning partner), walk through them. When doors close, trust that another will open. You are not doing this alone.

## The Book of Ruth and the Holiday of Shavuot

The Book of Ruth is traditionally read on Shavuot, the holiday celebrating the giving of the Torah at Mount Sinai. Why this connection?

**1. Receiving the Torah is like conversion**
Just as Ruth accepted the God of Israel and His commandments, the entire Jewish people "converted" at Sinai. We stood at the mountain and said, "We will do and we will hear" (*na'aseh v'nishmah*) — accepting God's Torah even before fully understanding it. This is the same leap of faith Ruth made.

**2. Shavuot is the harvest festival**
The story of Ruth is set during the barley and wheat harvests, making it a natural reading for Shavuot, which celebrates the first fruits (*bikkurim*).

**3. King David's ancestry**
King David, Ruth's great-grandson, was born and died on Shavuot according to tradition. Reading Ruth's story honors his lineage.

**4. The centrality of *chesed***
Shavuot celebrates the Torah, and the Torah's essence is *chesed*. Ruth embodied *chesed*, making her story a perfect companion to the holiday.

## Key Takeaways

- **Ruth's declaration** — "Your people will be my people, and your God my God" — is the eternal model for conversion to Judaism
- **Converts are not outsiders** — Ruth became the great-grandmother of King David and an ancestor of the Messiah, proving that converts are fully and completely Jewish
- **Conversion requires commitment** — Ruth gave up everything to follow Naomi and embrace the God of Israel; true conversion is *lishmah*, for the sake of Heaven
- **Kindness (*chesed*) is Judaism's core** — Ruth's loyalty and humility made her worthy of becoming part of Jewish royalty
- **God guides those who choose Him** — The "coincidences" in Ruth's story show Divine Providence at work in every sincere conversion journey
- **The Book of Ruth is read on Shavuot** — Linking Ruth's acceptance of Judaism to the Jewish people's acceptance of the Torah at Mount Sinai
- **Cultural parallel: Ruth embodies *xiao* (filial piety)** — Her devotion to Naomi reflects the Confucian virtue of loyalty to elders, elevated to a spiritual level
