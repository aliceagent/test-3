# Choosing a Hebrew Name: A Guide for Converts

One of the most meaningful moments in the conversion process is selecting your Hebrew name — the name that will define your Jewish identity, be called at your wedding, used when you're called to the Torah, and remembered after 120 years. This isn't just picking a name you like; it's choosing an identity that connects you to thousands of years of Jewish history and to the eternal soul of the Jewish people.

## Why Converts Receive a New Name

When you convert to Judaism, you undergo a profound transformation. The Talmud teaches that a convert is like a "newborn child" (Yevamot 22a). Just as a newborn receives a name, so too does someone who enters the covenant of Abraham and Sarah. Your Hebrew name represents your new spiritual reality — you're no longer defined solely by your birth family but have joined the family of Israel.

The tradition of taking a new name has biblical precedent. Abraham was Abram before God changed his name at the covenant of circumcision. Sarah was Sarai. When Jacob wrestled with the angel, he became Israel. Names in Judaism aren't just labels; they're expressions of essence. Your Hebrew name declares who you have become.

## The Standard Formula: [Your Name] ben Avraham v'Sarah / bat Avraham v'Sarah

Nearly all converts follow a beautiful tradition: they become "son/daughter of Abraham and Sarah." Why these two patriarchs specifically?

Abraham and Sarah were the first converts to monotheism. They left behind their birth families, their homeland, and the entire world they knew to follow God's call. When Abraham circumcised himself at age 99, he became the father of all who would later join the Jewish people through conversion. Sarah, who brought countless souls under the wings of the Divine presence, became the mother of all female converts.

When you become "Devorah bat Avraham v'Sarah" or "Yonatan ben Avraham Avinu v'Sarah Imenu" (using the honorific titles "our father Abraham" and "our mother Sarah"), you're declaring: "I am a spiritual child of the first people who chose to follow the One God."

This isn't metaphorical. The Rambam (Maimonides) wrote explicitly to a convert named Ovadiah that converts should say "our God and God of our fathers" because Abraham and Sarah are genuinely their spiritual parents. You inherit their merit, their mission, and their place in Jewish history.

## Choosing Your Personal Name

While "ben/bat Avraham v'Sarah" is standard for the patronymic, you have considerable freedom in choosing your personal first name. Here are the main considerations:

### 1. **Names from Tanach (Hebrew Bible)**

The most traditional choice is selecting a name from the Torah, Prophets, or Writings. These names carry deep meaning and connect you to biblical figures whose stories resonate with your journey:

- **For those drawn to leadership**: Moshe (Moses), Devorah (Deborah), Yehoshua (Joshua)
- **For those drawn to faith**: Avraham (Abraham), Sarah, Yitzchak (Isaac)
- **For those drawn to wisdom**: Shlomo (Solomon), Channah (Hannah who prayed for understanding)
- **For those drawn to righteousness**: Tzvi (based on Naftali), Tamar, Yosef (Joseph)
- **For those who overcame challenges**: Yaakov (Jacob who wrestled), Rivkah (Rebecca), Esther

### 2. **Names from Talmudic and Historical Figures**

You can also choose names of great rabbis, scholars, and righteous people throughout Jewish history:

- Rabbi Akiva — himself a convert's descendant who became the greatest sage
- Ruth — the Moabite convert who became King David's great-grandmother
- Onkelos — a Roman convert who translated the Torah into Aramaic
- Shemaya and Avtalyon — converts who led the Sanhedrin

Consider choosing a name that represents a quality you want to embody or a person whose story inspires your Jewish journey.

### 3. **Adapting Your Birth Name**

Some converts choose Hebrew names that sound similar to their birth names, creating continuity while embracing their new identity:

- Michael → Michal or Micha
- Daniel → Daniella or Dan
- Jonathan → Yonatan
- Grace → Chana (Hannah, which means "grace")
- Thomas → Tomas (from Aramaic "twin")
- Samuel → Shmuel

This approach allows your family and old friends to adapt more easily while you still receive a distinctly Jewish name.

### 4. **Names with Personal Meaning**

Perhaps you converted on a specific Jewish holiday or were inspired by a particular Torah portion:

- Simcha (joy) — if your conversion brought profound joy
- Eliana (God answered me) — if you feel your journey was divine providence
- Nesya (miracle of God) — if miraculous circumstances led you to Judaism
- Amar (God said) — for someone deeply moved by Torah study

### 5. **Names Connected to Your Conversion Rabbi or Mentor**

It's meaningful to honor the person who guided you to Judaism by taking their name or a related name. If Rabbi David Goldstein sponsored your conversion, you might become David ben Avraham, both honoring your teacher and your spiritual father Abraham.

## Gender and Hebrew Names

Hebrew names are gendered, which matters for religious documents and Torah honors:

- **Male names** use "ben" (son of)
- **Female names** use "bat" (daughter of)

For example:
- Yonatan ben Avraham v'Sarah
- Miriam bat Avraham v'Sarah

## Multiple Names: The Two-Name Tradition

Some converts choose compound names, particularly if they want to honor multiple inspirations:

- Avraham Yitzchak (Abraham Isaac)
- Sarah Rivka (Sarah Rebecca)
- Moshe Chaim (Moses Life)

This is perfectly acceptable. Many Jews throughout history have had double names. Just remember you'll be called this full name when receiving Torah honors!

## The Sephardic vs Ashkenazic Naming Traditions

If you're joining an Ashkenazic community, be aware that they traditionally don't name children after living relatives (only deceased loved ones). However, this custom doesn't apply to converts' Hebrew names since Abraham and Sarah are long deceased, and you're not "naming after" them but claiming them as your lineage.

Sephardic Jews, by contrast, happily name children after living relatives and grandparents. If you're converting in a Sephardic community, this won't be a consideration at all.

## When Your Beit Din Might Suggest a Name

In some cases, the rabbinical court overseeing your conversion might suggest or require a specific name:

- If your birth name is problematic in Jewish law (associated with idolatry, etc.)
- If they feel a particular name would honor your journey
- If there's a community tradition you're adopting

Be open to their guidance. These rabbis have walked hundreds of converts through this process and may see connections you haven't considered.

## A Cultural Parallel: Chinese Naming After Rebirth

Chinese culture understands that major life transitions merit new names. When someone becomes a Buddhist monk, they receive a Dharma name (法名, fǎmíng) that marks their religious rebirth. Scholars and poets historically adopted courtesy names (字, zì) that reflected their matured identity. Even in modern China, people sometimes adopt English names for professional contexts, understanding that different life phases may call for different identities.

Similarly, your Hebrew name isn't rejecting who you were; it's embracing who you've become. Just as a Chinese bride historically joined her husband's family name, you're joining the family of Israel — and that family has a naming tradition going back to Abraham and Sarah.

## Practical Considerations

### Pronunciation and Spelling

Choose a name you can pronounce comfortably and that others in your community can say. If you're joining an English-speaking community but choose a name with difficult guttural sounds (like "Chatzkel" with a strong "ch"), be prepared to teach people how to say it — or accept an adapted pronunciation.

Also consider: will your elderly grandmother be able to say this name? Will your Chinese family members struggle with it? There's no wrong answer, but these are practical realities to think about.

### Legal Name Changes

Some converts legally change their secular names to match their Hebrew names. This isn't required, but it can feel powerfully unifying. Many converts live with two names: their Hebrew name for Jewish contexts (synagogue, Jewish documents, wedding ketubah) and their birth name for secular life (work, passport, family).

There's no "more authentic" choice here. Ruth the Moabite seems to have changed her name; Onkelos the convert kept his Roman name. Do what feels right for your circumstances.

## The Moment You Receive Your Name

Traditionally, your Hebrew name becomes official at the mikvah (ritual bath) during your conversion. As you emerge from the waters, you are called by your new name for the first time: "Please welcome Yonatan ben Avraham v'Sarah" or "Please welcome Miriam bat Avraham v'Sarah."

This is an emotional moment for most converts — the moment when your transformation becomes real, when you hear your Jewish identity spoken aloud for the first time. Choose a name that will make your heart sing when you hear it called.

## Names to Avoid

A few guidelines on what not to choose:

- **Names of evil biblical figures**: Don't name yourself Korach, Bilam, Haman, or Avimelech
- **Names of other religions' deities**: Avoid names that are explicitly connected to other faiths' theology
- **Jokey or frivolous names**: This name will be on your ketubah (marriage contract), called when you receive honors, and used in prayers for your health. Choose seriously.
- **Names that will cause confusion**: If you're female, don't choose a male name and vice versa

## Your Name Is Your Prayer

In Jewish thought, every time someone calls your name, it's a mini-prayer. The name "Moshe" (drawn from water) reminded Moses of his origins. The name "Yitzchak" (he will laugh) reminded Isaac of the joy of his miraculous birth. Your name will be called out in synagogue, whispered in prayers, used when people bless you.

Choose a name that represents what you want to be reminded of. Choose a name that reflects your values, your journey, or your aspirations. Choose a name that connects you to the story of the Jewish people.

When you become Chana bat Avraham v'Sarah or David ben Avraham v'Sarah, you're not just picking a name. You're claiming your inheritance from the first couple who heard God's call and responded, "Hineni — here I am."

## Taking the Name Seriously

After conversion, make an effort to use your Hebrew name in Jewish contexts:

- Introduce yourself with your Hebrew name at synagogue
- Use it when signing up for Jewish classes or events  
- Ask to be called to the Torah by your Hebrew name
- Use it in Jewish correspondence and documentation

Your Hebrew name isn't a secret identity or an "also known as." It's your real name in the Jewish world. Honor it by using it, and over time, it will feel as natural as the name you were born with — perhaps even more so.

## Conclusion: A Name That Bridges Two Worlds

Your Hebrew name is a bridge between the person you were and the Jew you've become. It connects you to Abraham and Sarah, to 4,000 years of Jewish history, and to the Jewish people worldwide. Choose it thoughtfully, wear it proudly, and know that from the moment you emerge from the mikvah with that name, you are completely, fully, and eternally a member of the Jewish people.

May your name bring you blessing, may it reflect your highest aspirations, and may you hear it called in joy for all your days.
