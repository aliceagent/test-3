## Introduction

Congratulations — you've taken the leap. Whether you've just completed your conversion, or you're beginning to observe Judaism seriously for the first time, you're standing at the threshold of an extraordinary journey. But right now, you might also be feeling a little overwhelmed.

You know the basics: Shabbat starts Friday night, there's something called "kashrut," and Jewish holidays follow a different calendar. But how does it all fit together? What will your first year actually look like? Which holidays come when? What should you learn first?

This article is your roadmap. We'll walk through the Jewish year month by month, showing you what to expect, which holidays you'll encounter, what to study, and how to build a sustainable rhythm of Jewish life. By the end of your first year, you'll have experienced the full cycle of the Jewish calendar — and you'll be ready to do it again, deeper this time.

The Jewish calendar begins in autumn (not January), around September or October, with the High Holidays. That's where we'll start this guide. But no matter when you're reading this, you can jump in wherever the calendar is right now. Judaism is a cycle, not a line — every point is both a beginning and a continuation.

Ready? Let's walk through your first year as a Jew.

---

## Month 1: Tishrei (September/October) — The High Holidays

**What Happens:** This is the most intense month of the Jewish year. Tishrei packs in more holidays than any other month: Rosh Hashana (New Year), Yom Kippur (Day of Atonement), Sukkot (Festival of Booths), and Simchat Torah (Rejoicing with the Torah). If you're starting your Jewish journey now, buckle up.

**Rosh Hashana (1-2 Tishrei):** The Jewish New Year. Unlike the secular New Year's party vibe, this is a day of judgment and introspection. You'll hear the shofar (ram's horn) blown 100 times. You'll eat apples dipped in honey and say, "May we have a sweet new year." Services are long — expect 4-5 hours in synagogue. It's both joyful and sobering.

**Yom Kippur (10 Tishrei):** The holiest day of the year. You'll fast for 25 hours (no food or water). You'll spend the entire day in synagogue — from Kol Nidrei the night before to Ne'ilah at sunset. Wear white if you can; it symbolizes purity. This day is about teshuvah (return/repentance) — taking stock of the year and committing to change. It's hard, but it's also transformative.

**Sukkot (15-21 Tishrei):** Five days after Yom Kippur, you'll build (or sit in) a sukkah — a temporary outdoor hut with a roof of branches. You'll eat meals there for a week. If it's your first time, borrow or visit someone else's sukkah. You'll also shake the Four Species (lulav and etrog). It's joyful, communal, and surprisingly fun after the intensity of the Days of Awe.

**Simchat Torah (22-23 Tishrei):** The most joyous day of the year. We finish reading the Torah scroll and immediately start again from the beginning. There's dancing, singing, and everyone gets called up for an aliyah (Torah honor). Don't be shy — join the dancing even if you don't know the songs yet. This is celebration.

**What to Study:**
- How to blow the shofar (or at least understand the sounds: tekiah, shevarim, teruah)
- The meaning of teshuvah (repentance) — read a short book like *A Practical Guide to Repentance* by Rabbi Zelig Pliskin
- How to build a sukkah or at least recognize a kosher one
- The vidui (confession) prayer recited on Yom Kippur

**Tip for Beginners:** If this is your first Tishrei, don't try to do everything perfectly. Just show up. Go to synagogue even if you don't understand the prayers. Sit in a sukkah even if you didn't build it yourself. The point is to experience the rhythm. Understanding comes later.

## Month 2: Cheshvan (October/November) — The Quiet Month

**What Happens:** Nothing! Cheshvan (also called Marcheshvan) is the only month with no holidays at all. After the whirlwind of Tishrei, this is your chance to catch your breath.

**What to Study:**
- Now that you've experienced Rosh Hashana and Yom Kippur, go back and study them deeply. Read about the shofar's symbolism, the concept of the "Book of Life," and the High Holiday liturgy.
- Start learning the daily prayer structure: Shacharit (morning), Mincha (afternoon), Maariv (evening). If you haven't started davening (praying) daily, this is a good month to begin — even just with the Shema.
- Pick up a basic book on Jewish philosophy, like *To Be a Jew* by Rabbi Hayim Halevy Donin, and read a chapter a week.

**Tip for Beginners:** Use this quiet month to establish daily habits. Set a time for morning prayers. Commit to keeping Shabbat weekly. Build routines now, while there's space to breathe. Don't wait for the next big holiday to "be Jewish" — that's a recipe for burnout.

---

## Month 3: Kislev (November/December) — Chanukah Lights

**What Happens:** Chanukah, the eight-day Festival of Lights, usually falls in Kislev (sometimes spilling into Tevet). This is your first "minor" holiday — meaning you still work, but there are rituals and joy.

**Chanukah (25 Kislev and onward):** Each night for eight nights, you'll light the menorah (chanukiah), adding one candle each night. You'll say blessings, sing "Maoz Tzur," and eat oily foods like latkes (potato pancakes) and sufganiyot (jelly donuts) to commemorate the miracle of the oil. The story: the Maccabees reclaimed the Temple from Greek occupation, and a one-day supply of pure oil lasted eight days.

**What to Study:**
- The historical background of the Maccabean revolt (around 165 BCE, during what corresponds to China's Western Han Dynasty 西汉)
- The deeper meaning of the holiday: the battle against assimilation, the tension between Greek and Jewish worldviews
- The laws of lighting the menorah — which direction, what kind of oil, where to place it
- The Al HaNissim (prayer for miracles) added to the Amidah and Birkat HaMazon during Chanukah

**Tip for Beginners:** Chanukah is low-pressure and fun. Invite friends over to light the menorah together. Make latkes. Sing songs even if you don't know them well yet. This is a great "gateway" holiday for people new to Jewish practice.

---

## Month 4: Tevet (December/January) — A Fast and a New Cycle

**What Happens:** Tevet is mostly quiet, but it contains one solemn day: the Fast of the Tenth of Tevet, commemorating the beginning of the Babylonian siege of Jerusalem (586 BCE, during China's Spring and Autumn Period 春秋时代). If you're new to fasting, this is a "minor fast" — dawn to nightfall, not 25 hours.

**What to Study:**
- The history of the destruction of the First Temple and the Babylonian Exile
- Continue your daily prayer practice. By now, you should be comfortable with Shacharit and Maariv.
- Start learning about kashrut (kosher laws) in more depth. Read about the separation of meat and milk, kosher slaughter, kosher fish, etc. If you're planning to fully kasher your kitchen, start researching now.

**Tip for Beginners:** The winter months (Tevet, Shevat, Adar) are less holiday-dense. Use this time to deepen your learning. Consider starting a weekly class — either in person at a local Chabad, or online through partners like TorahAnytime or Sefaria. Don't coast; build.

---

## Month 5: Shevat (January/February) — The New Year for Trees

**What Happens:** Tu BiShvat (15 Shevat) is the "New Year for Trees." In Israel, this is when the sap begins to rise in the trees after winter. We celebrate by eating fruits — especially the seven species mentioned in the Torah (wheat, barley, grapes, figs, pomegranates, olives, dates).

**Tu BiShvat Seder:** Many communities hold a Tu BiShvat Seder — a structured meal with four cups of wine (or grape juice) and different categories of fruit. It's mystical, ecological, and delicious. If you can join one, do.

**What to Study:**
- The seven species of the Land of Israel and their spiritual symbolism
- Environmental teachings from Torah (bal tashchit, the prohibition against wasteful destruction)
- The Kabbalistic four worlds and how they map onto the Tu BiShvat Seder

**Tip for Beginners:** This is a gentle, beautiful holiday. Buy some exotic fruits you've never tried. Read psalms. Reflect on growth and renewal. Judaism isn't just about fasting and intensity — there's also sweetness.

---

## Month 6: Adar (February/March) — The Month of Joy

**What Happens:** Purim! This is the most fun, raucous holiday in the Jewish calendar. The rule is: "When Adar begins, we increase in joy." And boy, do we.

**Purim (14 Adar):** The story is from the Book of Esther: Haman plots to destroy the Jews of Persia; Esther and Mordechai save the day. We read the Megillah (scroll of Esther) in synagogue, drown out Haman's name with noisemakers, dress in costumes, give gifts of food to friends (mishloach manot), give charity to the poor, and eat a festive meal. Many people drink more than usual (the Talmud says "until you can't tell the difference between 'blessed is Mordechai' and 'cursed is Haman'").

**Four Mitzvot of Purim:**
1. Hear the Megillah (twice — night and day)
2. Give mishloach manot (at least two ready-to-eat foods to one person)
3. Give matanot la'evyonim (charity to at least two poor people)
4. Eat a festive meal

**What to Study:**
- The Book of Esther — read it in English first, then in Hebrew if you can
- The concept of hidden miracles (God's name never appears in the Megillah, yet His hand is everywhere)
- The laws of Purim — when to give mishloach manot, how much charity, etc.

**Tip for Beginners:** Purim is participatory. Make a costume. Bake hamantaschen. Deliver mishloach manot to friends. This holiday is about community, joy, and not taking yourself too seriously. Embrace it.

---

## Month 7: Nissan (March/April) — Passover and Freedom

**What Happens:** Nissan is the "month of redemption." Passover (Pesach) is the big event — an eight-day holiday celebrating the Exodus from Egypt. This is when the Jewish people went from slavery to freedom, from a mixed multitude to a nation receiving the Torah at Sinai.

**Preparation (weeks before):** Cleaning for Pesach is no joke. You need to remove all chametz (leavened bread products) from your home. This means cleaning your kitchen, selling your chametz to a non-Jew (through a rabbi), and buying matzah and kosher-for-Passover food. If this is your first Pesach, start early. Read a guide. Ask for help.

**The Seder (first two nights):** This is the centerpiece — a ritual meal with the Haggadah (the telling). You'll eat matzah (unleavened bread), maror (bitter herbs), charoset (a sweet paste symbolizing mortar), drink four cups of wine, recline, sing, ask the Four Questions, and retell the story of the Exodus. It's long (3-4 hours), but it's unforgettable.

**The Omer Count:** Starting the second night of Pesach, you'll count each day for 49 days leading up to Shavuot. It's a period of spiritual growth and anticipation.

**What to Study:**
- The entire Haggadah — understand each section, each question, each song
- The Exodus story from the Torah (Exodus chapters 1-15)
- The laws of chametz and matzah
- The ten plagues and their deeper meanings

**Tip for Beginners:** Your first Seder should ideally be with an experienced family or community. If you're alone, many Chabad houses host communal Seders — find one and attend. Don't try to run your first Seder solo unless you have no choice. This is a night of questions and tradition; learn by watching, then lead next year.

---

## Month 8: Iyar (April/May) — Counting and Preparation

**What Happens:** Iyar is mostly defined by the Omer count — you're now halfway between Pesach and Shavuot. It's a time of self-improvement. The Kabbalists teach that each of the 49 days corresponds to refining a specific character trait (a combination of the seven sefirot: chesed, gevurah, tiferet, netzach, hod, yesod, malchut).

**Lag BaOmer (18 Iyar):** The 33rd day of the Omer. This is a break in the semi-mourning period of the Omer (no weddings, no haircuts). On Lag BaOmer, we light bonfires, go on outings, and celebrate the teachings of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai, author of the Zohar.

**What to Study:**
- The Kabbalistic meaning of the Omer count and the sefirot
- Pirkei Avot (Ethics of the Fathers) — traditionally studied on Shabbat afternoons during this season
- Begin learning about Shavuot, which is coming next month

**Tip for Beginners:** Don't skip the Omer count. It's easy to forget, but it builds a daily spiritual rhythm. Set a daily reminder on your phone. Use a counting app. The discipline is part of the point.

---

## Month 9: Sivan (May/June) — Receiving the Torah

**What Happens:** Shavuot (6-7 Sivan) — the Festival of Weeks, exactly 50 days after Pesach. This is when we commemorate receiving the Torah at Mount Sinai. It's one of the three pilgrimage festivals (along with Pesach and Sukkot).

**Customs:**
- **All-night Torah study:** Many communities stay up all night studying Torah (Tikkun Leil Shavuot)
- **Dairy foods:** Cheesecake, blintzes, etc. — there are various reasons given for this custom
- **Reading the Book of Ruth:** We read the Megillat Ruth in synagogue (the story of Ruth's conversion)
- **The Ten Commandments:** We read the giving of the Torah from Exodus 19-20

**What to Study:**
- The story of the revelation at Sinai (Exodus 19-20)
- The Book of Ruth
- The meaning of "accepting the yoke of Torah" — what does it mean to commit to mitzvot?

**Tip for Beginners:** If you're a convert, Shavuot has special resonance. Ruth the Moabite, the great-grandmother of King David, is the paradigm of the righteous convert. You're following in her footsteps. This is your holiday. If there's a Tikkun (all-night study), go — even if you only last a few hours. The energy is incredible.

---

## Month 10: Tammuz (June/July) — The Summer Breach

**What Happens:** The Fast of the 17th of Tammuz (dawn to nightfall). This commemorates the day the walls of Jerusalem were breached by the Babylonians (586 BCE) and later by the Romans (70 CE, during China's Eastern Han Dynasty 东汉). This fast begins the Three Weeks — a period of semi-mourning leading up to Tisha B'Av.

**What to Study:**
- The history of the destruction of the First and Second Temples
- The concept of exile (galut) and redemption (geulah)
- The laws of the Three Weeks (no weddings, no music, no haircuts)

**Tip for Beginners:** The Three Weeks are somber, but they're also a chance to connect with 2,000 years of Jewish longing for return. Don't skip it just because it's sad. Grief is part of the story.

---

## Month 11: Av (July/August) — The Ninth of Av

**What Happens:** Tisha B'Av (9 Av) — the saddest day of the Jewish year. Both Temples were destroyed on this day (the First in 586 BCE, the Second in 70 CE). We fast for 25 hours, sit on low stools, read the Book of Lamentations (Eicha) by candlelight, and mourn.

**What to Study:**
- The Book of Lamentations
- The history of the destruction and exile
- Why Tisha B'Av matters today — what are we mourning, and what are we hoping for?

**Shabbat Nachamu (the Shabbat after Tisha B'Av):** The Shabbat of Comfort. The haftarah begins with Isaiah 40: "Nachamu, nachamu ami" — "Comfort, comfort My people." The mood lifts. The month of Elul is coming.

**Tip for Beginners:** Tisha B'Av is hard. It's a long fast, and the liturgy is heavy. But it's also profound. If you've never fasted before (aside from Yom Kippur), this will test you. Prepare with extra hydration the day before. Break the fast gently with something light.

---

## Month 12: Elul (August/September) — Back to the Beginning

**What Happens:** Elul is the month of preparation. In exactly 30 days, Rosh Hashana begins. We blow the shofar every morning (except Shabbat) to wake us up spiritually. We recite Selichot (penitential prayers) starting a week or so before Rosh Hashana. The King is in the field — God is accessible, close, waiting for us to return.

**What to Study:**
- Teshuvah (repentance/return) — the three steps: regret, cessation, resolve never to repeat
- Self-reflection practices: cheshbon hanefesh (soul accounting)
- Review everything you learned this year. What stuck? What didn't? What will you do differently in year two?

**Tip for Beginners:** Elul is intense, but in a good way. This is your chance to look back at the entire year. You started in Tishrei (or whenever you began); now you've come full circle. You've experienced the holidays, built habits, learned, stumbled, grown. Take stock. Write in a journal. What does being Jewish mean to you now that wasn't clear a year ago?

---

## And Then... It Begins Again

After Elul comes Tishrei. Rosh Hashana. Yom Kippur. Sukkot. You'll do it all again — but this time, you'll know what's coming. You'll recognize the tunes. You'll have your own sukkah-building skills. You'll have a year of Shabbatot under your belt.

The Jewish year is a spiral, not a circle. Each time you come around, you're not back where you started — you're higher, deeper, more yourself.

---

## Practical Tips for Your First Year

**1. Don't Try to Be Perfect**

You will forget to count the Omer. You will mess up a blessing. You will struggle with Hebrew. That's fine. The goal isn't perfection; it's direction. Keep moving forward.

**2. Find a Community (Even Online)**

Judaism is not meant to be practiced alone. If you're in a city with a Chabad or a synagogue, go weekly. If not, join online communities — there are vibrant groups on WhatsApp, Telegram, Discord, and Facebook for converts, beginners, and Jews in isolated areas.

**3. Build Daily Habits, Not Just Holiday Observance**

It's easy to get excited about Purim and Pesach, then coast for months. Don't do that. Daily prayer, daily Torah study (even 10 minutes), weekly Shabbat — these are the backbone. Holidays are the peaks, but the path is daily.

**4. Keep a Journal**

Document your first year. Write down your first Shabbat, your first fast, your first Seder. In five years, you'll read it and be amazed at how far you've come.

**5. Ask Questions**

You will have a thousand questions. Ask them. Find a rabbi, a mentor, a more experienced friend. There are no stupid questions in Judaism — only opportunities to learn.

**6. Celebrate Milestones**

Mark your Hebrew birthday. Celebrate your conversion anniversary. Make Shehecheyanu when you do something Jewish for the first time. Acknowledge growth. You've joined a 4,000-year-old story; that's worth celebrating.

---

## Looking Ahead: Year Two and Beyond

By the end of your first year, you'll have:
- Observed every major and minor holiday
- Kept 52 Shabbatot (or close to it)
- Read through the entire Torah cycle (or heard it in synagogue)
- Built a foundation in prayer, blessings, and Jewish practice
- Connected with a community (in person or online)
- Developed your own relationship with God, Torah, and the Jewish people

In year two, you go deeper. You start learning Talmud. You take on more stringencies in kashrut or Shabbat. You mentor someone newer than you. You become not just a Jew, but a link in the chain.

And in year five, or ten, or twenty? You'll look back at your first year and smile. You were so earnest, so nervous, so unsure. And yet you did it. You showed up. You built a Jewish life, one day at a time.

Welcome home.
