# A Chinese Convert's Story: From Beijing to the Beit Din

---

When Li Wei first encountered Judaism, she was standing in a kosher restaurant in Jerusalem's Old City, holding a plate of shakshuka and wondering why everyone around her was singing. It was Friday night, and she had stumbled into Shabbat without realizing it.

That moment—five years ago now—set in motion a journey that would transform her life completely. Today, she goes by the Hebrew name Devorah Leah, and she's a member of an Orthodox community in Ramat Gan. But the path from Beijing to the beit din was anything but straightforward.

## The First Spark

Li Wei grew up in Beijing in a thoroughly secular household. Her parents were both engineers who valued education above all else. Religion wasn't discussed—it simply wasn't part of their world. Like many educated Chinese families, theirs was a household of pragmatism, Confucian respect for elders, and an emphasis on hard work and family duty.

"I learned about Judaism the way most Chinese people do—through history books about Einstein and Freud," she recalls. "Jews were these distant, successful people. Very smart, very persecuted. That's about all I knew."

Her first real encounter came during a work trip to Israel. Li Wei was an engineer for a tech company, and she was sent to Tel Aviv for a three-month project collaboration. On her first Friday in Israel, a colleague invited her home for Shabbat dinner.

"I thought it would be like a regular dinner party," she laughs. "I brought a bottle of wine and showed up in jeans. Everyone was dressed nicely, and there were candles and singing and... rituals. So many rituals. I was completely lost, but also fascinated."

That colleague's family—the Goldbergs—became her gateway into Jewish life. They patiently explained the blessings, the challah, the significance of resting on the seventh day. Mrs. Goldberg even gave her a Chinese-English siddur that someone had brought back from Hong Kong.

## The Pull of Something Deeper

What struck Li Wei most wasn't the prayers or the food—it was the sense of structure and meaning woven into every moment.

"In China, we have traditions," she explains. "We celebrate the Lunar New Year, we honor our ancestors, we follow customs. But these felt... empty to me. Like we were going through the motions without really believing anything. Judaism was different. Every action had a reason, connected to God, connected to history, connected to purpose."

The parallel between Jewish and Chinese culture became a recurring theme in her journey. Both emphasized family, education, and continuity across generations. Both had ancient texts that guided modern life—the Torah for Jews, the Confucian classics for Chinese scholars. Both had experienced tremendous suffering yet somehow survived and thrived.

"When I read about the destruction of the Temple and the Jewish exile, I immediately thought of China's century of humiliation—the Opium Wars, the foreign occupations, the chaos," she says. "Both peoples carried trauma but rebuilt themselves. That resilience spoke to me."

But there was something Judaism had that Chinese culture lacked, at least in her experience: a living relationship with the Divine. The idea that God was not an abstract force or distant ancestor, but an active presence you could speak to, argue with, thank—this was revolutionary.

## The Decision to Convert

After her three months in Israel ended, Li Wei returned to Beijing. But she couldn't shake what she'd experienced. She found a small Jewish community in Beijing—mostly expatriates and business people—and started attending their Friday night gatherings. She ordered books online: "To Be a Jew" by Rabbi Hayim Halevy Donin, "Jewish Literacy" by Rabbi Joseph Telushkin, and a copy of the Artscroll Chumash.

"My parents thought I was going through a phase," she remembers. "They worried I was joining a cult. In China, religion is suspicious, especially foreign religions. My mother kept asking, 'Why can't you just be Chinese? Why do you need this?'"

It was a question Li Wei asked herself many times. Converting to Judaism meant leaving behind not just her old identity, but potentially alienating her family and creating complications in a society that prized conformity. Orthodox conversion would require keeping Shabbat, keeping kosher, dressing modestly—all things that would mark her as different in Beijing.

But the pull was too strong. After a year of study and soul-searching, she contacted the chief rabbinate in Israel about beginning a formal conversion process.

"There's a saying in Chinese: 择善固执 (zé shàn gù zhí)—'choose what is good and hold fast to it,'" she says. "That's what I was doing. I had found something good, something true, and I couldn't let it go."

## The Conversion Process

Orthodox conversion is rigorous under any circumstances, but converting from China presented unique challenges. There was no established conversion court in Beijing, which meant Li Wei would need to relocate. After researching her options, she decided to move to Jerusalem to study and convert.

The decision required courage. She was thirty-two years old, leaving a good job, moving to a country where she didn't speak the language, to join a people who weren't hers by birth.

"My father refused to speak to me for six months," she says quietly. "He thought I was throwing my life away. My mother cried. They saw it as a rejection of them, of everything they'd taught me."

In Jerusalem, Li Wei enrolled in a conversion program for women at Machon Miriam. The curriculum was intensive: Torah, Prophets, Writings, Oral Law, Jewish philosophy, practical halachah, Hebrew language, and Jewish history. She studied from morning until evening, immersing herself in a tradition that stretched back thousands of years.

The cultural adjustments were significant. Chinese culture emphasizes indirect communication, saving face, and group harmony. Israeli culture was shockingly direct by comparison. People said exactly what they thought. Arguments in the beit midrash could sound like fights to an outsider, but were actually signs of intellectual engagement.

"I had to learn that 'no' doesn't mean 'I hate you' in Israeli culture," she laughs. "And that questions aren't challenges to authority—they're how you learn. Chinese students are taught to respect the teacher and absorb knowledge. Jewish learning is different. You're supposed to argue, to question, to wrestle with the text."

## The Beit Din

After eighteen months of intensive study, her rabbis told her she was ready to appear before the beit din—the rabbinical court that would examine her knowledge and sincerity.

The night before, she could barely sleep. She reviewed halachot about Shabbat, kashrut, family purity. She rehearsed her answers to anticipated questions about theology and Jewish law. But she also prepared for something more personal: explaining why she, a Chinese woman with no Jewish ancestry, wanted to take on the yoke of Torah and mitzvot.

The beit din took place in a simple room with three rabbis seated at a table. They asked her questions in Hebrew, which she answered as best she could. They tested her knowledge of blessings, her understanding of Shabbat laws, her commitment to raising Jewish children.

But the question she remembers most clearly came near the end: "You come from a ancient civilization with its own wisdom and traditions. Why leave that for Judaism?"

Li Wei took a breath. "I'm not leaving Chinese culture," she said. "I'm adding to it. My ancestors valued learning, family, and living ethically. Judaism values those same things, but connects them to the Creator of the universe. I'm not rejecting where I came from—I'm completing it."

There was a long pause. Then the head of the beit din smiled. "That's a very Jewish answer," he said.

## Immersion and New Name

After passing the beit din's examination, Li Wei went to the mikvah—the ritual bath where the conversion would be completed. Three rabbis waited outside while she immersed herself in the waters, reciting the blessing upon emerging: "Blessed are You, Lord our God, King of the universe, who has sanctified us with His commandments and commanded us concerning immersion."

She immersed three times, and with each immersion, she felt layers of her old life being washed away—not erased, but transformed. When she emerged from the water, she was no longer Li Wei. She was Devorah Leah bat Avraham v'Sarah—Devorah Leah, daughter of Abraham and Sarah, the first converts.

The name Devorah (Deborah) was chosen for its meaning: "bee" in Hebrew, symbolizing someone who is productive and contributes to the community. Leah was added because she was the wife of Jacob, "who was not chosen first but whose children became the majority of the Jewish people." Li Wei saw herself reflected in Leah—not the obvious choice, not born into this, but ultimately central to the story.

## Life After Conversion

Five years later, Devorah Leah has built a life in Israel she could never have imagined in Beijing. She works as a software engineer for a startup in Tel Aviv, keeps Shabbat with her community, and has become a resource for other Asian Jews and converts who reach out for advice.

Her relationship with her parents has slowly healed. They attended her wedding two years ago—she married a ba'al teshuvah from Brooklyn named Yaakov—and while they don't fully understand her choices, they see she's happy.

"My mother told me recently, 'At least you found a people who value education,'" Devorah Leah laughs. "That's as close to approval as I'm going to get, and I'll take it."

She's also found unexpected connections between her Chinese heritage and Jewish practice. The emphasis on respect for parents mirrors the Confucian concept of filial piety (孝, xiào). The Jewish approach to text study—analyzing, debating, seeking multiple interpretations—reminds her of classical Chinese scholarship. Even the Chinese concept of 天命 (tiānmìng, the Mandate of Heaven) finds an echo in the Jewish idea of accepting God's will.

## Advice for Others on the Journey

When asked what she would tell others from non-Western backgrounds who are considering conversion, Devorah Leah's advice is practical and heartfelt:

**First, don't rush.** "Conversion is not a light decision. Take time to learn, to experience Shabbat and holidays, to understand what you're taking on. If it's meant to be, it will still be true in a year."

**Second, find community.** "You can't convert to Judaism in isolation. Find a rabbi, find a community, find people who will support you. The Jewish people are your new family—start building those relationships early."

**Third, don't abandon your culture.** "Being Jewish doesn't mean stopping being Chinese, or Indian, or Ethiopian. Jews come from everywhere. Your background enriches the Jewish people. Don't see conversion as subtraction—see it as addition."

**Fourth, prepare for sacrifice.** "Conversion will cost you something. Maybe it's family relationships, maybe it's career opportunities, maybe it's just the comfort of fitting in. Count the cost honestly. But also remember—you're gaining something far more valuable."

**Finally, trust the process.** "The conversion process is designed to be challenging because Judaism is asking you to make a lifetime commitment. The rabbis who question you aren't your enemies—they're making sure you're ready for this incredible responsibility and privilege."

## The Long View

Looking back on her journey from Beijing to the beit din, Devorah Leah sees a path that was neither easy nor straightforward, but ultimately inevitable.

"There's a teaching in Pirkei Avot," she says, "that says, 'You are not obligated to complete the work, but neither are you free to desist from it.' That's how I felt about conversion. I didn't choose it the way you choose a hobby. It chose me. And once I recognized that pull—that divine invitation—I couldn't walk away."

She pauses, then adds: "In Chinese, we say 缘分 (yuánfèn)—fate or destiny, but specifically the fate that brings people together. I believe it was my yuánfèn to become Jewish. God brought me from Beijing to Jerusalem, from Li Wei to Devorah Leah. And I'm grateful every single day."

For those standing at the beginning of their own conversion journey—especially those from backgrounds far removed from Jewish tradition—Devorah Leah's story offers both realism and hope. The path is not easy, but it's well-traveled. You won't be the first person from your culture or country to make this journey, and you won't be the last.

As she often tells prospective converts who reach out to her: "Judaism has survived for four thousand years by welcoming sincere souls who seek truth. If God is calling you, the Jewish people will find a place for you. Trust that. And welcome home."
