# Your First High Holidays as a Jew: What to Expect

*A guide for new Jews navigating Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur for the first time*

---

The High Holidays — Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur — are unlike anything else in the Jewish calendar. They're not about celebrating a historical event or commemorating a miracle. They're about you: your relationship with God, your relationships with others, and the person you want to become in the year ahead.

If you're experiencing your first High Holidays as a Jew, you might feel excited, nervous, or overwhelmed. That's completely normal. These days are emotionally intense by design. This guide will help you know what to expect and how to prepare, so you can focus on the spiritual experience rather than logistics.

## The Big Picture: What Are the High Holidays?

The High Holidays, also called the *Yamim Noraim* (Days of Awe), span ten days from Rosh Hashanah to Yom Kippur. Rosh Hashanah marks the Jewish New Year and the beginning of a period of introspection and repentance. Yom Kippur, ten days later, is the Day of Atonement — a 25-hour fast when we stand before God and ask for forgiveness.

Think of it as an annual spiritual audit. Rosh Hashanah opens the books; Yom Kippur closes them. The days in between — called the *Aseret Yemei Teshuvah* (Ten Days of Repentance) — are your chance to fix what needs fixing before the books are sealed.

The imagery used in the liturgy is stark: God sits as King and Judge, deciding "who shall live and who shall die, who by fire and who by water." But this isn't meant to terrify you into submission. Rather, it's a reminder that life is fragile, time is precious, and we have agency in shaping our destiny through teshuvah (repentance), tefillah (prayer), and tzedakah (charity).

## Rosh Hashanah: The Day of Judgment

### Dates and Timing
Rosh Hashanah falls on 1-2 Tishrei (the first month of the Jewish civil year), usually in September. It's a two-day holiday observed even in Israel, making it unique among biblical festivals. The reason? In ancient times, witnesses had to confirm the new moon sighting, and the second day ensured everyone observed the holiday correctly.

### Synagogue Services
Prepare yourself: Rosh Hashanah services are *long*. Morning services can run 4-5 hours, especially if your synagogue includes a full Torah reading and extensive prayers. Don't panic if you need to step out for a break. Many people do.

**What makes Rosh Hashanah services special:**

1. **The Shofar** — The ram's horn is sounded 100 times during the service (in most communities). Its sound is meant to wake up your soul, pierce through spiritual complacency, and call you to repentance. When you hear it for the first time, you'll understand why it's considered the voice of God speaking directly to the Jewish people.

2. **Musaf Amidah** — The additional Amidah prayer on Rosh Hashanah has three unique sections: *Malchuyot* (Kingship), *Zichronot* (Remembrances), and *Shofarot* (Shofar Verses). Together, they proclaim God as King, recall His relationship with humanity, and invoke the sound of the shofar as a call to return.

3. **Unetaneh Tokef** — This haunting liturgical poem describes the Day of Judgment in vivid detail: "On Rosh Hashanah it is written, and on Yom Kippur it is sealed…" Many people cry during this prayer. It's emotionally overwhelming.

4. **Avinu Malkeinu** — "Our Father, Our King" — a responsive prayer that balances intimacy (God as Father) with awe (God as King). It's recited throughout the Ten Days of Repentance.

### Customs and Traditions

**Simanim (Symbolic Foods)** — At the Rosh Hashanah evening meals, it's customary to eat foods that symbolize our hopes for the new year:

- **Apple and honey** — "May it be Your will that You renew for us a good and sweet year"
- **Pomegranate** — to merit as many mitzvot as a pomegranate has seeds
- **Fish head** — that we be like the head and not the tail
- **Round challah** — symbolizing the cyclical nature of the year and the crown of God's kingship

Different communities add their own symbolic foods (dates, leeks, carrots, beets), each with a Hebrew pun or blessing.

**Tashlich** — On the afternoon of the first day (or second, if the first falls on Shabbat), many communities go to a body of water and symbolically "cast away" their sins by shaking out their pockets or tossing breadcrumbs into the water. It's based on the verse from Micah: "You will cast all their sins into the depths of the sea" (Micah 7:19). This ritual is deeply moving — a physical act of releasing the past and committing to change.

**White Clothing** — Many people wear white on Rosh Hashanah and especially on Yom Kippur. White symbolizes purity, humility, and the fresh start we're seeking. The Torah mantle and ark curtain in synagogues are also changed to white for the season.

### What Should You Feel?

People experience Rosh Hashanah differently. Some feel joy at the New Year and the opportunity for renewal. Others feel anxiety about being judged. Still others feel gratitude for life, health, and community.

All these feelings are valid. Judaism doesn't demand a single emotional response. The liturgy gives voice to fear, hope, love, and awe — often in the same prayer. Let yourself feel whatever arises.

## The Ten Days of Repentance

The days between Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur are set aside for serious introspection and interpersonal repair. This is when you:

1. **Apologize to people you've wronged** — Yom Kippur atones for sins between you and God, but it cannot atone for sins between you and another person until you've sought their forgiveness. This is a core principle of Jewish ethics. You need to make things right.

2. **Increase prayer and charity** — Many people wake early for *Selichot* (penitential prayers) during this period. Giving tzedakah is especially emphasized.

3. **Study texts about repentance** — Rambam's *Hilchot Teshuvah* (Laws of Repentance) is a classic choice. It's clear, practical, and deeply moving.

4. **Fast on Tzom Gedaliah** — The day after Rosh Hashanah is a minor fast day commemorating the assassination of Gedaliah, the Jewish governor of Judea. It's a reminder that even after redemption, we can fall again if we're not careful.

The Saturday between Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur is called *Shabbat Shuvah* (the Sabbath of Return). Traditionally, rabbis give their most important sermon of the year on this Shabbat, focusing on the themes of repentance and return.

## Yom Kippur: The Day of Atonement

Yom Kippur is the holiest day of the Jewish year. It's a 25-hour fast (from before sunset until nightfall the next day) spent almost entirely in synagogue. If Rosh Hashanah wakes you up, Yom Kippur strips you down to your essence and rebuilds you.

### The Five Prohibitions

On Yom Kippur, we abstain from five physical pleasures to focus entirely on the spiritual:

1. **Eating and drinking** — The full fast, from sunset to nightfall (about 25 hours)
2. **Washing or bathing** — Only washing for cleanliness (like after using the bathroom) is permitted; no showers or baths
3. **Applying oils or lotions** — This includes cosmetics and perfumes
4. **Wearing leather shoes** — Many wear canvas sneakers or non-leather dress shoes
5. **Marital relations**

These prohibitions aren't meant as punishment. They're designed to help you transcend physical needs and approach the day as the angels do — focused solely on connecting with God.

**Important exception:** Anyone who is ill, pregnant, or for whom fasting poses a health risk should not fast. *Pikuach nefesh* (saving a life) overrides Yom Kippur. Consult a rabbi if you're unsure, but always prioritize health.

### The Five Services

Yom Kippur has five prayer services (most Jewish days have three):

1. **Kol Nidrei (Evening)** — The opening service, famous for its haunting melody. *Kol Nidrei* is technically not a prayer but a legal formula annulling vows made to God that we couldn't fulfill. The melody is so emotionally powerful that many people cry just from hearing it. The Torah scrolls are removed from the ark and held by congregants as *Kol Nidrei* is chanted three times.

2. **Shacharit (Morning)** — Includes a full Torah reading and the recitation of the *Avodah*, describing the Temple service performed by the High Priest on Yom Kippur.

3. **Musaf (Additional)** — Contains the detailed description of the Yom Kippur Temple service. When the cantor reaches the moment where the High Priest pronounced God's explicit Name, the entire congregation prostrates themselves on the floor. It's a rare and powerful moment.

4. **Mincha (Afternoon)** — Includes the reading of the book of Jonah, which teaches about repentance, second chances, and God's compassion. It's a message of hope as the day nears its end.

5. **Neilah (Closing)** — The final service, unique to Yom Kippur. *Neilah* means "locking" — the gates of heaven are closing, and this is your last chance. The intensity builds throughout, culminating in a final shofar blast at nightfall. When you hear it, you've made it through. The fast is over.

### What to Expect Physically

Your first Yom Kippur fast might be challenging. Here's what helps:

**Before the fast:**
- Hydrate well for several days beforehand
- Avoid caffeine the day before if possible (to prevent withdrawal headaches)
- Eat a substantial but not overly heavy pre-fast meal

**During the fast:**
- Expect to feel hungry, thirsty, tired, and possibly headachy
- These sensations are part of the process — acknowledge them and return to prayer
- Sit down when you need to; no one expects you to stand for 12 hours straight
- If you feel faint, step outside for air

**Breaking the fast:**
- Start with something light — water, juice, tea, and simple foods
- Don't rush into a heavy meal immediately; your stomach needs time to adjust

Remember, the physical discomfort is not the goal. It's a tool to help you focus. If you need to drink for health reasons, do so without guilt.

## Navigating the Emotional Landscape

The High Holidays bring up deep feelings. You might experience:

- **Vulnerability** — Standing before God in judgment is humbling
- **Longing** — If you're far from family or new to the community, you might feel lonely
- **Relief** — The chance to wipe the slate clean can be profoundly freeing
- **Inadequacy** — Comparing yourself to more experienced Jews
- **Joy** — The beauty of the liturgy and the community's warmth
- **Overwhelm** — The length and intensity of services
- **Gratitude** — Especially as a convert, experiencing these days as a full member of the Jewish people

All of this is normal. Don't judge yourself for whatever you feel. The High Holidays are designed to bring buried emotions to the surface so you can examine and transform them.

## Chinese Cultural Parallels

If you come from a Chinese cultural background, some aspects of the High Holidays might feel familiar:

**Lunar New Year vs. Rosh Hashanah** — Both mark a time of fresh beginnings, family gatherings, and looking ahead to the year. Just as Chinese families clean their homes before Spring Festival to sweep away bad luck, Jews engage in spiritual housecleaning through repentance.

**Self-reflection and rectification** — The Confucian emphasis on self-cultivation (修身, *xiūshēn*) and the practice of examining one's conscience (反省, *fǎnxǐng*) resonate with the Jewish concept of *cheshbon hanefesh* (accounting of the soul). Both traditions value honest self-assessment and moral improvement.

**Respecting elders and ancestors** — The *Yizkor* memorial prayers recited on Yom Kippur (and other holidays) honor deceased family members, similar to ancestor veneration in Chinese culture. Both cultures emphasize continuity across generations and the obligation to honor those who came before.

**Community and relationship repair** — The emphasis on apologizing and seeking forgiveness before Yom Kippur parallels Chinese cultural values around maintaining harmony (和, *hé*) in relationships and not carrying grudges into the new year.

## Practical Tips for Your First High Holidays

1. **Arrive early** — Services start on time, and it's easier to settle in before the crowd arrives
2. **Bring tissues** — The prayers are emotionally powerful
3. **Bring a cushion** — Synagogue seats can get uncomfortable during long services
4. **Dress modestly and comfortably** — You'll be there for hours
5. **Mark your machzor (holiday prayer book)** — Use sticky notes to find important prayers
6. **Don't worry about understanding everything** — Even lifelong Jews don't catch every word; focus on being present
7. **Stay for the whole service if you can** — The arc of the liturgy builds to powerful conclusions
8. **Find a prayer partner** — An experienced community member can help you follow along
9. **Read translations** — If you don't know Hebrew well yet, focus on the English; God understands all languages
10. **Give yourself grace** — This is your first time; you're not expected to be perfect

## After the Holidays

When Yom Kippur ends and you hear the final shofar blast, there's usually a palpable sense of relief and joy. People wish each other *"Gmar chatimah tovah"* (May you be sealed for good) and head home to break the fast.

But the work doesn't end there. The High Holidays plant seeds; the rest of the year is for nurturing them. The question isn't whether you prayed perfectly or fasted easily. It's whether you're different a month from now, six months from now, a year from now.

In the days after Yom Kippur, reflect on what you experienced:
- What moved you most during the services?
- What commitments did you make to yourself or God?
- Which relationships need repair or strengthening?
- What's one concrete change you can make in the year ahead?

Write these reflections down. They'll be valuable to revisit next year.

## A Final Word of Encouragement

Your first High Holidays as a Jew are a threshold moment. You're entering the rhythm of Jewish time fully, as a complete participant rather than an observer. It might feel overwhelming, but remember: every single Jew in that sanctuary was once standing where you are, feeling the weight and wonder of these days for the first time.

The synagogue is full of people at different stages of their Jewish journey — some born into it, some who chose it like you, some reconnecting after years away. You belong there just as much as anyone else.

The High Holidays remind us that we're all works in progress, that change is possible, and that we're never alone in our struggles. As you stand before God on these awesome days, know that you're standing with millions of Jews across the world and throughout history — all of us seeking the same thing: to be better, to return, to begin again.

*Shanah tovah u'metukah* — May you be inscribed and sealed for a good and sweet year.
