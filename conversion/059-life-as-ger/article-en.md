## Introduction

"Your people shall be my people, and your God my God." These words, spoken by Ruth the Moabite nearly three thousand years ago — during China's Western Zhou Dynasty (西周, ~1046-771 BCE) — remain the defining declaration of every person who chooses to become Jewish.

A *ger* (Hebrew: גֵּר, plural *gerim*) is a convert to Judaism. But this simple definition barely scratches the surface of what it means to join the Jewish people. Conversion is not merely adopting a religion; it's joining a family, embracing an ancient civilization, and taking on a sacred mission that has endured for four millennia.

In this article, we share the real stories of converts from around the world — including those from China and other Asian countries. Their journeys reveal the profound transformation, the unexpected challenges, and the deep joy that come with choosing to live as a Jew.

## The Universal Nature of Conversion

Judaism has always welcomed sincere converts. The Talmud teaches that *gerim* are beloved by God, and many of our greatest ancestors were either converts themselves or descended from converts:

- **Ruth**, the great-grandmother of King David (and therefore ancestor of the future Messiah), was a Moabite convert
- **Onkelos**, who translated the Torah into Aramaic, was a Roman convert
- **Rabbi Akiva**, one of the greatest sages in Jewish history, was descended from converts

The sages even say that in some ways, a *ger* is more beloved than a born Jew, because they chose this path voluntarily rather than inheriting it.

### What Does Conversion Require?

Jewish law requires three things for a valid conversion:

1. **Acceptance of the mitzvot** (commandments) — a commitment to live according to Jewish law
2. **Circumcision for men** (or symbolic drop of blood if already circumcised) — a physical covenant
3. **Immersion in a mikvah** (ritual bath) — a spiritual rebirth

But the formal ceremony is just the culmination. The real journey involves months or years of study, practice, and community integration.

## Stories from Converts Around the World

### Sarah's Story: From Shanghai to Jerusalem

Sarah Chen grew up in a secular household in Shanghai. She first encountered Judaism through business contacts in her tech job. Curious about the Jewish holidays her colleagues celebrated, she attended a Friday night dinner at the Shanghai Chabad House.

"That first Shabbat changed everything," Sarah recalls. "The warmth of the community, the depth of the prayers, the wisdom in the Torah discussion — I felt like I had found something I didn't know I was searching for."

Sarah's journey took three years. She studied Hebrew, learned Jewish law and history, and gradually began keeping Shabbat and kosher. Her family was confused at first, but eventually came to respect her commitment.

"The hardest part wasn't the rituals or the learning," she says. "It was being away from family during major Chinese holidays. But my parents now visit me in Israel for Passover, and they love the Seder. They say it reminds them of family reunion dinners."

Today, Sarah lives in Jerusalem, works for a tech startup, and is raising her two children in an observant Jewish home. "I chose Judaism," she says, "but it also feels like Judaism chose me."

### Michael's Story: Finding Judaism in Rural America

Michael grew up in a small farming town in Iowa where the nearest synagogue was two hours away. His interest in Judaism began in high school when he read *Night* by Elie Wiesel and became obsessed with understanding how the Jewish people survived the Holocaust and remained faithful.

"I started reading everything I could find," Michael remembers. "Torah, Talmud, Jewish philosophy. I ordered books online and taught myself Hebrew from YouTube videos."

At 19, Michael contacted the nearest rabbi and began formal conversion studies, making the four-hour round trip every week. His conservative Christian family was devastated.

"My mother cried for months," he says. "She thought I was rejecting her, rejecting my upbringing. But eventually she saw that I was becoming a better person — more disciplined, more ethical, more dedicated to family and community."

Michael's conversion took two years. He now lives in Brooklyn, New York, works as a Jewish educator, and is engaged to marry a woman from an Orthodox Jewish family.

"The irony," he laughs, "is that I came from a town of 2,000 people and now I live in one of the most densely Jewish neighborhoods in the world. But I needed that isolation at first — it made my choice truly mine."

### Leah's Story: A Korean Convert in Seoul

Leah Kim discovered Judaism while studying comparative religion at Seoul National University. Fascinated by the Talmud's argumentative style and the Jewish emphasis on questioning rather than blind faith, she began attending lectures at the Chabad of Korea.

"In Korean culture, we're taught to respect authority and not challenge teachers," Leah explains. "But in Judaism, I found a tradition that *wants* you to ask questions, to argue, to think deeply. That intellectual freedom was intoxicating."

Leah's conversion was complicated by the fact that there's only one Orthodox rabbi in Seoul. She studied for three years, joined an online community of Korean converts, and spent a summer studying at a women's yeshiva in Israel to immerse herself in Jewish life.

"My family thought I was joining a cult," she laughs. "My mother kept asking, 'Why can't you just be Buddhist like everyone else?' But when they saw how serious I was, how it made me more respectful toward them, not less, they began to support me."

Leah now runs a Hebrew tutoring business in Seoul, teaches Korean to Israeli tourists, and hosts Shabbat dinners for other Korean Jews and converts. She's building the community she wished she'd had when she started.

"Being a *ger* in Korea is lonely sometimes," she admits. "But that's when I remember Ruth. She left her entire world behind. I just have to take the subway to find a minyan. I'm blessed."

### David's Story: From Atheism to Orthodoxy in London

David Thompson grew up in a thoroughly secular British household. His parents were academics who raised him to be skeptical of all religion. He became interested in Judaism purely by accident — through a Jewish girlfriend who invited him to her family's Passover Seder.

"I went expecting to be bored," David recalls. "Instead, I was riveted. The Seder wasn't just a ritual — it was an educational experience, a family bonding event, and a philosophical discussion all rolled into one. I'd never seen religion like this."

The relationship ended, but David's fascination with Judaism didn't. He began reading Jewish philosophy — Maimonides, the Kuzari, modern thinkers like Rabbi Jonathan Sacks. The more he read, the more convinced he became that Judaism's worldview was true.

"It wasn't an emotional conversion," he says. "It was intellectual. Judaism made sense to me in a way no other system did. It balanced reason and tradition, law and ethics, universalism and particularism."

David's conversion was particularly rigorous because he came from a completely non-religious background. The rabbinical court challenged him extensively, wanting to ensure his commitment was genuine and lasting. After two years of intensive study and practice, he converted under London's prestigious Beth Din.

"My parents still think I'm insane," he laughs. "But they respect that I found something meaningful. And they love my wife, who's from a Sephardic family. My mother is learning to cook Moroccan Jewish food."

### Maya's Story: A Brazilian Convert Discovers Her Ancestors

Maya Santos grew up Catholic in São Paulo, Brazil, but always felt disconnected from the Church. In her twenties, after a DNA test revealed Sephardic Jewish ancestry, she began researching her family history.

"I discovered that my ancestors were *conversos* — Spanish Jews who were forced to convert to Catholicism during the Inquisition," Maya explains. "Some of them came to Brazil in the 1600s, maintaining secret Jewish practices for generations."

Maya began attending synagogue, initially just to understand her heritage. But the more she learned, the more she felt she was returning home rather than converting.

"The rituals my grandmother did — covering her head on Friday nights, lighting candles, refusing to mix milk and meat — they weren't just Catholic folk customs like I'd been told. They were remnants of Judaism."

Maya's conversion was emotional and complex. Technically, she wasn't Jewish (since her maternal line had been interrupted centuries ago), but spiritually, she felt she was reclaiming her identity rather than adopting a new one.

"The rabbinical court asked me why I wanted to convert," she says. "I told them: 'Because my ancestors died for this. Because their hidden prayers in dark rooms matter. Because I'm finishing what they started.'"

Today, Maya is a teacher of Ladino (Judeo-Spanish) and a passionate advocate for *Anusim* (descendants of forced converts) who are returning to Judaism.

## Common Challenges Converts Face

### 1. Family Relationships

Almost every convert struggles with family dynamics. Parents may feel rejected, siblings may not understand, and cultural family events can become complicated.

**Advice from converts:**
- Start conversations early and be patient
- Invite family to Jewish events; exposure often reduces fear
- Remember that love transcends religious difference
- Give parents time to adjust; initial shock often softens

### 2. Cultural Adjustment

Judaism isn't just a religion; it's a civilization with its own language, foods, humor, and cultural references.

"I felt like I was learning to be Jewish twice," says Sarah from Shanghai. "Once in law and ritual, and again in culture and community. It took years to get the jokes."

**Advice from converts:**
- Watch Jewish movies and TV shows
- Learn Yiddish or Hebrew phrases
- Ask questions without shame; "I'm a convert" is not an excuse, it's context
- Remember that even born Jews from different communities feel cultural differences

### 3. Imposter Syndrome

Many converts report feeling "not Jewish enough" even years after conversion, especially when surrounded by multi-generational Jewish families.

"I used to panic when people asked about my Jewish childhood," admits David from London. "I felt like a fraud. It took me years to understand that my conversion made me 100% Jewish — no asterisk, no qualifier."

**What the law says:**
Once you convert, you are completely Jewish. The Talmud forbids reminding a *ger* of their past or treating them differently. You are not a "convert" — you are a Jew.

### 4. Finding Community

Converts often struggle to find their place, especially those from ethnic backgrounds rarely seen in Jewish communities.

Leah from Seoul: "Walking into a synagogue where everyone is Ashkenazi and I'm Korean — it's awkward. But I've learned that the awkwardness is mostly in my head. Once people talk to me, they're welcoming."

**Building community:**
- Seek out other converts; many cities have convert support groups
- Find a rabbi or mentor who understands your journey
- Use online communities when local ones are sparse
- Remember: diversity is increasingly normal in global Jewish communities

## The Joys of Being a Ger

Despite the challenges, every convert we interviewed expressed profound gratitude for their Jewish life.

### The Gift of Choice

"Born Jews sometimes take Judaism for granted," says Michael from Iowa. "I never will. Every Shabbat is a miracle I chose. Every prayer is something I fought to access."

### Discovering Depth

Maya from Brazil: "I spent my whole life in churches feeling nothing. Then I walked into a synagogue and felt *everything*. The prayers, the texts, the debates — there's infinite depth here. I'll spend my whole life learning and never be bored."

### Joining History

Sarah from Shanghai: "When I light Shabbat candles, I'm doing what Sarah, Rebecca, Rachel, and Leah did. When I read Torah, I'm joining a conversation that's been going for 3,000 years. That connection to history is overwhelming."

### Finding Purpose

David from London: "Judaism gave me a framework for living ethically. It's not just 'be nice' — it's a detailed system for sanctifying every moment of life. I wake up with purpose now."

## Advice from Converts to Potential Converts

We asked our interviewees what they would tell someone considering conversion:

**1. "Take your time."**
This is a life-changing decision. There's no rush. Study, attend services, live with Jewish families, and make sure this is truly your path.

**2. "Find a rabbi you trust."**
The conversion process can take 1-3 years. You need a rabbi who respects you, challenges you, and supports you.

**3. "Start practicing before you convert."**
Don't wait for the official ceremony. Begin keeping Shabbat, learning Hebrew, and observing mitzvot. Conversion should be a formalization of what you're already living.

**4. "Prepare for loneliness."**
Especially if you're the only Jew in your family or city, there will be moments of isolation. Build online communities and be ready to travel for Jewish connection.

**5. "Don't romanticize it."**
Judaism is beautiful, but it's also demanding. Shabbat restrictions are real. Kosher is inconvenient. Prayers are long. Make sure you're ready for the reality, not just the idea.

**6. "Your conversion is valid."**
Don't let anyone tell you you're "not really Jewish." If you converted through an Orthodox beit din, you are 100% Jewish, full stop.

## Conclusion: The Beauty of Choosing

There's a famous teaching from the Talmud: "A convert who comes to study Torah is like the High Priest on Yom Kippur entering the Holy of Holies." Why? Because born Jews inherit their Judaism — it's given to them. But a convert *chooses* it, and that choice is sacred.

Ruth chose to join the Jewish people when she had every reason to return home. Abraham chose to smash his father's idols and discover the one God. And today, thousands of people around the world — from Shanghai to Seoul to São Paulo to London — are choosing to join this ancient people and carry forward its mission.

If you're considering this path, know that you're not alone. Thousands before you have walked this road, and thousands will walk it after you. The Jewish people are enriched by those who choose to join us, bringing fresh perspectives, deep commitment, and passionate love for Torah.

As our teacher Hillel said: "One who converts is like a newborn child." You are beginning a new life. May it be filled with joy, meaning, and connection to the Eternal.
