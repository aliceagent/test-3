# Raising Jewish Children When You're a Convert

When you choose to become Jewish as an adult, you make that decision with full awareness, deep thought, and personal conviction. But what happens when you become a parent? How do you raise children in a tradition you didn't grow up with yourself? How do you answer their questions when you're still learning the answers? And how do you give them the confidence of heritage when yours is freshly minted?

These questions keep many converts awake at night. The good news is that you're not alone, and you have unique strengths that born Jews sometimes take for granted.

## Your Greatest Advantage: You Chose This

Children of converts have something extraordinary: they grow up watching someone who *chose* Judaism with their whole heart. While your children may not remember your conversion, they will absorb the passion, commitment, and intentionality you bring to Jewish life.

Born Jews often inherit their Judaism like they inherit their eye color—it's just there, unquestioned. Your children inherit something different: they inherit *choice*. They see you studying Hebrew as an adult. They watch you learn new prayers. They witness you asking questions and seeking answers. You model intellectual curiosity and spiritual growth as living, daily practices.

In traditional Chinese culture, there's a saying: "言传身教" (yán chuán shēn jiào)—"teach through words and demonstrate through actions." Your actions speak louder than any inherited tradition. Your children see Judaism as something vibrant and actively pursued, not just passively received.

## It's Okay Not to Have All the Answers

Many converts worry that they can't answer their children's questions about Jewish history, customs, or beliefs. Here's a liberating truth: *you don't need to have all the answers*.

Some of the best moments in Jewish parenting happen when you say, "That's a great question! Let's find out together." Then you pull out a book, call a rabbi, or research online as a family. You're teaching your children something more valuable than facts—you're teaching them *how to learn*.

The Talmud (Taanit 7a) teaches: "I learned much from my teachers, more from my colleagues, and most of all from my students." Judaism values the learning process itself, not just the accumulation of knowledge. When you learn alongside your children, you're living out an authentic Jewish value.

This mirrors the Confucian educational ideal: "学而时习之,不亦说乎" (xué ér shí xí zhī, bú yì yuè hū)—"To learn and constantly practice what you've learned, isn't that a pleasure?" Learning together as a family embodies this principle.

## Creating Jewish Memory When You Don't Have Jewish Memories

One of the hardest parts of raising Jewish children as a convert is that you can't share stories about your own childhood Passovers, your grandmother's challah recipe, or the Chanukah traditions your family kept for generations.

But you *can* create new memories together. And the beautiful thing about Judaism is that it's designed for exactly this purpose. The Jewish holidays, Shabbat rituals, and life-cycle events are all structured to create powerful family memories.

**Start where you are:**

- **Make Shabbat dinner special.** Even if you didn't grow up with it, you can start the tradition now. Light candles together, bless your children, sing Shalom Aleichem. Your children won't care that this didn't happen in your childhood—they'll cherish that it happens in theirs.

- **Tell your conversion story.** Your children should know why you chose Judaism. Make it age-appropriate, but don't hide it. "I wasn't born Jewish, but I chose to become Jewish because..." is a powerful narrative. It teaches them that Judaism is worth choosing.

- **Build holiday traditions.** Maybe you make a special Purim costume together every year. Maybe you have a family tradition of acting out the Exodus story during the Seder. These become *their* memories, and they're just as valid as multi-generational customs.

- **Connect with your community.** Invite families with born-Jewish parents for Shabbat. Let your children see different ways of being Jewish. They'll realize that there's diversity within Judaism, and their family's way is one beautiful expression of it.

In Chinese culture, there's a deep appreciation for "传承" (chuán chéng)—passing down traditions from generation to generation. As a convert, you become the first link in a new chain. You're not breaking tradition; you're *beginning* it. Your children will pass what you teach them to their children, and your grandchildren's grandchildren will trace their Judaism back to your brave decision.

## Addressing the "You're Not Really Jewish" Myth

Unfortunately, some children of converts encounter ignorance or prejudice. Someone might say, "Your mom's not really Jewish," or "You're not as Jewish as I am."

This hurts. But it's factually and halachically wrong.

According to Jewish law, a person who converts through an Orthodox beit din (rabbinical court) is 100% Jewish in every way. The Talmud (Yevamot 47b) explicitly teaches: "A convert is like a newborn child." Your past is erased; your Jewish identity is complete. The great Rabbi Akiva, one of the most important figures in Jewish history, was descended from converts.

When your children face such comments, arm them with knowledge:

1. **Explain that converts are fully Jewish.** The Torah commands us 36 times to love the convert—more than any other commandment. That's how important this issue is.

2. **Teach them about famous converts.** Ruth, King David's great-grandmother, was a convert. Every Jew, including the Messiah, descends from a convert. Onkelos, who translated the Torah into Aramaic, was a convert. Rabbi Meir's teacher, Rabbi Akiva's ancestor—converts shaped Judaism.

3. **Build their confidence.** Make sure they know their Hebrew names, can tell you their tribe (children of converts are adopted into the tribe of their choosing, often Levi), and understand their place in Jewish history.

4. **Contact the rabbi or teacher if needed.** Don't let ignorance go unchecked in educational settings. Most Jewish communities strongly oppose this kind of discrimination.

## Practical Tips for Convert Parents

### 1. **Create a Jewish Home Environment**

Fill your home with Jewish books, music, and art. Hebrew letters on the walls, a tzedakah box by the door, Jewish-themed books on the shelf—these make Judaism feel natural and omnipresent.

### 2. **Embrace Your Questions**

When your child asks, "Why do we do this?" and you don't know, say: "Great question! Let's learn together." Then look it up in a book, ask your rabbi, or post in a Jewish parenting forum. Make seeking knowledge a family value.

### 3. **Find Mentors**

Connect with born-Jewish families who can share their traditions. Find a rabbi or rebbetzin who can answer your questions. Join a convert parents' support group (many exist online). You don't have to figure everything out alone.

### 4. **Tell Torah Stories**

You might not have grandparents' stories about the old country, but you have something even older: the stories of Abraham and Sarah, Moses and Miriam, Ruth and Naomi. Make these your family stories. They belong to you now.

### 5. **Be Patient With Yourself**

You're learning to be Jewish and learning to be a Jewish parent simultaneously. That's a lot. Give yourself grace. You don't need to be perfect; you just need to be present and committed.

### 6. **Celebrate Your Unique Perspective**

Converts often have a deeper appreciation for Judaism precisely because they chose it. You notice details that born Jews overlook. You feel wonder at customs they take for granted. Share that sense of amazement with your children. Let them see Judaism through your fresh eyes.

## When Your Child Asks: "Why Didn't You Grow Up Jewish?"

This question will come. Here's how to approach it:

**Be honest:** "I grew up in a different religion (or no religion), but when I learned about Judaism, I fell in love with it. I wanted to be part of the Jewish people, so I studied hard and converted."

**Be positive:** Don't bad-mouth your birth religion or upbringing. Simply explain that you found your spiritual home in Judaism.

**Be proud:** "I'm so grateful I became Jewish because now I get to raise you as a Jew. We get to celebrate Shabbat and holidays together, learn Torah together, and be part of this amazing tradition together."

**Answer follow-up questions:** They might ask about grandparents, Christmas, or other relatives. Be matter-of-fact: "Grandma and Grandpa aren't Jewish, but they love us and respect our traditions. We're Jewish, and this is our family's path."

## The Special Gift of Convert Parents

In Chinese philosophy, there's a concept called "后天" (hòu tiān)—the acquired, cultivated nature, as opposed to "先天" (xiān tiān)—the inborn, innate nature. While some might see converts as lacking "inborn" Jewish identity, the truth is that your *acquired, chosen* Jewish identity carries tremendous power.

Your children will grow up with parents who:
- **Never take Judaism for granted**
- **Actively study and grow**
- **Model sincere religious commitment**
- **Demonstrate that Judaism is worth choosing**
- **Bring fresh enthusiasm to ancient traditions**

These are incredible gifts. Your children won't have bubbe's gefilte fish recipe passed down through five generations, but they'll have something equally valuable: parents who consciously, deliberately, passionately chose to be Jewish and raise Jewish children.

## Building Connections Across Generations

Even though you don't have Jewish grandparents to draw on, you can create "adopted" Jewish family connections:

- **Find a "Jewish grandparent" in your synagogue.** Many elderly Jews would love to share their traditions and stories with your family.
- **Learn your rabbi's family traditions.** Ask if you can join their family for a Shabbat or holiday meal to see how they do things.
- **Read memoirs and watch documentaries.** Let your children hear the stories of Jewish families through history—these become part of their heritage too.
- **Create a "Jewish family tree."** Instead of tracing biological ancestry, trace your spiritual ancestry: Abraham and Sarah are your parents, and your children are their descendants. Map out the chain of Torah transmission from Sinai to your rabbi to you to your children.

## Final Thoughts: You Are Enough

If you're reading this article, you care deeply about raising your children with a strong Jewish identity. That care, that commitment, that love—these are what matter most.

The Talmud teaches that Abraham and Sarah didn't just bring people into their tent; they brought them under the wings of the Divine Presence. When you converted, you entered that sacred space. Now you're bringing your children in with you.

You don't need to be born Jewish to raise proud, knowledgeable, committed Jewish children. You need to be present, curious, loving, and dedicated. You already are all those things.

Your children are watching you choose Judaism every single day. That's the most powerful lesson they could ever learn.
