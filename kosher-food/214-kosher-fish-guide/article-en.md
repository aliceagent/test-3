# Kosher Fish Guide: Which Fish Are Kosher and How to Verify

**Understanding the simple rules that determine which fish are permissible to eat**

Walking through a fish market can be overwhelming when you're trying to keep kosher. Rows of glistening fish, some whole, some filleted, some you recognize and many you don't. Which ones can you eat? The Torah provides clear signs, but applying them in practice requires knowledge. This comprehensive guide will teach you everything you need to know about kosher fish.

## The Two Torah Signs

The Torah states the requirements for kosher fish with remarkable simplicity:

> "These you may eat of all that are in the waters: whatever has fins and scales" (Leviticus 11:9)

That's it. Two signs: **fins** and **scales**. Both must be present for a fish to be kosher.

### What Counts as Fins?

Fins are the appendages fish use for swimming and steering. The Torah doesn't specify how many fins or what type — any functional fin qualifies. This includes:

- Dorsal fins (on the back)
- Pectoral fins (on the sides)
- Pelvic fins (on the belly)
- Anal fins (near the tail)
- Caudal fins (the tail itself)

Even a single small fin is sufficient, as long as it's a true fin that helps the fish move through water.

### What Counts as Scales?

Scales must be visible to the naked eye and removable from the fish's skin without tearing the skin itself. This is the critical detail. According to Jewish law, kosher scales can be:

- Removed by hand (by scraping or picking)
- Removed by knife
- Come off when the fish is taken from water

Scales that are embedded in the skin and cannot be removed without damaging the flesh do not qualify. This distinction eliminates fish like sharks and eels, which have skin-like coverings but not true removable scales.

## The Rabbinic Principle: All Fish with Scales Have Fins

Our Sages taught an important principle: any fish that has kosher scales will always have fins (Talmud, Niddah 51b). This means in practice, you only need to verify scales — if the scales are kosher, the fins are guaranteed to be present.

However, the reverse is not true. Many fish have fins but lack kosher scales. The presence of fins alone does not make a fish kosher.

## Fish That Are NOT Kosher

The following are commonly consumed but forbidden:

**Shellfish (all varieties):**
- Shrimp
- Lobster
- Crab
- Oysters
- Clams
- Mussels
- Scallops
- Squid
- Octopus

**Fish without kosher scales:**
- Shark
- Eel (鳗鱼, mán yú)
- Catfish (鲶鱼, nián yú)
- Sturgeon (all species, including caviar)
- Swordfish (controversial — many authorities forbid it)
- Monkfish
- Ray
- Skate

## The Swordfish Controversy

Swordfish deserves special mention. Adult swordfish do not have scales, but young swordfish do have small scales that they lose as they mature. Because of this, there is debate among authorities:

- **Lenient opinion:** Since they have scales when young, they are kosher (the fins and scales don't need to be present at the time of eating, only at some point in the fish's life)
- **Stringent opinion:** Since the scales are lost, swordfish should be avoided

Most Orthodox communities follow the stringent opinion and do not eat swordfish. When in doubt, consult your local rabbi.

## How to Verify Kosher Status

### 1. Buy Whole Fish When Possible

The easiest way to ensure kashrut is to purchase whole fish with the head, fins, and scales intact. You can see the signs with your own eyes.

### 2. Scales Must Be Visible on Fillets

If buying fillets, insist on skin-on portions where you can see and feel the scales. Skinless fillets are harder to verify and should be purchased only from a trusted kosher fish market.

### 3. Know Your Fish Species

Learn to recognize kosher fish by sight. Familiarize yourself with what salmon, tuna, carp, and other common kosher fish look like. This helps when shopping at non-kosher markets.

### 4. Ask Questions

Don't be shy at the fish counter. Ask:
- "What species is this fish?"
- "Can I see it with the skin on?"
- "Does this fish have scales?"

Fishmongers are usually happy to help and can show you a whole specimen if you're unsure.

### 5. Use a Kosher Fish List

Many Orthodox organizations publish lists of kosher fish with their common and scientific names. The Orthodox Union (OU), Star-K, and other kashrut agencies maintain comprehensive lists online. Keep one on your phone for reference while shopping.

## Special Kashrut Considerations

### Checking for Infestation

Some fish, particularly herring and mackerel, can contain worms. While the fish itself is kosher, consuming the worms is forbidden. Halacha requires checking fish that are prone to infestation. Flash-frozen fish or commercially processed fish are generally acceptable because freezing kills parasites, but fresh fish may need inspection.

### Fish Prepared in Non-Kosher Establishments

Fish itself does not require ritual slaughter (shechita) like meat or poultry. However, buying fish from non-kosher establishments raises concerns:

- **Shared equipment:** Knives, cutting boards, and grills may have been used for non-kosher fish or other non-kosher foods
- **Misidentification:** Non-kosher fish might be sold under a similar name

If purchasing from a non-kosher establishment:
- Buy whole fish only
- Have them use clean equipment
- Supervise the cutting yourself if possible
- Or better yet, patronize a kosher fish market

### Fish and Meat Together

Fish and meat should not be cooked or eaten together, according to the Talmud (Pesachim 76b), due to health concerns. This means:

- Don't cook fish in a meat pot
- Don't eat fish and meat in the same bite
- Rinse your mouth or eat something between fish and meat courses

However, fish and dairy may be eaten together (though some communities avoid this as well).

### Gelatin from Fish

Gelatin derived from kosher fish is kosher and pareve (neutral). This is different from animal gelatin, which is often not kosher. Fish gelatin is increasingly used in kosher products as a substitute for pork or non-kosher beef gelatin.

## Cultural Adaptations: Kosher Fish Around the World

Different Jewish communities have different traditional fish:

- **Ashkenazi Jews:** Gefilte fish (made from carp, pike, and whitefish), pickled herring
- **Sephardi Jews:** Fried fish, fish in tomato sauce (pescado con tomat)
- **Middle Eastern Jews:** Spiced fish, fish with rice (samak)
- **Chinese Jewish Communities:** Carp varieties, yellow croaker, pomfret

The beauty of kosher fish laws is their universality — the same Torah signs apply whether you're shopping in Brooklyn, Shanghai, Mumbai, or Tel Aviv.

## Teaching Children About Kosher Fish

Make kashrut education fun and practical:

- **Fish market trips:** Bring children to see whole fish and identify fins and scales
- **Picture books:** Create or buy illustrated guides showing kosher and non-kosher fish
- **Cooking together:** Let children help prepare traditional fish dishes
- **Memory games:** Quiz them on which fish are kosher and why

When children understand the "why" behind the rules, they internalize them better.

## Common Questions

**Q: Are all types of salmon kosher?**  
A: Yes. Atlantic salmon, Pacific salmon, sockeye, king, coho — all salmon species have fins and scales and are kosher.

**Q: What about smoked fish?**  
A: Smoked fish is kosher if the base fish is kosher and it's processed in kosher facilities. Smoked salmon, whitefish, and sable are popular kosher choices.

**Q: Can I eat sushi?**  
A: Yes, if made from kosher fish. Ensure the sushi restaurant uses kosher fish varieties and that equipment isn't shared with non-kosher seafood. Many kosher sushi restaurants exist worldwide.

**Q: Is fish oil kosher?**  
A: Fish oil derived from kosher fish is kosher. Check for reliable kosher certification on supplements.

**Q: Are fish sticks kosher?**  
A: Only if they're made from kosher fish in a kosher facility and bear kosher certification. Read the label carefully.

## Conclusion: The Gift of Clear Signs

The Torah's system for kosher fish is elegant in its simplicity. Unlike the complex laws of kosher slaughter for animals, fish require no special preparation beyond selecting the right species. Fins and scales — two visible, verifiable signs that anyone can learn to check.

This accessibility makes kosher fish an easy entry point for those beginning their kashrut journey. You don't need a rabbi to slaughter the fish or a mashgiach to supervise the process. You only need to use your eyes and your knowledge.

As you become more familiar with kosher fish, shopping becomes second nature. You'll recognize salmon and tuna at a glance, know which Chinese fish to choose at the market, and confidently navigate any fish counter in the world.

The laws of kashrut connect us to generations of Jews who asked the same questions at fish markets from ancient Babylonia to medieval Spain to modern-day Beijing. Each time you choose kosher fish, you're participating in that unbroken chain of tradition.
