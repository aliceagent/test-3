# Reading Kosher Symbols: OU, OK, Star-K and What They Mean

---

When you walk into a supermarket and start checking food packages for kosher certification, you'll encounter a bewildering array of symbols: OU, OK, Star-K, Kof-K, and many others. These small marks on packaging represent entire systems of supervision, rabbinic authority, and Jewish law. For someone new to keeping kosher, these symbols can seem like a secret code. This guide will help you understand what they mean and how to read them.

## What Is a Hechsher?

The Hebrew word *hechsher* (הכשר) means "approval" or "certification." A hechsher is a stamp of approval from a rabbinic authority certifying that a food product meets the requirements of kashrut (Jewish dietary laws). 

In ancient times, Jews lived in small communities where everyone knew the local butcher and baker. If you wanted kosher meat, you went to the Jewish butcher who slaughtered animals according to *shechita* (ritual slaughter). If you wanted bread, you bought from a Jewish baker who carefully separated *challah* (a portion of dough) and ensured no prohibited ingredients entered the dough.

Today's food industry is vastly different. A single box of cereal might contain dozens of ingredients sourced from multiple countries, processed in facilities that also handle non-kosher products, and include chemical additives with complex origins. The hechsher system emerged to navigate this complexity.

## The Major Kosher Certification Agencies

### OU (Orthodox Union) — The Most Recognized Symbol

The **OU** symbol—a letter U inside a circle—is the world's most widely recognized kosher certification. Established in 1923, the Orthodox Union certifies over 1 million products manufactured in more than 13,000 plants across 105 countries.

**What it looks like:** Ⓤ

**What it means:** The OU employs hundreds of rabbinic field supervisors (*mashgichim*) who make unannounced visits to manufacturing facilities, inspect ingredient lists, review production processes, and ensure that kosher standards are maintained. When you see an OU symbol, it means that the product has undergone rigorous inspection.

**Variations:**
- **OU-D**: Contains dairy ingredients or was processed on dairy equipment
- **OU-P**: Certified kosher for Passover (no *chametz*/leavened products)
- **OU-M**: Contains meat ingredients
- **OU-Fish**: Contains fish (important for those who separate fish from meat)
- **OU-Parve** (or just OU): Neither meat nor dairy; can be eaten with either

The OU maintains one of the strictest kosher standards in the world. Many observant Jews rely exclusively on OU certification.

### OK (Organized Kashrut) — The Circle K

**OK Kosher Certification**, founded in 1935 by Rabbi Don Yoel Levy, is another major international kosher certification agency. It's sometimes called "Circle K" because of its circular design.

**What it looks like:** A circle containing the letters OK

**What it means:** OK Kosher certifies thousands of companies worldwide and is particularly strong in the chemical, pharmaceutical, and food additive industries. Many ingredients used in kosher food production carry OK certification.

**Variations:**
- **OK-D**: Dairy
- **OK-P**: Parve
- **OK-M**: Meat
- **OK-Fish**: Fish
- **OK Passover**: Kosher for Passover

OK Kosher is highly respected and widely accepted across the Orthodox community.

### Star-K — The Star of David Symbol

**Star-K Kosher Certification**, based in Baltimore, Maryland, was founded in 1968. Its symbol is a five-pointed star (not a Star of David, despite the name) enclosing the letter K.

**What it looks like:** A star with a K inside

**What it means:** Star-K is known for its meticulous standards and detailed approach to kosher supervision. It certifies thousands of products and also provides extensive consumer education through its website, offering guidance on everything from Passover products to restaurant kosher supervision.

**Variations:**
- **Star-D**: Dairy
- **Star-Parve**: Parve (often just "Star-K" without a letter)
- **Star-K P**: Kosher for Passover

Star-K has a reputation for being particularly strict on Passover products and is trusted by many strictly observant communities.

### Kof-K — The Letter Kaf in a Circle

**Kof-K Kosher Supervision** was established in 1968. The symbol shows the Hebrew letter *kaf* (כ) inside a circle—representing the first letter of the word *kosher*.

**What it looks like:** A Hebrew letter כ inside a circle

**What it means:** Kof-K certifies thousands of products and is particularly strong in the dairy and bakery industries. It maintains strict standards and is widely accepted.

**Variations:**
- **Kof-K Dairy**
- **Kof-K Parve**
- **Kof-K Passover**

### CRC (Chicago Rabbinical Council)

The **CRC** symbol comes from the Chicago Rabbinical Council, one of the oldest kosher certification agencies in the United States, founded in 1939.

**What it looks like:** The letters CRC (often with additional letters for dairy/parve)

**What it means:** CRC certification is highly respected, particularly in the Midwest United States. The CRC also publishes the annual *Passover Guide*, widely used by observant Jews.

### Other Important Hechsherim

- **Triangle K**: One of the older certifications, though its standards are sometimes debated
- **KOF-K**: Similar to Kof-K but different organization
- **Tablet K**: A newer certification that has gained acceptance
- **Chof-K**: Based in California
- **Ner Tamid K**: Used by various smaller agencies
- **Local Vaad HaKashrus**: Many cities have local rabbinic councils that certify local businesses (e.g., Vaad Hakashruth of Boston, Vaad Harabanim of Queens)

## Understanding the Letter Codes

Once you identify the certification symbol, you'll often see additional letters:

### D — Dairy (*Chalavi*)

A **D** next to the symbol means the product contains dairy ingredients OR was produced on equipment also used for dairy. According to Jewish law, you must wait between eating meat and dairy (the waiting period varies by custom, from one to six hours). A dairy product cannot be eaten with meat.

**Example:** OU-D yogurt, OK-D cheese, Star-D butter

### P — Parve (Neutral)

**Parve** (also spelled *pareve* or *parev*) means the product contains neither meat nor dairy. Parve foods can be eaten with either meat or dairy meals.

**Common parve foods:** Fresh fruits and vegetables, eggs, fish, grains, coffee, tea, many candies and snacks

**Example:** OU-P vegetable oil, OK-Parve black beans, Star-K fruit juice

### M — Meat (*Basari*)

An **M** indicates the product contains meat ingredients or was processed on meat equipment. Meat and dairy cannot be mixed.

**Example:** OU-M beef jerky, OK-M chicken soup

### Fish

Fish is technically parve but many people separate fish from meat (they don't eat fish and meat together on the same plate). Some certifications mark fish products separately.

**Example:** OU-Fish tuna, OK-Fish salmon

### Passover Certifications

Products kosher for Passover carry special symbols:

- **OU-P**: Kosher for Passover
- **OK Passover**
- **Star-K P**

These products contain no *chametz* (leavened grain products) and meet stricter Passover requirements. Many require special supervision during production.

## How Kosher Supervision Actually Works

Understanding what happens behind the symbol helps appreciate its significance.

### 1. Initial Plant Inspection

A mashgiach (kosher supervisor) visits the manufacturing facility to review:
- Complete ingredient lists for each product
- Production processes and equipment
- Cleaning procedures
- Storage areas
- Vendor certifications for incoming ingredients

### 2. Ingredient Approval

Every single ingredient must be approved. Even "natural flavors" require investigation—they might be derived from non-kosher animal sources. Chemical additives, processing aids, and production lubricants all require certification.

### 3. Ongoing Supervision

Depending on the product and facility, supervision might involve:
- **Full-time mashgiach**: A supervisor present throughout production (required for meat and some other products)
- **Periodic visits**: Unannounced inspections at regular intervals
- **Remote monitoring**: Some facilities have cameras monitored by the kosher agency

### 4. Equipment Koshering

When equipment is used for both kosher and non-kosher products, or for both meat and dairy, it must be koshered (ritually cleaned) according to Jewish law. This might involve:
- Thorough cleaning
- Heating equipment to high temperatures
- Waiting periods between different production runs

### 5. Documentation and Traceability

Modern kosher certification involves extensive documentation. Every ingredient, production run, and inspection is documented to ensure traceability.

## Practical Guide: Reading Labels in the Supermarket

### What to Look For:

1. **Find the symbol**: Usually near the nutrition facts or ingredient list
2. **Check the letter code**: D, P, M, or Fish
3. **Verify the symbol**: Make sure it's from a recognized agency
4. **Check for Passover**: During Passover, look for specific Passover certification

### Common Scenarios:

**Making a meat meal:** 
- Main dish: OU-M beef
- Side dish: OK-Parve rice
- Dessert: Star-K-Parve cookies
- ❌ Avoid: Any dairy (OU-D, OK-D, etc.)

**Making a dairy meal:**
- Main dish: OU-D cheese
- Side dish: OK-Parve pasta
- Dessert: Star-D ice cream
- ❌ Avoid: Any meat (OU-M, OK-M, etc.)

**Parve snacks** (can be eaten anytime):
- OU-Parve chips
- OK-P fruit snacks
- Star-K nuts

## Special Considerations

### Cholov Yisroel (Jewish Milk)

Some dairy products carry additional certifications like **Cholov Yisroel** (חלב ישראל), meaning a Jewish supervisor was present during the entire milking process. Some observant Jews only consume dairy products with this certification.

**Symbol variations:** OU-D Cholov Yisroel, CRC-D Cholov Yisroel

### Yoshon vs. Chodosh

*Yoshon* (old grain) vs. *Chodosh* (new grain) refers to when grain was harvested relative to Passover. Some observant Jews only eat yoshon grain. Products might be marked "Yoshon" or "Year-Round Yoshon."

### Bishul Yisroel (Jewish Cooking)

Certain cooked foods require Jewish involvement in the cooking process. Look for "Bishul Yisroel" on products like canned vegetables or prepared foods.

### Pat Yisroel (Jewish-Baked Bread)

Similar to bishul Yisroel but for baked goods. Some communities require bread to be "Pat Yisroel"—baked with Jewish participation.

## When in Doubt

If you're unsure about a kosher symbol:

1. **Check the certifying agency's website**: Most maintain online databases of certified products
2. **Ask your rabbi**: Every community has different standards
3. **Use kosher apps**: Apps like "Is It Kosher?" and "Kosher GPS" help identify symbols
4. **Start with major symbols**: When beginning to keep kosher, stick with widely recognized certifications (OU, OK, Star-K, Kof-K, CRC)

## The Meaning Behind the Symbols

These small marks on packaging represent something profound: a commitment to living according to Jewish law in the modern world. Every OU, OK, or Star-K symbol represents:

- Rabbis who study ancient texts to apply timeless laws to modern food technology
- Mashgichim who travel the world ensuring compliance
- Companies that invest in kosher certification to serve the Jewish community
- Consumers who choose to eat according to divine commandment

When you check a label for a hechsher, you're participating in a system that connects you to Jews throughout history who asked the same question: "Is this kosher?"

## Conclusion

Learning to read kosher symbols is like learning a new language—it seems complicated at first, but with practice, it becomes second nature. Start by recognizing the major symbols (OU, OK, Star-K), understand the basic letter codes (D for dairy, P for parve, M for meat), and don't hesitate to ask questions.

The kosher certification system is a modern miracle: it enables millions of Jews worldwide to maintain their dietary laws even in an age of global food production. Those small symbols represent thousands of hours of rabbinic scholarship, factory inspections, and commitment to tradition.

Next time you're at the supermarket, take a moment to appreciate that tiny OU or OK symbol. It represents an unbroken chain of tradition stretching back thousands of years, adapted to meet the challenges of the contemporary world.
