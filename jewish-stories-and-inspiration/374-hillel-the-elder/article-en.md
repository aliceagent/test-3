## Introduction

In the bustling streets of Jerusalem more than two thousand years ago — during what corresponds to China's Han Dynasty (汉朝, 206 BCE – 220 CE) — a poor woodcutter from Babylonia arrived at the city's gates. He had walked for days, sleeping along the way, carrying nothing but his love for Torah and a burning desire to learn. Unable to afford the entrance fee to study, he climbed onto the roof of the study hall and pressed his ear against the skylight, listening through the snow that fell upon him through the night.

This was Hillel the Elder (הִלֵּל הַזָּקֵן), one of the most beloved figures in Jewish history. His story embodies the Jewish values of patience, humility, and the transformative power of Torah study. Unlike many religious leaders who came from privileged backgrounds, Hillel rose to become the Nasi (President) of the Sanhedrin through sheer dedication and character — proving that wisdom and righteousness know no social boundaries.

## The Journey from Babylon to Jerusalem

Hillel was born in Babylonia (modern-day Iraq) around 110 BCE, during the final century of the Second Temple period. At that time, Jewish communities flourished throughout the Diaspora, but Jerusalem remained the spiritual center of the Jewish world. Like many aspiring scholars, Hillel felt an irresistible pull toward the Holy City.

The Talmud records that Hillel was forty years old when he came to study under Shemayah and Avtalyon, the leading sages of his generation. These teachers were themselves descendants of converts, which may have influenced Hillel's famously welcoming attitude toward those seeking to join the Jewish people.

Hillel's early years in Jerusalem were marked by extreme poverty. He worked as a woodcutter to support himself while studying Torah day and night. The famous story of him listening from the roof illustrates both his determination and his humility — he was willing to endure physical discomfort and indignity for the sake of learning. When the teachers discovered him nearly frozen on the rooftop the next morning, they recognized in him a student of extraordinary commitment.

## The Patience of Hillel: A Contrast with Shammai

Perhaps no story better illustrates Hillel's character than the famous encounter with a potential convert. The Talmud (Shabbat 31a) records that a non-Jew once came to Shammai, the stern and exacting sage, and said: "Convert me to Judaism on the condition that you teach me the entire Torah while I stand on one foot."

Shammai, recognizing the insincerity and impatience in the request, chased him away with a builder's measuring rod. The man then came to Hillel with the same demand. Hillel's response has echoed through the ages: "What is hateful to you, do not do to your fellow. That is the entire Torah — the rest is commentary. Now go and study."

This response reveals profound wisdom. Hillel didn't dismiss the man's impertinence; instead, he transformed the challenge into a teaching moment. He recognized that the convert's impatience itself demonstrated the need for the very lesson he sought to impart — that Torah study requires patience and cannot be rushed. By giving him the ethical core of Judaism in a single sentence, Hillel planted a seed that would grow through continued study.

## The Golden Rule: Judaism's Ethical Foundation

Hillel's famous statement — "What is hateful to you, do not do to others" — is often compared to the "Golden Rule" found in many religious traditions. However, Hillel formulated it in the negative: don't do to others what you wouldn't want done to yourself. This formulation acknowledges human limitations — we may not know what others want, but we certainly know what we don't want.

For Chinese readers, this teaching bears a striking resemblance to Confucius's formulation in the Analects: "己所不欲，勿施于人" (Do not impose on others what you yourself do not desire). Confucius expressed this around 500 BCE, several centuries before Hillel, demonstrating how profound ethical insights can emerge independently across different civilizations. Both sages recognized that empathy — the ability to project oneself into another's position — forms the foundation of moral behavior.

Hillel's genius was in connecting this universal ethical principle specifically to Torah. He wasn't saying "be a good person and forget about Judaism" — he was saying "this principle is the gateway to everything else." The convert who truly internalizes this teaching will naturally want to learn more, to understand how Judaism develops and applies this core insight.

## Poverty and Humility: The Marks of True Leadership

Despite eventually becoming the most respected rabbi of his generation, Hillel never forgot his humble origins. He continued to live simply and maintained his characteristic patience and gentleness. When he became Nasi of the Sanhedrin, he didn't use his position for personal gain or prestige.

The Talmud records that Hillel would say: "My self-abasement is my elevation, and my elevation is my self-abasement." He understood that true leadership requires humility — the willingness to put others first and to serve rather than be served. This stands in contrast to many leaders throughout history who have used power to enrich themselves.

Hillel's poverty also taught him compassion for the poor. He established numerous legal leniencies to help those struggling financially, always balancing strict adherence to Torah with human kindness. His famous teaching — "Be of the disciples of Aaron, loving peace and pursuing peace, loving your fellow creatures and drawing them near to Torah" — reflects his belief that warmth and patience are more effective tools than harshness.

## The House of Hillel: A Legacy of Compassion

Hillel founded a school of thought that would shape Judaism for centuries. The House of Hillel (Beit Hillel) generally took more lenient positions on Jewish law compared to the House of Shammai (Beit Shammai), emphasizing mercy and the needs of the vulnerable.

The Talmud records hundreds of debates between these two schools, and while both were considered to teach "the words of the living God," Jewish law almost always follows the House of Hillel. The Talmud explains this by noting that the students of Hillel were kind and humble — they would teach both their own opinions and those of their opponents, and they would even teach the opposition's views first. Their character reflected their teachings.

This legacy continues in Jewish tradition. The Passover Haggadah quotes Hillel's method of eating the matzah and maror together. The Ethics of the Fathers (Pirkei Avot) preserves several of his most famous sayings, including: "If I am not for myself, who will be for me? And if I am only for myself, what am I? And if not now, when?"

## Practical Application: Living Hillel's Teachings Today

Hillel's wisdom remains remarkably relevant in our modern world. His famous three questions — "If I am not for myself, who will be for me? And if I am only for myself, what am I? And if not now, when?" — provide a framework for ethical living:

**Self-care is essential.** We have a responsibility to advocate for ourselves and meet our own needs. Hillel recognized that we cannot help others if we are depleted ourselves.

**Community matters.** But if we focus only on ourselves, we lose our humanity. We are social beings who find meaning in connection and service to others.

**The time is now.** Procrastination in matters of growth and righteousness is a form of spiritual laziness. Hillel urged immediate action.

In daily life, we can practice Hillel's "Golden Rule" by pausing before acting and asking: "How would I feel if someone did this to me?" This simple practice can transform our relationships with family, colleagues, and strangers. When we encounter difficult people — like the impatient convert who approached Hillel — we can choose patience over anger, seeking to understand rather than to judge.

## Key Takeaways

- **Hillel's journey from poor woodcutter to revered sage** demonstrates that dedication and character matter more than background or wealth.
- **"What is hateful to you, do not do to others"** encapsulates the ethical heart of Judaism, similar to Confucius's teaching in the Analects.
- **Patience in teaching and leadership** can transform even challenging situations into opportunities for growth.
- **True humility elevates** — Hillel showed that leadership is about service, not status.
- **The House of Hillel's legacy** of kindness and compassion continues to shape Jewish law and practice today.
