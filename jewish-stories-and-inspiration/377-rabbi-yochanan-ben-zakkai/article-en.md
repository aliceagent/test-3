## Introduction

In the year 70 CE — during what corresponds to China's Eastern Han Dynasty (东汉, 汉朝) — a tragedy unfolded that threatened to extinguish Judaism forever. The Romans had destroyed the Second Temple in Jerusalem, the very center of Jewish religious life for nearly five centuries. The Jewish revolt had failed. Most of the people were dead, enslaved, or scattered. The priestly class that had conducted the Temple rituals was gone. The Sanhedrin, the great assembly of sages, was in hiding.

In this darkest hour, one man's courage and wisdom transformed an impending catastrophe into a new beginning. Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai — a sage in his seventies — orchestrated one of history's most audacious escapes. Smuggled out of besieged Jerusalem in a coffin, he negotiated with the Roman general Vespasian and secured permission to establish a Jewish academy in the coastal town of Yavneh.

This act of spiritual audacity saved Judaism. Without it, the Jewish people might have faded into history like so many ancient peoples. Rabbi Yochanan taught his generation — and all future generations — that Judaism could survive without the Temple. His vision created the foundation for the rabbinic Judaism we practice today.

## The Siege of Jerusalem and a Desperate Plan

### A City on the Brink

By 68 CE, Jerusalem was engulfed in chaos. The Roman legions had surrounded the city, determined to crush the Jewish rebellion once and for all. Inside the walls, Jewish factions were fighting each other with a violence that matched the Roman threat. Food supplies ran out. The population was starving. And through it all, the Romans waited, tightening their grip.

The Talmud records that Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai had been urging peace throughout the rebellion. He understood what the zealots refused to see: military resistance against the Roman Empire was suicide. But his warnings went unheeded. As the siege dragged on, the city became increasingly desperate.

### The Coffin Escape

Realizing that Jerusalem would fall and all its scholars perish, Rabbi Yochanan devised an extraordinary plan. According to the Talmudic account (Gittin 56a), he instructed his disciples to smuggle him out of the city:

He would pretend to be dead.

His students placed him in a coffin and carried him through the gates of Jerusalem. The zealot guards, suspicious of deserters, stopped the procession and were about to pierce the coffin with their spears to verify that it truly contained a corpse. But one quick-thinking student — Rabbi Eliezer — suggested they say their master had died of disease, and any disturbance might spread contagion. The guards stepped back, and the coffin passed through.

Once outside the Roman lines, Rabbi Yochanan emerged and presented himself to General Vespasian. What followed was a display of prophetic insight that would change history.

## The Prophecy and the Negotiation

### "You Will Be Emperor"

As the Talmud recounts, when Rabbi Yochanan entered Vespasian's presence, he greeted him with an extraordinary phrase: "Peace unto you, O King."

Vespasian responded sharply: "You are worthy of death on two counts. First, you called me 'King' when I am not king. Second, earlier you did not come forward, and now you come?"

Rabbi Yochanan explained: "If I am not to die, I will tell you. You are a king — because Jerusalem will not fall except into the hands of a king. And as for why I did not come earlier — the zealots among us did not let me."

At that moment, a messenger arrived from Rome: the emperor was dead, and the Senate had named Vespasian as his successor.

Impressed by this prophetic accuracy, Vespasian granted Rabbi Yochanan a wish. The sage's request was surprising in its humility: "Give me Yavneh and its sages."

He could have asked for Jerusalem to be spared. He could have asked for the Temple to be preserved. Instead, he asked for a small town on the coast and permission for Jewish scholars to gather there.

### Why Yavneh?

Yavneh (Jamnia in Greek) was a modest town, but it was safe — far enough from Jerusalem to escape the worst of the Roman retaliation, yet close enough to maintain connection with the land. Most importantly, it was small enough that the Romans wouldn't see it as a threat.

Rabbi Yochanan understood that buildings could be destroyed, but wisdom could live on. The Temple was stone and mortar, but the Torah was spirit and word. If he could preserve the scholars and their academy, he could preserve Judaism itself.

## The Academy at Yavneh: Birth of Rabbinic Judaism

### A New Center of Learning

Rabbi Yochanan's request was granted. He established his academy in Yavneh, and gradually, surviving scholars and students gathered there. The Sanhedrin — the great court of Jewish law — was reconstituted under his guidance.

This was revolutionary. For centuries, Jewish religious life had centered on the Temple in Jerusalem. The Temple was where sacrifices were offered, where the three pilgrimage festivals brought the nation together, where the Divine Presence was believed to dwell. Without it, how could Judaism continue?

Rabbi Yochanan provided the answer: through Torah study and prayer.

### Replacing Sacrifice with Prayer

The most dramatic transformation occurred in Jewish prayer. The daily sacrifices in the Temple had been the primary form of divine service. Now, with the Temple destroyed, Rabbi Yochanan and his colleagues developed a structured prayer service — the Amidah, or Shemoneh Esrei — that could be recited anywhere, by anyone.

The Talmud (Berachot 26b) records that the rabbis established the times for the three daily prayers — morning, afternoon, and evening — corresponding to the times of the daily sacrifices. Prayer became, in the words of the prophets, "the offerings of our lips" (Hosea 14:3).

This democratized Jewish worship. No longer did service of God require access to the Temple or participation in the priestly rituals. Any Jew, anywhere, could stand before God in prayer.

### The Shift from Priests to Scholars

Rabbi Yochanan's academy also marked a shift in religious authority. The priests and their Temple rituals gave way to the rabbis and their interpretation of Torah. This was a profound transformation in Jewish leadership.

The rabbis at Yavneh began the work that would culminate in the Mishnah — the codification of Jewish oral law. They developed new legal frameworks to address a world without Temple sacrifices, without the priestly gifts, without the agricultural laws that had depended on the land centered around Jerusalem.

The Sanhedrin at Yavneh continued to function as the supreme religious authority, but its nature changed. It became a court of scholars rather than priests — men whose authority derived from their learning and wisdom rather than their lineage.
