# Pikuach Nefesh: Why Saving a Life Overrides Almost Everything


## Introduction: Shabbat in the Emergency Room

---

It's Friday night. The Shabbat candles are lit, dinner is on the table, and your phone rings. (Yes, you're not supposed to use phones on Shabbat, but bear with this scenario.)

Your neighbor has collapsed. They need immediate medical help. Do you:

A) Wait until Shabbat ends to call for help?
B) Call immediately and worry about the religious violation later?

Jewish law is unequivocal: **Call immediately.** Drive them to the hospital. Perform CPR. Do surgery. Whatever it takes.

Why? Because of *pikuach nefesh* — the principle that saving a human life overrides nearly every commandment in the Torah.

Not some commandments. Not most commandments. *Nearly all* of them.

## The Principle: Life Takes Precedence

The term *pikuach nefesh* literally means "watching over a soul" or "saving a life." The principle is stated clearly in the Mishnah:

**"Danger to life overrides Shabbat"** (Yoma 8:6)

But it goes far beyond Shabbat. The Talmud explains:

**"Violate one Shabbat for someone, so that they may observe many Shabbatot"** (Yoma 85b)

The logic: commandments exist to give life meaning. If following them would cost a life, they've become self-defeating. Better to violate them once to preserve the life that will observe them in the future.

## Biblical Sources

Several Torah verses establish this principle:

### "And You Shall Live By Them"
**"You shall keep My statutes and My ordinances, which if a person does, *he shall live by them*"** (Leviticus 18:5)

The Talmud emphasizes: "live by them" — not *die* by them (Yoma 85b). The commandments are given to enhance life, not to demand death.

### "Choose Life"
**"I have set before you life and death, blessing and curse; *choose life*, so that you and your descendants may live"** (Deuteronomy 30:19)

When there's a conflict, choose life.

### "Love Your Neighbor"
**"You shall love your neighbor as yourself"** (Leviticus 19:18)

If you would want someone to violate Shabbat to save your life, you must do the same for others.

### "Do Not Stand Idly By"
**"Do not stand idly by your neighbor's blood"** (Leviticus 19:16)

Inaction that leads to death is prohibited. You must intervene.

## What Gets Overridden?

Almost everything. Specifically:

### Shabbat and Holidays
All Shabbat prohibitions may be violated to save a life:
- Driving to the hospital
- Calling emergency services
- Cooking food for a sick person
- Turning on lights for surgery
- Using medical equipment

The same applies to Yom Kippur, Passover, and all other holidays.

### Kashrut (Dietary Laws)
If non-kosher food is necessary for survival (perhaps the only available food, or medically prescribed), you must eat it.

If pig-derived medication (like insulin from porcine sources) is necessary, you must take it.

### Ritual Purity Laws
Kohanim (priests) normally cannot touch corpses or enter cemeteries. But to save a life, they may (and must).

### Fast Days
Someone who is ill or whose health would be endangered by fasting must not fast, even on Yom Kippur.

### Modesty Laws
If providing medical care requires violating modesty standards (male doctor treating female patient, or vice versa), it's permitted when necessary for health.

## The Three Exceptions

Only three commandments are *not* overridden by pikuach nefesh:

1. **Murder** (*shefichat damim*) — You cannot kill an innocent person to save your life or someone else's
2. **Sexual immorality** (*giluy arayot*) — Specifically incest and adultery
3. **Idolatry** (*avodah zarah*) — Worshipping idols or denying God

The Talmud explains: **"Better to be killed than to transgress"** these three (Sanhedrin 74a).

Why these three?

**Murder**: If you'd kill an innocent person to save yourself, why is your blood "redder" (more valuable) than theirs? (Sanhedrin 74a)

**Sexual immorality**: Preserves family structure and human dignity at the most fundamental level

**Idolatry**: Negates the entire relationship with God that gives meaning to all other commandments

Even here, there are nuances. For instance, if coerced publicly to commit idolatry, you must refuse. But if forced privately, some authorities permit compliance to save your life (though martyrdom is praised).

## What Counts as Pikuach Nefesh?

### Immediate Life Threat
Clearly applies to:
- Heart attacks, strokes, severe injuries
- Poisoning, choking, drowning
- Severe allergic reactions
- Life-threatening infections

### Potential Future Danger
The principle extends to situations that *might* become life-threatening:

**Pregnancy and childbirth**: A woman's needs during pregnancy and labor override Shabbat and fast days, even if there's no immediate danger (Shulchan Aruch, Orach Chaim 330:1).

**Chronic illness management**: Taking medications, monitoring conditions (diabetes, heart disease), and preventive care override religious restrictions.

**Mental health**: Severe depression with suicidal risk, psychotic breaks, and other psychiatric emergencies count as pikuach nefesh.

### Preventive Medicine
Most authorities permit violating Shabbat for vaccinations when necessary (during an outbreak), preventive surgeries (like removing a suspicious tumor), and other measures that prevent future life threat.

### The "Doubtful" Case
**When in doubt, save the life.** (Yoma 84b)

If you're unsure whether someone's condition is life-threatening, treat it as if it is. Better to violate Shabbat unnecessarily than to risk a life.

The Talmud gives an example: if someone is buried under rubble on Shabbat, you dig them out immediately. Even if you're not certain they're alive, even if they might already be dead — you assume life and act accordingly.

## Who Must Act?

### Everyone
Pikuach nefesh obligates everyone, not just doctors:

- Calling emergency services
- Performing CPR
- Moving someone to safety
- Providing first aid

You don't need to be an expert. You must do what you can.

### Doctors Have Extra Obligations
Medical professionals have heightened responsibilities:

**They must treat on Shabbat** — refusing to treat because it's Shabbat would be a serious violation.

**They must work on holidays** — saving lives overrides holiday rest.

**They may not refuse patients** — even if treating violates their religious preferences in minor ways.

Rabbi Moshe Feinstein ruled that doctors should volunteer for Shabbat shifts when possible, since their work saves lives and thus fulfills a high mitzvah (Iggerot Moshe, Orach Chaim 4:79).

### Specialists Over Non-Specialists
If a trained professional is available, they should act rather than an amateur. However, if delay would risk the patient, whoever is present must act.

## Practical Applications

### Emergency Medical Services
- Calling ambulances on Shabbat: required
- Driving to hospital on Shabbat: required
- Accompanying a patient to provide translation or comfort: permitted (as it helps their care)

### Medications and Treatments
- Taking daily medications on Shabbat: permitted (if medically necessary)
- Mixing medications (even if it involves "cooking"): permitted
- Using electricity for medical devices: fully permitted

### Pregnancy and Childbirth
- A woman in labor may travel to hospital, have her needs met, and have lights/phones used for her care — all on Shabbat
- Even in early labor without immediate danger, her needs override Shabbat

### Mental Health
- Therapy sessions on Shabbat for acutely suicidal patients: permitted
- Psychiatric medications: must be taken as prescribed
- Hospitalization when necessary: permitted

### Organ Donation
Saving another person's life through organ donation is considered pikuach nefesh. Most authorities permit organ donation after death, and living donation (like kidney) when medically safe for the donor.

## The Spirit: Rushing to Save

Jewish law doesn't just *permit* violating Shabbat to save life — it *requires* doing so **enthusiastically and without hesitation**.

**"The zealous rush to perform mitzvot"** (Pesachim 4a) — and saving a life is a supreme mitzvah.

The Talmud criticizes anyone who delays or debates about whether to violate Shabbat in an emergency. Act first, ask questions later.

### Don't Ask Permission
If there's a medical emergency on Shabbat, **don't ask a rabbi for permission to act**. Act immediately. The delay could cost a life.

The Shulchan Aruch (Code of Jewish Law) states:

**"One who is quick [to violate Shabbat to save a life] is praiseworthy, and one who asks a question [instead of acting] is a shedder of blood"** (Orach Chaim 328:2).

Strong language: hesitation is murder.

### Use the Best Resources
Don't use inferior methods just because they're less "Shabbat-violating." Use the best available medical care, even if it requires more violations.

For instance: drive to the best hospital, not the closest one with inferior care, if the better hospital offers significantly better treatment.

## Long-Term Health

While pikuach nefesh clearly applies to immediate danger, what about long-term health?

### Preventive Care
Most authorities permit Shabbat violations for:
- Vaccinations during outbreaks
- Cancer screenings when medically indicated
- Surgical removal of potentially dangerous growths

### Chronic Disease Management
Taking medications for chronic conditions (diabetes, hypertension, thyroid disease) is obligatory, even on Shabbat.

Monitoring vital signs, using medical equipment, and dietary adjustments all override ritual restrictions when medically necessary.

### Mental and Emotional Health
The principle extends to psychological wellbeing:

**Stress and burnout**: If strict observance causes dangerous stress, accommodations may be made.

**Social isolation**: If rigid observance leads to severe loneliness affecting mental health, communal participation in slightly less-strict settings may be permitted.

## Boundaries: What Doesn't Qualify

While pikuach nefesh is expansive, it has limits:

### Minor Ailments
A headache, mild cold, or minor discomfort doesn't override Shabbat. Use Shabbat-permissible methods (like pre-prepared medications).

### Enhancement vs. Treatment
Cosmetic procedures purely for appearance (not treating medical conditions) don't qualify.

Elective procedures that can wait don't override Shabbat.

### Convenience
Easier access to care isn't sufficient. The care must be *necessary*, not just preferable.

## The Deeper Message

Why does pikuach nefesh override almost everything?

### Human Life is Infinite Value
The Mishnah teaches:

**"Whoever destroys a single soul, Scripture considers it as if he destroyed an entire world. And whoever saves a single soul, Scripture considers it as if he saved an entire world"** (Sanhedrin 4:5)

One life = one world. Its value is infinite. Against infinity, other values must yield.

### Commandments Serve Life
The purpose of mitzvot is to sanctify life, not to replace it. If a commandment would cost a life, it's being misused.

### Imitating God's Mercy
God is *rachum* (merciful). A legal system that values ritual over life would contradict God's essential attribute.

### The Sanctity of the Present
You might save many Shabbatot in the future by violating this one. But more: this person's life *right now* is sacred. Future possibilities don't outweigh present reality.

## Modern Medical Ethics

Pikuach nefesh informs Jewish medical ethics:

### Abortion
Most authorities permit abortion when the mother's life is at risk (physical or severe mental health). Some permit it for serious fetal abnormalities incompatible with life.

### End-of-Life Care
Prolonging life is generally obligatory, even if quality is poor, because life has infinite value.

However, Jewish law distinguishes between *preserving* life and *prolonging dying*. Removing impediments to natural death (like a loud noise preventing someone from dying peacefully) is permitted.

### Experimental Treatment
Trying experimental treatments to save life is generally encouraged, even with risks.

### Triage
When resources are limited (pandemic, disaster), triage decisions must prioritize who can be saved, applying principles of justice and maximum life-saving.

## Conclusion: Life First

The principle of pikuach nefesh reveals Judaism's deepest priorities.

Yes, Shabbat is holy. Kashrut is important. All the commandments matter. But they exist to serve life, not to replace it.

When a life is at stake, everything else becomes secondary. Not because the other commandments don't matter, but because *this* is their purpose: creating a world where life flourishes.

The Talmud imagines God saying to the Jewish people:

**"Better that you violate one Shabbat, so you can keep many Shabbatot in the future"** (Yoma 85b).

This isn't permission grudgingly granted. It's God's will, enthusiastically commanded.

Save the life. Ask questions later. Rush to help. Don't hesitate.

Because every person is a world. And saving a world — that's the highest commandment of all.

**"You shall live by them"** (Leviticus 18:5).

Live.
