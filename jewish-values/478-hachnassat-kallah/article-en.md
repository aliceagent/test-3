# Hachnassat Kallah: Helping a Bride and Groom — The Ultimate Chesed


## Introduction: Greater Than Welcoming the Divine Presence

---

The Talmud tells an astonishing story about Rabbi Tarfon, a sage of the first century CE:

While studying Torah late into the night, Rabbi Tarfon heard music from a wedding nearby. He immediately left his studies, joined the celebration, and danced before the bride.

Later, when students asked why he'd interrupted his learning — considered one of the highest activities — he explained that attending to a bride and groom's needs takes precedence even over Torah study (Ketubot 17a).

Another passage says: **"One who gladdens a bride and groom, it is as if they have rebuilt one of the ruins of Jerusalem"** (Berakhot 6b).

This mitzvah — *hachnassat kallah*, literally "bringing in the bride" — encompasses helping a couple marry, celebrating with them, and ensuring they can start their life together with dignity. It's considered one of the greatest acts of *chesed* (loving-kindness) in Jewish tradition.

## What Is Hachnassat Kallah?

The term originally referred to escorting the bride to the wedding ceremony. But it expanded to include:

### Before the Wedding
- Helping poor couples afford to marry
- Providing wedding necessities (clothing, rings, food)
- Arranging matches (traditional matchmaking)
- Offering counseling and preparation

### During the Wedding
- Attending the ceremony and celebration
- Dancing and entertaining to bring joy
- Serving as witnesses
- Providing music and festivities

### After the Wedding
- Helping newlyweds set up their home
- Providing household items
- Offering support as they build their life together

## Biblical and Rabbinic Sources

The obligation has deep roots:

### God as the First Matchmaker
The Midrash notes that after creating Eve, **"God brought her to Adam"** (Genesis 2:22). God Himself performed the first *hachnassat kallah*, escorting the bride to her groom (Berakhot 61a).

If God does this, we should too.

### Chesed as Foundational
**"The world is built on chesed"** (Psalms 89:3). Helping couples marry builds the foundation of society: families, the next generation, communal continuity.

### Rejoicing With the Bride and Groom
**"Go forth and see, O daughters of Zion, King Solomon with the crown his mother made for him on his wedding day, on the day of his heart's gladness"** (Song of Songs 3:11).

The Talmud interprets this as an obligation to celebrate with the couple (Sukkah 49b).

### The Talmudic Priority
**"All must rise before a Torah scroll, before a bride, and before a funeral procession"** (Ketubot 17a).

These three warrant universal respect and participation. Notice: the bride is mentioned alongside the Torah itself.

## Why Is It So Important?

### 1. Building the Future
Marriage isn't just a personal event; it's the foundation of the next generation. Helping a couple marry is investing in the community's future.

The Talmud says: **"Any person who does not engage in procreation, it is as if they diminish the divine image"** (Yevamot 63b). By extension, helping others marry enables them to fulfill this profound purpose.

### 2. Dignity and Inclusion
Without community support, poor couples might not be able to afford a proper wedding — or marry at all. Hachnassat kallah ensures that economic status doesn't prevent anyone from marrying with dignity.

### 3. Joy Multiplies
The Talmud teaches that **"there is no joy without food and drink"** (Pesachim 109a), but more: **"there is no joy like the joy of a bride and groom"** (Pirke d'Rabbi Eliezer 16).

Joy is contagious. When you gladden a couple, you spread joy through the community.

### 4. Imitating God
God attended to Adam and Eve's wedding. Abraham and Sarah welcomed guests. We imitate divine and patriarchal chesed by helping couples.

## How the Rabbis Helped

The Talmud preserves beautiful stories of sages going to extraordinary lengths:

### Dancing Before the Bride
**Rabbi Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak** would dance before brides holding a myrtle branch (Ketubot 17a).

**Rabbi Yehuda bar Ilai** would take a myrtle branch and dance before brides, saying, "Beautiful and graceful bride!" (Ketubot 16b-17a).

When asked how to dance before a bride, the school of Hillel said to call every bride "beautiful and graceful" — praising her to gladden her heart, even if objectively she wasn't beautiful (Ketubot 16b-17a).

### Providing Necessities
**Abba Yudan** would lend his jewelry to poor brides so they'd feel beautiful at their weddings. His wife continued this practice after his death (Ketubot 17a).

**Rav Huna** tore fine silk to make decorations for weddings, showing that material possessions are worth less than bringing joy to a couple (Ketubot 17a).

### Interrupting Study
As mentioned, **Rabbi Tarfon** left Torah study to attend a wedding.

The Talmud also records sages closing their study halls when a wedding procession passed, joining the celebration (Ketubot 17a).

**The message:** No matter how important your work, making a couple happy takes priority.

## Communal Funds: Gemachim

Traditional Jewish communities established *gemach* funds (abbreviation for *gemilut chasadim*, acts of loving-kindness) specifically for weddings:

**Providing dowries** for poor brides (a biblical precedent: Nehemiah 8:10)

**Lending household items** to newlyweds

**Offering interest-free loans** for wedding expenses

**Donating wedding dresses and suits** for reuse

**Providing catering** or volunteer cooking

The Talmud records that in Jerusalem, wealthy families would display their fine foods at weddings, but when poor brides felt embarrassed, the practice was banned — everyone received the same simple meal (Ketubot 17a). Even communal leaders adjusted customs to preserve dignity.

## Modern Applications

Hachnassat kallah remains vibrant:

### Wedding Funds
Many Jewish communities maintain funds to help couples with:
- Venue costs
- Catering
- Photography
- Music
- Clothing
- Rings

These funds operate discreetly to preserve the couple's dignity.

### Loan Dresses and Suits
Organizations collect and lend formal wear, enabling couples to have dignified weddings without crushing debt.

### Volunteer Services
Photographers, musicians, florists, and makeup artists donate services to couples in need.

### Wedding Registries
Communal registries allow friends and family to contribute specific items (dishes, furniture, linens) directly, helping couples start their households.

### Mentorship and Counseling
Experienced couples offer guidance to engaged pairs, preparing them for marriage's realities.

### Shidduch (Matchmaking)
Traditional matchmakers and modern dating coaches help singles meet appropriate partners, viewed as profound chesed.

### Attending Celebrations
Simply showing up matters. Your presence says: "Your joy matters. I celebrate with you."

## The Joy Principle: Simchat Chatan v'Kallah

The obligation isn't just financial — it's emotional:

**"Gladdening the groom and bride"** (*l'same'ach chatan v'kallah*) is a distinct mitzvah.

How do you gladden them?

### Dancing
The tradition of dancing before the bride continues. In many communities, guests form circles, dance energetically, and perform for the couple.

### Music
Live music, traditional wedding songs (*nigun*), and joyful celebration create an atmosphere of happiness.

### Compliments and Blessings
Praising the couple, offering blessings, and speaking words of encouragement gladden their hearts.

### Humor and Entertainment
Jesters, comedians, and creative performances were traditional at weddings. Laughter is joy.

### Your Full Presence
Distracted attendance doesn't gladden anyone. Be present, engaged, and genuinely joyful with them.

The Talmud emphasizes that **"one who gladdens a groom is as if they brought a thanksgiving offering"** (Berakhot 6b) — one of the highest forms of worship.

## What If You Don't Like Them?

What if you're invited to a wedding for someone you're not close to, or even someone you find difficult?

Jewish law is clear: **attend and gladden them anyway**.

**"Love your neighbor as yourself"** (Leviticus 19:18) applies even when it's hard. Your personal feelings shouldn't prevent them from experiencing full communal support.

This is especially true if you're part of their regular community (family, synagogue, workplace). Absence would be noticed and could cause hurt.

However, if attending would cause significant personal hardship or genuine halachic problems, you may decline — but send a gift and warm wishes.

## The Sheva Brachot Week

Hachnassat kallah extends beyond the wedding day:

For the week following the wedding, it's customary to host meals for the couple where the *sheva brachot* (seven blessings) are recited.

Friends and family take turns hosting, ensuring the couple is celebrated and fed for seven days. This:

- Extends the joy
- Relieves the couple from cooking during their first week
- Integrates them into community life as a married couple
- Creates ongoing celebration and support

Hosting or attending sheva brachot meals is considered part of the mitzvah.

## Dignity in Practice

Several principles guide hachnassat kallah:

### 1. Preserve Dignity
Never make couples feel like charity cases. Offer help discreetly, respectfully, as gifts among equals.

### 2. Match Their Vision
Don't impose your taste. If they want a simple wedding, respect that. If they want something grander (within means), support it.

### 3. Avoid Embarrassment
The Talmud forbade displaying wealth gaps at weddings. Today: don't ask intrusive questions about costs, don't compare weddings, don't make couples feel inadequate.

### 4. Focus on the Marriage, Not Just the Wedding
Help them prepare for life together, not just a one-day party. Pre-marital counseling, mentorship, and realistic expectations matter more than decorations.

## When Hachnassat Kallah Means Saying No

Sometimes the greatest chesed is helping couples *not* marry:

If a match is clearly harmful (abuse, incompatibility, deception), helping them separate is chesed.

If a couple is rushing into marriage without preparation, encouraging counseling and delay is chesed.

Hachnassat kallah means wanting the best for the couple — which is a healthy, enduring marriage, not just a wedding.

## The Symbolic Dimension

Why does helping a bride and groom "rebuild the ruins of Jerusalem"?

### Jerusalem Represents Wholeness
The Temple was the center of divine presence. Its destruction represents brokenness; its rebuilding represents restoration.

Every marriage creates a *bayit ne'eman b'Yisrael* — a faithful house in Israel. Each couple becomes a mini-sanctuary where God's presence dwells (**"if husband and wife are worthy, the Shechinah is between them"** — Sotah 17a).

### Creating Sacred Space
Marriages generate families, communities, continuity. They're building blocks of a restored, whole world.

### Redemption Through Joy
Jewish tradition holds that the final redemption will be one of joy. Every wedding prefigures that ultimate celebration.

When you help a couple marry, you're not just organizing logistics — you're participating in cosmic repair.

## The Ultimate Chesed

Why is hachnassat kallah considered among the greatest mitzvot?

**It requires sacrifice** — time, money, effort, sometimes dignity (dancing awkwardly).

**It's fully selfless** — you gain nothing material; the joy is theirs.

**It has lasting impact** — the couple and their descendants benefit forever.

**It combines multiple mitzvot** — chesed, rejoicing with others, building community, honoring God's image.

**It's concrete love** — not abstract goodwill, but tangible, costly help.

The Talmud teaches: **"Greater is one who causes others to do good than one who does good themselves"** (Bava Batra 9a). By helping a couple establish a Jewish home, you enable countless future mitzvot — theirs and their children's.

## Practical Steps

To practice hachnassat kallah today:

1. **Attend weddings you're invited to** — even if inconvenient
2. **Dance and celebrate** — bring genuine joy
3. **Contribute to wedding funds** — support couples in need
4. **Offer your skills** — photography, music, cooking, decorating
5. **Help with matchmaking** — introduce compatible singles thoughtfully
6. **Mentor engaged couples** — share wisdom from your experience
7. **Loan or gift household items** — help newlyweds start strong
8. **Host sheva brachot meals** — extend the celebration
9. **Be present** — put away your phone, engage fully, show you care

## Conclusion: Rebuilding Jerusalem One Wedding at a Time

The sages who danced before brides, lent their jewelry, closed their study halls, and tore their fine silk understood something profound:

Weddings aren't just parties. They're acts of faith in the future. They're statements that despite uncertainty, hardship, and brokenness, we believe in love, commitment, and continuity.

When you help a couple marry — whether through financial support, joyful presence, or simple attendance — you're saying: "Your love matters. Your future matters. You're not alone."

You're rebuilding Jerusalem. One couple at a time. One dance at a time. One blessing at a time.

**"One who gladdens a bride and groom, it is as if they have rebuilt one of the ruins of Jerusalem"** (Berakhot 6b).

So dance. Even if you feel foolish. Especially if you feel foolish.

Give. Even when it's costly. Especially then.

Celebrate. Even when you're tired. Especially when you're tired.

Because somewhere in that wedding — in the music and laughter, the blessings and tears, the hope and commitment — Jerusalem is being rebuilt.

And you get to be part of it.
