# Bal Tashchit: The Torah's Environmental Ethic


## Introduction: A Tree in Wartime

---

Imagine an army laying siege to a city. Food is scarce, soldiers need resources, and the temptation to cut down fruit trees for timber or siege weapons is strong. Yet the Torah commands:

**"When you besiege a city for many days... you shall not destroy its trees by wielding an axe against them. You may eat from them, but you shall not cut them down. Are trees of the field human to withdraw from you into the besieged city? Only trees that you know do not produce food may you destroy and cut down..."** (Deuteronomy 20:19-20)

This seems like an oddly specific law about wartime conduct. But from this single commandment, Jewish tradition developed *bal tashchit* — "do not destroy" — one of the most expansive environmental and ethical principles in Jewish law.

## From Trees to Everything

The Torah's prohibition is explicit: don't cut down fruit trees during warfare, even when you need resources. The Rabbis asked: if this applies during war, when normal rules are suspended and survival is at stake, how much more should it apply during peace?

The Talmud extends bal tashchit far beyond trees:

**"Whoever breaks vessels, tears garments, destroys a building, stops up a spring, or wastes food in a destructive manner violates the prohibition of bal tashchit"** (Kiddushin 32a)

Maimonides (Rambam, 1138-1204) codified this broadly:

**"Not only trees, but anyone who breaks vessels, tears clothing, demolishes a building, stops up a spring, or destroys food violates bal tashchit"** (*Mishneh Torah*, Laws of Kings 6:10)

The principle had grown from a specific wartime prohibition to a comprehensive ethic against wastefulness and unnecessary destruction.

## The Three Dimensions of Bal Tashchit

Jewish legal discussions reveal three interconnected reasons for this prohibition:

### 1. Stewardship, Not Ownership

In Genesis, God places Adam in the Garden of Eden "to work it and to guard it" (*l'ovdah u'l'shomrah*) (Genesis 2:15). The Hebrew verb *shamar* (to guard/protect) suggests humans are caretakers, not absolute owners.

Psalm 24:1 reinforces this: "The earth is the Lord's, and everything in it." We don't own the world; we're tenants with responsibilities.

Rabbi Samson Raphael Hirsch (1808-1888) wrote that bal tashchit teaches us we have "no right to destroy even a blade of grass without sufficient reason."

### 2. Respect for Resources

The Sefer HaChinuch (13th century) explains that bal tashchit trains us "in the path of goodness" by teaching respect for resources:

**"This is the way of the pious and those of good deeds: they love peace and rejoice in the good fortune of people... and do not cause even a grain of mustard to be lost from the world. They are distressed at any loss or destruction they see, and if they can save anything from destruction, they do so with all their might"** (Mitzvah 529)

Wastefulness reflects a character flaw — a failure to appreciate value and an attitude of carelessness toward creation.

### 3. Practical Consequences

Wasting resources harms others who could use them. Destroying productive trees means less food for future generations. Polluting water sources affects entire communities.

The Talmud notes: "The righteous are careful with their property" (Chullin 91a), understanding that waste ultimately hurts people.

## What Counts as Wasteful Destruction?

Jewish legal literature offers nuanced guidelines:

### Clearly Prohibited:
- Burning usable food
- Breaking functional objects out of anger
- Unnecessary demolition of buildings
- Polluting water sources
- Cutting down fruit trees without good reason
- Using more resources than necessary

### Permitted When There's Benefit:
- Cutting a fruit tree if it damages neighboring trees or property
- Destroying something to create something more valuable
- Demolishing a building to build a better one
- Using resources efficiently for genuine needs

The key question: Is there constructive purpose, or is it needless destruction?

### Gray Areas:

**Over-consumption**: Some authorities apply bal tashchit to eating more than needed, linking it to health and waste.

**Planned obsolescence**: Products designed to fail prematurely raise bal tashchit concerns.

**Disposable culture**: Single-use items when reusable alternatives exist may violate the spirit of the law.

## Bal Tashchit in Daily Life

Modern applications of this ancient principle:

### Food Waste
The Talmud is explicit: "One who wastes food is forbidden to derive benefit from it" (Pesachim 52b). In practical terms:

- Take portions you'll actually eat
- Store food properly to prevent spoilage
- Use leftovers creatively
- Compost when possible rather than sending food to landfill

One third of global food production is wasted — a staggering violation of bal tashchit affecting both environment and hungry people.

### Energy and Water
While ancient sources didn't discuss electricity or running water, the principle applies:

- Turn off lights in empty rooms
- Fix leaking faucets (the Talmud specifically mentions stopping up springs)
- Use energy-efficient appliances
- Consider the environmental cost of energy production

### Consumer Goods
The "throwaway culture" conflicts with bal tashchit:

- Repair before replacing
- Choose durable over disposable
- Buy what you need, not excess
- Donate usable items rather than trashing them

Rabbi Moshe Feinstein (1895-1986) ruled that discarding usable items when others could benefit violates bal tashchit.

### Digital Waste
Contemporary scholars debate: Does unnecessary data storage, which requires energy-consuming servers, fall under bal tashchit? Does excessive printing? These questions extend ancient principles to new contexts.

## Environmental Protection: A Torah Value

Many see bal tashchit as Judaism's environmental mandate. Rabbi Saul Berman wrote: "Bal tashchit is not simply a prohibition against waste; it is an affirmative obligation to preserve and protect the world."

This connects to other environmental concepts in Jewish law:

**Tza'ar Ba'alei Chaim** (preventing animal suffering) — animals are part of creation deserving protection

**Protecting Public Health** — pollution that causes illness is prohibited under "do not stand idly by your neighbor's blood" (Leviticus 19:16)

**The Sabbatical Year** (*Shmita*) — every seventh year, agricultural land rests, reminding us the earth needs renewal

**Urban Planning** — Talmudic law required tanneries, which produced noxious fumes, to be located downwind of cities (Baba Batra 25a)

The 20th century saw rabbinic leaders explicitly connect bal tashchit to environmental activism. Rabbi Aryeh Kaplan wrote: "Ecology and conservation are not new concerns... They are basic to the Torah's world view."

## The Limits: When Destruction Is Permitted

Jewish law is pragmatic. Bal tashchit prohibits *unnecessary* destruction:

**Building and development**: If cutting trees enables constructive building, it's permitted (though alternatives should be considered).

**Economic benefit**: Selling a fruit tree for lumber, if the financial benefit outweighs the fruit value, is allowed by most authorities.

**Safety**: Dangerous trees, contaminated resources, or hazardous materials may be destroyed.

**Human needs**: Judaism is not eco-fundamentalism. Human welfare takes priority, but "need" must be genuine, not frivolous.

The Talmud permits cutting down a fruit tree "if its value as wood exceeds its value for fruit" (Baba Kamma 91b-92a). The calculation must be honest and consider long-term impacts.

## Bal Tashchit and Modern Environmentalism

There's significant overlap between bal tashchit and contemporary environmentalism:

**Both oppose wastefulness** — using resources without purpose

**Both value sustainability** — preserving resources for future generations

**Both see interconnection** — harm to environment affects everyone

However, there are differences in emphasis:

- Jewish law centers on *wasteful human action*, not pristine nature for its own sake
- The focus is ethical behavior, not only pragmatic outcomes
- Human needs are central, though not unlimited

Rabbi Jonathan Sacks described this balance: "Care for the environment is a religious duty... but nature exists to serve mankind, and mankind exists to serve God."

## A Spiritual Practice

Beyond legal obligation, bal tashchit cultivates spiritual awareness:

**Gratitude**: Recognizing every resource as a gift from God

**Mindfulness**: Considering the impact of our actions

**Discipline**: Controlling impulses toward excess and waste

**Humility**: Acknowledging our place as caretakers, not masters

The Baal Shem Tov (1698-1760) taught that every created thing contains a divine spark. Wasting or destroying needlessly is a spiritual loss, not just a material one.

## Conclusion: Trees Are Not Human

The Torah's original question echoes through millennia: "Are trees of the field human to withdraw from you into the besieged city?" 

The obvious answer is no — trees are not human. But the verse implies a deeper truth: trees are not *merely* resources to exploit. They have value, purpose, and a right to exist. Even in war, even when we have pressing needs, we must restrain ourselves from needless destruction.

Bal tashchit teaches that everything has value, waste is wrong, and how we treat the world reflects who we are. In an era of climate crisis, resource depletion, and staggering waste, this 3,000-year-old principle offers not just prohibition but vision: a world where we consume thoughtfully, create sustainably, and pass on a thriving planet to future generations.

As the Talmud teaches: "All that the Holy One created in His world, He created only for His glory" (Pirkei Avot 6:11). Bal tashchit reminds us that respecting creation is respecting the Creator — and protecting the future is honoring the past.
