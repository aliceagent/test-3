## Introduction

What if giving to others wasn't just a nice thing to do, but an actual obligation — a fundamental requirement of being human? In Judaism, the concept of *tzedakah* (צדקה) transforms charitable giving from an optional act of kindness into a non-negotiable duty. The word itself reveals this truth: *tzedakah* comes from the Hebrew root צ-ד-ק (*tzedek*), meaning "justice" or "righteousness." When you give tzedakah, you're not being generous or magnanimous — you're simply doing what's right.

This idea might sound foreign to modern ears, where charity is often framed as voluntary benevolence. But in Jewish thought, which developed over millennia — beginning in the time of the Patriarchs nearly four thousand years ago (corresponding to China's Shang Dynasty, 商朝, ~1600-1046 BCE) — tzedakah has always been understood as a fundamental pillar of a just society. It's not your money to give away; it's a redistribution of resources that were never entirely yours to begin with.

For someone new to Judaism, especially coming from a cultural background that values harmony, social responsibility, and mutual support (concepts deeply embedded in Confucian and Daoist philosophy), tzedakah offers a powerful framework for understanding our obligations to one another. It's about creating balance, ensuring dignity, and recognizing that wealth flows through us, not to us.

## What Tzedakah Really Means

### Justice, Not Charity

The first thing to understand about tzedakah is that it's fundamentally different from the English word "charity." Charity implies a voluntary gift from someone who has excess to someone who lacks. Tzedakah, by contrast, is an obligation of justice. The poor person isn't receiving your generosity — they're receiving what is rightfully theirs.

The Talmud (compiled during the period corresponding to China's Three Kingdoms through Jin Dynasty, 三国至晋朝, ~200-500 CE) teaches: "Give him his, for both you and yours are His" (Pirkei Avot 3:7). In other words, the resources in your possession belong ultimately to God, and you are merely the steward. When you give tzedakah, you're distributing God's resources according to His instructions, which mandate support for those in need.

This is similar to traditional Chinese concepts of social harmony and the ideal ruler's responsibility to ensure the people's welfare — but Judaism extends this obligation to every individual, not just to rulers or the wealthy elite.

### The Biblical Foundation

The Torah establishes tzedakah as a commandment, not a suggestion. Deuteronomy 15:7-8 states clearly: "If there is among you a poor person... you shall not harden your heart nor shut your hand from your poor brother. You shall surely open your hand to him."

The language is unambiguous: "you shall" (*lo tikpotz*, לא תקפץ) — this is a command. The double verb "you shall surely open" (*patoach tiftach*, פתוח תפתח) emphasizes the obligation's strength. You must give, and you must give generously.

Moreover, the Torah commands that fields should not be harvested completely to the edges, and farmers should not gather fallen grain or forgotten sheaves. These must be left for the poor to collect (Leviticus 19:9-10, 23:22). This system, which existed during the period corresponding to the Spring and Autumn period in China (春秋, ~770-476 BCE), built dignity into the giving process — the poor could "earn" their sustenance through the labor of gathering, rather than receiving handouts.

### More Than Money

While tzedakah is often translated as "charity" and commonly understood as monetary giving, it encompasses much more. The Talmud discusses various forms of tzedakah:

- **Financial support**: Direct monetary assistance to those in need
- **Food and clothing**: Providing basic necessities
- **Hospitality**: Welcoming guests and strangers (*hachnasat orchim*)
- **Visiting the sick**: Offering comfort and practical help (*bikur cholim*)
- **Comforting mourners**: Supporting those in grief (*nichum aveilim*)
- **Helping brides and grooms**: Contributing to weddings for those who cannot afford them
- **Ransoming captives**: Historically, freeing those held for ransom (considered the highest priority)

All of these acts fall under the umbrella of tzedakah because they address human need and restore dignity and balance to the community.

## How Much Should You Give?

Jewish law is remarkably specific about tzedakah obligations. The baseline is simple: **every Jew is obligated to give tzedakah**, even a poor person who receives tzedakah themselves. This underscores that tzedakah isn't about wealth; it's about participation in a just system.

### The Standard Measures

The general guideline, established in the Talmud, is that one should give **between one-tenth (10%) and one-fifth (20%) of one's income** to tzedakah. This practice is called *ma'aser kesafim* (מעשר כספים), literally "tithing money."

- **Minimum**: 10% is considered the baseline standard for most people.
- **Ideal**: 20% is regarded as the measure of the generous (*middat chassidut*).
- **Maximum**: One should generally not give more than 20%, to avoid impoverishing oneself and becoming dependent on others.

There are exceptions to the maximum: very wealthy individuals whose giving will not impact their lifestyle, or people in special circumstances, may give more. But the 20% cap exists to ensure that tzedakah doesn't create new cases of need.

This system bears some resemblance to traditional Chinese practices of grain storage and redistribution during times of famine, and to Confucian ideals of social responsibility — but it's formalized as religious law binding on every individual.

### Priorities in Giving

Not all tzedakah is equal. Jewish law establishes a hierarchy of priorities to guide your giving:

1. **Your own family first**: Parents, children, siblings in need take precedence.
2. **Your city's poor**: Those in your immediate community.
3. **Your country's poor**: Fellow citizens and residents.
4. **Jews in other lands**: The broader Jewish community.
5. **Non-Jews in your city**: General humanitarian needs.

The principle is simple: responsibility radiates outward from those closest to you. You can't neglect your own children to help strangers across the world. However, once your immediate obligations are met, the circle expands.

Additionally, certain types of need take precedence:
- **Life-saving needs**: Ransoming captives, medical emergencies.
- **Torah education**: Supporting schools and teachers.
- **Marriage support**: Helping poor brides and grooms establish households.
- **Dignity preservation**: Helping formerly wealthy people maintain a lifestyle close to what they knew, to minimize their suffering.

## The Eight Levels of Tzedakah

The great Jewish philosopher and legal codifier Moses Maimonides (the Rambam, 1138-1204 CE, during China's Song Dynasty, 宋朝) outlined eight levels of tzedakah, ranked from lowest to highest. This framework helps us aspire to higher forms of giving:

### 8. Giving Begrudgingly
Giving with a frown, reluctantly, or making the recipient feel uncomfortable. The mitzvah is fulfilled, but poorly.

### 7. Giving Less Than Appropriate, But Cheerfully
Giving inadequately but with a good attitude. Better to give with joy than to give resentfully, but the amount still matters.

### 6. Giving After Being Asked
Waiting to be solicited rather than seeking out opportunities to help. Reactive rather than proactive.

### 5. Giving Before Being Asked
Anticipating need and offering help before the person has to ask. This preserves the recipient's dignity.

### 4. The Recipient Knows the Donor, But Not Vice Versa
The giver is anonymous to the recipient, sparing them embarrassment. Historical example: dropping coins into the poor person's hand while looking away.

### 3. The Donor Knows the Recipient, But Not Vice Versa
Giving anonymously so the recipient doesn't know who helped them. Example: secret donations through a third party.

### 2. Both Are Anonymous
Neither party knows the other. This was facilitated in the Temple period through special collection chambers where donors and recipients never met. Pure, impersonal justice.

### 1. The Highest Level: Enabling Self-Sufficiency
Giving in a way that helps the person become independent: a loan, a partnership, a job, or business training. This is the ultimate form of tzedakah because it eliminates future need. As the proverb says: "Give someone a fish, feed them for a day; teach them to fish, feed them for life."

This highest level resonates deeply with Chinese philosophical emphasis on self-cultivation and the Confucian value of enabling people to fulfill their potential and contribute to society.

## Practical Applications Today

### Setting Up Your Tzedakah Practice

If you're ready to begin giving tzedakah systematically:

1. **Calculate your income**: Determine your net income (after basic taxes and living expenses).
2. **Set your percentage**: Start with 10% if you're new to this; aim for 20% if you can afford it.
3. **Automate it**: Set up monthly automatic transfers to a dedicated tzedakah account or directly to organizations.
4. **Keep a pushke**: A *pushke* (פושקע) is a tzedakah collection box kept at home. Drop coins or bills into it regularly, and distribute the contents periodically.
5. **Give before Shabbat and holidays**: It's customary to give tzedakah before candle-lighting on Friday evening and before major Jewish holidays.
6. **Involve your children**: Let children participate in giving, teaching them the mitzvah from a young age.

### Who Should You Support?

Consider directing your tzedakah toward:
- **Local Jewish organizations**: Your synagogue, Jewish schools, community support services.
- **Torah study institutions**: Yeshivas and programs that educate the next generation.
- **Israel**: Organizations supporting the poor, security needs, or immigrant absorption.
- **Medical and rescue services**: Organizations like Hatzalah or hospitals.
- **Worthy non-Jewish causes**: General humanitarian efforts, disaster relief, medical research (within the priority framework).

### Tzedakah in the Digital Age

Modern technology makes giving easier than ever:
- Online donation platforms allow instant, anonymous giving.
- Automatic monthly donations ensure consistency.
- Crowdfunding enables targeted support for individuals in crisis.

However, verify that organizations are legitimate. In Jewish law, you bear responsibility for ensuring your tzedakah actually reaches those in need.

### When You're the One in Need

If you find yourself needing assistance, remember: **accepting tzedakah is not shameful**. Jewish law recognizes that circumstances change. The same person who gives tzedakah one year might need to receive it the next. There is no stigma in accepting help — it's part of the system of justice that ensures everyone's dignity.

That said, Jewish sources also emphasize the value of accepting less than you might need, if possible, to preserve resources for others. The goal is balance: take what you need, but not more.

## The Spiritual Dimension

### Tzedakah Saves from Death

The Talmud teaches that "tzedakah saves from death" (*Proverbs 10:2, 11:4*). This is understood in several ways:
- **Literally**: Your tzedakah might save someone's life.
- **Spiritually**: Acts of righteousness protect you in times of divine judgment.
- **Communally**: A society that practices tzedakah creates a safety net that protects everyone.

### Tzedakah and Prayer

During the High Holy Days (Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur), Jews recite a liturgical formula: "Repentance, prayer, and tzedakah avert the severe decree." Tzedakah is grouped with prayer and repentance as one of the three pillars that can change your fate for the coming year.

This doesn't mean you can "buy" divine favor — God doesn't need your money. Rather, the act of giving demonstrates that you understand your place in the world, that you recognize your blessings, and that you're committed to justice. That transformation of character is what matters spiritually.

### Tzedakah Connects You to God

Jewish mysticism teaches that when you give tzedakah, you become a partner with God in sustaining the world. God could provide for everyone directly, but He chooses to work through human hands. By giving, you participate in the divine work of creation and maintenance.

This is similar to Daoist concepts of aligning oneself with the Tao (道) and the natural flow of the universe — but in Judaism, that flow is directed by divine will toward justice and compassion.

## Cultural Connections: Jewish and Chinese Values

Both Jewish and Chinese cultures place enormous emphasis on social harmony, mutual responsibility, and the obligations of the prosperous toward the less fortunate. Some parallels:

- **Confucian *ren* (仁, benevolence)**: Like tzedakah, *ren* emphasizes that virtue is expressed through action toward others, not just internal feeling.
- **The Mandate of Heaven**: Just as Chinese emperors could lose the Mandate if they failed to care for the people, Jews who neglect tzedakah violate their covenant with God.
- **Communal responsibility**: Both traditions reject radical individualism in favor of recognizing our interdependence.
- **Dignity in giving**: Chinese face culture (面子, *miànzi*) and Jewish emphasis on anonymous giving both seek to preserve the recipient's honor.

The key difference is that Judaism formalizes these values as legal obligations, with specific percentages and priorities, rather than leaving them as general moral principles.

## Common Questions

**Q: What if I can't afford 10%?**  
A: Give what you can. Even the poorest person should give something, even if it's a single coin. The amount matters less than the act of participation.

**Q: Can I give to non-Jewish causes?**  
A: Yes, especially for general humanitarian needs. However, Jewish law prioritizes Jewish recipients when both are equally needy.

**Q: Is it tzedakah if I give to family members?**  
A: Yes, supporting family in need is not only tzedakah but actually the highest priority. However, ordinary parental support of children doesn't count as tzedakah — that's a separate obligation.

**Q: What if the beggar might use the money for alcohol or drugs?**  
A: This is a debated question. Some authorities say you must still give (the obligation is on you, not on how they use it). Others say you can give to organizations instead of individuals to ensure responsible use. A middle path: give something small to the individual, and direct larger amounts to vetted organizations.

**Q: Does volunteering time count as tzedakah?**  
A: Time and effort are valuable forms of tzedakah, especially for causes that need manpower more than money. However, monetary tzedakah obligations aren't fully met by volunteering alone.

## Key Takeaways

- **Tzedakah is justice**, not optional charity. It's a commandment binding on every Jew.
- **Give 10-20% of your income** as a baseline, with 10% being the minimum and 20% the ideal.
- **Prioritize family and community**, then expand outward. Not all needs are equal.
- **Aspire to higher levels**: Anonymous giving and helping people become self-sufficient are the highest forms.
- **Give regularly and joyfully**: Consistency and attitude matter as much as amount.
- **It's a partnership with God**: Through tzedakah, you participate in sustaining the world.
- **Even recipients give**: Everyone participates in the system, preserving universal dignity.

Tzedakah transforms society from a collection of individuals into a true community (*kehillah*). It reminds us that wealth is a trust, not a possession, and that justice requires action, not just good intentions.
