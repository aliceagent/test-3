# Derech Eretz: Good Manners Come Before Torah

## Why the Rabbis Insisted Proper Conduct Is a Prerequisite for Torah Study—and Parallels with Chinese Li (礼)


---

Beyond the gate, sacred scrolls and books are visible on a study table beneath ancient pine trees. The scholar's posture conveys deep humility and proper conduct before the pursuit of wisdom. In one of Judaism's most striking teachings, the ancient rabbis declared: *"Derech eretz kadma laTorah"*—"Proper behavior preceded the Torah" (Vayikra Rabbah 9:3). The statement appears paradoxical. If Torah is divine revelation, the foundation of Jewish life, how could anything come before it? Yet this principle—that basic decent behavior, proper conduct, and good manners are prerequisites for meaningful Torah study—runs through Jewish tradition. It suggests something profound: religious learning divorced from ethical behavior and respectful conduct is not just incomplete; it's fundamentally corrupted.

For Chinese readers, this concept will likely resonate deeply. Confucius taught that ritual propriety (*li* 礼) and proper conduct must be cultivated before one can truly understand deeper wisdom. Mencius emphasized that without basic human feelings and moral cultivation, intellectual learning becomes dangerous. The parallel between Jewish *derech eretz* and Confucian *li* offers a bridge for understanding how two ancient civilizations, separated by geography but united in wisdom, reached remarkably similar insights about the relationship between behavior and learning, manners and meaning.

## What Is Derech Eretz?

The Hebrew term *derech eretz* (דרך ארץ) literally means "the way of the land" or "the way of the world." But its meaning extends far beyond this literal translation. Derech eretz encompasses:

### Basic Courtesy and Manners

- Greeting people respectfully
- Speaking politely, not interrupting
- Respecting elders
- Showing gratitude
- Not causing embarrassment to others
- Appropriate dress for occasions
- Table manners

### Ethical Conduct

- Honesty in business
- Keeping promises
- Not stealing or cheating
- Respecting others' property
- Being reliable
- Taking responsibility for your actions

### Social Responsibility

- Earning a living, not being a burden
- Contributing to community
- Maintaining hygiene and health
- Being a good neighbor
- Following societal laws (unless they contradict Torah)

### Interpersonal Sensitivity

- Reading social situations appropriately
- Understanding context
- Knowing when to speak and when to be silent
- Respecting boundaries
- Showing empathy

### Self-Respect and Dignity

- Maintaining personal dignity
- Dressing appropriately
- Conducting yourself with self-respect
- Managing your affairs responsibly

In essence, derech eretz is the foundation of civilized behavior—what makes it possible for people to live together in society. It's the common sense decency that transcends specific religious law, the basic humanity that should characterize any person, religious or not.

## The Rabbinic Principle: Derech Eretz Before Torah

The statement "derech eretz kadma laTorah" appears in multiple forms across rabbinic literature:

### Midrashic Teaching

The Midrash (Vayikra Rabbah 9:3) notes that in the Torah's account of creation, humanity was given basic instructions—work the land, don't eat from one tree—before any ritual or ceremonial commandments were given. God first taught humans how to live civilized lives, and only later (after the Exodus, at Sinai) gave the Torah with its detailed religious laws.

This chronology is pedagogically significant: basic decent behavior must come first; religious refinement comes after.

### Pirkei Avot (Ethics of the Fathers)

The Mishnaic tractate Pirkei Avot, which focuses on ethical wisdom, contains numerous teachings emphasizing the priority of character over learning:

"An excellent thing is the study of Torah combined with some worldly occupation, for the exertion demanded by both of them causes sinful inclinations to be forgotten. All study of Torah without work will ultimately fail and lead to sin." (2:2)

"Anyone whose fear of sin precedes their wisdom—their wisdom will endure. But anyone whose wisdom precedes their fear of sin—their wisdom will not endure." (3:9)

"Anyone whose deeds exceed their wisdom—their wisdom will endure. But anyone whose wisdom exceeds their deeds—their wisdom will not endure." (3:9)

### Talmudic Discussions

Throughout the Talmud, the rabbis emphasize that Torah knowledge without proper character is not just inadequate—it's potentially dangerous:

"A person should always first learn derech eretz and then study Torah." (Tanna Devei Eliyahu Rabbah 1)

The famous sage Rabbi Yochanan said: "One who studies Torah but does not act with derech eretz is comparable to someone who has no God." (Berachot 47b, according to some interpretations)

The logic is clear: if someone knows God's law but treats people poorly, their knowledge hasn't connected them to God's values—they've missed the entire point.

### The Story of Hillel

Perhaps the most famous illustration comes from the story of Hillel and the potential convert:

A gentile came to the great sage Hillel saying he wanted to convert to Judaism but only if Hillel could teach him the entire Torah "while standing on one foot" (i.e., in a brief summary). Rather than dismissing this seemingly disrespectful request, Hillel responded:

"What is hateful to you, do not do to your fellow. That is the entire Torah; the rest is commentary. Now go and study." (Shabbat 31a)

Notice what Hillel identified as the essence: the ethical principle of not harming others. The ritual laws—important as they are—are "commentary" on this fundamental ethical core. Start with basic decency; build from there.

## Why Derech Eretz Must Come First

Several reasons explain this priority:

### 1. Torah Study Requires Humility

You cannot genuinely learn if you're arrogant, dismissive, or feel superior to others. Torah study requires:
- Listening to teachers (respect)
- Accepting correction (humility)
- Engaging in debate with peers (mutual respect)
- Acknowledging you don't know everything (intellectual humility)

Someone who lacks basic derech eretz—who interrupts, dismisses others, or thinks they already know everything—cannot truly learn. Their poor character blocks knowledge from entering.

The Talmud states: "Let the honor of your student be as dear to you as your own, the honor of your colleague as the reverence for your teacher, and the reverence for your teacher as the reverence of Heaven" (Pirkei Avot 4:12). This describes an attitude of consistent respect—impossible without derech eretz.

### 2. Knowledge Without Character Is Dangerous

A person with Torah knowledge but poor character is worse than an ignorant person with poor character. Why? Because they use their knowledge to:
- Justify their bad behavior
- Manipulate others with citations and arguments
- Feel righteous while acting unethically
- Create chilul Hashem (desecration of God's name)—making Judaism look bad

The rabbis understood that learning can inflate ego rather than deflate it if not accompanied by character development. Someone who knows Talmud but lies in business is worse than someone who knows no Talmud and lies in business—the former should know better and brings shame on Torah itself.

### 3. The Purpose of Torah Is Ethical Transformation

Torah study isn't primarily about accumulating information. It's about transformation—becoming a better, more ethical, more holy person. The Talmud asks: "What is the purpose of wisdom? Repentance and good deeds" (Berachot 17a).

If your Torah study doesn't make you kinder, more honest, more humble, and more ethical, you're doing it wrong. The content is correct, but you're not engaging with it properly. This is only possible if you begin with basic derech eretz—the foundation on which Torah builds.

### 4. Modeling for Others

Torah scholars are public figures, models for communities. If they display poor character—arrogance, cruelty, dishonesty, lack of empathy—they drive people away from Torah.

The concept of *chilul Hashem* (desecrating God's name) often refers to religious people behaving badly. When someone who studies Torah and wears religious garb acts unethically, observers think: "If that's what Torah produces, I want nothing to do with it." The scholar has not only failed personally; they've damaged Torah's reputation.

Conversely, someone who embodies derech eretz—kind, honest, humble, reliable—creates *kiddush Hashem* (sanctification of God's name), making others respect Torah and Judaism.

### 5. Social Cohesion Precedes Religious Refinement

On a practical level, communities need basic functional relationships before they can engage in sophisticated religious practice. If people don't treat each other with basic respect, you can't have:
- Productive Torah study groups
- Meaningful prayer communities
- Charitable organizations
- Dispute resolution
- Lifecycle celebrations
- Any sustained communal life

Derech eretz is the social glue that makes community possible. Without it, religious life fractures into factions, conflicts, and dysfunction. Torah law is sophisticated, but it assumes a foundation of people who know how to behave decently toward each other.

## Examples from Jewish Sources

### Ben Azai's Teaching

The Talmudic sage Ben Azai asked, "What is the great principle of Torah?" His answer: "This is the book of the generations of Adam... when God created man, He made him in the likeness of God" (Genesis 5:1).

Before anything about Jews, commandments, or religious practices—the fundamental principle is that every human is created in the divine image. This universal human dignity is the foundation. Everything else builds on this.

### The Kohen Gadol (High Priest)

Even the holiest role in ancient Judaism—the Kohen Gadol who entered the Holy of Holies on Yom Kippur—had a character requirement. If a candidate was brilliant in Torah knowledge but lacked proper character (*middot*), he was disqualified. Holiness requires ethics; knowledge without character is insufficient.

### The Story of Kamtza and Bar Kamtza

One of the Talmud's most famous stories illustrates the catastrophic consequences of lacking derech eretz:

A man made a party and meant to invite his friend Kamtza, but through an error, the invitation went to his enemy Bar Kamtza. When Bar Kamtza arrived, the host humiliated him publicly, demanding he leave. Bar Kamtza offered to pay for his meal, then for half the party, then for the entire party—anything to avoid public humiliation. The host refused and threw him out.

Rabbis were present at the party and said nothing, failing to defend Bar Kamtza or criticize the host. Their silence—a failure of derech eretz—was catastrophic. The humiliated Bar Kamtza went to the Roman authorities and slandered the Jews, eventually leading (according to the Talmud) to the destruction of the Temple.

The message: a single act of public humiliation and the failure to defend someone being mistreated had cosmic consequences. Derech eretz isn't a minor nicety; it's fundamental to communal survival.

## Derech Eretz in Daily Life

How does this principle manifest practically?

### In Learning Settings

- Torah study sessions should model respect—students listening to teachers, teachers honoring students' questions, all participants engaging civilly even in disagreement
- No showing off knowledge to humiliate others
- Making space for quieter voices
- Acknowledging when you don't know something

### In Synagogue

- Appropriate dress and behavior during services
- Not talking during prayers (a common issue!)
- Respecting others' prayer experience
- Helping newcomers feel welcome
- Greeting everyone, not just your friends

### In Business

- Honesty in all transactions
- Paying workers on time
- Not taking advantage of customers' ignorance
- Following secular law (assuming it doesn't contradict Torah)
- Being a reliable business partner

### In Family Life

- Treating spouse with respect and kindness
- Teaching children proper behavior before teaching them religious rituals
- Respecting parents, even when you disagree
- Managing household affairs responsibly

### In Community

- Being a good neighbor—quiet at night, maintaining property, helping when needed
- Contributing to community needs
- Respecting public spaces
- Participating in solving communal problems

### Online and Digital Spaces

In our modern context, derech eretz extends to:
- Not engaging in online shaming or bullying
- Respecting others' privacy
- Being truthful, not spreading rumors
- Disagreeing respectfully, not viciously
- Recognizing that screens mediate human beings who deserve dignity

## Parallels with Confucian Li (礼)

Chinese readers will recognize striking parallels between derech eretz and the Confucian concept of *li* (礼):

### Proper Conduct as Foundation

Confucius taught that *li*—ritual propriety, proper conduct, manners—must be cultivated as the foundation of moral development. Before studying the classics or pursuing wisdom, one must learn basic proper behavior. This exactly parallels the Jewish principle that derech eretz comes before Torah.

### Ritual and Ethics Intertwined

In both traditions, ritual/religious practice and ethical conduct are inseparable. Performing rituals perfectly while treating people poorly is a fundamental failure. Confucius criticized those who focused on ritual correctness while lacking *ren* (仁, humaneness/benevolence). The Talmud criticizes Torah scholars who lack derech eretz. Both traditions insist that religious knowledge without ethical behavior is worthless or worse.

### Social Harmony

Both derech eretz and *li* aim at creating social harmony. They're the behaviors that make it possible for people to live together peacefully, respectfully, and productively. Without this foundation, communities fragment and conflict escalates.

### Respect for Hierarchy and Relationships

Both traditions emphasize proper conduct within relationships—parent-child, teacher-student, ruler-subject (or community leader-member), friend-friend. The behaviors appropriate to each relationship aren't identical across cultures, but the principle that relationships have proper forms of conduct is shared.

### Character Before Knowledge

Both traditions emphasize developing good character as more fundamental than accumulating knowledge. A person of good character with limited knowledge is preferable to a learned person of poor character.

### Cultivation Through Practice

Neither tradition sees proper behavior as merely following rules. Both emphasize cultivation—repeatedly practicing proper conduct until it becomes natural, part of your character. The Hebrew term *middot* (character traits) and the Confucian emphasis on moral cultivation through practice reflect similar understanding: character is formed through repeated action, not just knowledge.

### Modeling by Leaders

Both traditions emphasize that leaders—whether Torah scholars or Confucian junzi (君子, exemplary persons)—must model proper conduct. Their behavior shapes others, and their failures have outsized impact.

## Potential Tensions and Challenges

While beautiful in theory, applying this principle raises questions:

### Who Defines Derech Eretz?

Cultural standards vary. What's considered polite in one culture may be neutral or even rude in another. How much of derech eretz is universal, and how much is culturally specific?

Jewish tradition navigates this by distinguishing between universal ethical principles (honesty, not harming others) and culturally specific behaviors (particular greetings, dress codes), while recognizing that respectful engagement with local customs is itself derech eretz when not contradicting Torah values.

### Can't Bad People Study Torah?

If derech eretz is prerequisite, does that mean imperfect people shouldn't study? Of course not—everyone has flaws. The point is that study should improve character, and if it's not doing so, something's wrong with how you're studying.

The requirement is aspiration and effort, not perfection. Everyone begins imperfect; the question is whether you're trying to improve.

### Conflict Between Derech Eretz and Torah?

Rare situations arise where what society considers proper behavior conflicts with Torah law. In such cases, Torah takes precedence. But these are exceptional; in the vast majority of cases, Torah and proper social behavior align.

### Does Emphasizing Derech Eretz Minimize Torah's Uniqueness?

If basic decent behavior is what matters most, why have Torah at all? But this misunderstands the teaching. Derech eretz is the foundation, not the entire building. Torah builds a magnificent structure on that foundation—specifying divine will, creating holy community, enabling spiritual elevation far beyond basic ethics.

The point is: you need the foundation before building; you can't skip to the roof. But the roof is still essential.

## Contemporary Applications

In our time, this principle has special relevance:

### Online Discourse

Social media and online forums often bring out the worst in people—mockery, cruelty, quick judgments. The derech eretz principle says: even in digital spaces, even when anonymous, even when you disagree strongly—basic decency applies. Before engaging in online Torah discussions (or any discussions), cultivate derech eretz.

### Political Divisions

Jews, like everyone, are politically divided. Derech eretz means you can strongly disagree with someone's politics while treating them with basic respect. You don't demonize, you don't assume the worst, you don't engage in character assassination. Disagreement can coexist with decency.

### Religious Pluralism

Within the Jewish community, there are vast differences in observance and interpretation. Derech eretz means ultra-Orthodox Jews treat Reform Jews with basic respect, and vice versa. You can believe the other is wrong while still according them dignity.

### Professional Life

In workplaces increasingly diverse in religion, ethnicity, and background, derech eretz translates to professional ethics—honesty, reliability, respect for colleagues, fair dealing, maintaining boundaries. These aren't just secular values; they're Torah prerequisites.

## Conclusion: Building from the Ground Up

The teaching that derech eretz precedes Torah is humbling and liberating. Humbling because it says no amount of religious knowledge excuses poor character. Liberating because it means the foundation of spiritual life is accessible to everyone—you don't need years of study to begin; you need to treat people decently.

This principle keeps religion grounded. It prevents the pathology of sacred knowledge divorced from sacred behavior. It insists that the divine cares about how you treat the cashier, your neighbor, your employees, the person who disagrees with you—not just about your ritual precision or theological sophistication.

For Jews, it's a call to embody Torah's values in every interaction. For anyone, regardless of religious tradition, it's a reminder that wisdom traditions across cultures have recognized a fundamental truth: cultivating good character and proper conduct isn't preliminary to the real spiritual work. It *is* the real spiritual work. Everything else builds on this foundation or, lacking it, collapses into hypocrisy.

The Torah itself, after all, can be summarized (as Hillel did) in a single principle rooted in derech eretz: What's hateful to you, don't do to others. Master that, and you've grasped the essence. The rest—all the depth and beauty of Torah—is commentary, elaboration, and application of that fundamental insight.

Start there. Build from there. And remember: good manners aren't superficial niceties. They're the foundation of holiness, the prerequisite of wisdom, the beginning of bringing the divine into the world through how we treat each other in the most ordinary moments of daily life.
