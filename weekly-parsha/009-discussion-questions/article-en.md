## Introduction: Unlocking the Weekly Torah Portion

Every week, Jewish communities around the world read a new portion of the Torah. The cycle never stops: from Genesis to Deuteronomy and back again, year after year. But here's the challenge many families face: how do you turn this ancient text into something alive and meaningful at your Shabbat table?

You've read the summary. You know the basic story. But when you sit down for Friday night dinner, the conversation drifts to work stress, weekend plans, or — more often — awkward silence. The Torah portion you read about Moses or Abraham feels distant, disconnected from your life in 21st-century Beijing or Shanghai.

**What if you had a simple framework** — five questions you could ask about any Torah portion that would transform your Shabbat table into a place of discovery, debate, and deeper connection?

This article gives you exactly that: a reusable template of five discussion questions that work for every single parsha. Whether you're a complete beginner or you've been studying Torah for years, these questions will help you and your family extract meaning, spark conversation, and make the weekly portion personally relevant.

---

## Why Questions Matter in Jewish Learning

Before we dive into the five-question framework, it's worth understanding why Judaism places such emphasis on asking questions.

### The Tradition of Asking

In Jewish tradition, asking questions isn't a sign of doubt — it's a sign of engagement. The Talmud is structured almost entirely as questions and answers. The Passover Seder begins with a child asking the Four Questions. Torah study traditionally happens in pairs (chavruta), where partners challenge each other with questions.

The medieval commentator Rashi (11th century CE — during China's Song Dynasty 宋朝) famously began his commentary on the very first verse of Genesis with a question: "Why does the Torah begin with the story of Creation rather than with the first commandment given to the Jewish people?" This question set the tone for centuries of Jewish learning: start with curiosity.

### Questions Create Ownership

When you ask a question about a Torah text, you're no longer a passive listener — you become an active participant. You're wrestling with the same words that your ancestors have wrestled with for thousands of years. You're joining a conversation that spans continents and centuries.

### Questions Build Critical Thinking

Judaism encourages you to think deeply, question assumptions, and seek understanding. Even children are taught to ask "Why?" The goal isn't blind acceptance, but informed conviction. This approach mirrors classical Chinese educational methods, where students of Confucius were encouraged to question their teacher and refine their understanding through dialogue.

## The Five-Question Framework

Here is your template. These five questions can be applied to **any Torah portion**, from Bereishit to V'Zot HaBerachah. Each question approaches the text from a different angle, ensuring rich and varied discussion.

### Question 1: **What Happens in This Parsha?**

**Purpose:** Establish the basic facts and narrative.

Before diving into deep analysis, make sure everyone at the table knows the story. This question is especially important if you have young children, guests who are new to Judaism, or family members who didn't have time to read the parsha beforehand.

**How to use it:**
- Go around the table and let each person contribute one event or detail from the parsha
- Keep it simple: "Abraham left his homeland." "The Israelites crossed the Red Sea." "Moses went up Mount Sinai."
- No interpretation yet — just facts

**Why it works:**
This question levels the playing field. Even someone who only read a one-paragraph summary can participate. It also ensures that everyone is on the same page before moving to deeper questions.

**Example (Parshat Lech Lecha):**
"God tells Abraham to leave his land. Abraham travels to Canaan. God promises Abraham that his descendants will be numerous. Sarah and Abraham go to Egypt during a famine. Abraham and Lot separate. God makes a covenant with Abraham."

---

### Question 2: **What Surprises You or Seems Strange?**

**Purpose:** Identify curiosities, contradictions, or unusual details that invite deeper exploration.

This is where the real learning begins. Torah is full of strange moments: repetitions, odd word choices, characters who behave unexpectedly, laws that seem harsh or unclear. Noticing these oddities is the first step toward insight.

**How to use it:**
- Encourage everyone to share something that struck them as unusual
- Don't worry if you don't have answers yet — the question itself is valuable
- Children are often excellent at this because they haven't learned to gloss over strange details

**Why it works:**
Classical Jewish commentators built their entire methodology on asking "Why is this word repeated?" or "Why did the character act this way?" When you ask what seems strange, you're thinking like Rashi, Ibn Ezra, and the Talmudic sages.

**Example (Parshat Lech Lecha):**
- "Why does God tell Abraham to 'Go to a land that I will show you' instead of just telling him where to go?"
- "Why does Sarah laugh when she hears she'll have a child, but Abraham doesn't?"
- "God promises Abraham the land, but Abraham doesn't actually settle there permanently. Why?"

**Historical Note:**
This method of questioning texts was also central to Chinese scholarly tradition. During the Han Dynasty (汉朝, 206 BCE - 220 CE — roughly contemporary with the Second Temple period in Jewish history), Confucian scholars developed meticulous commentaries on the Five Classics, asking similar questions: Why this word? Why this order? What seems contradictory?

---

### Question 3: **What Does This Teach About God, Humanity, or the World?**

**Purpose:** Extract theological and philosophical lessons.

Torah isn't just history — it's a book of ideas. Every story contains layers of meaning about the nature of God, the purpose of human life, and how the world works.

**How to use it:**
- Look for what the parsha reveals about:
  - **God's attributes:** Is God merciful here? Just? Patient? Demanding?
  - **Human nature:** What does this story teach about temptation, courage, jealousy, faith?
  - **Moral principles:** What values are being upheld or violated?

**Why it works:**
This question transforms narrative into wisdom. It moves the conversation from "What happened?" to "What does it mean?"

**Example (Parshat Bereishit):**
- "God creates the world with words ('Let there be light'). This teaches that language and speech have creative power."
- "God gives Adam the task of naming the animals. This shows that humans are meant to be active partners in creation, not passive observers."
- "God rests on the seventh day. This teaches that rest is sacred, not laziness — it's built into the fabric of existence."

**Example (Parshat Noach):**
- "God saves Noah and his family, but destroys the world. This teaches that while God is patient, there are limits to tolerance of evil."
- "Noah builds an ark over many years while people mock him. This teaches that doing the right thing often requires enduring ridicule."

---

### Question 4: **How Does This Apply to My Life Today?**

**Purpose:** Make the Torah personally relevant.

This is the bridge between ancient text and modern life. Judaism isn't a museum religion — it's meant to be lived. Every Torah portion contains guidance for how to navigate contemporary challenges.

**How to use it:**
- Think about:
  - **Personal challenges:** Does this parsha speak to something you're struggling with?
  - **Ethical dilemmas:** Does it offer guidance for a moral choice you're facing?
  - **Character development:** What trait or behavior can you work on based on this story?
- Encourage honesty and vulnerability — this isn't about giving the "right" answer

**Why it works:**
When Torah becomes personal, it stops being abstract. You're no longer studying ancient history; you're receiving guidance for your life right now.

**Example (Parshat Vayeira — Abraham argues with God to save Sodom):**
- "When I see injustice at work, do I speak up like Abraham, or do I stay silent?"
- "Abraham challenges God respectfully. This teaches me that I can question authority while still maintaining respect."

**Example (Parshat Shemot — Moses at the burning bush):**
- "Moses says 'Who am I to go to Pharaoh?' He doubts himself. This reminds me that even great leaders feel inadequate at first."
- "God doesn't accept Moses's excuses — He pushes him to step up. Sometimes I need to stop making excuses and take action."

**Cultural Parallel:**
The Confucian concept of **修身** (xiūshēn — self-cultivation) emphasizes that learning must lead to personal transformation. It's not enough to know the teachings of the sages; you must apply them to become a better person. The Jewish approach to Torah study shares this ethic: learning without application is incomplete.

---

### Question 5: **What's One Thing I Want to Remember or Do Differently This Week?**

**Purpose:** Turn insight into action.

The final question moves from discussion to commitment. Judaism teaches that study must lead to action (תלמוד מביא לידי מעשה — "study leads to practice"). This question ensures that your Shabbat table conversation doesn't end when you clear the dishes.

**How to use it:**
- Each person shares one concrete takeaway
- It can be:
  - A mitzvah to observe
  - A character trait to work on
  - A perspective to remember
  - A habit to change
- Make it specific and achievable

**Why it works:**
When you commit to one small action, Torah study becomes transformative. You're not just learning; you're growing.

**Example (Parshat Vayishlach — Jacob wrestles with the angel):**
- "Jacob wrestles all night and refuses to give up. This week, I want to persevere on a project I've been avoiding."
- "Jacob's name is changed to Israel ('one who wrestles with God'). I want to remember that struggling with big questions is part of faith, not a sign of failure."

**Example (Parshat Terumah — building the Mishkan/Tabernacle):**
- "Everyone contributed to building the Mishkan according to their ability. This week, I want to contribute to my community — even if my contribution feels small."
- "The Mishkan was beautiful and sacred. I want to make one corner of my home feel more spiritually elevated — maybe a bookshelf with Jewish books or a nicer space for Shabbat candles."

## Putting It All Together: A Sample Shabbat Table Conversation

Let's see how these five questions work in practice with **Parshat Lech Lecha** (Genesis 12-17):

**Question 1: What happens in this parsha?**
- God tells Abraham (then called Abram) to leave his homeland and go to Canaan
- God promises Abraham land, descendants, and blessing
- Abraham and Sarah go to Egypt during a famine
- Abraham and his nephew Lot separate
- God makes a covenant with Abraham (Brit Bein HaBetarim)
- Hagar gives birth to Ishmael
- God establishes the covenant of circumcision and changes Abram's name to Abraham

**Question 2: What surprises you or seems strange?**
- God tells Abraham to leave his land, but doesn't immediately tell him where to go
- Abraham is 75 years old when he starts this journey — why wait so long?
- God keeps repeating the promise of descendants, but Sarah remains childless for many years
- The parsha ends with circumcision — why is this physical sign so important?

**Question 3: What does this teach about God, humanity, or the world?**
- **About God:** God initiates relationships with humans. Abraham doesn't find God; God calls Abraham.
- **About humanity:** Faith often means taking action before you have all the answers. Abraham left without knowing the destination.
- **About righteousness:** Abraham is chosen not because he's perfect, but because he's willing to journey toward something greater.

**Question 4: How does this apply to my life today?**
- "I'm in a career transition and don't know where I'll end up. Abraham's journey reminds me that uncertainty is part of growth."
- "Abraham left his family and culture to follow God. I feel that tension between honoring my non-Jewish family and embracing my new Jewish identity."
- "God kept promising Abraham descendants even when it seemed impossible. This gives me hope when I'm waiting for something important."

**Question 5: What's one thing I want to remember or do differently this week?**
- "I want to take one step toward a goal even if I don't have the whole plan figured out yet."
- "I want to trust more — both in God and in the process of my own journey."
- "I want to reach out to my family and reassure them that my Jewish path doesn't mean I'm rejecting them."

## Tips for Making This Framework Work at Your Table

### 1. **Assign Questions in Advance**
If you have family members or regular Shabbat guests, assign each person one question to prepare during the week. This distributes the work and ensures everyone participates.

### 2. **Keep a Parsha Journal**
Write down your answers to these five questions each week. Over the course of a year, you'll have a personal commentary on the entire Torah.

### 3. **Use Resources Wisely**
You don't need to be a Torah scholar to lead this discussion. Use websites like:
- **Chabad.org** — Weekly parsha summaries
- **Aish.com** — Parsha insights and commentaries
- **Sefaria.org** — Full Torah text with English translation and classical commentaries

### 4. **Don't Rush**
You don't need to cover all five questions in one meal. Sometimes one or two questions will generate such good conversation that you'll spend the whole meal on them. That's perfectly fine.

### 5. **Welcome All Answers**
There's rarely one "correct" answer in Torah discussion. Encourage multiple perspectives. A child's innocent question might spark the deepest insight.

### 6. **Make It Age-Appropriate**
- **Young children (5-10):** Focus on Questions 1 and 4 (What happens? How does it apply to me?)
- **Teenagers:** Questions 2 and 5 are great for critical thinking and personal growth
- **Adults:** All five questions work well

### 7. **Build on Each Other's Ideas**
Jewish learning is meant to be collaborative. When someone shares an insight, ask follow-up questions: "Why do you think that?" "What made you notice that detail?" "How does that connect to what we discussed last week?"

## A Chinese Cultural Parallel: The Tradition of Family Discussion

In traditional Chinese culture, particularly among scholarly families during the Ming (明朝, 1368-1644 CE) and Qing (清朝, 1644-1912 CE) dynasties, multi-generational households would gather to discuss classical texts. Grandfathers would quiz grandsons on passages from the Four Books and Five Classics. Family members would debate interpretations of Confucius or Mencius.

This wasn't just academic exercise — it was **character formation**. The texts were studied not to pass exams, but to cultivate virtue: **仁** (rén — benevolence), **义** (yì — righteousness), **礼** (lǐ — propriety), **智** (zhì — wisdom), **信** (xìn — integrity).

The Jewish Shabbat table serves the same purpose. When you discuss the weekly Torah portion, you're not just learning history or law — you're shaping character. You're asking: How do I become more compassionate? More just? More faithful? More wise?

Both traditions understand that **wisdom is transmitted not through lecture, but through dialogue**. The questions we ask shape the people we become.

## Conclusion: Your Shabbat Table as a House of Learning

The great Jewish philosopher Maimonides (Rambam, 1138-1204 CE — during China's Southern Song Dynasty 南宋) taught that every home should be a **בית מדרש** (beit midrash) — a house of learning. You don't need a rabbi at your table to make this happen. You don't need years of yeshiva study. You just need curiosity, a Torah portion, and five good questions.

This Shabbat, try this framework. Open the parsha. Read the story. Ask the questions. Listen to each other. You might be surprised by what you discover — not just about the Torah, but about your family, your values, and yourself.

The Torah has been studied for over three thousand years — since the time of Moses (approximately 1300 BCE, during China's late Shang Dynasty 商朝). Every generation finds new meaning in the same ancient words. Your questions, your insights, your family's discussion — they all add to that conversation.

Welcome to the table. The Torah is waiting.

1. What happens in this parsha?
2. What surprises you or seems strange?
3. What does this teach about God, humanity, or the world?
4. How does this apply to my life today?
5. What's one thing I want to remember or do differently this week?

✅ **Questions are central to Jewish learning** — asking is a sign of engagement, not doubt

✅ **Torah study is meant to be collaborative** — involve the whole family, welcome all perspectives

✅ **Learning must lead to action** — always end with a concrete commitment

✅ **Your Shabbat table can become a house of learning** — no rabbi required, just curiosity and willingness to engage
