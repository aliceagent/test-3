## Introduction

Imagine your boss telling you to take an entire year off work — and promising you'll still have enough money to live. Sound impossible? Welcome to one of the Torah's most radical teachings: the Shmitah year, where every seventh year, all agricultural work stops, and an entire society learns to trust God with their livelihood.

This week's double Torah portion, Behar-Bechukotai (Leviticus 25:1-27:34), was given during what corresponds to the Shang Dynasty period in China (商朝, ~1600-1046 BCE). In it, God commands something that would have seemed financially insane to ancient farmers — and still challenges entrepreneurs and workers today.

Let your land rest. Trust Me to provide. Every seven years, without exception.

## The Radical Economics of Shmitah

The Torah states it simply: "Six years you may sow your field and six years you may prune your vineyard and gather in the yield. But in the seventh year the land shall have a sabbath of complete rest, a sabbath of the Lord; you shall not sow your field or prune your vineyard" (Leviticus 25:3-4).

In an agricultural society where 90% of people farmed for survival, this wasn't just an economic risk — it was a life-or-death test of faith. Unlike the weekly Sabbath that affects one day, the Shmitah year affects an entire year's income and planning.

### Historical Context: Faith During the Shang Dynasty

During the Shang Dynasty period when this law was given, Chinese civilization was developing sophisticated agricultural techniques and bronze technology. Like the ancient Israelites, Chinese farmers understood that agriculture was the foundation of society. The idea of voluntarily stopping all farming would have seemed equally radical to both cultures.

Yet the Torah demands exactly this leap of faith. Chinese Daoist philosophy would later develop concepts of wu wei (無為) — acting through non-action — that echo some aspects of the Shmitah's trust-based approach to providence.

## The Divine Promise: God's Economic Safety Net

The Torah anticipates the obvious question: "What are we going to eat in the seventh year, if we may not sow or gather in our crops?" (Leviticus 25:20).

God's answer is remarkable: "I will ordain My blessing for you in the sixth year, so that it shall yield a crop sufficient for three years" (Leviticus 25:21).

This isn't just about agriculture — it's about learning that our security ultimately comes from God, not from our constant striving. The sixth year's miraculous abundance would sustain them through the Shmitah year and until the next harvest in the eighth year.

## Modern Applications: Trusting God with Your Career

### For Entrepreneurs and Business Owners

Modern business rarely allows for a full Shmitah year, but the principles remain transformative:

**1. Sabbatical Planning:** Some contemporary entrepreneurs deliberately build sabbatical periods into their business model. One business owner shared how taking a full year off to study Torah — trusting God for provision — actually led to his most successful business launch when he returned.

**2. Rest Without Guilt:** In our hustle culture, the Shmitah teaches that productivity isn't always the highest value. Sometimes the most faithful act is to stop working and trust God's timing.

**3. Economic Trust:** The Shmitah challenges us to examine where our real security lies. Do we trust our bank account, our business plan, or our Creator?

### For Employees and Workers

Even if you can't take a full year off, the Shmitah spirit applies:

**1. Regular Rest:** Building true rest into your life — not just vacation, but purposeful stepping back from the anxiety of economic striving.

**2. Generous Giving:** The Shmitah year was also a year when fields were open to the poor. Are we generous with our resources, trusting that God will replenish what we share?

**3. Long-term Perspective:** Planning for regular periods of renewal and spiritual focus, even within a conventional career.

### Cultural Connection: Chinese Values of Trust and Harmony

Traditional Chinese culture values balance between work and harmony, reflected in concepts like the Middle Way and seasonal rhythms. The Shmitah shares this wisdom but adds a crucial element: explicit trust in divine providence rather than human calculation.

During the Tang Dynasty (唐朝, 618-907 CE), Chinese Buddhism incorporated similar ideas about trusting the dharma rather than worldly accumulation — showing how different cultures can arrive at parallel insights about faith and livelihood.

## The Jubilee: Ultimate Economic Reset

Beyond the seven-year Shmitah, the Torah describes the Jubilee — every 50th year when all land returns to its original owners and all Hebrew slaves go free (Leviticus 25:8-55). This represents perhaps history's most radical economic system: a society that regularly redistributes wealth and prevents permanent inequality.

The Jubilee shows that God's economic vision isn't just about individual trust, but about creating social structures that ensure no one remains permanently disadvantaged.

## Practical Application: Building Shmitah Principles into Modern Life

### 1. Create "Mini-Shmitahs"

Consider implementing smaller versions of the Shmitah principle:
- One day per week for complete rest from business concerns
- One week per year for spiritual retreat and renewal  
- One month every few years for deeper reflection on life direction

### 2. Financial Trust Practices

- Set aside savings specifically to enable periods of reduced income for spiritual pursuits
- Practice generous giving that requires trusting God for provision
- Avoid excessive work that stems from anxiety rather than genuine calling

### 3. Community Support

The Shmitah worked because it was communal — everyone participated together. Create or join communities that support each other in prioritizing spiritual growth over constant productivity.

## Key Takeaways

- **Trust over security:** The Shmitah teaches that real security comes from trusting God, not from constant economic striving
- **Rest is commanded:** Taking time away from work isn't laziness — it's obedience to God's design for human flourishing
- **Community matters:** The Shmitah worked as a social system that cared for everyone's needs during the rest year
- **Divine provision is real:** God promises to provide for those who trust Him with their livelihood
- **Modern applications exist:** While we can't all take a full Shmitah year, we can build its principles into contemporary life

The Shmitah year remains one of the Torah's most challenging teachings because it strikes at the heart of our deepest anxieties about security and provision. Yet for those willing to trust, it opens a pathway to freedom from the endless cycle of economic anxiety and into the peace that comes from knowing our Provider never sleeps.

In a world that tells us to hustle without rest, the Shmitah whispers a different message: Stop. Trust. Rest. Watch God provide in ways you never imagined.
