# Va'etchanan: The Second Version of the Ten Commandments — What Changed and Why

*A careful comparison of the Ten Commandments in Exodus vs. Deuteronomy and what the differences reveal*

In Parashat Va'etchanan, we encounter something remarkable: Moses presents the Ten Commandments for a second time, yet not quite the same as before. When we place Exodus 20:2-17 alongside Deuteronomy 5:6-21, subtle but significant differences emerge—changes that reveal profound theological insights about divine revelation, human understanding, and the evolving nature of religious law.

## The Setting: Why Repeat at All?

Moses stands before a new generation on the plains of Moab, forty years after Sinai. These are the children of the wilderness generation, poised to enter the Promised Land. But why repeat the Decalogue? Why not simply refer back to Exodus?

The Ramban suggests that Moses is offering his own interpretation of what was heard at Sinai, while the Rasag (Sa'adia Gaon) proposes these differences reflect the second set of tablets after Moses broke the first. Each explanation hints at a deeper truth: revelation requires translation, even—or especially—divine revelation.

## The Major Differences

### 1. Remember vs. Observe (זכור vs. שמור)

**Exodus 20:8**: "Remember (זכור) the sabbath day, to keep it holy"  
**Deuteronomy 5:12**: "Observe (שמור) the sabbath day, to keep it holy"

This isn't mere synonym variation. "Remember" (zachor) implies active mental engagement with the past—specifically God's rest on the seventh day of creation. "Observe" (shamor) emphasizes present action and vigilant protection of the Sabbath boundary.

The Talmud resolves this apparent contradiction by teaching that both words were spoken simultaneously at Sinai—an impossibility for human speech but perfectly natural for divine communication. The message: Sabbath observance requires both historical consciousness and present mindfulness.

### 2. The Great Sabbath Divide: Creation vs. Liberation

The most striking difference appears in the Sabbath commandment's rationale:

**Exodus**: "For in six days the LORD made heaven and earth, the sea, and all that is in them, and rested on the seventh day; therefore the LORD blessed the sabbath day and hallowed it." (20:11)

**Deuteronomy**: "Remember that you were a slave in the land of Egypt and the LORD your God freed you from there with a mighty hand and an outstretched arm; therefore the LORD your God has commanded you to observe the sabbath day." (5:15)

Here we see two faces of the same truth. In Exodus, Sabbath connects us to cosmic order—God's rhythm of creation and rest that governs the universe. In Deuteronomy, it becomes a celebration of human dignity—remembering slavery to ensure freedom for all, including servants and animals.

This dual rationale reflects Deuteronomy's humanistic emphasis. While Exodus focuses on theological principles, Deuteronomy consistently stresses social responsibility and remembrance of Egypt.

### 3. Textual Variants: The Devil in the Details

Several smaller changes carry weight:

- **Animal specifications**: Deuteronomy specifically mentions "your ox or your ass" while Exodus uses the general "your cattle"
- **Conjunctions**: Deuteronomy adds "and" (ו) before "you shall not commit adultery" and "you shall not steal," potentially grouping them as related offenses
- **Deuteronomic formula**: The phrase "as the LORD your God commanded you" appears only in Deuteronomy, emphasizing Moses' role as intermediary

## What the Differences Reveal

### The Nature of Divine Communication

These variations suggest that divine revelation doesn't work like dictation. God communicates truths that must be expressed through human language and understanding. The differences between Exodus and Deuteronomy reflect not divine inconsistency but the richness of divine meaning that transcends any single formulation.

As the Chabad tradition explains, one version expresses "how God said it," while the other shows "how divine wisdom was expressed through the intellect of His faithful prophet Moses." Both remain equally divine and authoritative.

### Historical Context Shapes Understanding

The Deuteronomy version addresses a generation about to settle in the land, establish cities, and create a society. Its emphasis on social justice (Sabbath for slaves) and historical memory (remembering Egypt) speaks to people transitioning from wandering to governing.

### The Fluidity of Sacred Text

Archaeological evidence from the Dead Sea Scrolls and the Nash Papyrus shows that even in ancient times, scribes created "mixed texts" that harmonized the two versions. This suggests that ancient Jewish communities recognized both versions as authentic and sought to preserve their combined wisdom.

## Chinese Philosophical Parallels

This tension between unchanging principle and contextual application resonates with Chinese philosophical concepts. Like the relationship between 理 (li, principle) and 氣 (qi, material manifestation) in Neo-Confucian thought, the Ten Commandments represent eternal principles that must manifest through particular cultural and historical contexts.

The Ming Dynasty scholar Wang Yangming taught that true knowledge comes through practice—knowing and acting are one. Similarly, the Decalogue's dual presentation suggests that divine law achieves its fullness not in abstract formulation but in lived application across different circumstances and generations.

## Lessons for Today

The two versions of the Ten Commandments teach us several crucial lessons:

1. **Multiple perspectives enrich truth** rather than threatening it
2. **Sacred texts evolve** not by changing their essence but by revealing new dimensions of meaning
3. **Divine law serves human flourishing**—note how Deuteronomy's social emphasis complements Exodus's theological focus
4. **Memory and action** are both essential to religious life (zachor and shamor)

## Conclusion: The Beauty of Difference

Rather than seeing the variations as problems to be solved, we might view them as windows into the inexhaustible depth of divine communication. Like light refracting through a prism, the single divine word breaks into a spectrum of meaning, each color beautiful and necessary.

Moses, standing before a new generation, didn't simply repeat the past but translated eternal truth for a new moment. In doing so, he modeled what each generation must do: receive the tradition while making it freshly relevant for their time and place.

The two versions of the Ten Commandments remind us that fidelity to tradition sometimes requires adaptation, that unchanging truth must speak in changing language, and that the divine word, precisely because it is divine, can support the weight of human interpretation and application.
