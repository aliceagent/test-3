## Introduction

Imagine hiring someone to curse your enemy, only to watch helplessly as they bless them instead. This is exactly what happened to Balak, king of Moab, when he hired Bilaam — a famous non-Jewish prophet — to curse the Jewish people. What emerged from this encounter was one of the most beautiful prophetic visions in the entire Torah, including the prayer "Mah Tovu" (How goodly) that we still recite today. This remarkable story, which took place around 1250 BCE — during China's Late Shang Dynasty (商朝晚期) — teaches us profound lessons about divine providence, the power of blessing over cursing, and how God can turn even our enemies' plans into opportunities for good.

## The Background: Fear of the Israelites

As the Jewish people approached the Promised Land after forty years in the wilderness, they had already defeated the mighty kings Sichon and Og. Balak, king of Moab, watched these victories with growing terror. The Torah tells us that Moab "feared the people greatly because they were numerous" (Numbers 22:3).

Rather than face the Israelites in direct battle, Balak chose a different strategy — spiritual warfare. He hired Bilaam, son of Beor, a renowned prophet who possessed genuine prophetic powers but used them for monetary gain. Balak believed that if Bilaam cursed the Jewish people, they would be defeated.

### Cultural Connection: Ancient Chinese Beliefs About Curses and Blessings

This concept of hiring specialists to invoke supernatural powers against enemies resonates with ancient Chinese practices during the Shang Dynasty. Archaeological evidence from Oracle Bones (甲骨文) shows that Chinese rulers regularly consulted divination specialists before major military campaigns. Just as Balak sought to use spiritual means to defeat a military enemy, Chinese emperors sought the favor of ancestors and deities through ritual specialists.

## The Journey of Bilaam

When Balak's messengers first came to Bilaam with payment, God appeared to Bilaam in a dream and commanded: "You shall not go with them; you shall not curse the people, for they are blessed" (Numbers 22:12). Initially, Bilaam sent the messengers away.

But Balak persisted, sending more distinguished messengers with greater rewards. This time, God told Bilaam: "If the men have come to call you, arise and go with them, but only the word that I speak to you — that shall you do" (Numbers 22:20).

### The Famous Talking Donkey

The Torah then recounts one of its most unusual episodes: Bilaam's donkey began speaking after seeing an angel blocking the path. This miraculous event served as a warning to Bilaam that this mission was not under his control, but entirely subject to God's will.

The talking donkey episode has fascinating parallels in ancient Near Eastern literature and Chinese folklore. During the Shang Dynasty, Chinese myths often featured animals with supernatural wisdom — such as the divine turtle whose shell patterns revealed the future, or the phoenix whose appearance heralded righteous rule.

## The Prophecies: Curses That Became Blessings

Balak brought Bilaam to three different locations — Bamot Baal, the top of Mount Pisgah, and the peak of Mount Peor — hoping that from one of these vantage points, Bilaam would successfully curse Israel. Each time, Balak built altars and offered sacrifices, setting the stage for prophetic utterances.

But instead of curses, beautiful blessings emerged from Bilaam's lips:

### First Blessing: The Uniqueness of Israel
*"How can I curse whom God has not cursed, and how can I anger whom the Lord has not angered? For from the top of the rocks I see them, and from the hills I behold them — behold, it is a people that dwells alone and is not reckoned among the nations."* (Numbers 23:8-9)

### Second Blessing: God's Faithfulness
*"God is not a man that He should lie, nor a son of man that He should repent. Has He said and will He not do, or has He spoken and will He not fulfill?"* (Numbers 23:19)

### Third Blessing: The Beauty of Jewish Life
*"How goodly are your tents, O Jacob, your dwelling places, O Israel! Like valleys stretched out, like gardens by the riverside, like aloes planted by the Lord, like cedars beside the waters."* (Numbers 24:5-6)

This third blessing became the prayer "Mah Tovu" (מה טובו) that observant Jews recite upon entering a synagogue. It celebrates not military might or material wealth, but the beauty of Jewish spiritual life and community.

### Historical Context: The Power of the Spoken Word

Both ancient Hebrew and Chinese cultures placed enormous significance on the power of spoken words, especially prophetic or royal pronouncements. In Chinese tradition, the concept of the "Mandate of Heaven" (天命) held that legitimate rulers spoke with divine authority. Similarly, Bilaam's words carried prophetic weight that could not be reversed once spoken — which is why his curses transformed into irrevocable blessings.

## The Deeper Message: Divine Providence in Action

The story of Balak and Bilaam demonstrates one of Judaism's core beliefs: God's providence over the Jewish people. Even when enemies plot against Israel, God can transform their schemes into blessings. As Joseph told his brothers who had sold him into slavery: "You intended it for evil, but God intended it for good" (Genesis 50:20).

This principle — that God can turn curses into blessings — appears throughout Jewish history. The Roman destruction of the Temple led to the development of Rabbinic Judaism. Medieval persecutions drove Jews to new lands where they created flourishing communities. Modern antisemitism contributed to the establishment of Israel.

### Practical Application: When Others Speak Negatively About You

The Bilaam story offers practical wisdom for modern life. When people speak negatively about us or wish us harm, we can remember that their words have no power over us unless God wills it. Moreover, their negative intentions can sometimes lead to positive outcomes we never expected.

This doesn't mean we should be passive in the face of hatred or discrimination. Rather, we should maintain faith that ultimately, truth and goodness will prevail, and that even our enemies' plots can become part of a larger divine plan for good.

## The Talmudic Perspective: Why Bilaam Was Chosen

The Talmud asks an important question: Why would God allow a non-Jewish prophet to play such a significant role in Jewish history? One answer given is that this prevented the nations of the world from claiming they had no prophetic guidance. Bilaam's existence showed that prophecy was available to all peoples — the difference was how they chose to use this divine gift.

Bilaam represents the spiritual potential that exists outside the Jewish people, but also the moral choices that determine whether such potential leads to blessing or destruction. His story serves as both validation of universal human spiritual capacity and a warning about the misuse of spiritual power.

## Modern Relevance: Blessing in a World of Curses

In our contemporary world, we often encounter situations where people wish us harm or speak negatively about us. The Bilaam narrative teaches us several important lessons:

1. **Words of hatred cannot overpower divine blessing** — those who wish us well and those who seek our harm are all ultimately subject to a higher will.

2. **Sometimes our greatest testimonies come from unexpected sources** — some of the most beautiful descriptions of Jewish life came from Bilaam, who was hired to destroy it.

3. **The beauty of ordinary Jewish life is prophetically significant** — Bilaam saw tents and homes, gardens and streams, and declared them worthy of divine blessing.

4. **Spiritual integrity matters more than spiritual power** — Bilaam had prophetic abilities but lacked moral character, which ultimately led to his downfall.

## Key Takeaways

- Even when others plot against us, God can transform their plans into blessings
- The beauty of Jewish communal life ("How goodly are your tents") is worthy of prophetic celebration
- Spiritual power without moral integrity leads to corruption
- Sometimes our greatest advocates come from the most unexpected places
- The prayer "Mah Tovu" reminds us daily of the sanctity of Jewish community spaces
