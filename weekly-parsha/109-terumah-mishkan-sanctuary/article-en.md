## Introduction

"Make for Me a sanctuary, and I will dwell among them" (Exodus 25:8). With these words, spoken nearly 3,400 years ago — during what corresponds to China's Shang Dynasty (商朝, ~1600-1046 BCE) — God commanded the Jewish people to build the Mishkan, the portable Tabernacle that would serve as a divine dwelling place in the wilderness.

But the Hebrew text contains a beautiful paradox. God doesn't say "I will dwell *in it*" but rather "I will dwell *among them*" — among the people themselves. This week's Torah portion, Parashat Terumah, teaches us that the ultimate sanctuary God desires is not a building of gold and precious stones, but the human heart prepared to welcome the Divine presence.

## The Architecture of the Sacred

The Torah devotes thirteen chapters to the detailed construction of the Mishkan, specifying everything from the precise measurements of the Holy Ark to the colors of the curtains. Why such meticulous attention to physical details for a spiritual purpose?

### The Outer Courtyard: Approaching the Divine

The Mishkan was surrounded by a courtyard (חצר), enclosed by linen curtains supported by sixty pillars. This outer space contained the copper altar for sacrifices and the laver for ritual washing. Like the traditional Chinese concept of feng shui (风水), which emphasizes the proper arrangement of space to channel positive energy, the Mishkan's design created a progressive journey toward increasing holiness.

### The Holy Place: Daily Service

Beyond the courtyard stood the Mishkan proper, divided into two chambers. The first, called the Holy Place (קדש), contained three essential items: the golden Menorah (seven-branched candelabrum), the Table of Showbread, and the Golden Altar for incense. These represented the spiritual illumination, sustenance, and prayers that characterize our daily relationship with God.

### The Holy of Holies: The Divine Presence

The inner chamber, the Holy of Holies (קדש הקדשים), housed the Ark of the Covenant containing the stone tablets of the Ten Commandments. This was where God's presence most intensely dwelled, accessible only to the High Priest on Yom Kippur. The Ark was crowned with the כפרת (kapporet), a golden cover with two cherubim whose wings touched above it — the space where God spoke to Moses.

## Cultural Connection: Sacred Spaces Across Cultures

The human need to create sacred spaces is universal. In traditional Chinese architecture, temples and palaces follow precise principles designed to harmonize earthly structures with heavenly energies. The Forbidden City's layout, with its successive courtyards leading to increasingly sacred inner chambers, mirrors the Mishkan's design of progressive holiness.

Similarly, the Buddhist concept of the Three Jewels — Buddha, Dharma, and Sangha — finds an echo in the Mishkan's three primary vessels in the Holy Place. Just as Chinese gardens create spaces for contemplation and connection with nature, the Mishkan created a space for the Jewish people to connect with their Creator.

## The Chassidic Teaching: Your Heart as a Mishkan

The Chassidic masters, particularly the Baal Shem Tov (1698-1760) — during China's Qing Dynasty (清朝) — taught that every person is called to make their heart into a Mishkan for God. This mystical interpretation transforms the ancient desert sanctuary into a template for personal spiritual development.

### The Three Levels of the Heart

Just as the Mishkan had three levels of holiness, our hearts contain three chambers:

**1. The Outer Courtyard (Actions):** This represents our external behavior — how we treat others, our ethical choices, and our observance of mitzvot. Like the copper altar where sacrifices were offered, this is where we "sacrifice" our selfish impulses for higher purposes.

**2. The Holy Place (Emotions and Thoughts):** Here reside our daily spiritual practices — prayer (incense altar), study (showbread table), and spiritual illumination (menorah). This is where we work on refining our character traits and cultivating positive emotions.

**3. The Holy of Holies (Soul Connection):** The deepest chamber of the heart is where our soul connects directly with God. This is accessed through meditation, deep prayer, and moments of spiritual transcendence that transform our entire being.

## Historical Context: Building in the Wilderness

The Mishkan was constructed during the Jewish people's forty-year journey through the Sinai Desert, beginning in 1313 BCE — corresponding to the late Shang Dynasty period in China. Unlike permanent temples, the Mishkan was designed to be portable, reflecting the reality that spiritual growth often happens during periods of transition and uncertainty rather than comfort and stability.

This portability carried a profound message: God's presence isn't confined to any single location or building. The Divine can dwell wherever hearts are prepared to receive it, whether in a desert wilderness or a modern city apartment.

## Practical Application: Building Your Personal Mishkan

How can we apply the Mishkan's blueprint to our daily spiritual lives?

### Create Sacred Space in Your Home

Designate a corner of your home for prayer, study, or meditation. It doesn't need to be elaborate — even a simple shelf with meaningful books and objects can serve as your personal "Holy Place." Like the Mishkan's vessels, choose items that elevate your consciousness and remind you of your spiritual aspirations.

### Establish Daily Spiritual Practices

The Mishkan required daily service from the priests. Similarly, create daily practices that feed your soul: morning prayers or meditation, evening reflection, regular Torah study, or acts of kindness. Consistency matters more than duration.

### Progressive Purification

Just as visitors to the Mishkan underwent purification before entering, work on purifying your thoughts, speech, and actions. Begin with external behaviors (like the outer courtyard), then address inner thoughts and emotions (the Holy Place), ultimately reaching for moments of pure spiritual connection (the Holy of Holies).

### Welcome Others Into Your Sacred Space

Remember, God said "I will dwell among them" — in the plural. Your personal Mishkan should not be a place of isolation but of connection. Share meals with friends, study with others, and create opportunities for spiritual community in your home.

## The Materials: Gifts of the Heart

The Torah specifies that the Mishkan should be built only from materials given "from every person whose heart inspires him to give" (Exodus 25:2). The Hebrew phrase "נדיב לבו" (nediv libo) suggests generosity that flows naturally from an willing heart, not coercion or obligation.

This principle applies to our personal spiritual work as well. Authentic spiritual growth cannot be forced or fabricated. Like the ancient Israelites who brought gold, silver, precious stones, and fine fabrics because they felt moved to contribute, our spiritual development must be fueled by genuine inspiration and joy, not guilt or external pressure.

## Key Takeaways

- The ultimate sanctuary God desires is the prepared human heart, not a physical building
- Sacred space can be created anywhere through intention, practice, and devotion
- Spiritual growth happens in progressive stages, like the Mishkan's three levels of holiness
- True spiritual service must flow from inner generosity and joy, not external obligation
- Building a "Mishkan" is both a personal and communal endeavor — we grow individually but not alone
