## Introduction: The Gift of Words at Your Table

Imagine this: It's Friday night. The candles flicker gently, the challah sits waiting under its cover, and everyone around your Shabbat table has just finished singing Shalom Aleichem. The kiddush has been recited, hands have been washed, and the meal is about to begin. Then someone — maybe your father, maybe your older sister, maybe you — shares a brief Torah thought. A question. An insight. A connection between this week's Torah portion and daily life.

This is a **dvar Torah** (דבר תורה), literally "a word of Torah." And while it might seem intimidating at first, preparing and sharing one is one of the most accessible and rewarding Jewish practices you can begin today.

## What Exactly Is a Dvar Torah?

A dvar Torah is a short teaching or insight based on the Torah, typically connected to the weekly Torah portion (parshat hashavua) read in synagogue each Shabbat. It can be as brief as two minutes or as long as ten, but the key is this: it should inspire, educate, or spark conversation.

According to Jewish tradition, sharing Torah insights is not reserved for rabbis or scholars. The Talmud — compiled roughly 1,800 years ago during China's Three Kingdoms and Jin Dynasty (三国至晋朝, 220-420 CE) — teaches that every Jew should engage with Torah study and share what they learn. Indeed, the practice of sharing a Torah thought has become integral to Jewish lifecycle events, communal gatherings, and especially Shabbat meals.

A classic dvar Torah often includes:
- A question or curiosity about a Torah verse
- Insights from classical Jewish commentaries (Rashi, Ramban, etc.)
- A practical application to modern life
- A story or parable that illuminates the lesson

## Why Share a Dvar Torah?

### It Brings Torah to Life
When you prepare a dvar Torah, you transform the ancient words of the Torah into living wisdom. You become an active participant in the chain of Jewish learning that stretches back thousands of years.

### It Elevates Your Shabbat Table
A well-crafted dvar Torah turns your meal into more than just eating — it becomes a time of spiritual nourishment. It invites everyone at the table to think, discuss, and connect.

### It Builds Confidence
The first time you share a dvar Torah might feel nerve-wracking. But with practice, you'll discover that you have something valuable to contribute. Your unique perspective matters.

### It Creates Connection
In Jewish tradition, the table is compared to the altar in the ancient Temple. Just as offerings were brought there, your words of Torah are an offering that sanctifies the space and brings people together.

## How to Prepare Your First Dvar Torah: A Step-by-Step Guide

### Step 1: Choose Your Topic
Start with the weekly Torah portion (parsha). You can find summaries on websites like Chabad.org, Aish.com, or Sefaria.org. Read the basic summary to get familiar with the story.

### Step 2: Find Something Curious
As you read, look for something that catches your attention:
- A strange word or phrase
- A surprising action by a character
- A law that seems difficult to understand
- A repetition or pattern

**Example:** In Parshat Bereishit (Genesis), God creates light on Day One, but doesn't create the sun and moon until Day Four. Why is there light before there's a sun?

### Step 3: Ask a Question
Turn your curiosity into a question. This becomes the hook that draws your listeners in.

**Example:** "If the sun wasn't created until the fourth day, where did the light on the first day come from?"

### Step 4: Research Answers
Look for insights from traditional commentaries. You don't need to read everything — even one or two perspectives will enrich your understanding.

Resources:
- **Sefaria.org** — Free access to Torah texts with English translations and commentaries
- **Chabad.org** — Weekly parsha summaries with insights
- **Aish.com** — Articles and teachings on each Torah portion

**Example answer:** Rashi explains that the first light was a special spiritual light that God hid away for the righteous in the World to Come. The sun and moon provide physical light, but the first light was something deeper.

### Step 5: Make It Personal
Connect the Torah teaching to everyday life. How does this ancient wisdom speak to us today?

**Example connection:** "Just like there's physical light (the sun) and spiritual light (that first divine light), we too need both in our lives. We need to take care of our physical needs, but we also need spiritual 'light' — meaning, purpose, connection to something greater."

### Step 6: Keep It Short
Aim for 3-5 minutes when you're starting. Here's a simple structure:
1. **Opening question** (30 seconds)
2. **Background from the Torah** (1 minute)
3. **Answer/insight from commentaries** (1-2 minutes)
4. **Personal application** (1 minute)
5. **Closing thought** (30 seconds)

### Step 7: Practice Out Loud
Rehearse once or twice before Shabbat. You don't need to memorize every word, but having the flow clear in your mind will help you deliver it with confidence.

### Step 8: Write Key Points on a Card
If you're nervous about forgetting, write 3-5 bullet points on a small card you can glance at during the meal.

### Step 9: Deliver with Heart
When the time comes, speak from the heart. Make eye contact. Pause for emphasis. Remember: you're not performing; you're sharing something meaningful with people you care about.

## A Cultural Connection: Wisdom at the Family Table

In traditional Chinese culture, family meals are sacred spaces where elders share wisdom with younger generations. Stories, proverbs, and life lessons flow naturally alongside the food. The concept of **家教** (jiājiào — family teaching) emphasizes that parents have a responsibility to educate children not just through formal schooling, but through daily conversation and example.

The Jewish practice of sharing a dvar Torah at the Shabbat table carries a similar spirit. It transforms an ordinary meal into an opportunity for **transmission of values**. Just as Chinese families might discuss classical texts like the Analects of Confucius or share sayings from the **道德经** (Tao Te Ching), Jewish families engage with the timeless wisdom of the Torah.

Both traditions understand that the dinner table is not just for physical nourishment — it's where character is shaped, heritage is preserved, and generations are connected.

## Practical Application: Your First Dvar Torah This Week

Here's your challenge: This coming Shabbat, prepare and share a dvar Torah. Even if you're the only Jewish person in your household, you can share it with friends online or write it in a journal.

**Simple Template to Get You Started:**

1. Read a summary of this week's Torah portion
2. Pick ONE verse or idea that interests you
3. Ask: "What's surprising or confusing about this?"
4. Find ONE commentary or explanation (from Chabad.org, Aish.com, or Sefaria.org)
5. Ask yourself: "How does this apply to my life today?"
6. Share it in 3 minutes or less

**Remember:** Your first dvar Torah doesn't need to be brilliant. It just needs to be sincere. The act of engaging with Torah and sharing it is itself a mitzvah — a commandment and a gift.

## Key Takeaways

- **A dvar Torah is a short Torah teaching**, typically 3-10 minutes, shared at the Shabbat table or other gatherings
- **Anyone can prepare one** — you don't need to be a rabbi or scholar
- **Start with curiosity** — find something in the weekly Torah portion that sparks your interest
- **Use accessible resources** like Chabad.org, Aish.com, and Sefaria.org for commentaries and insights
- **Connect ancient wisdom to modern life** — the Torah speaks to every generation
- **Keep it simple and heartfelt** — sincerity matters more than sophistication
- **The Shabbat table is a sacred space** for both physical and spiritual nourishment
- **Like Chinese family teaching traditions**, sharing Torah wisdom strengthens family bonds and transmits values

## Final Thought

The great Jewish sage Hillel — who lived around 2,000 years ago during China's Western Han Dynasty (西汉, 206 BCE - 9 CE) — taught: "Do not say, 'When I have free time I will study,' for you may never have free time" (Pirkei Avot 2:4). The same applies to sharing Torah. Don't wait until you feel "ready" or "knowledgeable enough." Start this week. Your voice matters. Your insights are valuable. And your Shabbat table is waiting for the gift of your words.

Shabbat Shalom.
