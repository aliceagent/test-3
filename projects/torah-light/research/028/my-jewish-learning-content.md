# Modeh Ani Analysis - My Jewish Learning

Source: https://www.myjewishlearning.com/article/modeh-ani-its-not-just-about-gratitude/

## Key Insights

### The First Prayer of the Day

**Purpose**: There is a prayer to be recited at the very moment we wake up, before we do or say anything else.

**Prayer text**:
Hebrew: מוֹדֶה אֲנִי לְפָנֶיךָ מלך חַי וְקַיָּם שֶהֶחֱזַרְתָּ בִּי נִשְׁמָתִי בְחֶמְלָה, רַבָּה אֱמוּנָתֶךָ

Transliteration: Modeh ani l'fanecha, melech chai v'kayam, shehechezarta bi nishmati b'chemla, raba emunatecha

Translation: I thank You, living and enduring King, for You have graciously returned my soul within me. Great is Your faithfulness.

### Sleep as 1/60 of Death

**Mystical concept**: There's a concept in Jewish mysticism that sleep is 1/60 of death. We are by no means guaranteed to wake up in the morning, so when we do, it's only proper that we take a moment to express gratitude for another day.

### Grammar and Word Order

**Important detail**: Grammatically, it would be correct to say "Ani modeh" — "I thank You." Yet, the words are in the reverse order, which translates literally as "Thank You, I."

**Significance**: The very first word that comes out of our mouths should be one of gratitude. We often spend the rest of our waking day focused on ourselves, our needs, our work. So we want to ensure that we never forget to have the foundation of our day be "Thank You." Only after that has been said, do we mention ourselves.

### Triple Meaning of "Modeh"

The word "modeh" has three distinct meanings:

1. **Thank** - Clearly about gratitude. Our lives are gifts. Another day means another opportunity to figure out why we are here and what we are supposed to be doing.

2. **Admit** - The acknowledgment and verbalization of our gratitude. When we take an honest look at ourselves, we must admit that we have made mistakes. Nonetheless, we were given another chance to transform our reality.

3. **Surrender** - The recognition that there is something bigger than us, that the same God who gave us life is the God who takes it away.

### Past vs Present Focus

**Hebrew teaching**: The Hebrew word for the past, *avar*, shares the same root as *aveira*, which means "sin." Living in the past, relying on the past, focusing on the past, whether good or bad, is unhealthy.

**Focus**: Our focus needs to be on the present, on today. So we begin the day with the admission that no matter who we have been up until now, today is a new opportunity to be that much more.

### Daily Renewal

**Concept**: Only after we thank, admit and surrender can we focus on ourselves — who we are, what we are capable of, and who we want to be. And with this in mind and on our lips, we can get out of bed and start our day ready for the new opportunities that await us.
