# Halachipedia Research - Birchot HaShachar (Morning Blessings)

## Key Information from Halachipedia:

### Institution and Purpose
- Birchot HaShachar were instituted by Chazal (the Sages) as a series of blessings to be recited every morning
- Each blessing corresponds to different worldly benefits and pleasures
- The Gemara explains that everything belongs to Hashem, but once a person recites a bracha over worldly pleasure, they acquire it
- The blessings help us reach the goal of saying 100 blessings daily (Meah Berachot)

### Historical Background (Gemara Brachot 60b)
- Originally, each blessing was connected to a specific morning action:
  - When you hear a rooster → Leshechvi Binah
  - When you open your eyes → Pokeach Ivrim  
  - When you sit up → Matir Asurim
  - And so on...

### Modern Practice
- Today we recite ALL the blessings regardless of whether we experience each specific situation
- This follows the view that these are general blessings praising God for the regular nature of the world
- Even if you don't hear a rooster, sleep with clothes on, or are blind, you still recite all corresponding blessings

### Who Recites Them
- All Jews recite Birchot HaShachar 
- Women have slight variations:
  - Instead of "Shelo Asani Isha" (Who did not make me a woman), they say "Sheasani Kirtzono" (Who made me according to His will) - without God's name
  - Instead of "Shelo Asani Aved" → "Shelo Asani Shifcha"  
  - Instead of "Shelo Asani Goy" → "Shelo Asani Goyah"

### Timing
- Earliest time: From midnight (Chatzot)
- One blessing (HaNoten Lesechvi Binah) should wait until dawn (Olot)
- Ideally recited before the 4th halachic hour of the day
- Can be said until one goes to sleep at night
- Usually recited at the beginning of morning prayers

### Special Blessings Included
1. **Elokai Neshama** - Thanking God for returning the soul each morning
2. **HaNoten Leyaef Koach** - Giving strength to the weary (added later, based on Arizal)
3. **Birchot HaTorah** - Blessings on Torah study (connected to morning blessings)
4. **The Three "Negative" Blessings** - Thanking God for not being made a non-Jew, slave, or (for men) woman

### Physical Practice
- Ashkenazi custom: Stand while reciting
- Sephardi custom: May sit or stand
- Should not be said while walking or doing other tasks
- Each person recites individually (not communally)
- Should be said with concentration

### If Forgotten
- Can be said after morning prayers (with some exceptions)
- Elokai Neshama has debate whether it can be said after prayers since it might be fulfilled by "Mechayeh HaMaytim" blessing in Amidah

### Chinese Cultural Parallels
- Similar to Confucian morning reflection practices
- Parallels with Buddhist morning gratitude meditations
- Echoes Daoist morning cultivation exercises
- All emphasize starting the day with proper spiritual orientation
