A concise, comprehensive guide to the holiday of Hanukkah.
Hanukkah celebrates the Jewish victory over the Syrian-Greek superpower in the second century BCE, during the time of the Second Holy Temple. It also celebrates the miracle of a little oil that lasted eight days. It is also known as the Festival of Lights.
What does Hanukkah celebrate?

Shabbat
The story of Hanukkah is about this clash of cultures.
When the Greeks challenged the Jews to sacrifice a pig to a Greek god, a band courageous Jews took to the hills (near modern-day Modi’in) in open revolt against this threat to Jewish life. The Jewish priest Matitiyahu, and later his son Judah the Maccabee, led a guerrilla warfare against the Syrian-Greek army. After three years, against incredible odds, the 
Maccabees
The Jewish warrior heroes recaptured the Holy Temple in Jerusalem, cleansed it of idols, and re-dedicated it on the 25th day of the Jewish month of Kislev. Wanting to re-light the Menorah, they searched the entire Temple, but found only one jar of pure oil bearing the seal of the High Priest. That small jar of oil miraculously burned for eight days straight, until a new supply of oil could be brought.
To publicize this historic victory and the miracle of the oil, Jews today observe a “Festival of Lights” for eight days – lighting a menorah for eight nights.
What is a Menorah?
A Menorah is the candelabra on which the Hanukkah candles are lit. Its origins date back to the Torah, in 
Exodus (25: 31-40)

The main way to celebrate Hanukkah is by lighting the candles of the menorah. Hanukkah also has many traditions such as eating special foods fried in oil, playing the dreidel game, saying 
Hanukkah
 prayers and listening to Hanukkah songs.
There are many traditional and more recently created Hanukkah songs sung during the holiday.The first song sung after lighting the candles is called "Maoz Tzur".
We play the dreidel game, which involves a four sided spinning top 
with a Hebrew letter on each side (called a 
sivivon
 in Hebrew).
In commemoration of the miracle of the oil, we eat foods 
cooked with olive oil such as sufganiyot and latkes.
On Hanukkah, many have the custom to 
share gifts
, giving 
Hanukkah "gelt"
 (money or foil-covered 
chocolate coins
) to children.
During Hanukkah, a special paragraph describing the Chanukah miracle – 
“Al Hanisim”
 – is added to the 
Amidah
 prayer, and also to 
Grace After Meals
. The “Hallel” prayer is said daily.
Hanukkah's Lessons
Faith and Perseverance: The Maccabees' victory against overwhelming odds teaches us the power of faith and determination. In our lives, facing challenges with resilience and trust in God can lead to miraculous outcomes.
Light in Darkness: Lighting the menorah symbolizes bringing light into the world. Even a small act of kindness or truth can illuminate the darkness around us, inspiring others to do the same.
Miracles in the Everyday: The miracle of the oil lasting eight days reminds us to recognize the miraculous in the mundane. Life's daily blessings are often overlooked, yet they are profound gifts.
The Challenge of Assimilation: Hanukkah commemorates the Jewish resistance to Hellenistic culture, which sought to erode Jewish identity and values. Today, assimilation can dilute our unique heritage and spiritual legacy. Chanukah reminds us to embrace our distinctiveness, to proudly live as Jews, and to pass on our traditions to future generations.
