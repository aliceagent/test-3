# OK Kosher Certification Content

Source: https://www.ok.org/consumers/kosher-basics/the-kosher-symbols-clarified/

## Understanding Kosher Symbol Restrictions

### Why Different Symbols?
- All reputable kosher certifiers use orthodox kosher standards
- Meat and dairy separation is a key principle
- Symbols must specify which kosher category a product falls under
- Letters next to logos indicate "symbol restrictions" (kosher status)

## The OK Kosher Symbol Types:

### 1. OK or OK Pareve (Neutral)
- Neither dairy nor meat
- Most common kosher category
- Contains no dairy, meat, or poultry ingredients or traces
- Not prepared with dairy, meat, or poultry equipment
- Kosher fish and eggs from kosher animals are also Pareve
- **Note:** Pareve ≠ vegetarian (fish is Pareve but not vegetarian)
- Can be eaten with anything, no waiting period needed

### 2. OK-D (Kosher Dairy)
- Contains dairy product or dairy ingredients
- Dairy only kosher if from kosher animals
- Cannot eat with meat; requires waiting period after meat

### 3. OK-DE (Dairy Equipment)
- Essentially Pareve product
- Made on thoroughly cleaned lines previously used for dairy
- Lines were NOT kosherized after dairy run
- Can eat soon after meat, but not AT THE SAME TIME as meat
- More flexible than full dairy designation

### 4. OK D CY (Cholov Yisroel - "Jewish Dairy")
- Higher level of supervision
- Observant Jew (Mashgiach) present from milking to packaging
- Originally to prevent dilution with non-kosher milk
- Today, FDA regulations make OK-D sufficient for most
- Required by some stringent communities
- Required for many products imported to Israel

### 5. OK-M (Kosher Meat)
- Contains kosher meat or poultry
- Land animals must be ruminants (chew cud) with cloven hooves
- Birds: Torah lists prohibited species by name
- All OK meat is **Glatt Kosher** by default

### Glatt Kosher Explained:
- Means "smooth" in Yiddish
- Refers to animal's lungs
- Expert checks for adhesions or lesions
- Technically only applies to land animals
- Colloquially used for high-standard poultry too
- Additional terms: Chissdishe Shechita, Beit Yosef (community-specific)

### 6. OK-P (Kosher for Passover)
- Special Passover certification
- Certain grains forbidden except for Matzah
- Jews cannot own, eat, or see forbidden grains during Passover
- Requires special supervision level
- Can be combined: OK-P-D (Passover dairy), OK-P-M (Passover meat)

### Kitniyot (Kitniyos):
- Category of legumes and some seeds
- Ashkenazi Jews don't eat on Passover
- Many Sephardi Jews do eat
- Products labeled "OK-P Kitniyot"

### 7. OK-PY (Pas Yisroel - "Jewish Baked Good")
- Applies to grain flour-based baked foods
- Observant Jew (Mashgiach) participated in baking
- Not absolute requirement but preferred
- Requirement for import to Israel

### 8. OK-F (Fish)
- Contains fish-based ingredients
- Fish is Pareve, but...
- Torah prohibits eating meat and fish together
- No separate cookware needed
- No waiting time between meat and fish
- Labeled to help consumers avoid mixing

## Key Principle:
Hechsher (Hebrew "approval") = trademarked kosher symbol of certifying agency
