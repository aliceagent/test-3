## Introduction

When most people think of Chanukah, they picture spinning dreidels, chocolate coins, and perhaps eight nights of presents. These festive elements certainly make the holiday joyful, especially for children. But the real story of Chanukah is far more profound and relevant to anyone facing cultural pressure, spiritual darkness, or the challenge of staying true to their deepest values in a world that doesn't always support them.

Nearly 2,200 years ago — during China's Western Han Dynasty (西汉, 206 BCE - 9 CE), when the Han Empire was consolidating Chinese culture — a small band of Jewish fighters refused to surrender their identity. They were up against the most powerful empire of their time, forced to choose between assimilation and resistance, between comfort and conviction. Their story, and the eight-day miracle that followed their victory, became one of the most beloved holidays in the Jewish calendar.

But Chanukah is not just a historical commemoration. Its lessons about preserving identity, the power of small acts of faithfulness, and finding light in darkness speak directly to modern life — especially for those exploring Judaism from outside the traditional Jewish world.

---

## The Historical Background: Greeks, Jews, and the Battle for the Soul

### Setting the Stage: The Greek Empire

After Alexander the Great conquered the ancient world in the 4th century BCE, Greek culture spread everywhere. This wasn't just military conquest — it was cultural domination. The Greeks brought philosophy, art, gymnasiums, theaters, and a worldview that celebrated the human body, intellect, and pleasure. Many Jews were attracted to this sophisticated culture and began adopting Greek customs, attending Greek schools, and even changing their Hebrew names to Greek ones.

By 167 BCE, the Seleucid Greek king Antiochus IV controlled the land of Israel. Antiochus was not content with political rule; he wanted cultural uniformity. He issued a series of harsh decrees specifically targeting Jewish religious practice:

- **Torah study was forbidden** under penalty of death
- **Shabbat observance was outlawed**
- **Circumcision was banned**
- **The Temple in Jerusalem was defiled** — pigs were sacrificed on the altar, and Greek idols were installed in the holiest site in Judaism

These weren't random persecutions. They were precisely calculated to strike at the heart of Jewish identity. Antiochus understood that if you want to destroy a people's soul, you don't just conquer their land — you erase their traditions, their learning, and their ability to pass their heritage to the next generation.

### The Jewish Response: Not Everyone Agreed

The Jewish community was divided. Many wealthy and educated Jews, known as "Hellenists," embraced Greek culture enthusiastically. They saw Torah observance as old-fashioned and embarrassing. They wanted to be cosmopolitan, sophisticated, and accepted by the Greek elite.

But another group refused to compromise. These were ordinary Jews — farmers, priests, merchants — who understood that abandoning Torah meant losing everything that made them who they were. Among them was an elderly priest named **Mattityahu** (Mattathias in Greek), who lived in the small village of Modiin.

### The Spark: One Man's Refusal

When Greek soldiers arrived in Modiin and built a pagan altar, demanding that Mattityahu publicly offer a sacrifice to Zeus, the old priest refused. A Hellenistic Jew stepped forward, ready to perform the sacrifice to curry favor with the Greeks.

Mattityahu's response was swift and shocking: he killed the traitor and the Greek officers, destroyed the altar, and cried out, "Whoever is for God, follow me!" Then he and his five sons — Yochanan, Shimon, Judah, Elazar, and Yonatan — fled to the hills of Judea.

This was the beginning of the Maccabean Revolt.

### The Maccabees: Few Against Many

Mattityahu's son **Judah** became the military leader of the revolt. He was called "Maccabee" — possibly from the Hebrew word *makevet* (hammer), or perhaps an acronym for the biblical phrase *Mi Kamocha Ba'eilim Hashem* — "Who is like You among the mighty, O Lord?" (Exodus 15:11).

The odds were impossible. The Maccabees were a guerrilla force of poorly equipped farmers and priests fighting the professional army of a global superpower. Greek soldiers numbered in the tens of thousands, with elephants, cavalry, and siege weapons. Judah's forces numbered in the hundreds, later thousands, armed mainly with courage and conviction.

Yet through brilliant tactics and sheer determination, they won battle after battle. In 164 BCE, three years after the revolt began, the Maccabees recaptured Jerusalem.

**Chinese Parallel:** This resonates with the Chinese concept of 以弱胜强 (*yǐ ruò shèng qiáng*) — "the weak defeating the strong" — similar to stories like the Battle of Red Cliffs, where a smaller force defeated a massive army not through brute strength but through strategy, conviction, and the refusal to give up.

## The Miracle of the Oil: A Quiet Wonder

After reclaiming Jerusalem, the Maccabees entered the Temple. What they found was heartbreaking: idols everywhere, the altar desecrated, and all the pure olive oil — needed to light the golden *menorah* (candelabrum) — either used up or ritually defiled by the Greeks.

According to Jewish law, the *menorah* in the Temple had to burn continuously, lit with pure olive oil pressed under conditions of ritual purity and sealed by the High Priest. The Maccabees searched the Temple and found only **one small cruse of oil**, still sealed with the High Priest's seal, containing enough oil to burn for just one day.

It would take eight days to prepare new pure oil.

The Maccabees made a choice: they would light the menorah anyway. Even though the oil wouldn't last, they would fulfill the commandment as best they could and trust in God.

Then the miracle happened.

**That one-day supply of oil burned for eight days.**

The Talmud (Shabbat 21b) records this simply and beautifully:

> "When the Greeks entered the Sanctuary, they defiled all the oils. When the Hasmonean monarchy overcame them and emerged victorious, they searched and found only one cruse of oil with the seal of the High Priest, enough to light for only one day. A miracle occurred and they lit from it for eight days. The next year, the Sages instituted those days as holidays with recitation of *Hallel* (psalms of praise) and special thanksgiving."

---

## Why This Miracle Matters: Big Victories and Small Lights

Here's what's striking: the Talmud barely mentions the military victory. When the Sages asked, "What is Chanukah?" they didn't answer, "We commemorate the great military triumph of the Maccabees!" Instead, they focused entirely on the oil.

Why?

Because the military victory, as impressive as it was, was temporary. Within a few generations, the Jewish kingdom fell again. But the miracle of the oil symbolizes something eternal: **the power of small acts of faithfulness**.

The Maccabees didn't have enough oil. By any practical calculation, lighting the menorah was pointless. But they did it anyway. They did what they could with what they had. And God responded to that small act of devotion with a miracle.

**The lesson:** We are responsible for our efforts, not the outcomes. We do what's right even when it seems insufficient. Success or failure is in God's hands.

This is deeply relevant to anyone beginning a Jewish journey in a place with little Jewish infrastructure. You light your Shabbat candles even if you're the only Jew in your city. You study Torah even if you have no teacher nearby. You keep trying even when the resources seem insufficient — because faithfulness, not success, is what God asks of us.

**Chinese Parallel:** This aligns with the famous saying 星星之火，可以燎原 (*xīngxīng zhī huǒ, kěyǐ liáoyuán*) — "A single spark can start a prairie fire." One small light, one act of persistence, can illuminate far more than seems possible.

## How to Observe Chanukah: The Menorah Lighting

Chanukah lasts for eight nights, beginning on the 25th of the Hebrew month of Kislev (usually falling in December). The central ritual is lighting the **chanukiah** (Chanukah menorah), a nine-branched candelabrum.

### The Menorah Setup

- **Eight branches** for the eight nights of Chanukah
- **One "shamash" (helper candle)**, elevated or set apart, used to light the others (we don't use the Chanukah lights themselves for any purpose other than publicizing the miracle)

### When to Light

- **Ideally at nightfall** (about 20-30 minutes after sunset) 
- **The menorah should be visible to the public** — traditionally placed in a doorway or window facing the street, to publicize the miracle
- In times or places of danger, it's acceptable to light indoors

### How to Light: Step-by-Step

**Night 1:**
1. Place one candle on the far right of the menorah, plus the shamash
2. Light the shamash first
3. Recite three blessings (see below)
4. Use the shamash to light the candle
5. Place the shamash in its holder
6. Let the candles burn for at least 30 minutes (they should be large enough to burn this long)

**Night 2:**
1. Place two candles — one on the right, one to its left
2. Light the shamash
3. Recite the first two blessings only
4. Light the newest candle first (the one you just added on the left), then the one to its right
5. The pattern: **add candles from right to left, but light them from left to right** (always lighting the newest one first)

Continue this pattern through all eight nights.

### The Blessings

**First Blessing (every night):**  
*"Baruch Atah Adonai, Eloheinu Melech ha-olam, asher kid'shanu b'mitzvotav v'tzivanu l'hadlik ner shel Chanukah."*  
"Blessed are You, Lord our God, King of the Universe, who has sanctified us with His commandments and commanded us to kindle the Chanukah light."

**Second Blessing (every night):**  
*"Baruch Atah Adonai, Eloheinu Melech ha-olam, she-asah nisim la-avoteinu ba-yamim ha-heim ba-z'man ha-zeh."*  
"Blessed are You, Lord our God, King of the Universe, who performed miracles for our ancestors in those days, at this time."

**Third Blessing (first night only):**  
*"Baruch Atah Adonai, Eloheinu Melech ha-olam, she-hecheyanu v'kiy'manu v'higiyanu la-z'man ha-zeh."*  
"Blessed are You, Lord our God, King of the Universe, who has granted us life, sustained us, and enabled us to reach this season."

After lighting, it's customary to sing "Hanerot Halalu" ("These lights...") and Chanukah songs like "Ma'oz Tzur" (Rock of Ages).

### Other Customs

- **The dreidel** — a spinning top with four Hebrew letters: נ (Nun), ג (Gimel), ה (Hei), ש (Shin) — standing for *Nes Gadol Hayah Sham* ("A great miracle happened there"). In Israel, the shin is replaced with פ (Pei) for *Po* ("here"). The dreidel game was historically used to disguise Torah study when it was forbidden.

- **Traditional foods** — Especially foods fried in oil (to commemorate the miracle): *latkes* (potato pancakes) in Ashkenazi tradition, *sufganiyot* (jelly donuts) in Israeli custom, and various fried delicacies adapted to local cuisines.

- **Gifts and charity** — While gift-giving is a modern custom (influenced by proximity to Christmas in Western countries), giving *tzedakah* (charity) and *Chanukah gelt* (money) to children for performing mitzvot is traditional.

- **No work restrictions** — Unlike Shabbat or most other Jewish holidays, work is permitted on Chanukah. However, there's a custom that women refrain from work while the Chanukah candles are burning (at least the first half-hour), in honor of the courage of Jewish women like Judith during the Maccabean period.

## Deeper Lessons: What Chanukah Teaches Us Today

### 1. The Danger of Assimilation

The real enemy in the Chanukah story wasn't the Greeks — it was the *Hellenists*, the Jews who abandoned Torah. Antiochus wouldn't have bothered with the Jews if many Jews hadn't already been eager to assimilate.

**Lesson for today:** Assimilation can dilute our unique heritage and spiritual legacy. Chanukah reminds us to embrace our distinctiveness, to proudly live as Jews, and to pass on our traditions to future generations — even when surrounded by a culture that doesn't share our values.

### 2. Light Drives Out Darkness

The Chanukah lights aren't just commemorative — they're *instructional*. We don't light all eight candles at once; we add one each night. The principle is called *ma'alin b'kedushah* — "we ascend in holiness."

**Lesson:** Spiritual growth is gradual. You don't become a fully observant Jew overnight. You add one mitzvah, then another. One more Shabbat. One more page of Torah study. Consistency over time creates transformation.

### 3. Publicizing the Miracle

Unlike almost every other Jewish ritual, which can be performed privately, the Chanukah menorah is meant to be *seen*. We light it in the window, visible to passersby.

**Why?** Because Chanukah occurred during a time of rampant assimilation. The Sages wanted to inspire all Jews — even those who had drifted far from tradition — to remember the miracles God performed and to return.

**Lesson:** When you're the only Jew in your city or workplace, your visible Jewish practice — your Shabbat candles, your mezuzah, your refusal to compromise on values — can inspire others. You don't have to hide your light.

**Chinese Parallel:** Just as Chinese families worldwide light lanterns during the Lantern Festival to symbolize reunion and hope, and display them publicly for all to see, the Chanukah menorah is a public declaration of Jewish identity and hope.

### 4. Few Can Overcome Many

The military lesson of Chanukah is clear: You don't need to be the majority. You don't need overwhelming resources. You need courage, clarity of purpose, and trust that when you stand for what is right, you are not alone.

**Lesson:** If you're a lone Jew in a sea of non-Jews, or a beginning convert with no community nearby, Chanukah says: your faithfulness matters. One person lighting one candle in a dark place can change everything.

## Practical Application: Chanukah in China (or Anywhere Without a Jewish Community)

**If you're the only Jew in your city**, here's how to observe Chanukah meaningfully:

1. **Order a chanukiah and candles online** — See Article #40 ("Buying Judaica Online") for trusted vendors who ship to Asia.

2. **Light in your window** — Make your light visible. You never know who might see it and be inspired.

3. **Connect virtually** — Join online Chanukah gatherings hosted by Chabad.org or other Jewish organizations. Light your menorah "together" via video call with Jews around the world.

4. **Study the story** — Read about the Maccabees, the miracle, and the spiritual lessons. The more you understand, the more meaningful the ritual becomes.

5. **Make it joyful** — Cook fried foods (Chinese cuisine has plenty of delicious fried options!), play dreidel, sing Chanukah songs. Joy is a mitzvah too.

6. **Invite others** — If you have non-Jewish friends or family curious about Judaism, Chanukah is a wonderful entry point. It's accessible, visually beautiful, and its message of standing for one's identity resonates across cultures.

## Key Takeaways

- **Chanukah commemorates** the Maccabean revolt (167-160 BCE, during China's Western Han Dynasty) and the eight-day miracle of the oil when the Temple was rededicated.

- **The real battle** was not just military but spiritual — resisting forced assimilation and preserving Jewish identity and Torah study.

- **The miracle of the oil** teaches that God responds to our small acts of faithfulness, even when our resources seem insufficient.

- **The menorah lighting** is done publicly to inspire others and to proclaim the miracle. We add one light each night, symbolizing gradual spiritual growth.

- **The holiday's message** — a small light can drive out great darkness; a committed few can overcome overwhelming odds — is timeless and universal.

- **For Jews in isolation**, Chanukah is especially relevant: your solitary light matters, your persistence counts, and you are part of a global people celebrating the same miracle.
