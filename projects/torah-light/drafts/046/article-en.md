## Introduction

Imagine a festival where you are commanded to feast, drink wine, dress in costumes, exchange gifts of food with friends, and give generously to the poor — all while celebrating one of history's greatest reversals, when a genocidal plot against the Jewish people was miraculously turned on its head. This is Purim, one of the most joyous and unusual holidays in Judaism.

Purim commemorates events that took place approximately 2,400 years ago — during what corresponds to China's Warring States period (戰國時代, 475-221 BCE) — in the ancient Persian Empire. At a time when the Jewish people faced annihilation at the hands of the wicked Haman, Queen Esther and her cousin Mordechai courageously intervened, and the plot was foiled. The day designated for Jewish destruction became instead a day of triumph and celebration.

What makes Purim unique among Jewish holidays is that God's name is never once mentioned in the Megillah (the Scroll of Esther). Yet the entire story is a testament to divine providence working behind the scenes — the hidden hand of God orchestrating a series of "coincidences" that saved an entire people.

## The Story of Purim: A Tale of Reversal

### The Setup

The story begins in the capital city of Shushan, where King Achashverosh (Ahasuerus) ruled over 127 provinces from India to Ethiopia. After deposing his queen, Vashti, for refusing his command, the king held a beauty contest to find a new queen. Esther, a Jewish orphan raised by her cousin Mordechai, was chosen — but she kept her Jewish identity secret on Mordechai's advice.

### The Villain Rises

Haman, a descendant of the ancient enemy of the Jewish people (Amalek), was promoted to the king's highest position. He demanded that everyone bow to him, but Mordechai the Jew refused. Enraged, Haman plotted not just to kill Mordechai, but to annihilate all the Jews in the empire. He cast lots (*pur* in Persian, hence the name "Purim") to determine the date of destruction: the 13th day of the month of Adar.

### The Hidden Miracle Unfolds

Through a series of seemingly coincidental events — each carefully orchestrated by divine providence — the tables were turned:

- Esther revealed her Jewish identity to the king at a carefully planned banquet
- The king discovered that Mordechai had once saved his life, and Haman was forced to honor his enemy publicly
- Haman's plot was exposed, and he was hanged on the very gallows he had built for Mordechai
- The Jews were granted the right to defend themselves, and their enemies were defeated
- The 13th of Adar, meant to be a day of destruction, became a day of victory

### The Reversal

As the Megillah states: "The month which had been transformed for them from one of sorrow to gladness, and from mourning to festivity" (Esther 9:22). This theme of reversal — from darkness to light, from despair to salvation — is the essence of Purim.

## The Hidden Hand of God

The Book of Esther is unique among biblical books: God's name never appears in it. Not once. This absence is deliberate and profoundly meaningful.

Unlike the Exodus from Egypt, where God split the sea and brought open plagues upon the oppressors, or the Chanukah story, where a miraculous oil burned for eight days, Purim's miracle is *hidden*. Everything appears to happen naturally. A beauty contest. A sleepless night. A forgotten good deed. A queen's courage.

Yet when we step back and see the whole picture, we realize: every "coincidence" was part of a divine plan. The king's insomnia on precisely the night he needed to remember Mordechai's loyalty. Esther's position as queen *just in time* to save her people. Haman's downfall through his own arrogance.

This is the essence of *Hester Panim* — the "hidden face" of God. In Hebrew, the name "Esther" (אסתר) shares a root with the word for "hidden" (*hester*, הסתר). Esther herself represents the hidden nature of God's involvement in the world.

### A Chinese Parallel: Hidden Blessings

This theme resonates with the famous Chinese story of 塞翁失马 (*Sàiwēngshīmǎ*) — "The old man at the frontier loses his horse." What seems like a disaster may turn out to be a blessing in disguise, and what appears fortunate may conceal misfortune. The wisdom is to recognize that we cannot always see the full picture from where we stand. Purim teaches that God works through the fabric of history itself, turning sorrow into joy and defeat into victory.

## The Four Mitzvot of Purim

Purim is observed through four specific commandments, each designed to capture a different dimension of the holiday:

### 1. **Reading the Megillah** (*Mikra Megillah*)

The Scroll of Esther is read twice — once at night and once during the day. Men, women, and children gather in synagogues to hear the dramatic story. Whenever Haman's name is mentioned, people make noise with *graggers* (noisemakers), stamping feet, and shouting to "blot out" his name — fulfilling the commandment to erase the memory of Amalek.

**Why twice?** Because the miracle happened both at night (when Esther risked her life approaching the king) and by day (when the Jews defended themselves).

### 2. **The Festive Purim Meal** (*Seudat Purim*)

A sumptuous feast is held on Purim afternoon, extending into the evening. It includes meat, wine, and special foods. The Talmud states: "A person is obligated to drink on Purim until he cannot distinguish between 'Cursed is Haman' and 'Blessed is Mordechai.'"

This does not mean drunkenness to the point of harm, but rather drinking enough wine to experience joy and perhaps take a nap — symbolizing that we "don't know" where salvation will come from, just as in the Purim story where salvation came from unexpected places.

**Connection to Chinese culture:** Like Chinese New Year (*Chūn Jié*, 春節) or Mid-Autumn Festival (*Zhōng Qiū Jié*, 中秋節), Purim is a time when families gather for a festive meal, celebrating together with special foods.

### 3. **Sending Gifts to Friends** (*Mishloach Manot*)

Every person is required to send at least two types of ready-to-eat food to at least one friend. This mitzvah strengthens communal bonds and spreads joy.

The gifts are traditionally delivered by a messenger (not personally), reminding us that just as the news of salvation in the Purim story was carried by messengers, we too become messengers of joy and kindness.

**Chinese parallel:** This is similar to the tradition of exchanging mooncakes during the Mid-Autumn Festival, or giving red envelopes (*hóngbāo*, 红包) during Chinese New Year — gestures that reinforce community bonds and shared celebration.

### 4. **Gifts to the Poor** (*Matanot La'evyonim*)

One must give monetary gifts to at least two poor people on Purim day. This is considered the most important of the four mitzvot.

The Rambam (Maimonides) writes: "It is preferable to give more to the poor than to spend lavishly on the festive meal or gifts to friends. For there is no greater joy than to gladden the hearts of the poor, the orphans, the widows, and the strangers."

On Purim, we do not scrutinize who is truly needy. Anyone who extends their hand is given. The day embodies the principle that no one should be excluded from the celebration.

**Resonance with Confucian values:** This aligns beautifully with Confucian ideals of benevolence (*rén*, 仁) and righteousness (*yì*, 义), as well as Buddhist compassion (*cíbēi*, 慈悲). Caring for the vulnerable is a universal virtue.

## Practical Application: Celebrating Purim Today

### For Someone New to Judaism

If you're celebrating Purim for the first time:

1. **Find a Megillah reading** — Check with your local Chabad house or Jewish community. In China, Chabad centers in Shanghai, Beijing, Hong Kong, and other major cities hold public Megillah readings.

2. **Prepare *Mishloach Manot*** — Fill a basket with at least two types of ready-to-eat food (cookies, fruit, wine, chocolates). Deliver it to a Jewish friend or neighbor.

3. **Give to the poor** — Set aside money to give to at least two people in need. Many communities have Purim charity funds where you can contribute.

4. **Dress up!** — Purim is the one day of the year when Jewish custom encourages wearing costumes. Children (and adults!) dress as Megillah characters or anything they choose. It's a reminder that things are not always as they appear — just like in the Purim story.

5. **Attend the Purim feast** — Join a community celebration or host your own festive meal with family and friends.

### A Word on Joy

Purim teaches that joy is not just a feeling — it's an act of faith. Even in difficult circumstances, we celebrate because we trust that God is working behind the scenes, weaving together a story of redemption.

This is not superficial happiness. It is joy rooted in the recognition that even when we cannot see God's presence, He is there. Even when events seem random, they are part of a larger plan.

## Key Takeaways

- **Purim celebrates the hidden miracle** of Jewish survival in ancient Persia, where God's name is never mentioned but His hand is evident throughout
- **The four mitzvot** — Megillah reading, festive meal, gifts to friends, and charity to the poor — create a framework for communal celebration and kindness
- **The reversal from mourning to joy** is central to Purim, teaching us that despair can turn to hope when we trust in divine providence
- **Giving to the poor is paramount** — true joy comes not from personal indulgence but from bringing happiness to others
- **Costumes and celebration** remind us that reality has hidden depths, and things are not always as they appear

## Cultural Connection: Purim and Chinese Wisdom

Both Jewish and Chinese traditions recognize that:

- **Appearances can be deceiving** — The Purim story unfolds through palace intrigue, hidden identities, and unexpected reversals. Similarly, Chinese philosophy teaches 道可道，非常道 (*Dào kě dào, fēi cháng dào*) — "The way that can be spoken is not the eternal Way." Truth often lies beneath the surface.

- **Adversity can become blessing** — The Chinese idiom 塞翁失马，焉知非福 teaches that misfortune may turn to fortune. Purim is the ultimate embodiment of this principle.

- **Community and charity elevate the soul** — Both traditions emphasize that festivals are not just for personal enjoyment but for strengthening communal bonds and caring for the vulnerable.
