## Introduction

Imagine coming home on a cold winter afternoon to the rich aroma of a stew that has been slowly simmering since the night before. The meat is so tender it falls apart at the touch of a spoon, the beans are creamy, and the potatoes have absorbed all the deep, complex flavors of the broth. This is *cholent* — perhaps the most iconic dish of the Jewish Shabbat table.

For many Chinese readers encountering Jewish traditions for the first time, the concept of "not cooking" on a special day might seem puzzling. How can a family enjoy hot, nourishing meals without cooking? The answer lies in centuries of Jewish culinary creativity that resulted in dishes like cholent, kugel, challah, and gefilte fish — foods specifically designed to honor Shabbat's prohibition on creative labor while still bringing warmth and delight to the table.

In this article, we'll explore these traditional Shabbat foods and show you how to adapt them for the Chinese kitchen, using ingredients and techniques familiar to Chinese cooks. Whether you're preparing Shabbat for the first time or simply curious about Jewish food culture, these recipes connect ancient wisdom with the flavors of home.

## Understanding Shabbat Food Laws

### Why We Don't Cook on Shabbat

During the period when the ancient Israelites built the Tabernacle in the wilderness — approximately during what corresponds to China's Shang Dynasty (商朝, ~1600-1046 BCE) — there were 39 categories of creative work necessary to construct this portable sanctuary. These 39 categories became the exact types of labor that Jews refrain from on Shabbat in order to distinguish this day of rest from an ordinary weekday.

One of these 39 prohibited labors is *cooking* — specifically, the act of applying heat to transform food from raw to cooked. This means that on Shabbat, Jewish families cannot light fires, turn on stoves, or actively cook food. However, keeping food warm that was cooked *before* Shabbat began is permissible.

This legal framework led to one of the most important principles in Jewish cooking: **preparation is everything**. Like the careful advance preparations Chinese families make before the Lunar New Year (春节) when shops close and work stops, Jewish families spend Friday afternoon (before sunset) preparing all their Shabbat meals.

### The Genius of Cholent

*Cholent* (also called *chamim* in Hebrew, meaning "hot foods," or *hamin*) emerged as the perfect solution to this challenge. This hearty stew is assembled on Friday afternoon, placed in a low-heat oven or on a covered flame, and left to cook slowly throughout Friday night and all day Saturday. By Shabbat lunchtime, it has transformed into a rich, aromatic one-pot meal.

The genius of cholent mirrors the Chinese culinary philosophy of 慢炖 (*màn dùn*, slow-braising): time and gentle heat create depth of flavor impossible to achieve with quick cooking. Just as Cantonese *bāo tāng* (煲汤, slow-simmered soups) are famous for their nourishing properties and complex taste, cholent develops its character through patient, overnight cooking.

## Classic Cholent: Recipe for the Chinese Kitchen

### Ingredients

**For the Base:**
- 500g beef stew meat (牛腩, *niú nán*, beef brisket, cut into 5cm chunks)
- 250g dried kidney beans (红豆, *hóng dòu*) or white beans (白豆, *bái dòu*), soaked overnight
- 4 medium potatoes (土豆, *tǔ dòu*), peeled and quartered
- 1 large onion (洋葱, *yáng cōng*), diced
- 6 cloves garlic (大蒜, *dà suàn*), minced
- 3 tablespoons vegetable oil (植物油, *zhí wù yóu*)

**Seasonings:**
- 2 teaspoons salt (盐, *yán*)
- 1 teaspoon black pepper (黑胡椒, *hēi hú jiāo*)
- 1 teaspoon sweet paprika (甜椒粉, *tián jiāo fěn*)
- ½ teaspoon ground cumin (孜然粉, *zī rán fěn*) — optional, adds Middle Eastern warmth
- 2 bay leaves (月桂叶, *yuè guì yè*)

**Liquid:**
- 6-8 cups hot water or beef stock (牛骨汤, *niú gǔ tāng*)

**Optional Add-ins (choose what appeals to you):**
- 2 beef marrow bones (牛骨, *niú gǔ*) for extra richness
- 200g pearl barley (大麦, *dà mài*) or wheat berries
- 3 hard-boiled eggs in their shells (they'll turn golden-brown and creamy)
- A handful of dried shiitake mushrooms (香菇, *xiāng gū*) for umami depth

### Instructions

**Friday Afternoon Preparation (Before Shabbat Begins):**

1. **Prepare the beans:** If you forgot to soak your beans overnight, place them in boiling water for 10 minutes, then drain. This quick-soak method works well enough for cholent.

2. **Brown the meat (optional but recommended):** In a large, heavy pot or Dutch oven (铸铁锅, *zhù tiě guō*), heat the oil over medium-high heat. Brown the beef chunks on all sides until they develop a deep golden crust. This step adds flavor, though many cooks skip it when pressed for time.

3. **Sauté aromatics:** In the same pot, add the diced onion and cook until softened and translucent, about 5 minutes. Add the minced garlic and cook for another minute until fragrant.

4. **Assemble the cholent:** Add the browned beef back to the pot (if you removed it). Add the soaked beans, quartered potatoes, seasonings (salt, pepper, paprika, cumin, bay leaves), and any optional ingredients. Nestle the eggs (in their shells) into the mixture if using.

5. **Add liquid:** Pour in enough hot water or beef stock to cover all ingredients by about 2-3 cm. The liquid will reduce during the long cooking time.

6. **Bring to a simmer:** Heat the pot on the stovetop until the liquid comes to a gentle simmer.

7. **Transfer to low heat:** You have several options:
   - Place in an oven preheated to 100°C (210°F)
   - Keep on the stovetop on the absolute lowest flame, using a *blech* (metal sheet) to diffuse heat
   - Transfer to an electric slow cooker set to "low"
   - Use an electric hot plate set to low temperature

8. **Cover and cook:** Cover the pot tightly with a lid. If your lid doesn't seal well, place aluminum foil under the lid to trap steam. The cholent will cook slowly for 18-24 hours.

**Shabbat Lunchtime (Saturday):**

9. **Serve:** Carefully remove the lid (watch for steam!). The cholent will be thick, rich, and deeply browned. The eggs (if you added them) will have a creamy, golden interior. Ladle generous portions into bowls.

### Serving Suggestions

- Serve with fresh challah bread (see recipe below) for dipping in the rich broth
- Top with a sprinkle of fresh herbs if prepared before Shabbat
- Accompany with pickled vegetables (泡菜, *pào cài*) for contrast
- Some families enjoy a dollop of Chinese hot sauce (辣椒酱, *là jiāo jiàng*) on the side

## Challah: The Shabbat Bread

While cholent simmers, the table is graced by *challah* (חלה), the special braided egg bread blessed at the start of every Shabbat meal. Challah is remarkably similar to Chinese sweet breads like *nǎi huáng bāo* (奶黄包, custard buns) in its soft, slightly sweet texture and eggy richness.

### Simple Challah Recipe

**Ingredients:**

- 500g all-purpose flour (中筋面粉, *zhōng jīn miàn fěn*)
- 60g sugar (白糖, *bái táng*)
- 7g instant yeast (酵母, *jiào mǔ*) — one packet
- 1 teaspoon salt (盐, *yán*)
- 2 large eggs (鸡蛋, *jī dàn*)
- 60ml vegetable oil (植物油, *zhí wù yóu*)
- 240ml warm water (温水, *wēn shuǐ*)

**For the Egg Wash:**
- 1 egg beaten with 1 tablespoon water

**Optional Toppings:**
- Sesame seeds (芝麻, *zhī ma*)
- Poppy seeds (罂粟籽, *yīng sù zǐ*)

**Instructions:**

1. **Make the dough:** In a large bowl, mix flour, sugar, yeast, and salt. In a separate bowl, whisk together eggs, oil, and warm water. Pour the wet ingredients into the dry ingredients and mix until a shaggy dough forms.

2. **Knead:** Turn the dough onto a floured surface and knead for 8-10 minutes until smooth and elastic. (Or use a stand mixer with dough hook for 5-6 minutes.) The dough should be soft but not sticky.

3. **First rise:** Place the dough in an oiled bowl, cover with a damp towel, and let rise in a warm place for 1-1.5 hours until doubled in size.

4. **Divide and shape:** Punch down the dough and divide into three equal pieces. Roll each piece into a long rope about 40cm long. Pinch the three ropes together at one end, then braid them just like braiding hair. Pinch the other end to seal.

5. **Second rise:** Place the braided challah on a lined baking sheet, cover, and let rise for another 30-45 minutes until puffy.

6. **Egg wash and bake:** Brush the challah gently with the egg wash and sprinkle with sesame seeds if desired. Bake in a preheated oven at 180°C (350°F) for 25-30 minutes until deep golden brown and hollow-sounding when tapped on the bottom.

7. **Cool:** Let the challah cool on a wire rack before slicing. Many families bake two challahs for Shabbat — we say a blessing over two loaves to remember the double portion of manna that fell in the wilderness on Fridays.

### Cultural Connection: Braided Bread Traditions

The braiding of challah has many symbolic interpretations in Jewish tradition — three strands can represent truth, peace, and justice, or the interweaving of the physical and spiritual. This resonates with Chinese cultural symbolism where the number three (三, *sān*) represents harmony and the balance of heaven, earth, and humanity. The act of braiding also appears in Chinese culture through intricate knot-work (中国结, *zhōng guó jié*) which carries symbolic meaning and brings together multiple strands into unified beauty.

## Kugel: The Beloved Casserole

*Kugel* (קוגל) is a baked casserole that can be either sweet or savory. Because it's baked before Shabbat and served at room temperature or kept warm, it perfectly fits the requirements of Shabbat food. The most popular varieties are potato kugel and noodle kugel.

### Potato Kugel (土豆焙盘, *Tǔ Dòu Bèi Pán*)

This savory dish is somewhat similar to Chinese *shredded potato pancakes* (土豆丝饼, *tǔ dòu sī bǐng*), but baked in a large pan instead of fried.

**Ingredients:**

- 6 large potatoes (土豆, *tǔ dòu*), peeled
- 1 large onion (洋葱, *yáng cōng*)
- 3 eggs (鸡蛋, *jī dàn*)
- 80ml vegetable oil (植物油, *zhí wù yóu*)
- 60g potato starch or flour (土豆淀粉/面粉, *tǔ dòu diàn fěn/miàn fěn*)
- 2 teaspoons salt (盐, *yán*)
- ½ teaspoon black pepper (黑胡椒, *hēi hú jiāo*)

**Instructions:**

1. **Preheat oven:** Set your oven to 200°C (400°F). Generously grease a 23x33cm (9x13 inch) baking dish with oil.

2. **Grate vegetables:** Using a box grater or food processor, coarsely grate the potatoes and onion. Place the grated mixture in a clean kitchen towel and squeeze out as much liquid as possible. (This is important for a crispy top!)

3. **Mix:** In a large bowl, beat the eggs with the oil, salt, and pepper. Add the grated potato mixture and potato starch. Mix until well combined.

4. **Bake:** Pour the mixture into the prepared baking dish and smooth the top. Bake for 1 hour, or until the top is deeply golden brown and crispy, and the kugel is cooked through.

5. **Serve:** Cut into squares. Potato kugel is delicious served alongside cholent or as part of the Friday night meal.

### Noodle Kugel (甜面条焙盘, *Tián Miàn Tiáo Bèi Pán*)

Sweet noodle kugel is a favorite of children and adults alike. It's somewhat reminiscent of Chinese sweet rice dishes served at festivals.

**Ingredients:**

- 500g wide egg noodles (宽蛋面, *kuān dàn miàn*)
- 4 eggs (鸡蛋, *jī dàn*)
- 100g sugar (白糖, *bái táng*)
- 120g cottage cheese or farmer's cheese (白软干酪, *bái ruǎn gān lào*) — or substitute with Chinese sweet cheese if available
- 80ml melted butter or vegetable oil (黄油/植物油, *huáng yóu/zhí wù yóu*)
- 1 teaspoon vanilla extract (香草精, *xiāng cǎo jīng*) — optional
- ½ teaspoon cinnamon (肉桂粉, *ròu guì fěn*)

**Optional add-ins:**
- 100g raisins (葡萄干, *pú táo gān*)
- Sliced apples or pears

**Instructions:**

1. **Cook noodles:** Boil the egg noodles according to package directions until tender. Drain and rinse with cold water.

2. **Mix wet ingredients:** In a large bowl, beat together the eggs, sugar, cottage cheese, melted butter, vanilla, and cinnamon until smooth.

3. **Combine:** Add the cooked noodles to the egg mixture and toss until the noodles are evenly coated. If using raisins or fruit, fold them in now.

4. **Bake:** Pour into a greased 23x33cm baking dish. Bake at 180°C (350°F) for 45-50 minutes until the top is golden and set.

5. **Serve:** Cut into squares and serve warm or at room temperature. Sweet kugel can be enjoyed as a side dish or even as a dessert.

## Gefilte Fish: A Shabbat Tradition

*Gefilte fish* (געפילטע פיש) — literally "stuffed fish" in Yiddish — has an interesting origin story connected to Shabbat observance. One of the 39 prohibited labors on Shabbat is *borer* (separating or sorting) — specifically, separating things you want from things you don't want. When eating fish, this means carefully removing the flesh from the tiny bones.

To make fish easier to eat on Shabbat, Jewish cooks in medieval Europe (during what corresponds to China's Song Dynasty, 宋朝, 960-1279 CE) developed a solution: they ground the fish meat, mixed it with seasonings, formed it into patties or loaves, and then poached it. The result is boneless fish that can be enjoyed without any work of separation.

Traditional gefilte fish is made from a mixture of freshwater fish — typically carp, pike, and whitefish. For the Chinese kitchen, you can adapt this using readily available fish like grass carp (草鱼, *cǎo yú*), crucian carp (鲫鱼, *jì yú*), or even tilapia (罗非鱼, *luó fēi yú*).

### Simple Gefilte Fish

**Ingredients:**

- 1kg boneless white fish fillets (白鱼肉, *bái yú ròu*), cut into chunks
- 2 medium onions (洋葱, *yáng cōng*), divided
- 2 eggs (鸡蛋, *jī dàn*)
- 60g matzah meal or fine breadcrumbs (面包糠, *miàn bāo kāng*)
- 2 teaspoons salt (盐, *yán*)
- 1 teaspoon sugar (白糖, *bái táng*)
- ½ teaspoon white pepper (白胡椒, *bái hú jiāo*)
- 2 carrots (胡萝卜, *hú luó bo*), sliced into rounds

**For the poaching liquid:**
- 8 cups water
- Fish heads and bones (if available)
- 1 onion, sliced
- 2 carrots
- 1 tablespoon salt
- 1 teaspoon sugar

**Instructions:**

1. **Prepare poaching liquid:** In a large, wide pot, combine water, fish scraps, onion, carrots, salt, and sugar. Bring to a simmer while you prepare the fish mixture.

2. **Make fish mixture:** Finely chop 1 onion. In a food processor, combine the fish chunks, chopped onion, eggs, matzah meal, salt, sugar, and white pepper. Pulse until the mixture is finely ground and holds together. (Or use a mortar and traditional grinding method if you prefer.)

3. **Form patties:** With wet hands, shape the fish mixture into oval patties about 8cm long and 3cm thick. You should get about 12-14 patties.

4. **Poach:** Gently lower the fish patties into the simmering poaching liquid. Add the carrot slices. Cover and simmer very gently for 1.5 to 2 hours. The liquid should barely bubble — vigorous boiling will make the fish fall apart.

5. **Cool:** Remove the pot from heat and let the fish cool in the liquid. Once cool, refrigerate the fish in the liquid overnight. The liquid will form a jelly that is traditional and flavorful.

6. **Serve:** Serve each piece of gefilte fish with a slice of carrot on top and a small amount of the jellied broth. Traditional accompaniments include *chrein* (horseradish sauce) — which Chinese palates might recognize as similar to wasabi (芥末, *jiè mò*) in its sharp, sinus-clearing heat.

### Modern Shortcut

Many modern Jewish families buy prepared gefilte fish in jars or frozen. There's no shame in this! The important thing is the tradition of enjoying fish at the Shabbat table, not whether you made it from scratch.

## Other Shabbat Delights

### Kubaneh (库巴内面包, *Kù Bā Nèi Miàn Bāo*)

This is a Yemenite overnight bread, similar in concept to challah but with a completely different texture and preparation method. Kubaneh is a pull-apart bread that bakes overnight in a covered pot, resulting in soft, buttery layers — somewhat like Chinese steamed *mantou* (馒头) in texture, but richer and with a golden crust.

The dough is made from simple ingredients (flour, water, yeast, salt, sugar), rolled thin, brushed with butter or oil, and then rolled up and placed in a pot in a spiral. It bakes overnight at low temperature alongside the cholent, and by morning it has transformed into pull-apart rolls with a slightly sweet flavor and incredibly soft interior.

### Jachnun (哈奇努面包, *Hā Qí Nǔ Miàn Bāo*)

Another Yemenite overnight bread, *jachnun* is rolled dough that's brushed with butter or oil and then slow-baked overnight. The result is crispy on the outside and layered like puff pastry inside — reminiscent of Chinese *shǒu zhuā bǐng* (手抓饼, hand-pulled flatbread) or *cōng yóu bǐng* (葱油饼, scallion pancakes) in its flaky layers, though jachnun is sweeter.

Jachnun is traditionally served with hard-boiled eggs, crushed tomatoes (often seasoned with *zhug*, a spicy herb sauce), and honey for dipping. The combination of savory and sweet might seem unusual, but it creates a complex and satisfying breakfast after morning Shabbat prayers.

## The Philosophy Behind Shabbat Food

### More Than Just Rules

While the specific rules about not cooking on Shabbat might seem technical or legalistic at first, they serve a deeper purpose that resonates across cultures. Shabbat food traditions teach several important lessons:

**1. The Value of Preparation**

The intensive preparation required before Shabbat cultivates mindfulness and intention. When you spend Friday afternoon preparing food for the next day, you are consciously creating space for rest. This mirrors the Chinese philosophical concept of *yù bèi* (预备, preparation) — the idea that proper preparation creates the conditions for success and peace of mind.

**2. Delayed Gratification**

Cholent sitting in the oven for 18 hours is the ultimate exercise in patience. You cannot rush it or speed it up. This teaches the value of *màn huó* (慢活, slow living) — allowing things to develop naturally in their own time. In our fast-paced modern world, there is wisdom in practices that require us to wait.

**3. Community and Sharing**

Many of these Shabbat dishes — cholent, large challahs, kugels — are designed to serve a crowd. They encourage gathering, hospitality, and sharing with others. This reflects the Chinese value of *tuán yuán* (团圆, reunion and togetherness), where food is never just sustenance but an expression of relationship and care.

**4. Continuity Across Generations**

When you make your grandmother's cholent recipe or braid challah the way your mother taught you, you connect with countless generations who did the same. Like Chinese families passing down recipes for *jiǎo zi* (饺子, dumplings) or *yuè bǐng* (月饼, mooncakes), Jewish food traditions create a living link to the past.

## Practical Tips for the Chinese Kitchen

### Ingredient Substitutions

**Kosher meat:** If you're in an area without kosher-certified beef, you can make vegetarian cholent using additional beans, lentils, and mushrooms. Some Chinese Jews use lamb, which is closer to kosher guidelines than pork.

**Matzah meal:** Substitute with fine breadcrumbs (面包糠, *miàn bāo kāng*) or even finely ground crackers.

**Cottage cheese for kugel:** If dairy products commonly used in Western cooking are hard to find, you can make sweet kugel with just eggs, sugar, and oil — it will be less creamy but still delicious.

### Equipment

**Dutch oven or heavy pot:** Essential for cholent. Look for a *zhù tiě guō* (铸铁锅, cast iron pot) with a tight-fitting lid. Le Creuset or similar enameled cast iron works perfectly, but a heavy stainless steel pot will do.

**Slow cooker:** An electric slow cooker (慢炖锅, *màn dùn guō*) is perhaps the easiest modern solution for cholent. Set it on "low" before Shabbat and let it work its magic.

**Blech:** This is a metal sheet placed over a stovetop flame to diffuse and lower the heat for overnight cooking. In Chinese kitchens, you can improvise with a thick aluminum sheet or even an overturned wok lid.

### Timing for Shabbat

Shabbat begins at sunset on Friday and ends at nightfall (when three stars are visible) on Saturday. The exact times change each week based on the solar calendar. If you're observing Shabbat for the first time, look up your city's candle-lighting time online.

**Friday afternoon checklist:**
- 3-4 hours before sunset: Begin cholent preparation
- 2 hours before: Bake challah and kugels
- 1 hour before: Set the table, prepare cold salads
- 18 minutes before sunset: Light candles and begin Shabbat

## A Week-by-Week Approach

If you're new to Shabbat cooking, don't feel you need to make everything at once! Here's a gentle progression:

**Week 1:** Try just challah. Serve with a simple purchased meal. Practice the blessing over the bread.

**Week 2:** Add cholent. Keep it simple — beef, beans, potatoes, onions. Let it cook overnight and enjoy it for lunch.

**Week 3:** Try potato kugel or noodle kugel. Serve alongside purchased rotisserie chicken and challah.

**Week 4:** Experiment with gefilte fish or kubaneh if you're feeling adventurous, or simply repeat and refine your favorites from previous weeks.

The goal is not perfection but presence — creating space for rest, family, and spiritual reflection through the medium of food.

## Conclusion

Cholent, challah, kugel, and gefilte fish are more than just recipes — they are edible expressions of a four-thousand-year-old tradition. Through these foods, Jewish families have carried their identity across continents and centuries, adapting local ingredients while preserving core practices.

For Chinese readers exploring Jewish traditions, these dishes offer a delicious entry point into understanding how Jews sanctify time, honor rest, and build community. Like the slow-simmered soups that Chinese families have perfected over millennia, Shabbat foods teach that the best things cannot be rushed — they require patience, preparation, and love.

Whether you're preparing for your first Shabbat or simply want to understand Jewish food culture better, remember that every great tradition started with someone willing to try something new. The warmth of challah fresh from the oven, the rich aroma of cholent filling your home, and the satisfaction of sharing these foods with family and friends — these are gifts that transcend culture and language.

*Shabbat Shalom* (שבת שלום) — may your Shabbat be filled with peace, rest, and delicious food.

## Key Takeaways

- **Cholent** is a slow-cooked stew that cooks overnight before Shabbat, allowing families to enjoy hot food without cooking on the day of rest
- **Challah** is the braided egg bread blessed at every Shabbat meal, symbolizing abundance and the double portion of manna
- **Kugel** (potato or noodle casserole) can be served warm or at room temperature, making it perfect for Shabbat
- **Gefilte fish** was created to make eating fish easier on Shabbat by removing the need to separate bones
- Jewish food traditions emphasize **preparation, patience, community, and continuity** — values that resonate across cultures
- You can adapt traditional recipes using ingredients commonly found in Chinese kitchens
- Start simple and build your Shabbat cooking skills gradually — the tradition welcomes newcomers
