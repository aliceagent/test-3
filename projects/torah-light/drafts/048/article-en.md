## Introduction

Picture this: It's late at night, and you're holding a single candle. Room by room, you search every corner of your home — not for treasure, but for bread crumbs. This isn't spring cleaning. This is *bedikat chametz* (בְּדִיקַת חָמֵץ), the ritual search for leavened products before Passover, and it's one of the most hands-on mitzvot in Judaism.

For someone new to Jewish practice — especially if you're preparing for Passover for the first time in a place like China, far from established Jewish communities — the prospect of removing all chametz from your home might feel overwhelming. Where do you even start? What exactly counts as chametz? And why does this matter so much?

This article will walk you through the complete chametz removal process, room by room, step by step. By the end, you'll understand not just *what* to do, but *why* we do it — and how this ancient practice connects to the Exodus story that happened nearly thirty-five hundred years ago, during what corresponds to China's Shang Dynasty (商朝, approximately 1600-1046 BCE).

## What Is Chametz?

Before we can remove chametz, we need to understand what it is.

**Chametz (חָמֵץ)** refers to any food product made from wheat, barley, rye, oats, or spelt that has come into contact with water and been allowed to ferment or rise for more than 18 minutes. This includes:

- Bread, bagels, rolls
- Pasta, noodles
- Crackers, pretzels
- Cookies, cakes, pastries
- Beer and certain alcoholic beverages
- Cereal containing these grains
- Any food with these ingredients

It also includes even the tiniest crumb of these products. During Passover, we cannot eat chametz, benefit from it, or even own it. This is based on the Torah's command: "Seven days you shall eat unleavened bread... for whoever eats leavened bread... that soul shall be cut off from Israel" (Exodus 12:15).

**Why such strictness?** When the Israelites left Egypt, they left in such haste that their bread didn't have time to rise. The unleavened bread (matzah) symbolizes humility and the speed of redemption. Chametz, by contrast, represents ego and spiritual inflation — the puffed-up self that enslaves us as surely as Pharaoh enslaved our ancestors.

**Cultural Parallel:** In traditional Chinese culture, the custom of sweeping and cleaning before the Lunar New Year (扫尘, sǎochén) shares a similar symbolic purpose — removing the old year's accumulated dust and bad luck to make room for fresh blessings. Pesach cleaning serves both a practical and spiritual renewal.

## The Three Stages of Chametz Removal

Removing chametz is not a one-step process. Jewish law divides it into three distinct stages:

### 1. **Cleaning (before the 13th of Nissan)**
General household cleaning to remove chametz from your living spaces.

### 2. **Searching (night of the 14th of Nissan)**
The ritual candle-lit search called *bedikat chametz* (בְּדִיקַת חָמֵץ).

### 3. **Burning (morning of the 14th of Nissan)**
The formal destruction of any remaining chametz, called *biur chametz* (בִּעוּר חָמֵץ).

Additionally, you may choose to **sell chametz** to a non-Jew (more on this later) and make a **nullification declaration** (more below).

Let's go through each stage in detail.

---

## Stage 1: Pre-Passover Cleaning

### When to Start

You can begin cleaning for Passover as early as 30 days before the holiday — that's the beginning of the Hebrew month of Nissan. Many families start even earlier. The key is to complete the bulk of your cleaning *before* the formal search on the night of the 14th of Nissan.

### Room-by-Room Cleaning Guide

#### **Kitchen**

The kitchen requires the most thorough cleaning, as this is where chametz is most concentrated.

**Countertops and surfaces:**
- Scrub all countertops, tables, and work surfaces thoroughly
- Use hot water and cleaning solution
- Cover surfaces that will be used during Passover with contact paper, foil, or special Passover tablecloths

**Cabinets and drawers:**
- Empty all kitchen cabinets and drawers
- Vacuum or wipe out crumbs
- Line shelves with fresh paper or foil if they will store Passover foods
- Or, lock/tape shut cabinets containing sold chametz

**Appliances:**
- **Oven:** Self-clean cycle if available, or clean thoroughly and kasher (make kosher) by heating to maximum temperature
- **Stovetop:** Clean grates and burners; kasher by heating until they glow
- **Microwave:** Clean thoroughly; kasher by boiling a cup of water for several minutes until steam fills the interior
- **Refrigerator & freezer:** Remove all shelves, clean thoroughly, remove any chametz products
- **Dishwasher:** Run empty with cleaning agent; some kasher it, others use separate Passover dishes
- **Toaster, toaster oven:** Cannot be kashered; must be stored with sold chametz
- **Coffee maker, mixer, blender:** Clean thoroughly or put away with sold chametz

**Sink:**
- Clean thoroughly, paying attention to drains and faucet
- Some cover the sink basins with inserts or use separate Passover dish bins

**Dining Room:**
- Vacuum under table and chairs
- Check between couch cushions where children might have dropped snacks
- Clean out any sideboards or display cabinets

#### **Bedrooms**

- Check all pockets in clothing (pants, jackets, coats)
- Look under beds
- Check nightstands and dressers
- Children's rooms: Check toy boxes, under beds, closets — anywhere a child might have stashed a snack

**Tip for families with young children:** Kids love to hide crackers, cookies, and snacks in the most creative places. Be especially thorough in their spaces.

#### **Living Room & Common Areas**

- Vacuum sofas and armchairs (check between and under cushions)
- Check bookshelves (yes, people eat while reading!)
- Look under furniture
- Check coat closets and their pockets

#### **Bathroom**

While less common, check:
- Medicine cabinet (some medications contain chametz — consult a rabbi)
- Cosmetics and toiletries (generally not a concern unless ingested)

#### **Car**

Don't forget your vehicle!
- Vacuum seats and floor mats
- Check under seats
- Clean out glove compartment
- Check child car seats thoroughly

#### **Office/Study**

- Clean out desk drawers
- Check backpacks and briefcases
- Vacuum under desks and filing cabinets

### What About Places That Don't Need Checking?

According to Jewish law (Shulchan Aruch, Orach Chayim 433:3-6), certain areas do *not* require checking:

- **Wine cellars** — where bread is never brought
- **Barns and animal pens** — animals would have eaten any chametz
- **Very high places** — beyond arm's reach where you never put food
- **Very low holes** — less than three handbreadths (about 9 inches) from the ground
- **Courtyards** — birds would have eaten any chametz

The principle: if there's no reason to think chametz was brought there, you don't need to search.

## Stage 2: Bedikat Chametz — The Formal Search

### When

The search for chametz takes place on the **night of the 14th of Nissan**, the evening before Passover begins. This must be done after nightfall, by candlelight.

If the 14th of Nissan falls on Shabbat, the search is conducted on Thursday night (the 13th).

### What You Need

Gather these items before nightfall:

1. **A candle** — traditionally a beeswax candle (not a flashlight, though some authorities permit adding a flashlight for better visibility in dark corners)
2. **A wooden spoon** — to collect chametz crumbs
3. **A feather** — to sweep crumbs onto the spoon
4. **A paper bag** — to hold the collected chametz
5. **Ten small pieces of bread** (optional but customary) — wrapped in foil or paper and hidden around the house before the search

### The Ten Pieces Custom

There's a widespread custom to place ten small pieces of bread (carefully wrapped so they don't crumble) in various locations throughout the house before the search begins. This ensures that:

- You will definitely find *something* during the search (fulfilling the mitzvah completely)
- Children can participate in the "treasure hunt"
- You can be certain you've found everything you hid

**Important:** Know exactly how many pieces you hid and where, so you can verify you found them all.

### The Procedure

**1. Make the blessing:**

Before beginning the search, recite this blessing:

**Hebrew:**
בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה יְיָ אֱלֹהֵינוּ מֶלֶךְ הָעוֹלָם, אֲשֶׁר קִדְּשָׁנוּ בְּמִצְוֹתָיו, וְצִוָּנוּ עַל בִּעוּר חָמֵץ.

**Transliteration:**
*Baruch atah Adonai, Eloheinu melech ha-olam, asher kid'shanu b'mitzvotav, v'tzivanu al bi'ur chametz.*

**Translation:**
"Blessed are You, LORD our God, King of the universe, who has sanctified us with His commandments and commanded us concerning the removal of chametz."

**After the blessing, do not speak** (except about the search itself) until the search is complete.

**2. Search room by room:**

- Hold the candle and go from room to room
- Use the feather to brush any chametz you find onto the wooden spoon
- Place the chametz into the paper bag
- Be thorough but don't take all night — this is a search, not a deep clean (you already cleaned!)

**3. Nullification declaration (Kol Chamira — first time):**

After completing the search, recite the *Kol Chamira* declaration. This nullifies any chametz you didn't find:

**Aramaic:**
כָּל חֲמִירָא וַחֲמִיעָא דְאִכָּא בִרְשׁוּתִי, דְּלָא חֲמִתֵּהּ וּדְלָא בִעַרְתֵּהּ, לִבָּטֵל וּלְהוֵי כְּעַפְרָא דְאַרְעָא.

**Transliteration:**
*Kol chamira va-chamiya d'ika virshuti, d'la chamitei ud'la vi'artei, libatel ul'hevei k'afra d'ara.*

**Translation:**
"Any chametz or leaven that is in my possession which I have not seen and have not removed, shall be nullified and become ownerless, like the dust of the earth."

**4. Set aside the search materials:**

Put the wooden spoon, feather, and bag of chametz in a safe place. You'll burn them the next morning.

## Stage 3: Biur Chametz — Burning the Chametz

### When

On the **morning of the 14th of Nissan**, before mid-morning (the exact time varies by location — check a Jewish calendar for your city).

If the 14th falls on Shabbat, the burning is done Friday morning (the 13th).

### The Procedure

**1. Burn the chametz:**

- Take the bag of chametz collected during the search (along with the spoon and feather)
- Burn it in a controlled fire (a metal garbage can, barbecue pit, or designated outdoor area)
- Make sure it's completely incinerated

**2. Final nullification (Kol Chamira — second time):**

After burning, recite the second, broader nullification:

**Aramaic:**
כָּל חֲמִירָא וַחֲמִיעָא דְאִכָּא בִרְשׁוּתִי, דַּחֲמִתֵּהּ וּדְלָא חֲמִתֵּהּ, דְבִּעַרְתֵּהּ וּדְלָא בִעַרְתֵּהּ, לִבָּטֵל וּלְהוֵי כְּעַפְרָא דְאַרְעָא.

**Transliteration:**
*Kol chamira va-chamiya d'ika virshuti, da-chamitei ud'la chamitei, d'vi'artei ud'la vi'artei, libatel ul'hevei k'afra d'ara.*

**Translation:**
"Any chametz or leaven that is in my possession, whether I have seen it or not, whether I have removed it or not, shall be nullified and become ownerless, like the dust of the earth."

This declaration covers *all* chametz — even any you might have missed.

## Selling Chametz to a Non-Jew

### Why Sell Chametz?

It's nearly impossible to consume or destroy *all* chametz products in a modern household. Unopened bottles of whisky, expensive pasta, pet food containing grain — these represent significant financial loss if simply discarded.

Jewish law provides a solution: **sell your chametz to a non-Jew** before Passover and buy it back afterward. This is not a symbolic sale — it's a legally binding transaction, and the non-Jew technically owns your chametz during Passover.

### How It Works

1. **Designate a storage area:** Choose a closet or cabinet where you'll store all chametz products you want to sell.

2. **Complete the sale:** You can authorize a rabbi to sell your chametz on your behalf. Many communities (including online services) offer this. The sale must be completed before the deadline on the 14th of Nissan.

3. **Lock or tape the area:** The sold chametz should be inaccessible during Passover. Lock the cabinet or tape it shut.

4. **Buy it back:** After Passover ends, the rabbi automatically buys the chametz back for you.

**Online option:** You can sell your chametz online through Chabad.org or other Jewish organizations that offer this service for Jews worldwide, including those in China and other remote locations.

## Practical Application: Living Chametz-Free

### During Passover Week

Once you've removed all chametz:

- Eat only matzah (unleavened bread) instead of bread
- Use separate Passover dishes and utensils (or kasher your regular ones)
- Check all food labels to ensure products are kosher for Passover
- Avoid restaurants unless they're certified kosher for Passover

### Special Challenges in China

If you're observing Passover in a place like China, where kosher-for-Passover products may be unavailable:

- **Order in advance:** Many Chabad houses in China (Shanghai, Beijing, Hong Kong) can help source matzah and kosher-for-Passover foods. Plan ahead — at least a month before Passover.

- **Simplify your diet:** Fresh fruits, vegetables, meat, fish, eggs, and dairy (if properly supervised) are naturally chametz-free.

- **Connect with community:** Reach out to the nearest Chabad house for guidance and support. Even if you're far from a major city, they can help you remotely.

## The Deeper Meaning: Why We Do This

### Physical and Spiritual Cleaning

The Talmud and later Jewish thinkers saw chametz removal as more than hygiene — it's spiritual discipline.

**Chametz represents ego and arrogance** — the "puffed up" self. Just as leaven causes dough to rise and expand, ego inflates our sense of self-importance. Passover calls us to humility, symbolized by the flat, humble matzah.

The Chassidic masters taught: Just as we search every corner of our homes for chametz, we must search every corner of our *souls* for pride, selfishness, and spiritual laziness. Cleaning for Passover becomes an exercise in self-examination.

**A teaching from the Baal Shem Tov:** The Hebrew word for Egypt, *Mitzrayim* (מצרים), comes from the root *meitzar* (מֵיצַר), meaning "narrow" or "constricted." Leaving Egypt — and removing chametz — means breaking free from whatever constricts us: bad habits, limiting beliefs, spiritual complacency.

### Connection to Chinese Thought

The Confucian concept of **self-cultivation (修身, xiūshēn)** emphasizes continuous moral self-improvement through discipline and reflection. Similarly, the Pesach cleaning process demands discipline, attention to detail, and introspection. Both traditions recognize that external actions (cleaning, rituals) can refine internal character.

## Key Takeaways

- **Chametz** is any leavened grain product made from wheat, barley, rye, oats, or spelt. During Passover, we cannot eat, own, or benefit from it.

- **Three stages:** Clean your home thoroughly in the weeks before Passover. Perform the ritual candle-lit search (*bedikat chametz*) on the night of the 14th of Nissan. Burn the collected chametz the next morning (*biur chametz*).

- **Room-by-room approach:** Focus especially on the kitchen, children's rooms, living areas, and car. Check pockets, under cushions, and anywhere food might have been.

- **Selling chametz** is a practical solution for expensive or hard-to-dispose chametz products. Authorize a rabbi to sell it on your behalf before Passover.

- **Nullification declarations** (*Kol Chamira*) cover any chametz you might have missed — said once after the search, and again after burning.

- **Spiritual dimension:** Removing chametz symbolizes removing ego and arrogance from our lives, preparing us to experience the humility and freedom of the Exodus.
