## Introduction

Imagine voluntarily going an entire day without food or water—not for health reasons, not for weight loss, but to connect with something deeper than yourself. In Judaism, fasting is not about deprivation; it's about transformation. Through the discipline of physical abstinence, Jews create space for spiritual reflection, repentance, and remembrance of pivotal moments in their history.

For readers familiar with Chinese spiritual traditions, this may resonate: the Taoist practice of *bigu* (辟谷, "avoiding grains") similarly uses dietary discipline as a path to spiritual transcendence. Both traditions recognize that sometimes we must quiet the body to awaken the soul.

This article will guide you through Judaism's six major fast days—when they occur, what they commemorate, and how to observe them healthily and meaningfully, whether you're considering conversion, deepening your Jewish practice, or simply curious about this ancient tradition.

## The Six Major Fast Days

The Jewish calendar contains six public fast days, each marking a significant event in Jewish history. The prophet Zechariah (8:19) mentions four of them directly: "The fast of the fourth month, the fast of the fifth month, the fast of the seventh month, and the fast of the tenth month shall become occasions for joy and gladness... but you must love honesty and integrity."

### **1. Yom Kippur (10 Tishrei) — The Day of Atonement**

**When:** The tenth day of the Hebrew month of Tishrei (September or October)  
**Duration:** 25 hours, from before sunset until nightfall the next day  
**Historical Period:** Commanded directly in the Torah (Leviticus 16, 23)  

**Why We Fast:**  
Yom Kippur is the holiest day in the Jewish calendar—the day when God seals our judgment for the coming year. It concludes the Ten Days of Repentance that begin with Rosh Hashanah. On this day, Jews seek forgiveness for sins committed against God and fellow humans. The fast is not merely about hunger; it includes five prohibitions: eating and drinking, washing for pleasure, applying oils or lotions, wearing leather shoes, and marital relations. These restrictions help us focus entirely on our spiritual state, approaching God with humility.

**Historical Context:**  
This practice dates back to approximately 1300 BCE, when the Israelites wandered in the wilderness after the Exodus from Egypt—during what corresponds to China's late Shang Dynasty (商朝, ~1600-1046 BCE). The High Priest would enter the Holy of Holies once a year on this day to atone for the nation's sins.

**Spiritual Significance:**  
Yom Kippur teaches that repentance (*teshuvah*) is always possible. No matter what we've done, sincere regret, confession, and commitment to change can restore our relationship with God. The fast humbles us, reminding us that we are more than physical beings—we possess immortal souls capable of transcending our mistakes.

---

### **2. Tisha B'Av (9 Av) — The Ninth of Av**

**When:** The ninth day of the Hebrew month of Av (July or August)  
**Duration:** 25 hours, from before sunset until nightfall  
**Historical Period:** Commemorates multiple tragedies spanning 2,500+ years  

**Why We Fast:**  
Tisha B'Av is the saddest day in the Jewish calendar, marking the destruction of both the First and Second Temples in Jerusalem. Astonishingly, both Temples were destroyed on the same Hebrew date, centuries apart—the First in 586 BCE by the Babylonians (late Spring and Autumn Period, 春秋时代) and the Second in 70 CE by the Romans (Han Dynasty, 汉朝). Many other Jewish tragedies also occurred on this date throughout history.

Like Yom Kippur, Tisha B'Av includes five prohibitions. Jews sit on low stools or the floor (as mourners do) and read the Book of Lamentations (*Eicha*), Jeremiah's poetic lament over Jerusalem's destruction. The synagogue is dimly lit, and the ark curtain is often removed.

**What Was Lost:**  
The Temples weren't just buildings—they were the center of Jewish spiritual life, where God's presence (*Shechinah*) dwelled visibly among the people. Sacrifices brought atonement, and pilgrims traveled from across the ancient world to stand before God. When the Temples fell, it was as if the channel between heaven and earth had been severed.

**The Hidden Hope:**  
Yet the sages teach that the Third Temple is waiting, hidden beneath the ruins, ready to be revealed when the Messiah (*Moshiach*) comes. Zechariah's prophecy promises that these fast days will one day transform into festivals of joy. Thus, Tisha B'Av contains both deep mourning and messianic hope.

---

### **3. Fast of Gedaliah (3 Tishrei) — Tzom Gedaliah**

**When:** The day after Rosh Hashanah (3 Tishrei)  
**Duration:** Dawn to nightfall (minor fast)  
**Historical Period:** 586 BCE, immediately after the First Temple's destruction  

**Why We Fast:**  
After the Babylonians destroyed the First Temple and exiled most Jews to Babylon, they appointed Gedaliah ben Ahikam as governor over the small Jewish community remaining in Judea. For a brief moment, it seemed Jewish autonomy might survive. But jealous rivals assassinated Gedaliah, and the last remnant of Jewish self-governance collapsed. The survivors fled to Egypt in fear, scattering the Jewish people completely.

**The Lesson:**  
Gedaliah's murder teaches that internal strife—baseless hatred between Jews—can be as destructive as external enemies. One man's death ended any hope of rebuilding quickly. This fast reminds us that leaders are precious, and unity is fragile.

---

### **4. Tenth of Tevet (10 Tevet) — Asarah B'Tevet**

**When:** The tenth day of the Hebrew month of Tevet (December or January)  
**Duration:** Dawn to nightfall (minor fast)  
**Historical Period:** 588 BCE, beginning of Jerusalem's siege  

**Why We Fast:**  
On this date, the Babylonian army under King Nebuchadnezzar laid siege to Jerusalem. They surrounded the city with massive walls and siege engines, beginning a 30-month blockade that ended with starvation, plague, and the Temple's destruction. The tenth of Tevet marks the beginning of the end—the day hope turned to dread.

**Why This Day Matters:**  
While the Temple hadn't yet fallen, the siege set tragedy in motion. It's a reminder that warning signs matter. If we ignore spiritual and moral decay, consequences will follow. This fast calls us to examine our lives before it's too late.

---

### **5. Fast of Esther (13 Adar) — Ta'anit Esther**

**When:** The day before Purim (13 Adar)  
**Duration:** Dawn to nightfall (minor fast)  
**Historical Period:** ~357 BCE, during the Persian Empire (Warring States Period, 战国时代, in China)  

**Why We Fast:**  
This fast commemorates the three days Queen Esther and the Jewish people fasted before she risked her life to approach King Ahasuerus without being summoned—a capital offense. The wicked Haman had obtained a decree to annihilate all Jews in the Persian Empire. Esther's courageous intervention, combined with the people's prayers and fasting, led to the decree's reversal and Haman's downfall.

**What Makes This Different:**  
Unlike the Temple-related fasts, this one celebrates human courage aided by divine providence. God's name never appears in the Book of Esther, yet His hidden hand guides every event. The fast teaches that sometimes God works through natural means—through brave individuals willing to act.

**Modern Observance:**  
If Purim falls on Sunday, the Fast of Esther is moved to the preceding Thursday (since we don't fast on Shabbat). This fast ends at nightfall, and immediately the joyous holiday of Purim begins with the reading of the Megillah (Book of Esther).

---

### **6. Seventeenth of Tammuz (17 Tammuz) — Shivah Asar B'Tammuz**

**When:** The seventeenth day of Tammuz (June or July)  
**Duration:** Dawn to nightfall (minor fast)  
**Historical Period:** 70 CE (Han Dynasty, 汉朝)  

**Why We Fast:**  
On this date, Roman legions breached the walls of Jerusalem during the Great Revolt. This began the "Three Weeks" of mourning that culminate in Tisha B'Av. During these three weeks, Jews traditionally refrain from weddings, live music, and haircuts as we mourn the approach of the Temple's destruction.

Five calamities occurred on this date throughout history:
1. Moses smashed the first set of tablets upon seeing the Golden Calf
2. The daily offerings in the First Temple ceased during the Babylonian siege
3. The Romans breached Jerusalem's walls
4. A Roman general burned a Torah scroll
5. An idol was placed in the Temple

**The Pattern:**  
Notice that these fasts form a narrative arc: the siege begins (10 Tevet), the walls are breached (17 Tammuz), and three weeks later, the Temple burns (9 Av). The Fast of Gedaliah concludes the story with exile. Together, they tell the complete tragedy of Jerusalem's fall.

## Two Types of Fasts: Major vs. Minor

### **Major Fasts (Yom Kippur and Tisha B'Av)**
- **Duration:** 25 hours (from before sunset to nightfall the next day)
- **Five Prohibitions:** 
  1. Eating and drinking
  2. Washing for pleasure
  3. Applying oils, lotions, or cosmetics
  4. Wearing leather shoes
  5. Marital relations
- **Who Must Fast:** All healthy adults (bar mitzvah age and older)
- **Exemptions:** Pregnant or nursing women experiencing discomfort, people who are ill, children

### **Minor Fasts (All Other Four)**
- **Duration:** Dawn (first light) to nightfall (stars visible)
- **Prohibition:** Only eating and drinking (other activities permitted)
- **Who Must Fast:** Healthy adults
- **More Lenient Exemptions:** Pregnant and nursing women who feel weak may break the fast; someone feeling unwell should consult a rabbi but may eat if necessary

### **Special Note on Pregnant/Nursing Women**  
Jewish law values life above almost all else. If fasting poses any health risk to mother or baby, the fast is broken without guilt. As the Talmud teaches, "pikuach nefesh"—saving a life—overrides almost every commandment. Even when exempt from fasting, however, one should skip treats and luxuries, focusing on the day's spiritual purpose.

## How to Fast: Practical Guidance

### **Before the Fast**
1. **Hydrate Well:** Drink plenty of water in the 24-48 hours before the fast begins
2. **Eat Strategically:** Before major fasts, eat a substantial meal rich in:
   - Complex carbohydrates (whole grains, rice)
   - Protein (fish, chicken, eggs, beans)
   - Healthy fats (avocado, nuts, olive oil)
   - Avoid: salty foods, excess sugar, caffeine (which dehydrates)
3. **Set Your Intention:** Understand *why* you're fasting. It's not just skipping meals—it's creating space for *teshuvah* (repentance) and reflection
4. **Wake Before Dawn:** For minor fasts, you can wake up early (before dawn) and eat a small meal—*if* you had this intention before going to sleep the night before

### **During the Fast**
1. **Attend Synagogue:** Special prayers are added during fast days. The Torah is read (Exodus 32:11-14 and 34:1-10), and a special haftarah portion is chanted
2. **Rest When Possible:** Fasting drains energy. Avoid strenuous exercise
3. **Study Torah:** Fast days are ideal for learning—fill the time normally spent eating with spiritual nourishment
4. **Reflect and Pray:** Use the hunger as a reminder of your purpose. Each pang of hunger can become a prayer
5. **Stay Busy:** Idle time makes fasting harder. Engage in meaningful activities

### **Breaking the Fast**
1. **Don't Rush:** Start with water and light foods—fruit, bread, soup
2. **Avoid:** Immediately gorging on heavy, greasy, or spicy foods
3. **Gratitude:** Recite the evening prayers (*Ma'ariv*) and blessings over food with renewed appreciation

### **Medical Conditions**
Anyone with diabetes, heart conditions, migraines triggered by fasting, or other health concerns should consult both a doctor and a rabbi. Judaism never demands endangering one's health. As Maimonides wrote: "A person should be more careful about their health than about property."

## Why Do We Fast? It's Not Our Fault the Temple Was Destroyed...

This is the question every beginner asks. We didn't worship idols or commit the sins that led to the Temple's destruction—so why must we mourn and fast?

The sages explain: **"Every generation for which the Temple is not rebuilt, it is as if they destroyed it."**

In other words, the Temple remains destroyed because we haven't yet perfected ourselves enough to merit its rebuilding. The same character flaws that caused its destruction—baseless hatred (*sinat chinam*), arrogance, dishonesty—still exist. By fasting and reflecting, we confront those flaws within ourselves and work to correct them.

The fast days are not punishments—they are *opportunities*.

Think of it this way: a doctor doesn't diagnose an illness to be cruel, but to offer healing. Similarly, these fasts diagnose our spiritual state and offer the medicine of repentance. Each fast day asks: *What needs to change in my life? Where am I falling short? How can I be better?*

When enough individuals transform themselves, the collective spiritual level of the Jewish people rises. And when we reach that threshold—when we finally embody the values the Temple represents—the prophet promises these fasts will transform into holidays of joy.

## The Spiritual Purpose of Fasting

### **1. Teshuvah (Repentance)**  
Fasting creates discomfort that jolts us out of autopilot. When we're physically uncomfortable, we become more receptive to examining our lives honestly. It's easier to ignore our flaws when we're comfortable—fasting removes that comfort zone.

### **2. Humility**  
Hunger reminds us we are fragile, dependent creatures. We like to think we're in control, but missing a few meals reveals how quickly we weaken. This humility opens us to God's presence. As King David wrote (Psalm 51:17): "A broken and contrite heart, O God, You will not despise."

### **3. Compassion**  
Experiencing hunger—even briefly—makes us more compassionate toward those who go hungry not by choice but by poverty. It's no accident that the prophets (particularly in the haftarah readings for fast days) emphasize social justice and caring for the poor alongside ritual observance.

### **4. Sharpening Spiritual Sensitivity**  
When we quiet the body's constant demands for food, our spiritual senses sharpen. Many people report that prayers feel more intense on fast days, that Torah study penetrates deeper. As one Chassidic teaching explains: "The body sleeps, but the soul awakens."

## Chinese Cultural Parallels: A Bridge of Understanding

For readers from Chinese cultural backgrounds, fasting may already feel familiar. The Taoist practice of *bigu* (辟谷) and Buddhist vegetarian fasting days share with Judaism the insight that physical discipline serves spiritual growth.

**Similarities:**
- **Self-Cultivation (修身):** Both traditions see fasting as part of refining one's character
- **Purification:** Cleansing the body to purify the spirit
- **Communal Practice:** Fasting together strengthens bonds and shared purpose
- **Remembrance:** Just as Chinese culture honors ancestors and historical events, Jewish fasts commemorate pivotal national moments

**Differences:**
- **Jewish fasts:** Complete abstention from food *and water* (major fasts)
- **Taoist/Buddhist fasting:** Often selective (avoiding grains or meat) rather than total abstention
- **Purpose:** Jewish fasts are primarily commemorative and connected to repentance, while Taoist practices often focus on health and longevity

What both share is the profound recognition that humans are not mere bodies—we possess souls that hunger for meaning, transcendence, and connection to something greater than ourselves.

## Key Takeaways

- **Six Major Fast Days:** Yom Kippur, Tisha B'Av, Fast of Gedaliah, Tenth of Tevet, Fast of Esther, and Seventeenth of Tammuz
- **Two Categories:** Major fasts (25 hours, five prohibitions) and minor fasts (dawn to nightfall, eating/drinking only)
- **Historical Commemoration:** Most fast days remember the destruction of the Temples and Jerusalem's fall
- **Spiritual Purpose:** Fasting creates space for repentance, humility, compassion, and spiritual awareness
- **Health Comes First:** Pregnant, nursing, ill, or medically fragile individuals should not fast if it poses health risks
- **Hope Within Mourning:** Zechariah promises these fasts will one day become festivals of joy when the Messiah comes

## Practical Application

If you're new to Jewish practice and want to begin observing fast days:

1. **Start Small:** Begin with one minor fast (perhaps the Fast of Esther before Purim, which has a joyful ending)
2. **Find Community:** Fasting alone is difficult. Connect with a synagogue or Chabad house for support and prayer services
3. **Study the Day's Meaning:** Read about the historical events being commemorated
4. **Set Spiritual Goals:** Don't just abstain from food—use the time for prayer, Torah study, or acts of kindness
5. **Be Gentle with Yourself:** If you need to break the fast for health reasons, do so without guilt. God values life above ritual

**For Those in Asia:**  
If you're observing fast days in China or elsewhere without easy access to a synagogue, you can still observe meaningfully:
- Use online resources for the Torah readings and haftarah
- Recite the special fast-day prayers (Selichot) found in prayer books or online
- Connect via Zoom with a Chabad house offering virtual services
- Find one other Jewish person and fast together—even two people create a community
