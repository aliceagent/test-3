## Introduction

When someone we love dies, the world doesn't stop — but perhaps it should, at least for a moment. Judaism understands this profound human need to pause, to grieve fully, and to be surrounded by community during the most difficult week of your life. This sacred time is called *shiva* (שִׁבְעָה), meaning "seven" — a seven-day period of mourning when the bereaved withdraw from everyday life to honor their loss and begin the long journey of healing.

Nearly four thousand years ago — during what corresponds to China's Shang Dynasty (商朝, ~1600-1046 BCE) — Jacob mourned his son Joseph for many days, believing him dead. The formal structure of Jewish mourning developed over millennia, reaching its current form during the Second Temple period (corresponding to China's Warring States through Han Dynasty periods, 戰國時代至漢朝, ~516 BCE-70 CE) and codified in the Talmud and later rabbinic writings.

Whether you've just experienced a loss, are preparing to comfort someone sitting shiva, or are simply curious about this ancient practice, this article will guide you through the laws, customs, and profound wisdom of Jewish mourning.

## What is Shiva?

Shiva is the seven-day period of intensive mourning observed by immediate relatives (parents, siblings, spouses, and children) following the burial of a loved one. During this week, mourners remain at home, sit on low seats or the floor, abstain from certain activities, and receive visits from community members who come to offer comfort and support.

The practice originates from the Torah itself. When Aaron the High Priest lost his two sons, Moses told him and his remaining sons to refrain from certain activities as a sign of mourning (Leviticus 10:6-7). The Talmud later established the seven-day framework, drawing from Genesis where Joseph mourned his father Jacob for seven days (Genesis 50:10).

### Who Sits Shiva?

According to Jewish law, you observe shiva for seven close relatives:
1. Father
2. Mother
3. Brother
4. Sister
5. Son
6. Daughter
7. Spouse

These are the relationships for which a full seven-day shiva is required. Other relatives are mourned differently, with varying levels of observance.

## The Laws and Customs of Shiva

### 1. Timing: When Does Shiva Begin?

Shiva begins immediately after the burial. If the burial takes place in the afternoon, that day counts as the first day. Shiva ends on the morning of the seventh day, after the mourner takes a symbolic walk around the block — a physical act representing re-entry into the world.

**Days When Shiva is Interrupted:**
- **Shabbat** counts as one of the seven days, but public mourning practices are suspended (visitors don't come, low stools may be left).
- **Major Jewish holidays** (Passover, Shavuot, Sukkot, Rosh Hashana, Yom Kippur) interrupt shiva entirely. If a festival begins during shiva, the shiva ends.

### 2. Sitting Low and Covering Mirrors

Mourners sit on low stools, benches, or cushions placed directly on the floor. This physical lowering symbolizes being "brought low" by grief. The custom echoes the biblical image of mourners sitting in dust and ashes.

Mirrors in the shiva house are traditionally covered. Several reasons are given: to prevent vanity during a time of grief, to avoid the distraction of appearance, and because the home has temporarily ceased to function as a normal social space. Some suggest mirrors represent self-focus, while shiva demands focus outward on the departed and inward on the soul.

### 3. What Mourners Refrain From

During the seven days of shiva, mourners abstain from:

- **Work and business activities** (except in cases of financial necessity)
- **Bathing for pleasure** (washing for hygiene is permitted)
- **Wearing leather shoes** (a sign of comfort and luxury)
- **Marital relations** (intimacy is suspended during the mourning period)
- **Studying Torah** (except texts related to grief and mourning, as Torah study is normally a source of joy)
- **Greetings and small talk** (conversation centers on the deceased and comforting the mourner)
- **Haircuts and grooming beyond basic hygiene**

These restrictions create a bounded space apart from ordinary life, allowing mourners to fully inhabit their grief without the pressure to "move on" too quickly.

### 4. The Memorial Candle

A special memorial candle (נר נשמה, *ner neshamah* — "soul candle") burns continuously throughout the seven days. This light symbolizes the soul of the deceased, which lives on. The image draws from Proverbs 20:27: "The soul of man is the candle of God."

The flame is never extinguished during shiva, representing the eternal quality of the soul and the ongoing connection between the living and the departed.

### 5. The Torn Garment (Kriah)

At the funeral or just before, mourners perform *kriah* (קריעה, "tearing") — a ritual rending of their clothes over the heart. This outward tear represents the internal tearing of the soul when someone we love dies. For parents, the tear is made on the left side (over the heart); for other relatives, on the right.

The torn garment is worn throughout the shiva week, a visible sign of grief. Unlike a black armband that can be removed, the tear in your clothing accompanies you, a constant reminder that loss has altered you.

## How to Comfort a Mourner: The Shiva Visit

If you've never been to a shiva house, it can feel intimidating. What do you say? What do you do? Judaism offers clear, compassionate guidance.

### When to Visit

Shiva visits typically occur during designated hours posted at the shiva house. Traditionally, visits take place in the afternoon and evening. Check with the family or community first.

**First Day Note:** In some communities, the first day (the day of burial) is reserved for close family only, with broader community visits beginning on the second day.

### What to Bring

In many communities, visitors bring food for the mourners, ensuring they don't need to cook during this difficult time. The tradition is to bring prepared meals in disposable containers (so the family doesn't need to worry about returning dishes).

A classical custom is to bring *seudat havra'ah* (the "meal of consolation") immediately after the burial — round foods like eggs and lentils, symbolizing the cycle of life and the wholeness of God's justice even in grief.

### What to Say (and What NOT to Say)

Here's where Jewish wisdom shines: **When you first arrive, say nothing.**

The Talmud teaches: "One may not speak in the presence of a mourner until the mourner speaks first" (*Moed Katan* 28b). This is radical and profoundly kind. You don't enter with cheerful chatter or nervous small talk. You sit quietly, offering your presence, and wait for the mourner to acknowledge you or begin speaking.

**When the mourner does speak:**
- Listen more than you talk
- Ask about the deceased: "Tell me about your mother," "What do you remember most about your brother?"
- Share your own memories if you knew the person
- Acknowledge the loss simply: "I'm so sorry," "This is such a hard time"

**What NOT to say:**
- ❌ "They're in a better place now"
- ❌ "At least they lived a long life"
- ❌ "God needed another angel"
- ❌ "I know how you feel"
- ❌ "Everything happens for a reason"

These phrases, though well-intentioned, often minimize the mourner's pain or impose a theological framework they may not share in that moment.

**The traditional Jewish farewell:**
When you leave, you offer the formal words of comfort: *"HaMakom yenachem etchem betoch she'ar availei Tzion v'Yerushalayim"* (הַמָּקוֹם יְנַחֵם אֶתְכֶם בְּתוֹךְ שְׁאָר אֲבֵלֵי צִיּוֹן וִירוּשָׁלָיִם) — "May the Omnipresent comfort you among the mourners of Zion and Jerusalem."

This blessing connects personal grief to collective Jewish memory, reminding mourners they're not alone in their suffering.

### Bringing Comfort Through Presence

The Hebrew word for "comfort" (*nechamah*, נֶחָמָה) shares a root with the word for "breath" (*neshamah*, נְשָׁמָה). Comfort, in Jewish thought, is about restoring the breath, the soul, the life force that grief has diminished.

Sometimes comfort comes not through words but through simple presence. Sitting with someone in their pain, bearing witness to their grief, helping with practical needs — these are profound acts of *chesed* (loving-kindness).

## Cultural Parallels: Mourning in Chinese Tradition

Both Jewish and Chinese cultures understand that grief requires structure, time, and community. In traditional Chinese mourning practice, there are also designated periods of grief:

- **守孝 (shǒu xiào)** — "keeping filial piety" — a formal mourning period, historically lasting up to three years for parents
- **Wearing white** (the Chinese color of mourning, parallel to the West's black)
- **Communal remembrance** — family gatherings, visits, and shared meals
- **Physical markers** — white armbands or sackcloth, similar to Jewish kriah
- **Restriction of festivities** — avoiding celebrations during mourning

The Confucian classics emphasized that mourning parents properly was among the highest filial duties. The *Book of Rites* (*Liji*, 禮記) prescribed detailed mourning practices, much as the Talmud does for Jewish mourning.

Both traditions recognize that grief cannot be rushed. Just as shiva creates a protected week for Jewish mourners, Chinese tradition carved out extensive time for children to honor deceased parents, understanding that true mourning requires stepping away from normal life.

## The Spiritual Purpose of Shiva

Why does Judaism mandate this intensive week of mourning? What is shiva meant to accomplish?

### 1. **Honoring the Deceased**
Shiva is a final act of *kavod hamet* (honoring the dead). By setting aside work and social obligations, mourners declare: "This person mattered profoundly. Their absence deserves my full attention."

### 2. **Processing Grief**
Judaism recognizes that grief unfolds in stages. Shiva is the first and most intense stage — a week when you don't have to be "strong," when you're permitted to fall apart, when the community surrounds you with support.

### 3. **Reconnecting the Soul**
When a loved one dies, part of our own soul feels severed. The rituals of shiva — sitting low, refraining from comforts, receiving visitors who speak of the deceased — slowly begin to reconnect our fractured selves. The week creates space for memory, for tears, for stories, for the slow recognition that life continues even in the face of death.

### 4. **Community Cohesion**
Shiva ensures that mourners are not alone. The practice of the *minyan* (prayer quorum of ten) gathering at the shiva house for daily prayers means the mourner doesn't have to go to synagogue — the synagogue comes to them. Food arrives. Faces appear. The community says, through action: "We are here. You are not abandoned."

## After Shiva: The Journey of Mourning Continues

Shiva is the first stage, but Jewish mourning is a graduated process designed to gently reintegrate mourners back into life:

### Sheloshim (30 Days)
After shiva ends, mourners enter *sheloshim* (שְׁלוֹשִׁים, "thirty") — the thirty-day period that includes the shiva week. During this time:
- Work resumes
- Social life gradually returns
- Some restrictions remain: no haircuts, shaving, or attending celebrations
- For siblings, children, and spouses, mourning concludes at the end of sheloshim
- For parents, mourning continues for a full year

### The Year of Mourning (for Parents)
Children mourning a parent continue certain practices for twelve months:
- Reciting the Mourner's Kaddish daily at services
- Avoiding parties and entertainment
- Refraining from purchasing new clothes (except as needed)

### Yahrzeit (Annual Anniversary)
Every year on the Hebrew date of the death, the yahrzeit (Yiddish for "year's time") is observed:
- Lighting a 24-hour memorial candle
- Reciting Kaddish
- Visiting the grave
- Studying Torah or doing acts of charity in memory of the deceased

This annual observance ensures the deceased is never forgotten, keeping their memory alive through generations.

## Practical Guidance for Chinese Jewish Learners

If you are mourning and sitting shiva:
- Inform your local Chabad rabbi or Jewish community immediately
- They will help arrange a minyan (prayer quorum) to come to your home
- Accept help: let others bring food, organize logistics, handle details
- Don't worry about hosting or entertaining — shiva is not a social event
- Speak about your loved one; tell stories; cry when you need to
- The community is there to support you, not to be impressed by you

If you are visiting someone sitting shiva:
- Check first about visiting hours and whether visits are welcome
- Enter quietly, offer a simple nod or greeting, then sit down
- Wait for the mourner to speak first
- Listen deeply; share memories if you have them
- Keep visits relatively brief (20-30 minutes is typical)
- As you leave, offer the traditional blessing (or simply say, "I'm here for you")

## Key Takeaways

- **Shiva is a seven-day mourning period** observed at home by immediate relatives of the deceased, beginning after burial
- **Mourners sit on low stools**, cover mirrors, refrain from work and certain comforts, and receive community visitors
- **Visitors offer comfort through presence** more than words — sitting quietly, listening, and sharing memories of the deceased
- **The traditional blessing upon leaving** is: "May the Omnipresent comfort you among the mourners of Zion and Jerusalem"
- **Shiva is the first stage** of a graduated mourning process: seven days (shiva), thirty days (sheloshim), and one year (for parents)
- **Judaism structures grief with wisdom**, creating protected time for mourning while gradually reintegrating the bereaved back into life
- **Cultural parallel**: Like Confucian mourning practices (守孝), Jewish shiva recognizes that honoring the dead and processing loss cannot be rushed
