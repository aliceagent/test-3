## Introduction

Nearly four thousand years ago — during what corresponds to China's Shang Dynasty (商朝, ~1800-1500 BCE) — Abraham became the first Jew. He didn't grow up in a Jewish home. He had no synagogue, no rabbi, no community. Yet he discovered God and began a journey that would change the world.

If you're reading this article, you may find yourself in a similar situation. Perhaps you live in Beijing, Bangkok, or Taipei. Perhaps you're the only person you know who's interested in Judaism. Perhaps you've been learning online for months or years, and you're wondering: *How do I find a real rabbi? How do I connect with an actual Jewish community?*

The good news is that even in Asia — far from the traditional centers of Jewish life — you are not alone. This article will guide you through the practical steps of finding a rabbi, connecting with community, and building meaningful Jewish relationships, even when you live thousands of kilometers from the nearest synagogue.

## The Importance of Having a Rabbi

### What Is a Rabbi?

The word "rabbi" (רַבִּי) means "my teacher" or "my master." A rabbi is not a priest or intermediary between you and God. Rather, a rabbi is:

- **A scholar of Torah** — someone who has dedicated years to studying Jewish texts, law, and wisdom
- **A teacher** — someone who can answer your questions and guide your learning
- **A mentor** — someone who knows you personally and can offer advice tailored to your situation
- **A connection to tradition** — someone who links you to the chain of Jewish learning stretching back to Mount Sinai

### The Talmudic Teaching: "Make for Yourself a Rabbi"

The Talmud (Ethics of the Fathers 1:6) teaches: **"Make for yourself a rabbi"** (עֲשֵׂה לְךָ רַב).

This teaching is profound in its wording. It doesn't say "find a rabbi" or "go to a rabbi." It says **make for yourself** a rabbi. This implies:

1. **It's your responsibility** — Don't wait passively. Actively seek out a teacher.
2. **It's personal** — Your rabbi should be someone who speaks *your* language, understands *your* background, and can answer *your* questions.
3. **It's a relationship** — You need to invest time, ask questions, build trust, and develop a real connection.

The Chinese tradition values the teacher-student relationship deeply. Confucius (孔子) said: "三人行，必有我师焉" — "When three people walk together, there must be one who can be my teacher." Similarly, Judaism teaches that every person needs a personal teacher who can guide their spiritual journey.

### What to Look for in a Rabbi

According to the Aish.com article "What Is A Rabbi?", when choosing a rabbi to be your mentor, look for someone who:

- **Speaks your language** — Both literally (someone you can communicate with) and figuratively (someone who understands your cultural background)
- **Gives meaningful answers** — A good rabbi doesn't just quote rules; they explain the *why* behind them
- **You respect** — You should admire their wisdom, character, and way of life
- **Challenges you** — A good teacher pushes you to grow, not just tells you what you want to hear
- **Understands you** — They should know your background, family situation, and personal struggles
- **Inspires trust** — You need someone you can be honest with, who keeps confidences and cares about your wellbeing

## Finding Jewish Community in Asia

### Chabad Houses: Your Gateway to Jewish Life

**Chabad-Lubavitch** is a Hasidic movement that has established Jewish centers in cities around the world — including many cities across Asia. Chabad houses serve as:

- Community centers where Jews gather for Shabbat and holidays
- Synagogues for prayer services
- Restaurants or cafés serving kosher food
- Educational centers offering classes and programs
- Resources for travelers and residents alike

**Chabad Centers in China include:**

- **Beijing** — The capital has an active Chabad house serving the Jewish diplomatic and business community
- **Shanghai** — Shanghai Jewish Center (87 South ShuiCheng Road, Villa #1) offers:
  - Synagogue services
  - Kosher café
  - Mikvah (ritual bath)
  - Gan preschool
  - Children's programs (Tzivos Hashem)
- **Hong Kong & Kowloon** — Multiple Chabad centers serving this major Jewish hub
- **Guangzhou** — Chabad house for the southern business community
- **Shenzhen** — Serving the tech and manufacturing center
- **Chengdu** — One of the most remote Chabad houses in China
- **Other cities** — Foshan, Yiwu, and growing presence in other commercial centers

**Beyond China**, Chabad houses operate in:

- **Thailand** — Bangkok, Phuket, Chiang Mai, Koh Samui
- **Singapore** — Multiple centers
- **Japan** — Tokyo, Kobe
- **South Korea** — Seoul
- **Taiwan** — Taipei
- **India** — Mumbai, Delhi, and throughout the country
- **Vietnam** — Ho Chi Minh City
- **Philippines** — Manila

### How to Connect with a Chabad House

1. **Find your nearest Chabad center** — Visit Chabad.org and use their Chabad Locator tool
2. **Reach out via email or phone** — Introduce yourself and explain your interest in Judaism
3. **Ask about Shabbat services** — Most Chabad houses host Friday night and Saturday morning services with communal meals
4. **Inquire about learning opportunities** — Many offer one-on-one study sessions with the rabbi
5. **Don't be shy about your background** — Chabad rabbis are warm, welcoming, and experienced with people from all backgrounds, including non-Jews exploring Judaism

### Other Jewish Communities in Asia

Beyond Chabad, other Jewish communities and organizations include:

- **Reform and Conservative synagogues** in major cities (Hong Kong, Singapore, Tokyo, Mumbai)
- **Independent Orthodox communities** in business centers
- **Jewish Community Centers (JCCs)** offering cultural and educational programs
- **Israeli embassies and consulates** — Can sometimes provide contacts for local Jewish communities
- **University Hillel organizations** — If you're a student in Asia

## Building Community When There's No Community Nearby

### Virtual Rabbis and Online Learning

If you live in a city without a Jewish presence, technology allows you to connect with rabbis and communities around the world:

**Aish.com offers:**
- **Aish Academy** (academy.aish.com) — Interactive online courses on Jewish topics with discussion forums
- **"Ask the Rabbi"** feature — Submit questions and receive personalized answers
- **Live classes** — Virtual Torah classes with real-time interaction

**Chabad.org provides:**
- **ChabadU** — Online video courses on Jewish topics from basic to advanced
- **Torah Classes** — Described as "the world's biggest virtual classroom for Torah study"
- **Ask a Rabbi** — Anonymous question service with quick responses
- **Email and SMS subscriptions** — Daily Torah insights, weekly parsha summaries, and holiday guides

**Other online resources:**
- **Sefaria.org** — Complete library of Jewish texts with translations and commentaries
- **Partners in Torah** — Free one-on-one Jewish study via phone or video call matched with a learning partner
- **MyJewishLearning.com** — Comprehensive articles and courses
- **OU.org** — Orthodox Union resources including guides to Jewish practice

### Finding a Personal Rabbi Online

Many rabbis offer **one-on-one online learning** via Zoom, WhatsApp video, or phone. To find a personal rabbi:

1. **Reach out to a Chabad house** — Even if it's far away, many rabbis are happy to learn with you remotely
2. **Join Partners in Torah** — This free program matches you with a learning partner/teacher for weekly study sessions
3. **Contact rabbis at yeshivas** — Many Torah study institutions have rabbis available for distance learning
4. **Try several teachers** — It's okay to "shop around" until you find someone who's the right fit

### Creating Your Own Mini-Community

If you're in a remote area, consider:

- **Connecting with other Jews online** — Join Facebook groups, WhatsApp communities, or forums for Jews in Asia or Jews-by-choice
- **Shabbat hospitality networks** — Websites that connect travelers with hosts (like Chabad.org's hospitality service)
- **Learning with family or friends** — Invite others to join your Jewish learning journey
- **Annual trips** — Plan yearly visits to major Jewish communities (Jerusalem, New York, or Asian hubs like Hong Kong/Singapore) for intensive learning and connection

## The Example of Ruth: The First Convert

The Book of Ruth tells the story of the most famous convert in Jewish history. Ruth was a Moabite woman who married into a Jewish family. When her husband and father-in-law died, her mother-in-law Naomi told her to return to her people.

Ruth's response (Ruth 1:16-17) has become the declaration of every convert:

> **"Do not urge me to leave you, to turn back and not follow you. For wherever you go, I will go; wherever you lodge, I will lodge; your people shall be my people, and your God my God. Where you die, I will die, and there I will be buried."**
> 
> **אַל־תִּפְגְּעִי־בִי לְעׇזְבֵךְ לָשׁוּב מֵאַחֲרָיִךְ כִּי אֶל־אֲשֶׁר תֵּלְכִי אֵלֵךְ וּבַאֲשֶׁר תָּלִינִי אָלִין עַמֵּךְ עַמִּי וֵאלֹהַיךְ אֱלֹהָי׃ בַּאֲשֶׁר תָּמוּתִי אָמוּת וְשָׁם אֶקָּבֵר**

Ruth didn't have a synagogue in Bethlehem waiting for her. She didn't have a formal conversion class. What she had was:

1. **A teacher** — Naomi, her mother-in-law, who taught her about Judaism
2. **Commitment** — She chose to join the Jewish people permanently
3. **Community** — She traveled to where the Jewish community was (Bethlehem)

Ruth became the great-grandmother of King David and an ancestor of the Messiah. Her story teaches that **it's not about where you start, but about your commitment to the journey.**

## Practical Steps: A Roadmap for Finding Your Rabbi and Community

### Step 1: Research Your Options (Week 1)
- Use Chabad.org Locator to find the nearest Chabad house
- Search for synagogues in your city or nearest major city
- Explore online learning platforms (Aish Academy, ChabadU, Partners in Torah)
- Join online Jewish communities and forums

### Step 2: Make Initial Contact (Week 2-3)
- Email or call the nearest Chabad house or synagogue
- Introduce yourself honestly: "I'm learning about Judaism and would like to connect with a rabbi"
- Ask about Shabbat services, classes, or one-on-one learning opportunities
- Don't be discouraged if the first response is slow — follow up!

### Step 3: Attend Services or Events (Week 4-8)
- Attend a Friday night Shabbat service and dinner
- Go to a holiday celebration (especially high-attendance holidays like Rosh Hashana or Passover)
- Participate in a class or lecture
- Observe how the community interacts

### Step 4: Build a Relationship (Months 2-6)
- Meet with the rabbi one-on-one for learning sessions
- Ask questions — lots of questions!
- Be honest about your background, doubts, and challenges
- Test if this rabbi is the right teacher for you

### Step 5: Commit to Regular Learning (Ongoing)
- Schedule weekly learning sessions (in-person or online)
- Read recommended books and texts
- Practice mitzvot (commandments) as you learn them
- Stay connected with the community through Shabbat and holidays

### Step 6: Consider Formal Conversion Process (If Applicable)
- If you're not Jewish by birth and wish to convert, discuss this with your rabbi
- Understand the conversion process will take 1-3 years of study and commitment
- Be patient — the journey is part of the transformation

## Cultural Bridge: The Chinese Tradition of Seeking Teachers

In Chinese culture, the relationship between teacher and student (师徒关系) has always been sacred. The saying goes: **"一日为师，终身为父"** — "One who teaches me for a day is like a father for life."

Similarly, in Judaism, the relationship between a rabbi and student is lifelong. Your rabbi becomes not just a teacher of texts, but a guide for life's major decisions — career choices, marriage, raising children, ethical dilemmas.

Just as Chinese scholars in ancient times would travel great distances to study with renowned masters, Jews throughout history have journeyed to sit at the feet of great rabbis. This tradition continues today — whether that journey is physical (traveling to Jerusalem or New York for study) or virtual (connecting via video call).

## Overcoming Challenges

### "I'm Embarrassed About My Lack of Knowledge"

**Rabbi's response:** Every rabbi has taught complete beginners. There is no shame in not knowing — only in refusing to learn. The Talmud teaches that "a shy person cannot learn." Be bold and ask your questions!

### "I'm Not Jewish Yet — Will They Accept Me?"

**Reality:** Chabad houses and many synagogues welcome sincere seekers regardless of background. If you're respectful and genuinely interested, you will be welcomed. Many rabbis have experience guiding converts through their journey.

### "The Nearest Community Is Hours Away"

**Solution:** 
- Start with online learning and build a relationship with an online rabbi
- Make monthly or quarterly trips to visit the nearest community
- Create a home practice of Shabbat and study
- Plan annual immersive trips to major Jewish centers

### "I Don't Speak Hebrew or English Well"

**Resources:**
- Many Chabad rabbis in Asia speak Mandarin, Cantonese, or other local languages
- Online resources are increasingly available in Chinese (Sefaria has Chinese translations)
- Learning can happen in your native language — Hebrew will come gradually

## Conclusion: The Journey of Connection

Finding a rabbi and community when you live in Asia is not just possible — it's the beginning of one of the most meaningful journeys of your life. Like Ruth leaving Moab for Bethlehem, like Abraham leaving Ur for Canaan, you are taking a brave step toward something greater than yourself.

You may start alone, studying at your computer late at night. But as you reach out — to Chabad houses, to online rabbis, to fellow seekers — you'll discover that the Jewish people have been waiting for you. We have a saying: **"Kol Yisrael arevim zeh bazeh"** (כל ישראל ערבים זה בזה) — "All Jews are responsible for one another."

When you join our people, you join a global family that spans continents, languages, and millennia. Your journey may begin in Shanghai, Seoul, or Singapore — but it connects you to Jerusalem, to Mount Sinai, and to every Jewish soul who has ever lived.

**Take the first step today.** Visit Chabad.org, send that email, make that call. Your rabbi is out there, and your community is waiting.
