## Introduction

In Chinese medicine, doctors speak of the "four examinations" (四诊): looking, listening, questioning, and feeling the pulse. Each examination reveals different aspects of the patient's condition, and only together do they provide a complete diagnosis. Similarly, Jewish tradition teaches that the Torah has four levels of meaning, each revealing different truths about the same text.

This system is called *PaRDeS* (פרדס) — Hebrew for "orchard" — an acronym formed from the first letters of four Hebrew words:
- **Peshat** (פשט) — Simple, literal meaning
- **Remez** (רמז) — Hints and allusions
- **Drash** (דרש) — Homiletical interpretation
- **Sod** (סוד) — Secret, mystical meaning

Just as an orchard contains different types of trees bearing different fruits, the Torah contains multiple layers of truth waiting to be discovered. Understanding PaRDeS transforms how you read Jewish texts, revealing depths you never imagined possible.

## Historical Background: Where PaRDeS Comes From

The concept of multiple levels in Torah interpretation isn't new. Even in ancient times, Jewish sages recognized that the Torah spoke in different voices. The Talmud mentions that "the Torah speaks in human language" (*Berakhot* 31b), acknowledging both simple and complex meanings.

However, the formal PaRDeS system developed during the medieval period (roughly corresponding to China's Song Dynasty, 960-1279 CE), particularly among Jewish mystics in Spain and southern France. These scholars, influenced by both Islamic philosophy and their own mystical traditions, created a systematic approach to understanding how the same biblical verse could carry multiple meanings simultaneously.

The great Rabbi Moses de León, who lived in 13th-century Spain (contemporary with the Yuan Dynasty in China), was among those who helped popularize this four-level system. The method became central to Jewish learning, especially in kabbalistic circles.

## The Four Levels Explained

### 1. Peshat (פשט) — The Simple Meaning

*Peshat* represents the straightforward, contextual understanding of the text. This is what the words mean in their plain sense, considering grammar, syntax, and historical context. Even when studying Peshat, Jewish interpreters pay careful attention to:

- The precise Hebrew words used
- Grammar and sentence structure
- Historical and geographical context
- How the passage fits within its larger narrative

**Example:** When the Torah says "Abraham took his son Isaac" (Genesis 22:2), the peshat is simply that Abraham physically brought Isaac with him to Mount Moriah.

In Chinese scholarly tradition, this resembles the *literal reading* (直讲) approach favored by Han Dynasty commentators, who focused on understanding texts in their historical context before exploring deeper meanings.

### 2. Remez (רמז) — Hints and Allusions

*Remez* looks for hints, subtle clues, and indirect references that point beyond the literal text. This level often involves:

- Numerical patterns in Hebrew (gematria)
- Unusual spellings or word choices
- Connections between distant passages
- Symbolic interpretations that don't contradict the literal meaning

**Example:** The same verse about Abraham and Isaac contains hints about the nature of divine tests. The Hebrew word for "take" (*kach*) appears only in critical moments throughout the Torah, suggesting this isn't just a journey but a pivotal spiritual moment.

This approach parallels the Chinese tradition of finding *hidden meanings* (隐意) in classical texts, where Confucian scholars would identify subtle patterns and allusions that revealed deeper political or moral messages.

### 3. Drash (דרש) — Homiletical Interpretation

*Drash* derives practical lessons, moral teachings, and contemporary applications from ancient texts. This level asks: "What does this teach us about how to live?" Characteristics include:

- Drawing moral lessons from narratives
- Finding contemporary relevance in ancient stories
- Using one biblical passage to explain another
- Creative interpretation that serves ethical or theological purposes

**Example:** The Binding of Isaac becomes a lesson about absolute faith, the willingness to sacrifice what we love most, and trust in divine providence even when we don't understand God's plan.

This resembles the Chinese practice of *moral commentary* (义疏) developed during the Tang Dynasty, where scholars extracted ethical teachings from historical narratives and classical literature.

### 4. Sod (סוד) — Secret, Mystical Meaning

*Sod* represents the deepest, most esoteric understanding, accessible only to advanced students. This level explores:

- Mystical symbolism
- Cosmic significance of biblical events
- Hidden relationships between divine and human realms
- Kabbalistic interpretations involving divine attributes

**Example:** Kabbalistic interpreters see the Binding of Isaac as representing the cosmic tension between divine mercy (*chesed*) and judgment (*gevurah*), with Isaac symbolizing the divine attribute of strength being tested and refined.

This parallels the Chinese tradition of *mysterious learning* (玄学) that emerged during the Wei-Jin period, where Daoist philosophers found cosmic principles hidden within seemingly simple texts.

## How PaRDeS Works in Practice

### Reading the Same Verse on Four Levels

Let's examine Genesis 1:1: "In the beginning, God created the heavens and the earth."

**Peshat:** God initiated the process of creating the physical universe.

**Remez:** The Hebrew word for "beginning" (*bereishit*) can be rearranged to spell "I created six" — hinting at the six days of creation that follow.

**Drash:** This teaches that God creates continuously; every morning, we should see the world as if it's being created anew, inspiring us to approach each day with wonder and gratitude.

**Sod:** Kabbalists see this as describing the emergence of divine light from the infinite (*Ein Sof*) into the finite world, with "heavens" representing spiritual realms and "earth" representing physical reality.

### Why All Four Levels Matter

Each level serves a different purpose:

- **Peshat** ensures we remain grounded in the text's actual meaning
- **Remez** trains us to notice divine patterns and connections
- **Drash** makes ancient wisdom relevant to contemporary life
- **Sod** connects us to the deepest spiritual truths

The genius of PaRDeS is that all four levels can be true simultaneously. A biblical story can be historically accurate (Peshat), contain numerical hints (Remez), teach moral lessons (Drash), and reveal cosmic truths (Sod) — all at once.

## Chinese Parallels: The Four Books and Layers of Meaning

Chinese scholars will recognize similarities between PaRDeS and their own interpretive traditions. Consider how Confucian commentators approached the *Analects*:

1. **Literal Understanding** (字义): Understanding what Confucius actually said
2. **Contextual Meaning** (文意): How his words applied to specific historical situations  
3. **Moral Teaching** (道德): What ethical principles emerge for all times
4. **Cosmic Significance** (天理): How human behavior reflects universal principles

During the Song Dynasty, Neo-Confucian philosophers like Zhu Xi developed sophisticated methods for finding multiple layers of meaning in classical texts, much as Jewish mystics were doing with the Torah during the same period.

## Practical Guidelines for Using PaRDeS

### For Beginners
1. **Start with Peshat**: Always understand the simple meaning first
2. **Ask Questions**: What seems unusual or surprising in the text?
3. **Look for Patterns**: Do certain words or phrases repeat?
4. **Seek Contemporary Relevance**: What does this teach about living today?

### For Advanced Students
1. **Learn Hebrew**: Deeper levels require understanding the original language
2. **Study Commentaries**: See how great interpreters applied these methods
3. **Practice Regularly**: Use PaRDeS on different types of texts
4. **Find a Study Partner**: Different perspectives enrich interpretation

### Common Mistakes to Avoid
- Never contradict Peshat without strong justification
- Don't force meanings that don't emerge naturally from the text
- Remember that not every passage contains all four levels
- Avoid using PaRDeS to support predetermined conclusions

## The Mystical Dimension: Entering the Orchard

The Talmud tells a famous story about four sages who "entered the orchard" (*Chagigah* 14b) — understood as attempting to reach the highest levels of mystical understanding. Three were unable to handle the experience; only Rabbi Akiva "entered in peace and departed in peace."

This story serves as both inspiration and warning: the deeper levels of Torah study can be spiritually transformative, but they require proper preparation, humility, and guidance. Like Chinese Daoist practitioners who spent years preparing before attempting advanced meditation techniques, Jewish students traditionally study for many years before approaching the deepest mystical interpretations.

## Modern Applications of PaRDeS

### In Jewish Education
Today, PaRDeS remains central to Jewish learning. Students in yeshivas, Jewish day schools, and adult education programs use these methods to unlock layers of meaning in traditional texts. The approach has proven especially valuable for helping modern students connect with ancient texts.

### In Interfaith Dialogue
PaRDeS provides a framework for explaining how Jews read the Bible differently from Christians while respecting both literal and symbolic interpretations. It shows how the same text can yield multiple valid understandings.

### In Personal Spiritual Practice
Many Jews use PaRDeS in their personal Torah study, finding that the four-level approach deepens their understanding and spiritual connection to the text. Reading the weekly Torah portion through all four lenses has become a popular Shabbat practice.

### In Academic Scholarship
Modern biblical scholars, while using different methodologies, often appreciate PaRDeS as an early example of sophisticated literary analysis that recognized multiple layers of meaning in complex texts.

## Conclusion: The Endless Orchard

PaRDeS teaches that Torah study is never finished. Like an orchard that produces fruit season after season, year after year, the Torah continues yielding new meanings to each generation of students. A passage you read at twenty will speak differently to you at forty, and differently again at sixty. The four levels ensure that the text remains alive, relevant, and spiritually nourishing throughout your lifetime.

For Chinese readers familiar with the concept that *"the Dao that can be spoken is not the eternal Dao"* (道可道，非常道), PaRDeS offers a parallel insight: the Torah that appears on the surface is not the complete Torah. Its deepest truths emerge only through patient study, careful attention, and willingness to see beyond the obvious.

Whether you're taking your first steps into Jewish learning or deepening an existing practice, PaRDeS provides both method and invitation: method for reading with greater sophistication, and invitation to enter the orchard where infinite meanings await discovery.

The four levels remind us that truth has many faces, that ancient wisdom speaks in multiple voices, and that the most profound teachings often hide in plain sight, waiting for students patient enough to look beyond the surface and wise enough to recognize that the journey of understanding never truly ends.
