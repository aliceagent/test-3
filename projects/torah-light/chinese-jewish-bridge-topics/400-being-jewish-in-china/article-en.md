# Being Jewish in China: A Practical Survival Guide

*The complete guide to living as an observant Jew in the Middle Kingdom*

## Introduction: A Journey Between Two Ancient Civilizations

Moving to China as a Jew means navigating the intersection of two of the world's oldest continuous civilizations. China, with its 5,000-year history, meets Jewish tradition spanning millennia. This guide is for those who want to maintain their Jewish identity and practice while thriving in Chinese society—whether you're an expat professional in Shanghai, a student in Beijing, an English teacher in a smaller city, or planning to raise a family in this fascinating country.

The good news: Jewish life in China is possible, vibrant in major cities, and surprisingly workable even in smaller centers. The challenges are real but surmountable. This comprehensive guide will help you not just survive, but thrive as a Jew in China.

## Part 1: The Landscape of Jewish Life in China

### Major Jewish Communities

**Shanghai** has the largest and most established Jewish community in China. The city hosts multiple synagogues, kosher restaurants, Jewish schools, and active community organizations. Shanghai's Jewish history dates back to the 19th century, including the famous story of Jewish refugees during World War II.

**Beijing** offers a substantial community with synagogues, kosher food options, and regular community events. As the capital, it attracts diplomats, businesspeople, and academics who strengthen the Jewish infrastructure.

**Hong Kong**, while technically separate, remains a major hub with well-established institutions, multiple Orthodox and Reform communities, a Jewish Community Center, and extensive kosher dining options.

**Guangzhou, Shenzhen, and other cities** have smaller but growing communities, often centered around expat populations and occasional visiting rabbis.

### Historical Context

Understanding China's relationship with Jews provides important perspective. The Kaifeng Jews, descendants of Silk Road traders, lived in China for over a thousand years. While this community has largely assimilated, it represents the longest Jewish presence in East Asia.

In the 1930s-40s, Shanghai became a haven for over 20,000 Jewish refugees fleeing the Holocaust—one of the few places accepting Jews without visas. This history creates a generally positive attitude toward Jews in China, though awareness of Judaism remains limited outside major cities.

## Part 2: Shabbat Observance in China

### Before You Arrive

**Plan your living situation:** If possible, choose housing within walking distance of a synagogue or Jewish community center. In Shanghai and Beijing, this is feasible. In smaller cities, you'll need to create your own Shabbat environment.

**Understand your work situation:** Negotiate Shabbat accommodations before accepting a position. Some international companies are flexible; others may require creative solutions like shift-swapping or working Sunday-Friday schedules.

### Practical Shabbat Preparations

**Thursday night is your new Friday night** for shopping and cooking. Chinese markets are typically open daily, but get your Shabbat supplies early:

- Fresh vegetables and tofu (widely available)
- Eggs (kosher-acceptable from regular stores)
- Rice and grains (stock up on basics)
- Fish with scales (check local markets carefully)

**Set up your apartment for Shabbat:**

- Install timers for lights (bring from home or order internationally—Chinese apps often require constant phone interaction)
- Prepare hot water in a thermos before Shabbat
- Pre-cook all meals—Chinese rice cookers with timers are helpful
- Consider a Shabbat-friendly hotplate for warming food

**Technology challenges:** Chinese smart home devices often require phone apps. Bring mechanical timers or simple electrical timers that don't need connectivity.

### Finding Community

**In major cities:**
- Chabad Houses in Shanghai and Beijing offer Shabbat meals and services
- Contact them weeks in advance during busy seasons
- Expect a warm welcome but diverse observance levels
- Services may be abbreviated compared to what you're used to

**In smaller cities:**
- WeChat groups connect isolated Jews
- Consider hosting Shabbat for others in similar situations
- Video calls with family/friends abroad (set up before Shabbat if needed for isolation)
- Some creative Jews organize "Shabbat clusters"—finding another Jewish family or two and rotating hosting

### Shabbat Meals

**Host with Chinese elements:** Incorporate Chinese-Jewish fusion:
- Steamed fish (Chinese style, kosher ingredients)
- Stir-fried vegetables (prepared before Shabbat)
- Rice as your starch (authentic to both traditions!)
- Chinese tea culture fits beautifully with Shabbat

**Challah challenge:** No Jewish bakeries in most cities means:
- Learn to bake (many Jews in China become excellent bakers)
- Join online communities sharing recipes for high-altitude or high-humidity areas
- Some expat bakeries take custom orders

### Walking on Shabbat

Chinese cities are generally very walkable, but:
- Crossing streets can be chaotic—plan routes carefully
- Sidewalk obstacles (bikes, vendors) require alertness
- Summer heat and winter pollution affect walking comfort
- Parks offer pleasant Shabbat afternoon strolls in most cities

## Part 3: Kashrut in China

### The Kashrut Reality Check

Full glatt kosher living is possible in Shanghai and Beijing, challenging but workable in other major cities, and requires significant compromise in smaller locations. Your approach depends on your observance level and personal choices.

### Available Kosher Food

**Shanghai:**
- Multiple kosher restaurants (meat and dairy)
- Kosher meat deliveries (imported, expensive)
- Kosher grocery stores with imported products
- Some Chinese restaurants certified for vegetarian dishes

**Beijing:**
- Chabad-affiliated kosher dining
- Imported kosher products at specialty stores
- Growing selection but more limited than Shanghai

**Other cities:**
- Occasional kosher catering for events
- Online ordering from Shanghai/Beijing (plan ahead)
- International product delivery (explore Taobao, JD.com)

### Navigating Non-Kosher Environments

**For those maintaining kashrut strictly:**

Bring from home:
- Kosher utensils, pots, pans, dishes
- Portable electric burner
- Serving pieces for when you're invited out
- Sealed kosher packaged foods

**The vegetarian/fish approach:**

Many Jews in China maintain kashrut by:
- Eating only vegetarian and fish dishes when dining out
- Bringing their own utensils to restaurants
- Choosing Buddhist vegetarian restaurants (often very strict)
- Ordering steamed or boiled dishes to avoid animal fat

**Identifying ingredients:**

Learn these Chinese characters:
- 猪肉 (zhūròu) - pork
- 虾 (xiā) - shrimp
- 蟹 (xiè) - crab
- 鱿鱼 (yóuyú) - squid
- 牛肉 (niúròu) - beef
- 鸡肉 (jīròu) - chicken
- 鱼 (yú) - fish
- 豆腐 (dòufu) - tofu
- 蔬菜 (shūcài) - vegetables

Download translation apps, but be aware: menus often don't list all ingredients, especially cooking oils and sauces.

### Building Your Kosher Kitchen

**Stock imported items:**
- Wine/grape juice (Chinese wine often contains additives)
- Matzah for Passover (order months ahead)
- Kosher-certified products when available
- Bring specialty items from visits home

**Local acceptable products:**
- Fresh fruits and vegetables (wash thoroughly)
- Rice, noodles, basic grains (check for additives)
- Eggs from regular stores
- Tea, coffee (unflavored, pure varieties)
- Some dairy (if you accept local standards—research carefully)
- Salt, sugar, oil (pure varieties)

**Check everything:** Chinese food regulations differ from Western standards. Products that seem simple may contain unexpected ingredients.

### Passover Preparation

Start preparing in January:
- Contact Chabad for community seder arrangements
- Order matzah and Passover products from overseas
- Connect with other families to share bulk orders
- Some communities organize group import shipments
- Plan for significantly higher costs than at home

## Part 4: Jewish Holidays in China

### High Holidays

**Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur:**

In major cities:
- Synagogues offer full services
- Register early—space fills quickly
- Expect crowds of Jews who rarely attend otherwise
- Beautiful blend of traditions from the international community

In smaller cities:
- Organize private services via WeChat groups
- Some fly to Shanghai/Beijing/Hong Kong for the holidays
- Live-streaming from abroad (check halakhic opinions)
- Reading services from prayer books

**Getting time off work:**

Foreign companies are usually accommodating, but:
- Explain holidays well in advance
- Chinese New Year vacation time may not align
- Consider negotiating flexible personal days
- Some use sick days as last resort

**The shofar challenge:**

Bringing a shofar into China is legal but:
- Declare it at customs as a musical instrument
- Carry documentation explaining religious use
- Some have been questioned; patience and explanation usually work
- Consider having one already in-country

### Sukkot

**Building a sukkah:**

Apartments with balconies:
- Possible if structurally safe and allowed by building management
- Check with neighbors about temporary structures
- Weather varies greatly by region

Community sukkahs:
- Synagogues and Chabad houses build them
- Join for meals and celebrations
- Often located in compound courtyards

Creative solutions:
- Some use parks for symbolic sukkah time
- Portable pop-up sukkahs (check import regulations)
- Community shares one sukkah, rotating meals

**Finding a lulav and etrog:**

Imported sets:
- Order through community organizations
- Expensive due to import costs
- Share with others to reduce costs
- Some communities organize group purchases

### Hanukkah

The easiest holiday logistically:
- Menorahs are portable and easy to bring
- Candles available at Western stores or online
- Olive oil works too
- Public menorah lighting in some cities
- Connects nicely with Chinese New Year if timing aligns
- Potato latkes work with local ingredients!

### Purim

Costumes, noise, and celebration:
- Chinese culture loves festive celebrations
- Megillah readings at communities
- Hamantaschen baking parties
- Some communities have impressive Purim spiels
- Great opportunity for cultural exchange

### Passover

The most challenging:

**Matzah:**
- Import or community bulk orders
- Very expensive
- Start planning in January
- Some bake their own (requires research and preparation)

**Avoiding chametz:**
- Difficult in China where wheat and soy sauce dominate
- Label your Passover utensils clearly
- Consider renting separate housing if very strict
- Many rely on packaged Passover products from abroad

**The seder:**
- Community seders fill up fast—register early
- Many host private seders for friends
- Beautiful fusion opportunity: Chinese Jews have unique traditions
- Haggadahs available in Chinese for cultural exchange

**Time off:**
- Negotiate well in advance
- Some spread vacation days across the holiday
- Working days may require creative kashrut solutions

### Shavuot, Tisha B'Av, and Minor Holidays

Smaller communities may only gather for major holidays. You'll often observe these privately:
- Online resources for learning and prayer
- Connect with Jews in similar situations
- Some travel to major cities for Shavuot
- Tisha B'Av observance may be very private

### Building Relationships

**Cultural considerations:**

Chinese society values:
- Respect for tradition and elders (align with Jewish values!)
- Education and scholarship (common ground)
- Family centrality (easy connection point)
- Long-term relationships (invest in building community)

**Explaining Judaism:**

Most Chinese people have limited knowledge of Judaism:
- Be prepared to answer basic questions
- Comparisons to Buddhism sometimes help (ancient tradition, books, practices)
- Many are fascinated by Jewish emphasis on education
- Share holiday celebrations as cultural exchange

**Interfaith appreciation:**

China's religious landscape:
- Buddhism, Taoism, folk religion, Christianity also present
- Government policy on religion varies
- Generally respectful interfaith environment in practice
- Jews often seen positively (smart, successful, good at business)

### For Families with Children

**Jewish education:**

Shanghai:
- Shanghai Jewish Community & Welfare Center offers programs
- Some Hebrew schools and Sunday schools
- Bar/Bat Mitzvah preparation available

Other cities:
- Online Jewish education programs
- Private tutors (sometimes via video)
- Summer trips to Israel or Jewish camps abroad
- Home-based Jewish education

**Raising Jewish kids in China:**

Challenges:
- Limited peer community in smaller cities
- Explaining difference from majority
- Maintaining Hebrew and Jewish literacy
- Missing extended family traditions

Opportunities:
- Exposure to another ancient culture
- Developing strong Jewish identity through active choice
- Learning resilience and adaptation
- Bilingual/bicultural upbringing
- Appreciation for diversity

**Creating Jewish home environment:**

- Jewish books and media (ship from abroad or download)
- Regular Shabbat and holiday observance
- Connect with Jewish kids via video
- Annual trips to Jewish communities
- Emphasize what makes being Jewish special

### Social Life and Dating

**For singles:**

Jewish dating in China is challenging:
- Small communities mean limited options
- Jewish dating apps work internationally
- Some fly to major cities for community events
- Inter-city connections via WeChat
- Consider whether China is long-term before settling

**Intermarriage considerations:**

Some Jews in China date non-Jews:
- Personal choice requiring serious thought
- Conversion resources extremely limited in China
- Long-term implications for Jewish identity
- Family reactions may be complex
- Support systems far from home

**For couples and families:**

- Finding couple friends in community
- Shabbat hospitality creates bonds
- Expat and Jewish communities overlap
- School connections for families with kids

## Part 6: Navigating Cultural Differences

### Understanding Chinese Perspectives on Jews

**Common perceptions:**

Positive stereotypes:
- Intelligent and educated
- Successful in business
- Strong family values
- Ancient, respected culture

Misconceptions to address:
- Confusion with Israel (political vs. religious)
- Limited understanding of religious practice
- Occasional assumptions based on stereotypes

**The Israel factor:**

China-Israel relations are complex:
- Generally positive business and trade relations
- Government position on Palestinian issues
- Most Chinese people separate Jews from politics
- Be prepared to distinguish Judaism from Israeli policy

### Language and Communication

**Learning Chinese helps:**

Key phrases for Jewish life:
- "我是犹太人" (Wǒ shì yóutàirén) - "I am Jewish"
- "我不吃猪肉" (Wǒ bù chī zhūròu) - "I don't eat pork"
- "这是素的吗?" (Zhè shì sù de ma?) - "Is this vegetarian?"
- "犹太教堂" (Yóutài jiàotáng) - "Synagogue"
- "安息日" (Ānxírì) - "Shabbat"

**Translation tools:**

- Pleco app for food characters
- WeChat's built-in translation
- Google Translate (via VPN)
- Photo translation for menus

### Work Environment

**Explaining religious needs:**

- Frame in terms of cultural practice
- Compare to Chinese New Year importance
- Emphasize advance planning and reliability
- Offer solutions, not just requests

**Friday afternoon Shabbat:**

- Winter sunset comes early
- Negotiate flexible hours or early start
- Some work from home Fridays
- Build trust first, then request accommodation

**Holiday negotiations:**

- Chinese companies often allow personal days
- Explain once, then annual reminder
- Show respect for Chinese holidays in return
- Offer to work during others' holidays

### Daily Life Adaptations

**Names:**

Consider a Chinese name:
- Helps with daily interactions
- Many expats adopt one
- Can reflect Jewish name meaning
- Jewish community will still use Hebrew/English name

**Food culture:**

Chinese business culture centers on meals:
- Vegetarian strategy for business dinners
- Explain dietary practices diplomatically
- Host dinners at kosher/vegetarian restaurants
- Turn limitation into interesting conversation

**Privacy and community:**

- Chinese culture is less private in some ways
- Questions about religion, family, money common
- Build boundaries respectfully
- Close friends will respect your practices

## Part 8: Thriving, Not Just Surviving

### The Growth Opportunity

Living as a Jew in China offers unique spiritual growth:

**Intentionality:** Every Jewish act requires conscious choice. This deepens observance rather than diluting it. Shabbat becomes special because you actively create it, not just inherit it.

**Adaptability:** Our ancestors thrived in Babylon, Spain, Poland, Morocco—every Jewish community adapted while maintaining core identity. You're continuing that tradition.

**Bridge-building:** You represent Judaism to Chinese friends and colleagues. This sacred responsibility sharpens your own understanding and pride.

**Appreciation:** Distance from large Jewish communities makes you treasure every Jewish moment—a melody in services, a Shabbat meal, a Jewish wedding.

### Creating Your Own Tradition

Chinese Jews develop unique practices:
- Fusion foods combining Chinese ingredients with Jewish recipes
- Celebrating lunar calendar awareness from both cultures
- Teaching children to code-switch between identities
- Finding parallels between Confucian and Jewish values

Your Chinese-Jewish life will be different from your parents' Jewish experience—and that's okay. Authenticity means living Jewishly in your context, not replicating Brooklyn or Jerusalem in Shanghai.

### The Bigger Picture

**You are not alone in history:**

- Jews of Kaifeng maintained identity for centuries
- Shanghai's Jewish refugees built vibrant community
- Today's Chinese Jews create modern synthesis
- You're part of the ongoing Jewish story

**You contribute to Jewish diversity:**

- Global Judaism is enriched by your experience
- Chinese-Jewish perspectives matter
- Your children may bridge cultures in unique ways
- Your presence strengthens world Jewish community

### Advice from Jews Who've Thrived in China

**"Start with community, not isolation."** Connect immediately with other Jews. Don't wait until you're lonely.

**"Flexibility with firmness."** Know your non-negotiables (Yom Kippur, Passover seder) and where you can adapt.

**"Invest in your home practice."** The strongest Jewish life comes from what you build personally, not just community attendance.

**"Learn the language."** Mandarin fluency dramatically improves daily life and work accommodation.

**"See it as adventure."** Maintaining Judaism in China is challenging—frame it as meaningful journey.

**"Stay connected globally."** Regular contact with family, teachers, and Jewish communities elsewhere keeps you grounded.

**"Document and share."** Your experience helps other Jews. Write about it, advise newcomers, contribute to collective knowledge.

## Conclusion: Your Jewish Journey in China

Being Jewish in China is an adventure in identity, commitment, and creativity. You will face challenges unknown to Jews in New York, London, or Tel Aviv. You will also experience the profound satisfaction of actively choosing and building your Jewish life.

Some days will be hard. You'll miss Jewish community, family Shabbat dinners, walking to shul with crowds of Jews, or grabbing kosher food effortlessly. You'll struggle with work-Shabbat conflicts, holiday loneliness, or explaining your practices for the hundredth time.

Other days will be magical. You'll celebrate Shabbat with Jews from six continents in a Shanghai apartment. You'll teach Chinese colleagues about Hanukkah and learn about Mid-Autumn Festival in return. You'll feel the strength of Jewish identity chosen consciously every day. You'll raise children fluent in two ancient civilizations.

This guide provides practical tools, but your Jewish life in China will ultimately be what you make it. Be resourceful, stay connected, build community, maintain flexibility, and hold fast to core values.

Two ancient peoples, two enduring civilizations, one remarkable journey.

**Welcome to Jewish life in China. May you not only survive, but truly thrive.**
