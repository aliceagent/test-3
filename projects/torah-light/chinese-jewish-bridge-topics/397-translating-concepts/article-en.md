# Translating Jewish Concepts into Chinese: The Challenges of Religious Language

## Introduction: The Bridge Between Worlds

When Rabbi Yitzhak Cohen first attempted to translate the Book of Psalms into Mandarin Chinese in the 1940s, he encountered a profound challenge that would resonate across generations: how does one convey the spiritual essence of Jewish thought in a language shaped by entirely different philosophical and religious traditions? This question remains as relevant today as Jewish communities in China grow, as interfaith dialogue deepens, and as Chinese speakers worldwide seek to understand Jewish wisdom.

The challenge of translating Jewish concepts into Chinese extends far beyond simple vocabulary substitution. It touches upon fundamental questions of meaning, cultural context, and spiritual experience. While modern technology has made translation more accessible than ever, the gulf between Hebrew theological language and Chinese linguistic structures reveals something profound about both traditions—and about the very nature of religious language itself.

## The Nature of the Challenge

### Linguistic Structures and Worldviews

Hebrew and Chinese represent two fundamentally different approaches to language. Hebrew, a Semitic language, builds meaning through a root system where three-consonant roots carry core meanings that expand through grammatical patterns. The language is inherently connected to the narrative of the Jewish people, with layers of biblical, rabbinic, and modern usage creating depth in even simple words.

Chinese, by contrast, is an isolating language where meaning emerges from the combination of characters, each carrying semantic and often etymological significance. Chinese philosophical and religious vocabulary developed primarily within Confucian, Daoist, and Buddhist frameworks—traditions with fundamentally different assumptions about the divine, human nature, and the cosmos than those found in Judaism.

This structural difference means that translating Jewish concepts isn't merely about finding equivalent words—it's about bridging between entire systems of thought.

### The Problem of Cultural Context

Every language carries within it the accumulated wisdom, assumptions, and worldview of its speakers. When a Jewish concept is embedded in centuries of rabbinic discussion, liturgical use, and lived religious practice, that context becomes inseparable from the meaning. A Chinese reader encountering a translation without this context may understand the words but miss the resonance.

Consider this: when a Jewish child hears the word "kedushah" (holiness) in synagogue, they hear it within a web of associations—Shabbat candles, the Kiddush blessing, separation from the ordinary, the special nature of the Jewish people. A Chinese reader seeing a translation has none of these associations, only the translated term itself.

## Three Central Concepts: Deep Dives

### Kedushah (קְדֻשָּׁה): The Untranslatable Holiness

#### The Hebrew Concept

Kedushah, typically translated as "holiness" or "sanctity," derives from the root קדש (k-d-sh), meaning to be set apart, consecrated, or dedicated. In Jewish thought, kedushah operates on multiple levels simultaneously:

1. **Divine holiness**: God's essential nature as wholly other, transcendent, and perfect
2. **Sanctified holiness**: Objects, times, and places set apart for divine service
3. **Moral holiness**: The ethical imperative to imitate divine attributes
4. **National holiness**: Israel's special relationship with God

The concept appears throughout Jewish life: in the Kedushah prayer where the congregation proclaims God's holiness, in the Kiddush that sanctifies Shabbat and holidays, in the laws of kashrut that create categories of permitted and forbidden foods, and in the ethical mandate to be a "holy nation."

#### Chinese Translation Challenges

The standard Chinese translation for kedushah is **神圣 (shénshèng)**, combining 神 (shén, "spirit/god/divine") and 圣 (shèng, "sage/holy"). This term effectively conveys the sense of something being sacred or divine. However, several nuances are lost:

1. **Separation vs. Perfection**: While kedushah emphasizes separation and distinctiveness, shénshèng tends toward moral perfection and sagacity, reflecting Confucian ideals rather than the Jewish concept of categorical difference.

2. **Active vs. Static**: Hebrew kedushah is often something one *does*—one makes something holy through action (kiddush). Chinese shénshèng is more inherently a state of being.

3. **Relational Dimension**: Kedushah in Judaism always exists in relationship to God; something is holy because of its connection to the divine. The Chinese term can carry this meaning but doesn't inherently require it.

Alternative translations have been attempted:
- **圣洁 (shèngjié)**: Emphasizes purity and cleanliness, closer to ritual purity
- **神性 (shénxìng)**: "Divine nature," perhaps too abstract
- **分别为圣 (fēnbié wéi shèng)**: "Separated as holy," more literal but awkward

Some contemporary translators use **神圣 (shénshèng)** while adding extensive footnotes explaining the Hebrew concept's full meaning, effectively creating a dual-text approach.

### Teshuvah (תְּשׁוּבָה): Return, Repentance, and Transformation

#### The Hebrew Concept

Teshuvah, from the root שוב (sh-u-v, "to return"), represents one of Judaism's most profound spiritual concepts. While often translated as "repentance," this fails to capture the word's full significance:

1. **Spatial Return**: Literally coming back to a place one has left
2. **Relational Return**: Restoring a broken relationship with God
3. **Moral Transformation**: Becoming a different person through the process
4. **Cyclical Pattern**: A recurring need and opportunity throughout life

Maimonides outlined the complete process of teshuvah: abandoning the sin, removing it from one's thoughts, resolving never to repeat it, confessing verbally, and regretting the past action. The concept assumes human free will, divine compassion, and the possibility of genuine transformation.

Teshuvah reaches its peak during the Ten Days of Repentance between Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur, when according to tradition, God's judgment can still be altered through sincere return.

#### Chinese Translation Challenges

The most common Chinese translation is **悔改 (huǐgǎi)**, combining 悔 (huǐ, "regret/repent") and 改 (gǎi, "change/correct"). This captures the notion of regret and reformation but misses several crucial elements:

1. **Return vs. Reform**: Teshuvah emphasizes returning to an original state of connection with God; huǐgǎi emphasizes changing from bad to good. The Jewish concept assumes an inherent bond that can be restored, not merely ethical improvement.

2. **Relational vs. Ethical**: While huǐgǎi can imply regret for wronging another person, its primary sense is ethical correction. Teshuvah is fundamentally about relationship with the divine.

3. **Process vs. Event**: Teshuvah is an ongoing spiritual journey; Chinese translations tend to sound more like discrete acts of repentance.

Alternative approaches include:
- **回归 (huíguī)**: "Return," capturing the spatial metaphor but losing the moral dimension
- **悔罪 (huǐzuì)**: "Regret sin," more explicitly religious but still missing the return concept
- **转向 (zhuǎnxiàng)**: "Turn around," used in some Christian contexts but unfamiliar to Chinese Jewish readers

Some translators have adopted **归正 (guīzhèng)** ("return to correctness") or created compound terms like **回转悔改 (huízhuǎn huǐgǎi)** ("turn back and reform"), but these lack the elegant simplicity of the Hebrew original.

### Chesed (חֶסֶד): Love Beyond Translation

#### The Hebrew Concept

Chesed represents perhaps the most translation-resistant concept in Jewish theology. Usually rendered as "loving-kindness," "mercy," or "grace," the word encompasses a complex web of meanings:

1. **Covenantal Loyalty**: Faithfulness to relational obligations, especially in the divine-human covenant
2. **Unmerited Kindness**: Grace extended beyond what justice requires
3. **Active Love**: Not merely feeling but doing—concrete acts of kindness
4. **Divine Attribute**: One of the thirteen attributes of God revealed to Moses
5. **Human Obligation**: The imperative to imitate divine chesed in human relationships

The prophet Micah's famous declaration captures its centrality: "What does the Lord require of you but to do justice, love chesed, and walk humbly with your God?" (Micah 6:8). Chesed appears over 250 times in the Hebrew Bible, always carrying nuances of loyalty, kindness, and relational commitment.

#### Chinese Translation Challenges

Chinese translators have struggled more with chesed than perhaps any other Jewish concept. Common translations include:

**慈爱 (cí'ài)**: "Compassionate love"
- Pros: Captures the loving aspect and divine compassion
- Cons: Lacks the covenantal loyalty dimension; feels more like general benevolence than committed relationship

**恩慈 (ēncí)**: "Grace and mercy"
- Pros: Implies unmerited favor, closer to the theological sense
- Cons: More passive than the active chesed; emphasizes what is received rather than relationship

**良善 (liángshàn)**: "Goodness/kindness"
- Pros: Simple and clear
- Cons: Too generic; misses the covenantal and theological depth

**怜悯 (liánmǐn)**: "Mercy/pity"
- Pros: Used in some Chinese Bible translations for related Hebrew concepts
- Cons: Implies superiority and distance rather than covenantal partnership

Some contemporary Jewish translators in China use **圣约之爱 (shèngyuē zhī ài)** ("covenantal love") or **信实之爱 (xìnshí zhī ài)** ("faithful love"), attempting to capture both the loving and loyal dimensions. However, these compound phrases lack the single-word power of chesed.

The difficulty reflects a fundamental difference: Chinese ethical philosophy, particularly in Confucianism, emphasizes proper relationships within a hierarchical order (仁 rén, filial piety, etc.), while chesed represents a kind of gracious loyalty that transcends what is merely proper or required.

## Broader Patterns and Principles

### The Compound Solution

One emerging approach in Chinese Jewish translation is creating compound terms that combine multiple Chinese concepts to approximate a single Hebrew idea. For example:

- Shalom (שָׁלוֹם) → **平安祝福 (píng'ān zhùfú)** ("peace-safety blessing")
- Emunah (אֱמוּנָה, "faith/faithfulness") → **信心与信实 (xìnxīn yǔ xìnshí)** ("trust and faithfulness")

This approach sacrifices elegance for accuracy, creating longer phrases that more fully capture the Hebrew meaning. It mirrors the challenge faced by Chinese Bible translators centuries ago and continues to evolve.

### Borrowing and Innovation

Another strategy involves borrowing terms from Chinese Buddhist translations. Buddhism faced similar challenges translating Sanskrit concepts into Chinese, leading to creative solutions:

- Creating new characters for untranslatable concepts
- Transliterating foreign words into Chinese sounds
- Adapting existing Chinese philosophical terms with new meanings

Jewish translators have occasionally borrowed Buddhist terminology where conceptual overlap exists. For example, some use **业报 (yèbào)**, the Buddhist concept of karmic consequence, when discussing divine justice, though this requires careful contextualization to avoid theological confusion.

### Footnotes and Dual Texts

Increasingly, Chinese Jewish texts use extensive footnoting, providing the Hebrew original alongside the Chinese translation and explanatory notes. This educational approach acknowledges that full translation may be impossible while giving readers tools to understand the original concept.

Some Chinese Jewish prayer books present text in three columns: Hebrew, transliteration, and Chinese, allowing readers to engage with all three levels simultaneously.

## Real-World Applications and Implications

### Jewish Education in Chinese Communities

For the small but growing Jewish communities in Beijing, Shanghai, and Hong Kong, translation challenges have practical implications. When teaching children who speak Chinese at home, how does one convey Jewish concepts effectively?

Some Jewish schools have adopted a bilingual approach, teaching key concepts in both Hebrew and Chinese from an early age, allowing children to develop fluency in both religious languages. Others use Chinese as the teaching language but introduce Hebrew terms for untranslatable concepts, creating a hybrid vocabulary.

### Interfaith Dialogue

Translation challenges also shape interfaith conversations between Jews and Chinese speakers of other faiths. When a Chinese Buddhist and a Chinese Jew discuss concepts of holiness, compassion, or spiritual return, are they talking about the same things? The linguistic gap can actually create productive dialogue, forcing both parties to articulate their traditions' unique features.

### Academic Scholarship

Scholars studying Judaism in China face the translation challenge in their research. Chinese academics writing about Jewish thought must choose whether to use established translations (which may be inadequate) or create new terminology (which may confuse readers). This tension has led to parallel vocabularies in different academic circles.

### Literary Translation

Translating Jewish literature into Chinese—from biblical texts to modern Hebrew poetry—requires translators to make constant choices about how to render religious concepts. Some translators prioritize readability, using familiar Chinese religious vocabulary even when imperfect. Others prioritize accuracy, creating neologisms or extensive notes.

## Looking Forward: The Future of Jewish-Chinese Translation

### Technology and AI

Machine translation tools are increasingly sophisticated, but they struggle especially with religious and cultural concepts. AI cannot capture the lived experience embedded in religious language. However, technology may help create better reference tools, databases of translation choices, and interactive texts that allow readers to explore multiple layers of meaning.

### Community-Driven Development

The most promising developments come from Jewish communities in Chinese-speaking contexts themselves. As these communities mature, they develop organic vocabularies that work in practice—terms that emerge from lived religious life rather than academic translation exercises.

### Cross-Pollination of Wisdom

Ultimately, the challenge of translation offers an opportunity: as Jewish concepts are rendered in Chinese, both traditions are enriched. Chinese readers gain access to Jewish wisdom, while the effort to articulate Jewish ideas in new linguistic frameworks can illuminate aspects of those concepts that Hebrew speakers might take for granted.

## Conclusion: Translation as Bridge-Building

The difficulty of translating kedushah, teshuvah, chesed, and countless other Jewish concepts into Chinese isn't merely a technical problem—it's a profound encounter between civilizations. Every translation choice represents an interpretation, a bridge built between different ways of understanding the sacred.

Perfect translation may be impossible, but the effort itself is sacred work. It embodies the Jewish value of sharing wisdom with all people, the Chinese appreciation for careful use of language, and the universal human drive to communicate across boundaries.

As Jewish communities in China grow and as Chinese speakers worldwide engage with Jewish thought, the work of translation continues. Each generation must wrestle anew with these challenges, finding fresh language for ancient truths. In this ongoing effort, both Hebrew and Chinese are enriched, and the possibility of genuine cross-cultural understanding comes closer to reality.

The words may never perfectly match, but the pursuit of understanding—the willingness to sit with difficulty, to acknowledge limits, to build bridges anyway—that itself is a form of chesed, a kind of holy work, a continuous return to the possibility of connection across difference.
