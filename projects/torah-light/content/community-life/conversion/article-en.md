# Conversion to Judaism: A Complete Guide

## Introduction: Opening the Door

Have you felt drawn to Judaism? Perhaps you've been studying Torah, observing Shabbat, or exploring Jewish practices, and now you're wondering: *Can I become Jewish?*

The answer is yes. Judaism has always welcomed sincere converts who wish to join the Jewish people. In Hebrew, this process is called **giyur** (גיור), and a person who converts is called a **ger** (גר, masculine) or **giyoret** (גיורת, feminine).

This guide will walk you through everything you need to know about conversion to Judaism—especially from an Orthodox perspective and with practical guidance for those in Asia and China.

## What is Conversion (Giyur)?

### More Than Changing Religion

Conversion to Judaism is unique. You're not just adopting new beliefs—you're joining a **people**, a **nation**, and a **covenant** with God that goes back nearly 4,000 years.

When you convert, you become part of the story that began with Abraham and Sarah, continued through the Exodus from Egypt, and reaches into the future redemption. The Talmud teaches that converts were present at Mount Sinai in spirit, and that their souls were always meant to receive the Torah.

### The Soul of a Convert

Jewish tradition teaches something beautiful: the soul of a sincere convert has always been Jewish. The process of conversion doesn't create something new—it reveals what was always there. Rabbi Yitzchak Luria (the Arizal) taught that converts have souls from the "mixed multitude" (*erev rav*) who left Egypt with the Jewish people, or they are reincarnations of Jewish souls from previous generations.

When someone feels an unexplainable pull toward Judaism, we see it as the soul calling to come home.

## The Conversion Process: Step by Step

### 1. Initial Exploration and Contact

**Finding Your Path:**
Before beginning formal conversion, spend time learning and experiencing Jewish life:

- **Study Torah**: Read, take classes, explore Jewish texts
- **Experience Shabbat**: Attend synagogue services, Shabbat meals
- **Connect with community**: Join Jewish gatherings, festivals, classes
- **Speak with Jews**: Ask questions, learn from their experiences

**Finding a Sponsoring Rabbi:**
This is the most critical step. You need a rabbi who will:
- Guide your study and spiritual growth
- Monitor your progress in Jewish observance
- Vouch for your sincerity before the beit din (rabbinical court)
- Support you through the process and beyond

In Orthodox Judaism, conversions must be performed according to *halakha* (Jewish law), and the sponsoring rabbi must be Orthodox and qualified to oversee conversions.

### 2. The Study Period

**How Long Does It Take?**
There is no fixed timeline. The process typically takes:
- **Minimum**: 1 year
- **Average**: 1.5-3 years
- **Sometimes longer**: 3-5 years or more

Why so long? Because you need to:
- Learn Hebrew (at least reading ability)
- Study Jewish law, philosophy, history
- Master the practical commandments (mitzvot)
- Experience a full cycle of Jewish holidays
- Integrate into Jewish community life
- Demonstrate sincere commitment

**What You'll Study:**
- **Jewish Law (Halakha)**: Shabbat observance, kashrut (dietary laws), family purity, daily prayers
- **Jewish Thought**: Torah, Prophets, Writings, Talmud, Jewish philosophy
- **Jewish Practice**: How to pray, make blessings, observe holidays
- **Hebrew**: Reading prayers and basic understanding
- **Jewish History**: From biblical times to the present
- **Lifecycle Events**: Weddings, brit milah, bar/bat mitzvah, mourning practices

### 3. Living as a Jew (Before Conversion)

During your study period, you'll begin living a Jewish life:

- **Shabbat Observance**: No work, use of electricity, travel; focus on prayer, study, family, rest
- **Kosher Diet**: Separating meat and dairy, eating only kosher animals prepared correctly
- **Daily Prayers**: Shacharit (morning), Mincha (afternoon), Ma'ariv (evening)
- **Holiday Observance**: Rosh Hashanah, Yom Kippur, Sukkot, Passover, Shavuot, and more
- **Modest Dress**: Covering knees, elbows, collarbone; married women covering hair
- **Torah Study**: Daily study of Torah and Jewish texts

**Important Note**: Before conversion, you are not yet obligated in mitzvot, but you practice them as preparation. This is why some candidates delay certain practices (like putting on tefillin for men) until after conversion.

### 4. The Beit Din (Rabbinical Court)

When your rabbi believes you're ready, you'll appear before a **beit din**—a court of three qualified Orthodox rabbis.

**What Happens:**
- **Questions**: The rabbis will ask about your knowledge, motivations, and commitment
- **Topics Covered**: Your understanding of Jewish law, your willingness to observe mitzvot, your reasons for converting
- **Not an Exam**: They're assessing sincerity and readiness, not trying to fail you
- **Acceptance**: If accepted, you proceed immediately to the next steps

**Sample Questions:**
- Why do you want to become Jewish?
- Do you understand that being Jewish means accepting all 613 commandments?
- Are you prepared to raise your children Jewish?
- How will you handle challenges (antisemitism, social isolation)?
- Explain your understanding of Shabbat observance / kashrut / [other mitzvah]

### 5. Immersion in the Mikvah

After the beit din accepts you, you immerse in a **mikvah** (מקווה)—a ritual bath of natural water.

**For Women:**
- Prepare as for monthly mikvah: bathe, remove all barriers (jewelry, makeup, nail polish)
- Immerse completely, with every part of body and hair underwater
- Recite the blessing while in the water (a rabbi or female attendant will be nearby)
- Immerse three times total

**For Men:**
- Similar preparation
- Immersion witnessed by the beit din (or their representatives)
- Some customs include immersing in natural water (sea, river) when possible

**The Blessing:**
After the first immersion, you recite:

בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה ה' אֱלֹקֵינוּ מֶלֶךְ הָעוֹלָם אֲשֶׁר קִדְּשָׁנוּ בְּמִצְוֹתָיו וְצִוָּנוּ עַל הַטְּבִילָה

*Baruch atah Adonai, Eloheinu melech ha'olam, asher kid'shanu b'mitzvotav v'tzivanu al ha'tevilah.*

"Blessed are You, Lord our God, King of the universe, who has sanctified us with His commandments and commanded us concerning immersion."

This is the moment of transformation. You emerge from the mikvah as a full Jew.

### 6. Brit Milah (For Men)

**If Uncircumcised:**
A ritual circumcision performed by a trained *mohel* (ritual circumciser) according to Jewish law.

**If Already Circumcised:**
A symbolic drop of blood is taken (*hatafat dam brit*) to signify entering the covenant of Abraham.

**When:**
Usually before the mikvah, but customs vary. Some communities do it after, or on the same day.

### 7. Receiving Your Hebrew Name

You'll be given a Hebrew name, usually:
- **Pattern**: [Your name] ben Avraham Avinu (son of Abraham our father) or bat Sarah Imeinu (daughter of Sarah our mother)
- **Meaning**: You become a spiritual child of Abraham and Sarah, the first converts
- **Example**: David ben Avraham Avinu, Rachel bat Sarah Imeinu

This is your official Jewish name, used for all religious documents (ketubah, get, burial, Torah honors).

## Orthodox vs. Other Conversions

### Why Orthodox Conversion?

In the Jewish world, not all conversions are equal. Let's be direct about this:

**Orthodox Conversion:**
- Accepted by all streams of Judaism
- Recognized in Israel for citizenship under the Law of Return
- Performed according to traditional Jewish law
- Requires full commitment to Torah observance
- Your children will be Jewish without question

**Non-Orthodox Conversion (Conservative, Reform, Reconstructionist):**
- May not be recognized by Orthodox communities
- May face challenges in Israel
- Often less demanding regarding observance
- Easier in some ways, but can lead to complications later

**Why This Matters:**
If you convert through a non-Orthodox process:
- You may not be able to marry in an Orthodox ceremony
- Your Jewishness may be questioned in Israel
- Your children's Jewishness may be challenged
- You may need to convert again if you later want Orthodox acceptance

**The Orthodox Perspective:**
Orthodox Judaism holds that conversion must include:
1. **Acceptance of the yoke of commandments** (*kabbalat ol mitzvot*)—a full commitment to observe all Torah laws
2. **Circumcision** (for men)
3. **Immersion in a valid mikvah**
4. **Supervision by a qualified beit din**

Without all four elements, according to halakha, the conversion is not valid.

### A Note on Stringency

Some Orthodox conversion courts are more stringent than others. In recent years, especially in Israel, standards have tightened. Some battei din (plural of beit din) require:
- Living in an Orthodox community for an extended period
- Multiple character witnesses
- Detailed investigation of your lifestyle
- Commitment to specific communities or locations

This can be frustrating, but remember: they're protecting the integrity of the Jewish people. A recognized conversion ensures you and your descendants face no questions.

## Life as a Ger/Giyoret

### Your Status in Jewish Law

Once you convert, you are **100% Jewish**—no asterisks, no second-class status.

- You can marry any Jewish person
- Your children are fully Jewish
- You can become a rabbi, cantor, Torah scribe
- You count in a minyan (prayer quorum)
- You are called to the Torah like any born Jew

**What's Different:**
- A *kohen* (priestly Jew) cannot marry a convert (this applies to a female convert; male converts can marry anyone)
- You cannot serve in certain Temple roles reserved for kohanim or Levites
- You may not be asked about your convert status in most contexts—it's considered insensitive

### Challenges You May Face

**Social Challenges:**
- Some born Jews may not understand or accept you at first
- You may feel "not Jewish enough" or like an outsider
- Family background questions can be awkward
- Dating can be complex (some families prefer born Jews)

**Practical Challenges:**
- Learning so much, so fast
- Leaving behind non-kosher foods, Shabbat activities
- Explaining to non-Jewish family and friends
- Financial costs (kosher food, Jewish books, tuition)
- Isolation if you live far from a Jewish community

**Emotional Challenges:**
- Feeling disconnected from your birth heritage
- Grief over losing parts of your former identity
- Imposter syndrome
- The weight of taking on mitzvot

### Strength and Honor

Jewish tradition honors converts deeply. The Torah commands us **36 times** (some say 46) to love and protect the convert—more than any other mitzvah.

You are following in the footsteps of:
- **Ruth**: The Moabite woman who became the ancestor of King David
- **Onkelos**: The Roman aristocrat who translated the Torah into Aramaic
- **Rabbi Akiva's teacher**: Who was a convert
- **Shemaya and Avtalyon**: Convert scholars who led the Jewish people

The Talmud says that in the messianic era, converts will be higher in status than born Jews, because they chose this path without being born into it.

## Famous Converts in Jewish History

### Ruth the Moabite

The most famous convert in Jewish history. A Moabite princess, she married a Jewish man who died. Her mother-in-law Naomi told her to return to her people, but Ruth refused:

> "Do not urge me to leave you or to return from following you. For wherever you go, I will go, and wherever you lodge, I will lodge. **Your people shall be my people, and your God my God.**" (Ruth 1:16)

Ruth converted, married Boaz, and became the great-grandmother of King David—and therefore an ancestor of the future Messiah.

### Onkelos

A Roman nobleman and nephew of the Emperor Titus (who destroyed the Second Temple). Despite his privileged position, Onkelos studied Torah, converted to Judaism, and produced the authoritative Aramaic translation of the Torah still studied today.

When Roman soldiers came to arrest him for converting, he converted them too. Three times.

### Rabbi Akiva's Teachers: Shemaya and Avtalyon

Two of the greatest sages of the Second Temple period were descendants of converts (some say they themselves were converts). They led the Sanhedrin and taught thousands, including the legendary Hillel.

### Medieval and Modern Converts

- **Count Valentine Potocki**: Polish nobleman burned at the stake in 1749 for converting to Judaism
- **Ivanka Trump**: Converted before marrying Jared Kushner (though her observance level is debated)
- **Countless others**: Throughout history, people of all backgrounds have been drawn to join the Jewish people

## Practical Steps: Starting Your Journey from China

### Phase 1: Learn and Explore (3-6 months)

1. **Read broadly** about Judaism (see book list above)
2. **Try Shabbat** observance at home
3. **Start learning Hebrew** (free apps: Duolingo, Memrise; websites: HebrewPod101)
4. **Connect online** with Chinese or Asian Jews
5. **Contact local Chabad** in your city (Beijing/Shanghai)

### Phase 2: Make Contact (Months 6-12)

1. **Visit Chabad** regularly if possible
2. **Speak with the rabbi** about your interest (be patient; they'll want to assess sincerity)
3. **Begin informal study** with community members or online
4. **Increase observance**: Keep kosher, pray daily, study Torah
5. **Assess your commitment**: Can you see yourself doing this for life?

### Phase 3: Formal Study (1-3 years)

1. **Find a sponsoring rabbi** (may be abroad, requiring travel)
2. **Enroll in structured learning** (yeshiva, conversion program, or distance learning)
3. **Spend time in a Jewish community** (Israel, US, Hong Kong, Australia)
4. **Live fully Jewish**: Shabbat, kashrut, prayer, modesty, all mitzvot
5. **Prepare for beit din**

### Phase 4: Conversion (When Ready)

1. **Appear before beit din**
2. **Immerse in mikvah**
3. **Brit milah or hatafat dam brit** (for men)
4. **Receive your Hebrew name**
5. **Celebrate!** You're Jewish!

### Phase 5: Integration (Lifelong)

1. **Continue learning** (Jews study Torah their entire lives)
2. **Deepen observance** and connection
3. **Find your place** in the Jewish community
4. **Build a Jewish home**
5. **Teach your children** (if you have them)

## Common Questions

### "Can I convert if I'm married to a non-Jew?"

Yes, but it's complicated. Orthodox conversion requires full Torah observance, which is difficult in a non-observant household. Many rabbis prefer candidates to be single or married to Jews. However, some will work with you if your spouse is supportive.

### "Will my non-Jewish family disown me?"

This varies widely. Some families are accepting, others struggle. Remember: honoring parents is a mitzvah, but so is following God's path for you. Many converts maintain loving relationships with their families while living Jewish lives.

### "Do I have to move to Israel?"

No. You can live anywhere with a Jewish community. However, many converts feel a strong pull to Israel, and it offers the most immersive Jewish environment.

### "What if I don't speak Hebrew?"

You need basic Hebrew reading ability (for prayers), but fluency isn't required. Most converts study Hebrew during the conversion process.

### "Can I convert if I'm over 40? 60?"

Absolutely. Judaism welcomes converts of all ages. It may take longer if you're starting from scratch, but age is no barrier.

### "Is conversion expensive?"

It can be. Costs include:
- Kosher food (more expensive than regular food)
- Jewish books and study materials
- Travel to Jewish communities
- Tuition for classes or yeshiva
- Mikvah fees, beit din fees (usually modest)

Some communities offer scholarships or assistance. Don't let finances alone stop you.

### "What if I mess up after converting?"

You're still Jewish. Converts (and born Jews!) make mistakes. Judaism has repentance (teshuvah). If you struggle with observance later, you're still fully Jewish—just a Jew who's struggling, like many others.

## Final Thoughts: Is This for You?

Conversion to Judaism is not easy. It's one of the hardest spiritual paths you can choose. You'll give up:
- Familiar foods and habits
- Saturdays as you knew them
- Casual relationships with the opposite sex
- Blending in

You'll gain:
- A people and a heritage
- A direct relationship with God through Torah
- The beauty of Shabbat and Jewish life
- A mission and purpose
- A place in the story of eternity

**This is not for everyone.** And that's okay.

But if you feel called—if your soul won't let you rest, if Torah feels like home, if Shabbat feels like coming up for air—then welcome.

We've been waiting for you.

The door is open. Come in.
