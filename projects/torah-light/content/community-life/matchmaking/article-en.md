# Shidduchim: The Jewish Path to Finding Your Life Partner

## Introduction

Finding a life partner is one of the most significant decisions you'll ever make. In Jewish tradition, this journey has a unique name and approach: *shidduchim* (שידוכים) — the sacred process of matchmaking that has guided countless Jewish couples toward building beautiful families and homes filled with Torah values.

Whether you're just beginning to think about marriage, actively dating, or supporting someone in their search, this guide will walk you through the wisdom, practices, and modern realities of the shidduch process. Our goal is to empower you with knowledge, encourage you with hope, and remind you that finding your *bashert* (destined one) is a partnership between your efforts and Divine providence.

## What is a Shidduch?

The word *shidduch* (plural: *shidduchim*) comes from the Hebrew root meaning "to match" or "to pair." In its simplest form, a shidduch refers to a suggested match between two eligible singles for the purpose of marriage. More broadly, the term encompasses the entire traditional Jewish approach to dating and marriage.

### The Concept of Bashert

At the heart of shidduchim lies a beautiful belief: forty days before a child is formed, a heavenly voice announces, "The daughter of so-and-so is destined for so-and-so" (Talmud, Sotah 2a). This concept of *bashert* — your destined match — provides profound comfort and perspective throughout the search.

However, the Talmud also teaches that bringing two people together requires Divine intervention comparable to the splitting of the Red Sea. This teaches us that while there is a destined partner, finding them requires effort, prayer, and sometimes patience.

### Traditional vs. Modern Shidduchim

Traditional shidduchim, as practiced in more insular Orthodox communities, often involve parents and matchmakers taking the lead, with young people meeting for the first time at the suggestion of others. The process is highly structured, focused, and efficient.

Modern Orthodox and other communities have adapted the system, blending traditional values with contemporary sensibilities. Young adults often take more initiative, dates may be longer and more casual, but the fundamental principles remain: dating with purpose, emphasis on character and values, and involvement of family and community.

## Why Matchmaking? The Purpose of Jewish Dating

### Purpose-Driven vs. Casual Dating

The shidduch system fundamentally differs from secular dating culture in its purpose and approach:

**Secular dating** often focuses on:
- Entertainment and casual companionship
- Physical attraction as primary
- "Seeing where things go" without clear goals
- Extended relationships without commitment
- Dating multiple people simultaneously

**Shidduch dating** emphasizes:
- Marriage as the explicit goal from the first date
- Character and values as primary criteria
- Focused evaluation of compatibility
- Respectful boundaries and modesty
- One person at a time, with intention

This doesn't mean shidduch dating is cold or unromantic. Rather, it acknowledges that marriage is too important to approach casually, and that clarity of purpose actually *enhances* the experience by removing ambiguity and game-playing.

### The Wisdom of Structure

The shidduch system provides several advantages:

1. **Efficiency**: By dating with clear purpose, people avoid years in directionless relationships
2. **Protection**: Boundaries protect both parties emotionally and physically
3. **Community Support**: Family and matchmakers provide perspective and guidance
4. **Focus on What Matters**: Emphasis on lasting qualities rather than superficial attraction
5. **Divine Partnership**: Prayer and trust in God's plan provide comfort and direction

## The Shadchan: Role of the Matchmaker

### Who is a Shadchan?

A *shadchan* (matchmaker) is someone who suggests potential matches and facilitates the shidduch process. Shadchanim can be:

- **Professional matchmakers** who do this as a career (often for a fee upon engagement)
- **Community volunteers** who love bringing people together
- **Family friends** who know both families well
- **Rabbis and rebbetzins** who know their community members
- **Friends and relatives** who think two people would be perfect together

### What Does a Shadchan Do?

A good shadchan:

1. **Gets to know both parties**: Understanding personalities, values, backgrounds, and what each person seeks
2. **Suggests appropriate matches**: Based on compatibility factors
3. **Provides information**: Sharing relevant details about both sides
4. **Facilitates the introduction**: Making the initial connection
5. **Offers guidance**: Providing perspective when asked
6. **Maintains confidentiality**: Respecting privacy throughout
7. **Celebrates success**: Rejoicing when a match leads to marriage

### Working with a Shadchan

If you're working with a shadchan:

- **Be honest and specific** about what you're looking for
- **Be open-minded** — your shadchan may see compatibility you don't initially recognize
- **Communicate clearly** after each date about whether to continue
- **Be respectful** of the shadchan's time and efforts
- **Provide feedback** to help them understand you better
- **Say thank you** — whether or not the suggestion works out

Remember: A shadchan's suggestion is just that — a suggestion. You are never obligated to agree to meet anyone, and the final decision is always yours.

## The Shidduch Process: How It Works

While every community and individual experience varies, here's a typical shidduch process flow:

### 1. Research and Suggestion (The Redd)

Someone suggests a match (gives a *redd*). This might come from:
- A shadchan who knows both parties
- A mutual friend or family member
- A dating website or organization
- Parents who heard about someone suitable

### 2. Initial Inquiry

Before agreeing to meet, both sides typically:

- **Gather information** about the other person through discreet inquiries (references)
- **Ask questions** about hashkafa (religious outlook), personality, family, education, goals
- **Pray for guidance** — this is a decision that deserves prayer
- **Discuss with parents/mentors** to get perspective

This "research" phase might feel unusual to those unfamiliar with the system, but it serves an important purpose: ensuring basic compatibility before investing emotional energy in dating.

### 3. Agreement to Meet

Once both sides agree, the shadchan or the young man typically contacts the young woman (or her family) to arrange the first meeting.

### 4. The Dating Period

Dates vary by community custom:

**More traditional communities:**
- Meet in hotel lobbies or quiet public places
- Dates last 1-3 hours
- Conversation-focused, getting to know each other
- Decision after each date whether to continue
- Process may take 3-10 dates before engagement

**Modern Orthodox communities:**
- May go to restaurants, cafes, or activities
- Dates may be longer (3-6 hours)
- More relaxed atmosphere while maintaining boundaries
- May take more dates to reach a decision
- Some integration with each other's lives (meeting friends/family)

### 5. Decision Making

After sufficient dates, both parties must decide:

- **Yes, this is the person I want to marry**
- **No, this isn't the right match**
- **I need more time/information to decide**

The decision should be based on genuine compatibility, shared values, mutual respect, and the feeling that you can build a Torah home together.

### 6. Engagement and Beyond

Once both agree to marry:

- The engagement is announced (often at a *vort* celebration)
- Wedding planning begins
- Pre-marital learning and preparation
- Building toward the wedding day

## What to Look For: The Real Criteria

### Character (Middot) Above All

The Talmud teaches: "Who is wealthy? One who has a wife of good deeds" (Shabbat 25b). Character — *middot* — should be your primary criterion:

- **Kindness** (*chesed*): How do they treat others, especially those who can't benefit them?
- **Honesty** and integrity: Can you trust their word?
- **Humility**: Are they able to admit mistakes and grow?
- **Responsibility**: Do they follow through on commitments?
- **Emotional maturity**: How do they handle stress, disappointment, disagreement?
- **Respect**: Do they respect you, your boundaries, your opinions?

### Hashkafa and Values

You need alignment on fundamental life questions:

- **Level of religious observance**: Are you on the same page about Shabbat, kashrut, prayer, modesty?
- **Torah learning**: How central is Torah study to your lives?
- **Community**: What kind of community do you want to be part of?
- **Life goals**: Career, family size, where to live
- **Parenting philosophy**: How do you envision raising children?

Perfect alignment isn't necessary, but you need to be moving in the same general direction.

### Compatibility and Chemistry

While character and values are primary, practical compatibility matters:

- **Personality fit**: Do you enjoy each other's company?
- **Communication style**: Can you talk through disagreements?
- **Interests**: Enough common ground to share life together
- **Life pace**: Are you both high-energy or more laid-back?
- **Family backgrounds**: While different backgrounds can work, being aware of differences helps

And yes, **physical attraction** matters too. You don't need to feel fireworks on date one, but there should be a developing attraction and respect for each other.

### The "List" vs. Reality

Many singles create lists of qualities they want. This can be helpful for clarity, but beware of:

- **Superficial criteria** that don't predict marital happiness (height, profession, family wealth)
- **Impossibly long lists** that no real human could fulfill
- **Rigidity** that prevents you from seeing someone wonderful who doesn't check every box

The goal is to find someone you can build a *bayit ne'eman b'Yisrael* (faithful home in Israel) with — not a perfect fantasy.

## Dating Guidelines: Navigating the Process

### Modesty and Boundaries

Jewish dating maintains boundaries (*tzniut*) to:

- Protect the emotional and physical purity of both parties
- Allow clear thinking unclouded by physical involvement
- Build a relationship on deeper foundations
- Honor the sanctity of marriage

Specific guidelines vary by community, but generally include:

- **No physical contact** before engagement (in most Orthodox communities)
- **Modest dress** and behavior
- **Public or semi-public meeting places** (especially in early dates)
- **Avoiding situations** that could lead to temptation

These boundaries aren't about being cold or unromantic — they're about building something lasting.

### How Many Dates?

There's no magic number. Some couples know within 3-5 dates; others need 15-20. Factors include:

- **Your community's typical timeline**
- **How much information you had before dating**
- **The complexity of compatibility questions**
- **Your own decision-making style**

What matters is reaching clarity, not hitting a specific number.

### Getting to Know Someone

Good topics for shidduch dates include:

- **Life goals and dreams**: What do you hope to accomplish?
- **Family background**: What was your upbringing like?
- **Hashkafa and practice**: How do you approach your Judaism?
- **Daily life**: How do you spend your time?
- **Values and priorities**: What matters most to you?
- **Challenges**: How do you handle difficulties?
- **Future vision**: What does your ideal life look like in 5, 10, 20 years?

Observe:
- How do they treat waiters, strangers, people they encounter?
- How do they speak about others?
- How do they handle when something goes wrong?
- Are they a good listener?
- Do they take responsibility or blame others?

### When to Say No

You should end a shidduch if:

- **Values misalignment**: Fundamental incompatibility on hashkafa or life goals
- **Character concerns**: Dishonesty, disrespect, anger issues, addiction
- **No developing connection**: After sufficient dates, you just don't feel it
- **Pressure or discomfort**: You feel pressured to continue when your gut says no

Saying no is not a failure. It's wisdom and clarity.

### When to Say Yes

Consider saying yes to engagement when:

- **You respect them deeply** and admire their character
- **You share core values** and life vision
- **You enjoy their company** and can see building a life together
- **Practical compatibility** exists (communication, lifestyle, goals)
- **You feel ready** to commit to marriage with this person
- **Your trusted mentors agree** this is a good match

You don't need absolute certainty — no one has that. But you need genuine confidence and peace with your decision.

## Engagement (Vort) and Wedding Planning

### The Vort

Once both sides agree to marry, Jewish custom includes a *vort* (literally "word") celebration where:

- The engagement is formally announced
- Families meet and celebrate
- A plate is broken (symbolizing the seriousness of the commitment)
- Terms are sometimes written in a *tena'im* (conditions) document
- Joy and blessings abound!

### The Engagement Period

This time is for:

- **Wedding planning**: Venue, date, details
- **Learning together**: Many couples learn Jewish laws of marriage, family purity, and building a Jewish home
- **Getting to know each other better**: Now with the commitment in place
- **Meeting extended family and friends**
- **Preparing emotionally and spiritually** for marriage

### Breaking an Engagement

While rare, engagements can be broken if serious concerns arise. This is painful but sometimes necessary. If you discover dealbreakers during engagement, consult a rabbi immediately.

## For Singles in China and Asia

### Unique Challenges

Jewish singles in China and Asia face special circumstances:

- **Small Jewish populations**: Fewer local options
- **Geographic isolation**: Far from major Jewish communities
- **Cultural differences**: Navigating between Chinese/Asian culture and Jewish tradition
- **Family understanding**: Families may not understand Jewish dating customs
- **Practical logistics**: Difficulty meeting other Jewish singles

### Resources and Strategies

**Long-Distance Dating:**
- Modern technology makes video dates possible
- Many couples successfully date across continents
- Consider traveling to meet in person after initial video meetings
- Be aware that long-distance requires extra patience and communication

**Finding Matches Abroad:**
- **Online platforms**: SawYouAtSinai, JWed, and other Jewish dating sites
- **Shadchanim who specialize** in international matches
- **Community connections**: Chabad houses, Jewish organizations
- **Visiting Jewish communities**: Plan trips to Israel, US, Europe to meet singles
- **Jewish conferences and events**: Birthright, Aish programs, Jewish learning seminars

**Chabad in China:**
Chabad houses in Shanghai, Beijing, Hong Kong, and other cities can:
- Connect you with other Jewish singles
- Provide introductions and suggestions
- Offer guidance and support
- Help you stay connected to Jewish community

**Considering Relocation:**
Some singles choose to:
- Move to Israel for a period to be in a larger dating pool
- Relocate to major Jewish communities (New York, London, Jerusalem)
- Spend extended time in communities with more singles

**Making It Work:**
- Be proactive in your search
- Stay connected to Jewish life wherever you are
- Be open to different backgrounds and locations
- Communicate clearly about your willingness to relocate
- Keep faith — distance hasn't stopped bashert matches!

## Converting and Dating

### For Those in Conversion or Recently Converted

If you're converting to Judaism or recently converted (*ger/giyoret*), you bring a beautiful passion and commitment to Judaism. However, the dating landscape has unique aspects:

**Challenges you may face:**
- Some families prefer someone born Jewish
- Questions about your Jewish knowledge and commitment
- Concerns about your family's non-Jewish background
- Need to prove your sincerity and dedication

**Your strengths:**
- Chosen Judaism consciously and deliberately
- Often very learned and committed
- Bring fresh perspective and deep appreciation
- Strong personal conviction

**Advice:**
- **Be upfront** about your conversion from the start
- **Find supportive communities** that value gerim
- **Connect with shadchanim** who work with converts
- **Know your worth** — you are fully Jewish and deserve a wonderful match
- **Be patient** — the right person will value your journey
- **Consider others** who converted or have converted family members
- **Stay connected** to rabbis and mentors who support you

The Torah teaches that Ruth the convert became the great-grandmother of King David, from whom Mashiach will descend. Your Jewish journey is precious and valued.

## Hishtadlut and Bitachon: Effort and Trust

### The Balance

One of the most important — and challenging — aspects of the shidduch process is balancing:

**Hishtadlut** (human effort):
- Being proactive in your search
- Working with shadchanim
- Going on dates with an open mind
- Working on yourself
- Doing your reasonable research

**Bitachon** (trust in God):
- Believing that God has your best interests at heart
- Trusting in Divine timing
- Not despairing when things don't work out
- Praying for guidance and help
- Surrendering outcomes you can't control

### When to Act, When to Trust

**Act when:**
- You can make reasonable efforts to meet people
- You need to work on personal growth
- You should follow up or communicate clearly
- You need to seek help or guidance

**Trust when:**
- You've done what you reasonably can
- Things aren't in your direct control
- You're tempted to despair or panic
- You need patience for timing beyond your understanding

### Prayer and the Shidduch Process

Prayer is essential:

- **Daily prayer** for finding your bashert
- **Asking others to pray** for you (give your Hebrew name for prayer lists)
- **Visiting holy places** when possible (Kotel, graves of tzaddikim)
- **Psalms**: Chapter 121 for protection, 32 for success, others
- **Segulot** (spiritual practices): Various customs exist, but primary focus should be on teshuva, tefillah, tzedakah

Remember: Prayer is not magic. It's building a relationship with God and inviting Divine blessing into your search.

### Dealing with Disappointment

The shidduch process can include:
- Suggestions that don't work out
- Being rejected (or having to reject others)
- Watching friends get engaged while you're still searching
- Frustration with timing or process

**Healthy responses:**
- Allow yourself to feel disappointed
- Speak with supportive friends or mentors
- Remember: A "no" moves you closer to your "yes"
- Focus on gratitude for what you have
- Stay busy with meaningful activities
- Give yourself breaks when needed

**Unhealthy responses to avoid:**
- Bitterness toward God or the process
- Desperation that clouds judgment
- Settling for someone clearly wrong
- Giving up on the search entirely
- Comparing yourself destructively to others

## Final Encouragement

The shidduch process is a journey — sometimes quick, sometimes long, always meaningful. Here are truths to hold onto:

### You Are Worthy

Regardless of your age, background, challenges, or journey, you are worthy of a beautiful marriage. Don't let anyone — including your own inner critic — convince you otherwise.

### Timing is Not in Your Hands

As hard as it is, the timing of meeting your bashert is ultimately in God's hands. Your friends' timelines are not yours. Trust your unique path.

### The Process Works

Thousands of happy couples met through shidduchim. The system has guided Jewish marriages for generations. It's not perfect, but it works.

### Growth is Part of the Journey

The search itself often brings growth:
- Clarity about what you truly value
- Patience and faith
- Self-awareness and maturity
- Deeper connection with God

This growth serves you whether you marry soon or later.

### Your Bashert is Also Searching

Somewhere, the person meant for you is also hoping, praying, and searching. You're moving toward each other, even if you can't see it yet.

### You're Building Something Holy

Marriage in Judaism is not just personal happiness — it's building a *bayit ne'eman b'Yisrael*, a faithful Jewish home. This sacred work deserves patience, intention, and faith.

## Moving Forward

Whether you're beginning your shidduch journey, in the midst of it, or supporting someone you love:

- **Be proactive** without being desperate
- **Be open-minded** without compromising your values
- **Be patient** with the process and yourself
- **Be prayerful** — invite God into every step
- **Be hopeful** — your bashert is out there
- **Be yourself** — the right person will love the real you

May you find your match in joy and in the right time. May you build a home filled with love, laughter, Torah, and blessing. And may you look back on this journey one day and see God's hand in every step.

*B'sha'ah tovah u'mutzlachat* — May it happen at a good and successful time!
