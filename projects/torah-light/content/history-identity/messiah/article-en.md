# MASHIACH: The Jewish Messiah

## Introduction

The concept of Mashiach—the Messiah—stands as one of Judaism's most profound and enduring beliefs. Unlike many other religions' understanding of a messianic figure, the Jewish conception of Mashiach is rooted in the Torah, developed through the words of the prophets, and clarified by our sages throughout the generations. The Mashiach is not a divine being, not a supernatural savior, but rather a mortal human king descended from the House of David who will usher in an era of unprecedented peace, knowledge of God, and the fulfillment of the divine promises to the Jewish people and all of humanity.

## What is the Mashiach?

The Hebrew word "Mashiach" (מָשִׁיחַ) means "anointed one," referring to the ancient practice of anointing kings with oil as a sign of their divine appointment. In Jewish tradition, the Mashiach will be:

**A Human Being**: The Mashiach will be a mortal man, born naturally to human parents. He will not possess supernatural powers, will not be a demigod, and will not be an incarnation of the Divine. He will be fully human, subject to the same physical limitations as any person.

**From the Davidic Line**: The Mashiach must be a direct patrilineal descendant of King David through his son Solomon. This lineage is essential and non-negotiable. The prophet Jeremiah declares: "Behold, days are coming, says the Lord, when I will establish for David a righteous shoot, and he will reign as king and prosper, and he will perform judgment and righteousness in the land" (Jeremiah 23:5).

**A Great Torah Scholar and Leader**: The Mashiach will be immersed in Torah study and observance. He will surpass all others in his generation in wisdom, understanding, and righteousness. He will be fluent in both the Written and Oral Torah and will inspire the Jewish people to return to Torah observance.

**A Military and Political Leader**: The Mashiach will lead the Jewish people in the ingathering of exiles and the rebuilding of the Temple. He will be a warrior-king who will defeat Israel's enemies and establish Jewish sovereignty over the Land of Israel.

## Biblical Sources: The Prophetic Vision

The prophets of Israel spoke extensively about the messianic era and the figure who would lead it. Their words, preserved in the Hebrew Bible, form the foundation of our messianic belief.

### Isaiah's Vision

The prophet Isaiah provided some of the most detailed and beautiful descriptions of the messianic age:

"And a shoot shall spring forth from the stem of Jesse, and a twig shall sprout from his roots. And the spirit of the Lord shall rest upon him, a spirit of wisdom and understanding, a spirit of counsel and heroism, a spirit of knowledge and fear of the Lord" (Isaiah 11:1-2).

Isaiah continues to describe an era of universal peace: "And the wolf shall live with the lamb, and the leopard shall lie with the kid... They shall neither harm nor destroy in all My holy mount, for the earth shall be full of knowledge of the Lord as water covers the seabed" (Isaiah 11:6-9).

Another powerful prophecy states: "For a child has been born to us, a son has been given to us, and the authority is upon his shoulder, and the wondrous adviser, the mighty God, the everlasting Father, called his name, 'the prince of peace.' To him who increases the authority, and for peace without end, on David's throne and on his kingdom, to establish it and to support it with justice and with righteousness; from now and to eternity, the zeal of the Lord of Hosts shall accomplish this" (Isaiah 9:5-6).

### Micah's Prophecy

The prophet Micah spoke of both the birthplace and the nature of the messianic king:

"And you, Bethlehem Ephrathah—you should have been the lowest of the clans of Judah—from you shall emerge for Me one to be a ruler over Israel; and his origin is from of old, from days of yore" (Micah 5:1).

### Ezekiel's Temple Vision

The prophet Ezekiel received detailed visions of the Third Temple that will be built in the messianic era. Chapters 40-48 of the Book of Ezekiel describe the structure, service, and sanctity of this future Temple. Ezekiel also prophesied about the ingathering of exiles:

"And I will take you from among the nations and gather you from all the countries, and I will bring you to your land" (Ezekiel 36:24).

### Other Prophetic Voices

Jeremiah spoke of a new covenant: "Behold, days are coming, says the Lord, when I will make a new covenant with the house of Israel and with the house of Judah... I will place My law in their innards and I will inscribe it upon their hearts, and I will be their God and they shall be My people" (Jeremiah 31:30-32).

Zechariah prophesied about universal recognition of God: "And the Lord shall become King over the entire earth; on that day the Lord shall be one, and His name one" (Zechariah 14:9).

## Rambam's Criteria: Who Qualifies as Mashiach?

Rabbi Moshe ben Maimon (Maimonides, known as the Rambam, 1138-1204) systematically codified the laws and beliefs regarding the Mashiach in his monumental work, the Mishneh Torah. In the final chapters of his legal code (Hilchot Melachim U'Milchamot, Laws of Kings and Wars, chapters 11-12), Rambam establishes clear criteria for identifying the Mashiach.

### The Presumptive Messiah (Chezkat Mashiach)

According to Rambam, if a king arises from the House of David who:
1. Is immersed in Torah study and observance like David his ancestor
2. Compels all Israel to follow the Torah and strengthen its breaches
3. Fights the battles of God

Then he is considered a "presumptive Messiah" (chezkat Mashiach).

### The Confirmed Messiah (Mashiach Vadai)

If he successfully accomplishes these tasks:
1. Builds the Holy Temple in its place
2. Gathers the dispersed of Israel

Then he is confirmed as the actual Mashiach (Mashiach vadai).

Furthermore, the Mashiach will:
- Perfect the entire world to serve God together
- Bring all nations to recognize and serve the One God

Rambam emphasizes that these are empirical, verifiable criteria. There is no room for doubt or faith-based claims once these conditions are met—the proof is in the accomplishment.

### Important Clarifications

Rambam makes several critical points:

**No Supernatural Signs Required**: The Mashiach need not perform miracles or supernatural wonders. If someone claims to be Mashiach but requires you to accept him based on miraculous signs, he is not the Mashiach.

**Adherence to Torah Law**: The Mashiach will not change or abolish any aspect of Torah law. As Rambam writes: "If he does not succeed to this extent or is killed, he is certainly not the one the Torah promised, and he is like all the other proper and valid kings of the House of David who died."

**Practical, Not Mystical**: The messianic redemption is a this-worldly event involving political sovereignty, military victory, Temple rebuilding, and the ingathering of exiles—not an otherworldly or purely spiritual transformation.

## The Messianic Era: A Vision of Peace and Perfection

What will the world look like when the Mashiach comes? Our sages describe an era of unprecedented peace, prosperity, and universal knowledge of God.

### Universal Peace

"Nation shall not lift sword against nation, and they shall no longer study warfare" (Isaiah 2:4). Wars will cease. The resources and energies previously devoted to conflict will be redirected toward wisdom, knowledge, and spiritual growth.

### The Third Temple

The Holy Temple will be rebuilt in Jerusalem. This will be the Third Temple, following Solomon's Temple (destroyed by the Babylonians in 586 BCE) and the Second Temple (destroyed by the Romans in 70 CE). The Temple service, sacrificial offerings, and the manifestation of the Divine Presence will return.

### Ingathering of Exiles

All Jewish people, from every corner of the world, will return to the Land of Israel. The prophet Isaiah declares: "And it shall come to pass on that day, that a great shofar shall be sounded, and those lost in the land of Assyria and those exiled in the land of Egypt shall come and bow to the Lord on the holy mount in Jerusalem" (Isaiah 27:13).

### Knowledge of God

"For the earth shall be filled with knowledge of the Lord as water covers the seabed" (Isaiah 11:9). In the messianic era, knowledge of God will be universal and profound. People will no longer need to teach one another about God, as this knowledge will be innate and widespread.

### Physical Abundance

The land will produce abundantly. Poverty and hunger will be eliminated. As Rambam writes: "In that era there will be neither famine nor war, neither jealousy nor competition. Good will be abundant, and all delicacies will be as common as dust."

### Eternal Duration

Unlike previous periods of Jewish sovereignty, the messianic era will last forever. The kingdom of the House of David will never again be destroyed.

## Techiyat HaMeitim: Resurrection of the Dead

One of the Thirteen Principles of Faith formulated by Rambam is belief in the resurrection of the dead (techiyat hameitim). This is the belief that at some point—whether during the messianic era or thereafter—the dead will return to bodily life.

### Biblical Foundations

The concept of resurrection appears in several biblical sources:

"And many who sleep in the dust of the earth will awaken—these for everlasting life, and these for shame, for everlasting abhorrence" (Daniel 12:2).

The prophet Ezekiel's vision of the Valley of Dry Bones (Ezekiel 37) is understood as a prophecy of resurrection: "Behold, I am opening your graves, and I will raise you up out of your graves, My people, and bring you to the Land of Israel" (Ezekiel 37:12).

### Rabbinic Elaboration

The Talmud discusses resurrection extensively. It is considered one of the fundamental beliefs of Judaism. The second blessing of the Amidah (the central Jewish prayer) praises God as "He who resurrects the dead."

### Nature of Resurrection

According to most opinions, the resurrected will return in bodily form—not as disembodied spirits but as complete human beings with both body and soul reunited. The righteous of all generations will merit resurrection, and they will live in the Land of Israel in the messianic era.

## Olam HaBa: The World to Come

Related to but distinct from the messianic era is the concept of Olam HaBa—the World to Come. This refers to the spiritual afterlife where the soul enjoys closeness to God after death.

### The Nature of Olam HaBa

Rambam describes Olam HaBa as a purely spiritual existence. The soul, freed from the body, experiences the radiance of the Divine Presence. This is the ultimate reward for a life of Torah observance and good deeds.

As Rambam writes: "In the World to Come, there is no body or physical form, only the souls of the righteous, without bodies, like the angels... They derive pleasure from the radiance of the Divine Presence."

### Earning Olam HaBa

Every Jew has a portion in the World to Come (Mishnah Sanhedrin 10:1), though this can be forfeited through certain severe transgressions or heretical beliefs. The World to Come is earned through Torah study, mitzvah observance, and ethical living in this world.

### Relationship to the Messianic Era

The messianic era is a this-worldly reality—a perfected physical world where humans live in peace and serve God. Olam HaBa is the spiritual reward in the afterlife. Some authorities distinguish between the two, while others see Olam HaBa as a subsequent stage following the messianic era and resurrection.

## False Messiahs in History

Throughout Jewish history, various individuals have claimed to be the Mashiach or have been proclaimed as such by followers. Each ultimately failed to meet the criteria established by the Torah and our sages.

### Bar Kochba (c. 132-135 CE)

Simon bar Kochba led a major Jewish revolt against Roman rule. Rabbi Akiva, one of the greatest sages of the Mishnaic period, initially believed he might be the Mashiach. Bar Kochba succeeded in briefly liberating Jerusalem and establishing an independent Jewish state. However, he was ultimately defeated and killed by the Romans, and the revolt ended in catastrophic destruction. His failure proved he was not the Mashiach.

### Shabbtai Tzvi (1626-1676)

Perhaps the most notorious false messiah was Shabbtai Tzvi, a charismatic figure from Smyrna (modern-day Turkey). In the 1660s, with the support of the self-proclaimed prophet Nathan of Gaza, Shabbtai Tzvi claimed to be the Mashiach. His movement spread rapidly throughout the Jewish world, creating mass hysteria and messianic fervor.

Communities sold their possessions, prepared for the imminent redemption, and engaged in ecstatic celebrations. However, in 1666, when confronted by the Ottoman Sultan and given the choice between death and conversion to Islam, Shabbtai Tzvi converted. This apostasy shattered the movement and left deep scars in Jewish communities worldwide. Some followers, incredibly, continued to believe in him even after his conversion, creating heretical sects that persisted for generations.

The Shabbtai Tzvi episode became a cautionary tale about messianic fervor and the importance of adhering to Rambam's clear, empirical criteria for identifying the Mashiach.

### Other Claimants

Throughout history, other figures have arisen claiming messianic status: David Alroy (12th century), Abraham Abulafia (13th century), David Reuveni and Solomon Molcho (16th century), and Jacob Frank (18th century), among others. Each failed to fulfill the prophecies and criteria, often leading followers into spiritual and sometimes physical destruction.

## Why We Wait: Active Preparation

Jewish belief in the coming of the Mashiach is not passive. It is an active hope, a daily anticipation, and a constant motivation to improve ourselves and the world.

### Daily Expression

One of Rambam's Thirteen Principles of Faith states: "I believe with complete faith in the coming of the Mashiach, and even though he may delay, nevertheless I anticipate every day that he will come."

This principle is incorporated into the daily prayer liturgy in the hymn "Ani Ma'amin" (I believe): "Ani ma'amin be'emunah sheleimah be'vi'at haMashiach, ve'af al pi she'yitmame'ah, im kol zeh achakeh lo bechol yom sheyavo"—"I believe with perfect faith in the coming of the Messiah, and even though he may tarry, still I await him every day."

### Hastening the Redemption

Jewish tradition teaches that while the redemption will ultimately come at its appointed time, our actions can hasten its arrival. The Talmud states: "Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi found Elijah the Prophet... He asked him: 'When will the Mashiach come?' He replied: 'Go ask him yourself.' 'Where is he?' 'At the gate of Rome, sitting among the poor and sick...'" (Tractate Sanhedrin 98a).

What hastens the redemption?
- **Teshuvah (Repentance)**: Returning to God and Torah observance
- **Mitzvah Observance**: Performing commandments, especially acts of loving-kindness
- **Torah Study**: Increasing knowledge and teaching of Torah
- **Unity**: Jewish unity and love between fellow Jews
- **Prayer**: Sincere prayer for the redemption

### Improving the World

The concept of "tikkun olam"—repairing the world—motivates Jews to work toward making the world a place fit for the Mashiach. This includes pursuing justice, helping the poor, spreading ethical monotheism, and working for peace.

## Jewish vs. Christian Views: A Respectful Clarification

It is important to understand how the Jewish conception of Mashiach differs fundamentally from Christian messianic beliefs, particularly regarding Jesus.

### The Core Differences

**Human vs. Divine**: Judaism emphatically rejects the notion that the Mashiach is or will be divine. The Mashiach is a mortal human being. The Christian belief in Jesus as the "Son of God" or as part of a divine trinity is completely incompatible with Jewish monotheism.

**Task Completion**: According to Jewish criteria, the Mashiach must actually accomplish his mission—rebuild the Temple, gather the exiles, bring peace. These are empirical, verifiable achievements. Jesus did not accomplish these tasks. The Christian concept of a "second coming" to complete the mission is foreign to Jewish messianic belief.

**Torah Observance**: The Mashiach will strengthen Torah observance, not abrogate it. Christianity teaches that Jesus fulfilled and superseded the Torah, establishing a "new covenant." This contradicts the eternal, unchanging nature of the Torah as understood in Judaism.

**Suffering and Atonement**: Christianity emphasizes Jesus's suffering and death as a vicarious atonement for humanity's sins. Judaism rejects the concept of human sacrifice for atonement and teaches that each person is responsible for their own sins and repentance.

**Lineage Questions**: Even setting aside theological differences, according to traditional Jewish law, Jesus would not qualify as the Mashiach due to questions about his patrilineal Davidic lineage (Jewish genealogy for kingship goes through the father, not the mother).

### Respectful Dialogue

These differences are stated not to denigrate Christian beliefs but to clarify authentic Jewish teaching. Jews respect the right of others to hold different beliefs while maintaining the integrity of their own tradition. The fundamental distinction remains: the Jewish Mashiach is a human king who will accomplish tangible, this-worldly goals; he is not a divine savior who died for humanity's sins.

As Rambam wrote, even Christianity and Islam have played a role in spreading knowledge of the Torah and preparing the world for the true messianic era: "All these matters of Jesus of Nazareth and that Ishmaelite [Mohammed] who arose after him are only to straighten the way for the Messianic King and to prepare the entire world to serve God together, as it says: 'For then I will turn to the peoples a pure language that all of them call in the name of God and serve Him with one accord.'"

## Conclusion: Living with Messianic Hope

The belief in the coming of the Mashiach is not an abstract theological concept—it is a living, breathing part of Jewish consciousness. It shapes how we pray, how we view history, how we relate to the Land of Israel, and how we conduct our daily lives.

We pray for the Mashiach three times daily in the Amidah. We conclude the Passover Seder with "Next year in Jerusalem!" We break a glass at weddings to remember the destruction of the Temple and yearn for its rebuilding. We look at the suffering and challenges in the world and say, "Surely the redemption is near."

This hope is not escapism or passivity. It is a call to action—to live righteously, to study Torah, to perform acts of kindness, to pursue justice, and to make ourselves and the world ready for the great transformation that is to come.

As we recite in our daily prayers: "Speedily cause the offspring of David Your servant to flourish, and lift up his glory by Your salvation, for we hope for Your salvation all day long. Blessed are You, Lord, who causes the glory of salvation to flourish."

May we merit to see the coming of Mashiach speedily in our days. Amen.
