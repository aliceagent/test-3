# Jews in Asia: A Journey Through History and Identity

## Introduction

The story of Jews in Asia is a remarkable tapestry woven with threads of trade, refuge, resilience, and renewal. From the ancient Kaifeng community that flourished during China's Song Dynasty to the vibrant Jewish centers of modern-day Shanghai, Hong Kong, and Singapore, Asian Jewish history spans more than a millennium. This journey encompasses Baghdadi merchant princes who built empires, Russian refugees fleeing pogroms, Holocaust survivors who found sanctuary in Shanghai, and contemporary communities maintaining Jewish life across the world's largest continent.

Today, Jewish communities in Asia continue to thrive, supported by Chabad houses, synagogues, kosher restaurants, and educational institutions. Whether you're a Jewish resident, a curious traveler, or someone exploring Jewish heritage, understanding this rich history illuminates how Judaism has taken root in diverse Asian cultures while maintaining its essential identity.

## The Kaifeng Jews: China's Ancient Jewish Community

### Origins in the Song Dynasty

The Kaifeng Jewish community represents one of the most fascinating chapters in Jewish diaspora history. According to tradition and historical evidence, Jewish merchants from Persia and India arrived in Kaifeng, then the capital of China's Song Dynasty, during the 10th or 11th century. The exact date remains debated, but most scholars place their arrival between 960-1127 CE.

These merchants traveled along the Silk Road, bringing cotton cloth, spices, and precious goods. The Chinese emperor welcomed them, reportedly saying: "You have come to our China; reverence and preserve the customs of your ancestors." This royal welcome set the tone for centuries of relative tolerance and integration.

### The Stele: Written Testimony

The most important historical evidence of the Kaifeng Jewish community comes from three stone steles (memorial tablets) erected in 1489, 1512, and 1663. These inscriptions, written in Chinese, provide invaluable information about the community's history, beliefs, and practices.

The 1489 stele records the reconstruction of the Kaifeng synagogue after a Yellow River flood. It describes the community's arrival, their reception by the emperor, and their loyalty to Chinese customs while maintaining Jewish practices. The stele refers to Judaism as "Yitzelie Jiao" (the religion of Israel) and mentions observance of the Sabbath, dietary laws, and circumcision.

The inscriptions reveal fascinating details:
- The community possessed Torah scrolls written in Hebrew
- They observed major Jewish holidays including Passover and Yom Kippur
- They maintained kosher dietary practices
- They practiced circumcision and observed traditional marriage customs
- Chinese names were adopted while Hebrew names were preserved for religious purposes

### Golden Age and Integration

During the Ming Dynasty (1368-1644), the Kaifeng Jewish community reached its peak, numbering perhaps 4,000-5,000 individuals. Community members achieved success in Chinese society, with many passing the imperial examinations and serving as government officials, physicians, and scholars.

The community built a substantial synagogue complex that included:
- The main prayer hall housing Torah scrolls
- A study hall for religious education
- A ritual bath (mikveh)
- A sukkah (temporary dwelling for the Feast of Tabernacles)
- Memorial tablets honoring ancestors

The architectural style was distinctly Chinese, with curved roofs and traditional ornamentation, yet the interior arrangements followed Jewish religious requirements. Torah scrolls were housed in ark-like structures facing west toward Jerusalem.

### Decline and Assimilation

The community's decline began in the 17th century due to multiple factors:

**Natural Disasters**: Devastating Yellow River floods in 1642 destroyed the synagogue and much of Kaifeng, killing hundreds of thousands including many Jews. While the synagogue was rebuilt in 1653, the community never fully recovered.

**Loss of Religious Leadership**: As China's Jewish population was small and isolated from other Jewish communities, maintaining educated religious leadership proved difficult. When the last rabbi died in the early 18th century, no qualified successor emerged. Without proper religious instruction, knowledge of Hebrew gradually faded.

**Assimilation**: Unlike Jewish communities elsewhere that faced persecution and thus maintained sharp boundaries, Kaifeng Jews experienced tolerance that paradoxically led to assimilation. Intermarriage with the Chinese population increased, especially as the community declined numerically. Chinese cultural practices increasingly replaced Jewish ones.

**Isolation**: The community's geographic isolation from other Jewish centers meant no reinforcement of religious knowledge or practice. By the 19th century, when Western Jewish travelers and missionaries began visiting Kaifeng, they found a community that remembered its Jewish identity but had lost most religious knowledge and practices.

### Modern Rediscovery

The Kaifeng Jewish community came to Western attention in the early 17th century when Jesuit missionary Matteo Ricci encountered a Kaifeng Jew named Ai Tian in Beijing in 1605. This meeting sparked Western interest in Chinese Jews.

In the 19th century, Protestant missionaries and Jewish travelers documented the declining community. They found families who identified as Jewish, possessed fragmentary knowledge of Jewish customs, but could no longer read Hebrew or conduct proper religious services. The synagogue fell into disrepair and was finally demolished in the 1850s.

**20th Century Revival**: In the late 20th and early 21st centuries, interest in Kaifeng Jewish heritage has grown tremendously:

- Several hundred Kaifeng residents identify as Jewish descendants
- Some have studied Judaism and sought formal conversion to reconnect with their heritage
- Israeli organizations have assisted some Kaifeng Jews in immigrating to Israel
- The Kaifeng Jewish History Museum opened to preserve this heritage
- DNA studies have confirmed genetic links between Kaifeng Jews and other Jewish populations

Today, while no functioning Jewish community exists in Kaifeng, the legacy endures through descendants who maintain their unique identity and through scholarly interest in this remarkable historical community.

## Shanghai: The Jerusalem of the East

### Baghdadi Jews: Builders of an Empire

Shanghai's Jewish story begins with Sephardic Jews from Baghdad, Bombay, and other Middle Eastern cities who arrived in the mid-19th century. These Baghdadi Jews, speaking Arabic, Persian, and eventually English, came as merchants and traders following the opening of China's treaty ports.

**The Sassoon Family**: No name is more synonymous with Shanghai's Jewish community than Sassoon. David Sassoon (1792-1864) built a vast trading empire spanning from Bombay to Hong Kong to Shanghai. His sons, particularly Elias David Sassoon and Albert Abdullah David Sassoon, expanded operations in Shanghai, dealing in cotton, opium, real estate, and banking.

The Sassoons became Shanghai's wealthiest family, known as the "Rothschilds of the East." They built:
- Sassoon House (today's Peace Hotel), an Art Deco masterpiece on the Bund
- Numerous warehouses, hotels, and commercial buildings
- The first Chinese-owned cotton mill
- Extensive real estate throughout Shanghai

**The Kadoorie Family**: Sir Elly Kadoorie arrived in Shanghai in 1880 and, along with the Sassoon family, built an enormous business empire. The Kadoories invested in:
- Hotels (including the Peninsula Hotel in Hong Kong)
- Electric power companies (China Light & Power)
- Rubber plantations
- Real estate development

The family became legendary philanthropists, funding schools, hospitals, and charitable institutions for all Shanghai residents, regardless of ethnicity or religion.

**Community Building**: The Baghdadi Jewish community established:
- Ohel Rachel Synagogue (1920), named after the matriarch of the Sassoon family, which still stands today
- The Shanghai Jewish School
- A Jewish Recreation Club
- The Jewish Cemetery
- Numerous charitable organizations

### Russian Jews: Refugees from Pogroms

The second wave of Jewish immigration to Shanghai came from Russia. Fleeing pogroms, the Russian Revolution, and civil war, thousands of Russian Jews arrived between 1900 and 1925, with major influxes following the 1917 Revolution.

These Ashkenazi Jews were culturally distinct from the Baghdadi community:
- They spoke Russian and Yiddish rather than Arabic and English
- They were often less wealthy, working as shopkeepers, craftsmen, and professionals
- They established their own synagogues, schools, and cultural organizations

The Russian Jewish community settled primarily in the Hongkew district (today's Hongkou) and established:
- Ohel Moishe Synagogue (1927), now the Shanghai Jewish Refugees Museum
- Russian-language newspapers and theaters
- Jewish cafés, bakeries, and shops
- Schools teaching Russian and Hebrew

By the 1930s, Shanghai had approximately 5,000 Russian Jews forming a vibrant community complete with Yiddish theater, Russian Jewish restaurants, and active political and cultural life.

### World War II Refugees: Haven of Last Resort

Shanghai's most dramatic chapter came during World War II when approximately 20,000 Jewish refugees from Nazi Germany, Austria, and Poland fled to Shanghai—one of the only places in the world that required no visa for entry.

**Why Shanghai?**: As Nazi persecution intensified after Kristallnacht (November 1938), Jews desperately sought refuge. Most countries, including the United States, Britain, and others, severely restricted Jewish immigration. Shanghai, then under separate Chinese and foreign administration with no central authority to impose visa requirements, became a haven of last resort.

**The Journey**: Refugees traveled by ship from European ports, often via Genoa, through the Suez Canal, to Shanghai—a journey taking 6-8 weeks. They arrived with little more than suitcases, having lost homes, businesses, and property to Nazi confiscation.

**Life in the Hongkew Ghetto**: Most refugees settled in Hongkew, already home to the Russian Jewish community. Living conditions were challenging:
- Overcrowded housing in a district already damaged by Japanese bombing
- Limited resources and few job opportunities
- Cultural and language barriers
- Tropical diseases and poor sanitation

Despite hardships, the refugee community exhibited remarkable resilience:
- Cafés, bakeries, and businesses opened along Chusan and Wayside Roads
- Schools taught children in German and Hebrew
- Cultural activities included orchestras, theater, and cabarets
- Jewish religious life continued with multiple synagogues
- Newspapers published in German kept the community informed

**The Japanese Occupation and "Designated Area"**: After Japan occupied Shanghai in 1937 (and completely after Pearl Harbor in December 1941), conditions worsened. In 1943, under German pressure, Japanese authorities established a "Designated Area" in Hongkew—essentially a ghetto restricting Jewish refugees' movement. While harsh, this was not a concentration camp:
- Jews were not systematically killed
- Religious and cultural life continued
- Those with jobs outside the designated area could obtain passes
- The Chinese population remained supportive, sharing scarce resources

**Liberation and Exodus**: When World War II ended in August 1945, Shanghai's Jews learned the full horror of the Holocaust. Most European Jewish refugees had lost family members in the Nazi genocide. The Shanghai community gradually dispersed:
- Many emigrated to the newly established State of Israel (1948)
- Others went to the United States, Australia, or Canada
- Some Russian Jews eventually reached Israel decades later
- Few remained in Shanghai after the Communist victory in 1949

**Legacy**: Today, the Shanghai Jewish Refugees Museum preserves this history. The Ohel Moishe Synagogue has been restored, and the surrounding streets feature plaques and memorials. Former refugees and their descendants have returned to Shanghai to honor the city that saved their lives.

## Hong Kong: A Thriving Community

### Early History

Jewish presence in Hong Kong dates to the colony's founding in 1842. Sephardic Jews from Baghdad and Bombay arrived as merchants and traders, seeing opportunities in Britain's new Chinese port.

**Early Families**: The Sassoon, Kadoorie, and Abraham families became prominent in Hong Kong's development:
- Sir Elly Kadoorie established China Light & Power, which still provides electricity to Hong Kong
- The family built schools, hospitals, and infrastructure
- Jewish merchants contributed to Hong Kong's development as a major trading hub

### Synagogues and Institutions

**Ohel Leah Synagogue**: Built in 1902 by Sir Jacob Sassoon in memory of his mother, this Sephardic synagogue remains Hong Kong's oldest and most prestigious. The building features distinctive Moorish architecture with beautiful stained glass windows.

**Chabad of Hong Kong**: Established in recent decades, Chabad provides services, kosher food, and community activities for both residents and the many Jewish tourists and business travelers visiting Hong Kong.

**Jewish Community Centre**: Founded in 1995, the center offers:
- Social and cultural programs
- Educational activities
- Support for families
- Youth programs
- Holiday celebrations

### Modern Community

Today's Hong Kong Jewish community numbers approximately 2,500-5,000, including:
- Long-term residents and families with multi-generational ties
- Expatriates working in finance, business, and international companies
- Israeli backpackers and young professionals
- Jewish tourists and business travelers

The community maintains:
- Two synagogues (Ohel Leah and United Jewish Congregation)
- Kosher restaurants and catering services
- A Jewish cemetery
- Regular services and educational programs
- Active connection with worldwide Jewish organizations

## Harbin: The Russian Jewish Frontier

In Manchuria's northern reaches, Harbin developed an unexpected Jewish community in the early 20th century. As Russia built the Chinese Eastern Railway through Manchuria (1898-1903), Russian Jewish engineers, merchants, and workers arrived, seeing opportunities in this frontier city.

### Growth and Golden Age

By the 1920s, Harbin's Jewish community numbered approximately 13,000-20,000—the largest in East Asia at the time. The community established:
- The Main Synagogue (1909), a grand building seating over 1,000
- Jewish schools teaching Russian, Hebrew, and Yiddish
- A Jewish hospital
- Multiple newspapers and cultural organizations
- Theatres, libraries, and social clubs

Harbin became a center of Jewish culture and learning. The community was predominantly Ashkenazi, creating a "Little Russia" with:
- Russian Jewish bakeries and restaurants
- Yiddish theater performances
- Hebrew and Yiddish publishing houses
- Zionist organizations
- Jewish sports clubs

### Decline and Exodus

The community declined rapidly after:
- The Russian Civil War (many returned to the USSR)
- The Japanese occupation of Manchuria (1932)
- World War II and the Holocaust
- The Communist victory in China (1949)

By the 1960s, virtually no Jews remained in Harbin. Today, the Main Synagogue still stands, converted to other uses but preserved as a historical monument. A small Jewish community has re-emerged in recent years, mostly consisting of Israeli and Western expatriates.

## Jews of India: Ancient Communities

India's Jewish communities represent some of the oldest continuous Jewish diaspora populations, with three distinct groups:

### Bene Israel

The Bene Israel (Children of Israel) community traces its origins to shipwrecked Jewish refugees who arrived on India's Konkan coast, possibly around 175 BCE. According to tradition, seven men and seven women survived, settling in villages near Bombay (Mumbai).

**Characteristics**:
- Practiced a form of Judaism mixed with local customs
- Observed the Sabbath and Jewish dietary laws
- Maintained circumcision and biblical festivals
- Spoke Marathi and integrated into local society
- Worked primarily as oil pressers, earning the name "Saturday oil-men" for refusing to work on Shabbat

By the 19th century, contact with other Jewish communities led to religious revival. The Bene Israel community grew to approximately 20,000 by the mid-20th century. Most emigrated to Israel after 1948, with smaller numbers in India, the UK, USA, and Canada.

### Cochin Jews

The Cochin Jewish community in Kerala claims ancient origins, with traditions dating settlement to King Solomon's time (10th century BCE) or to the destruction of the Second Temple (70 CE).

**Historical Evidence**:
- Copper plates from the 10th century CE grant privileges to a Jewish leader
- The ancient Paradesi Synagogue (1568) still stands in Cochin
- The community was divided into "White Jews" (later arrivals from Europe) and "Black Jews" (older residents)

The Cochin Jews maintained:
- Distinctive Malayalam-speaking Jewish culture
- Hebrew literacy and Jewish learning
- Synagogues with unique Kerala architectural features
- Trade networks throughout the Indian Ocean

Today, only a handful of elderly Jews remain in Cochin, maintaining the historic synagogue for tourists. Most emigrated to Israel, where they maintain Cochin Jewish traditions.

### Baghdadi Jews

Baghdadi Jews from Iraq, Syria, and Persia arrived in India during the 18th and 19th centuries, settling primarily in Bombay, Calcutta, and other cities. They:
- Spoke Arabic, Judeo-Arabic, and English
- Established successful trading businesses
- Built grand synagogues
- Maintained distinct Sephardic traditions
- Many later moved to Shanghai, Hong Kong, and Singapore

## Japan: A Small but Vibrant Presence

Japan's Jewish community has always been small, numbering 1,000-2,000 today, but it maintains an intriguing presence.

### Historical Context

During World War II, Japanese diplomat Chiune Sugihara saved approximately 6,000 Jews by issuing transit visas allowing escape from Nazi-occupied Europe through Japan to other destinations. This heroic act is commemorated in Israel, where Sugihara is honored as "Righteous Among the Nations."

### Modern Community

Today's Japanese Jewish community includes:
- Orthodox and liberal synagogues in Tokyo
- Jewish Community of Japan (JCJ) coordinating activities
- Chabad of Japan providing services and kosher food
- Small communities in Osaka and Kobe

**Kosher Resources**:
- Several kosher restaurants in Tokyo
- Kosher certification available
- Regular imports of kosher products
- Chabad-run kosher shops

**Cultural Interest**: Japan has shown remarkable interest in Jewish culture, with:
- Numerous books about Judaism
- Jewish-themed museums
- Academic study of Jewish history
- Fascination with Jewish business and educational success

## Singapore and Southeast Asia

### Singapore

Singapore's Jewish community dates to the 1830s when Baghdadi Jewish merchants arrived. The community built:
- Maghain Aboth Synagogue (1878), Singapore's oldest synagogue
- Chesed-El Synagogue (1905)
- A Jewish cemetery
- Jewish Welfare Board coordinating community activities

Today's community numbers approximately 1,000-2,000 and includes:
- Long-established Baghdadi families
- Modern Israeli and Western expatriates
- Active Chabad presence
- Multiple kosher restaurants and stores
- Regular services and educational programs

### Southeast Asia

Small Jewish communities exist throughout Southeast Asia:
- **Thailand**: Bangkok has Chabad centers, kosher restaurants, and services for tourists and residents
- **Philippines**: A small community in Manila with a synagogue
- **Vietnam**: Ho Chi Minh City has emerging Jewish community services
- **Indonesia**: Historical Jewish presence, minimal today

## Modern Jewish Life in Asia

### Chabad Movement

Chabad-Lubavitch has revolutionized Jewish life in Asia by establishing centers throughout the region. These Chabad houses provide:
- Synagogue services
- Kosher food and catering
- Holiday celebrations
- Jewish education
- Welcoming environment for all Jews
- Tourist and business traveler support

Major Chabad centers operate in:
- Beijing, Shanghai, Guangzhou (China)
- Hong Kong
- Bangkok, Phuket, Ko Samui (Thailand)
- Tokyo (Japan)
- Singapore
- Mumbai, Delhi (India)
- And many more locations

### Kosher Food

Kosher food availability has expanded dramatically:
- **Shanghai**: Multiple kosher restaurants, Chabad-supervised catering
- **Hong Kong**: Several kosher restaurants, imported kosher products
- **Bangkok**: Kosher restaurants and butchers
- **Tokyo**: Kosher options available
- **Singapore**: Multiple kosher establishments

Many luxury hotels in major Asian cities can now provide kosher meals with advance notice, and kosher airline meals are standard options.

### Community Resources

Resources for Jewish residents and travelers include:
- Online communities and WhatsApp groups
- Jewish schools (Shanghai, Hong Kong, Singapore, Bangkok)
- Mikvaot (ritual baths) in major cities
- Jewish libraries and learning centers
- Burial societies and cemeteries
- Youth movements and summer camps

## Notable Jews in Asia

### Historical Figures

**David Sassoon (1792-1864)**: Patriarch of the Sassoon business empire, built a trading dynasty spanning from Bombay to Shanghai.

**Sir Elly Kadoorie (1867-1944)**: Philanthropist and businessman who built infrastructure throughout China and Hong Kong, funding schools and hospitals for all communities.

**Chiune Sugihara (1900-1986)**: Though not Jewish himself, this Japanese diplomat saved thousands of Jewish lives during the Holocaust.

### Modern Figures

**Sir Michael Kadoorie**: Continuing his family's legacy, a major Hong Kong businessman and philanthropist.

**Jews in Asian business**: Numerous Jewish entrepreneurs, investors, and executives have contributed to Asian economic development.

**Scholars and educators**: Jewish academics have taught at major Asian universities, fostering cross-cultural understanding.

## Conclusion

The story of Jews in Asia is one of extraordinary diversity, resilience, and adaptation. From the ancient Kaifeng community that maintained Jewish identity for centuries in isolation to the Baghdadi merchant princes who built empires, from the desperate Holocaust refugees who found sanctuary in Shanghai to the thriving modern communities in Hong Kong and Singapore, Asian Jewish history is rich and profound.

Today, Jewish life in Asia continues to flourish. Chabad centers dot the landscape from Beijing to Bangkok, kosher restaurants serve traditional and fusion cuisine, and Jewish communities welcome both long-term residents and travelers. Whether you trace your family roots to Kaifeng, Shanghai, Bombay, or Baghdad, or whether you're discovering Jewish Asia for the first time, this remarkable heritage offers endless fascination.

The future of Jewish life in Asia looks bright, with growing communities, expanding resources, and renewed interest in this unique chapter of Jewish diaspora history. As Asia continues its economic and cultural rise, Jews will undoubtedly remain part of this dynamic region's story, contributing to its development while maintaining ancient traditions and identity.
