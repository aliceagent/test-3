# Ashkenazi and Sephardi Jews: Unity in Diversity

## Introduction

When you walk into synagogues around the world, you might notice something fascinating: the melodies are different, the pronunciations vary, the holiday foods don't always match—yet everyone is studying the same Torah, observing the same festivals, and living by the same fundamental principles. This beautiful diversity within unity is embodied in the distinction between Ashkenazi, Sephardi, and Mizrachi Jewish communities.

These terms describe not theological differences, but rather the rich tapestry of customs (minhagim) that developed as Jewish communities flourished in different parts of the world. Understanding these distinctions helps us appreciate how the same eternal Torah can express itself through diverse cultural lenses, all equally authentic and precious.

## What Do These Terms Mean?

### Geographic and Linguistic Origins

The terms Ashkenazi, Sephardi, and Mizrachi refer primarily to geographic origins and the cultural traditions that developed in those regions:

**Ashkenazi** comes from "Ashkenaz," the medieval Hebrew name for Germany. Ashkenazi Jews are descendants of Jewish communities that settled in the Rhineland (present-day Germany and France) during the early medieval period and later spread throughout Central and Eastern Europe—including Poland, Lithuania, Russia, Hungary, Romania, and beyond.

The distinctive language of Ashkenazi Jews was **Yiddish**, a fusion of medieval German dialects with Hebrew, Aramaic, and Slavic elements, written in Hebrew letters. For centuries, Yiddish served as the daily language of millions of Jews across Eastern Europe, while Hebrew remained the sacred language of prayer and study.

**Sephardi** comes from "Sepharad," the Hebrew name for the Iberian Peninsula (Spain and Portugal). Sephardi Jews trace their ancestry to the Jewish communities that flourished in medieval Spain and Portugal, particularly during the Golden Age of Spanish Jewry (roughly 900-1200 CE).

The Sephardi Jewish language was **Ladino** (also called Judeo-Spanish), a Romance language derived from medieval Spanish and Portuguese, enriched with Hebrew, Aramaic, Turkish, and Greek elements, also written in Hebrew script. After the Expulsion of 1492, Sephardi Jews carried Ladino with them to the Ottoman Empire, North Africa, the Netherlands, and the Americas.

**Mizrachi** means "Eastern" in Hebrew and refers to Jewish communities that remained in or near the Middle East and North Africa. These include ancient communities in Iraq (Babylonian Jews), Yemen, Persia (Iran), Syria, Egypt, Morocco, Tunisia, Libya, and other regions.

Mizrachi Jews never left the Middle East and therefore maintained continuous presence in Arabic and Persian-speaking lands. Many spoke Judeo-Arabic dialects or Judeo-Persian, incorporating Hebrew vocabulary and written in Hebrew letters.

### Important Nuances

The term "Sephardi" is sometimes used broadly to include Mizrachi communities, particularly in Israel where the distinction is often drawn simply between Ashkenazi and Sephardi/Mizrachi. However, technically, Sephardi refers specifically to those with Iberian ancestry, while Mizrachi refers to continuous Middle Eastern/North African heritage.

Additionally, there are other Jewish ethnic groups—such as Italian Jews (Italkim), Romaniote Jews (Greek), Georgian Jews, Indian Jews (Bene Israel, Cochin Jews), Ethiopian Jews (Beta Israel), and others—each with their own ancient traditions.

## Historical Development

### The Ashkenazi Story: From the Rhine to Eastern Europe

Jewish settlement in Germanic lands began during the Roman Empire, but the Ashkenazi community truly crystallized during the early medieval period (900-1100 CE) in the Rhineland cities of Speyer, Worms, and Mainz.

This period saw the emergence of towering rabbinic figures who would shape Ashkenazi Judaism:

- **Rabbenu Gershom Me'or HaGolah** (960-1040), the "Light of the Exile," who established crucial takkanot (rabbinic enactments) including the prohibition of polygamy and the requirement of consent for divorce
- **Rashi** (Rabbi Shlomo Yitzchaki, 1040-1105), whose commentary on the Torah and Talmud remains the foundational text for Jewish learning worldwide
- **Tosafists** (12th-13th centuries), Rashi's descendants and students who developed the dialectical method of Talmudic analysis

The Crusades (beginning 1096) brought terrible persecution to Rhineland Jewry, prompting gradual migration eastward into Poland, Lithuania, and eventually Russia and Ukraine. The Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth became the heartland of Ashkenazi Jewry, developing a sophisticated yeshiva culture and communal organization.

The Ashkenazi world produced:
- The **Vilna Gaon** (1720-1797), the towering Talmudic genius
- The **Baal Shem Tov** (1698-1760), founder of Hasidism
- The **Chofetz Chaim** (1839-1933), ethical teacher and legal authority
- Numerous rebbes, roshei yeshiva, and scholars who shaped modern Orthodox, Hasidic, and Lithuanian traditions

### The Sephardi Story: Golden Age and Exile

Jewish life in Iberia dates back to antiquity, but the community flourished during the Islamic period in Spain, particularly under the Umayyad Caliphate of Córdoba (929-1031 CE).

The **Golden Age of Spanish Jewry** (roughly 900-1200) produced extraordinary achievements:

**Poetry and Philosophy:**
- **Rabbi Yehuda Halevi** (1075-1141), physician, philosopher, and poet who wrote the Kuzari
- **Rabbi Shlomo ibn Gabirol** (1021-1070), philosopher and liturgical poet
- **Maimonides** (Rambam, 1138-1204), physician, philosopher, and legal codifier whose Mishneh Torah and Guide for the Perplexed remain central to Jewish thought

**Kabbalah and Mysticism:**
- The **Zohar**, the foundational text of Jewish mysticism, was published in Spain by Rabbi Moshe de León (13th century)
- Spanish kabbalistic traditions deeply influenced all later Jewish mystical thought

**Biblical Scholarship:**
- **Rabbi Abraham ibn Ezra** (1089-1167), biblical commentator, grammarian, and polymath

The Christian Reconquista gradually pressured Jewish life, culminating in the **Spanish Expulsion of 1492**. King Ferdinand and Queen Isabella issued the Alhambra Decree, forcing Jews to either convert or leave Spain. An estimated 200,000-300,000 Jews fled to:

- The **Ottoman Empire** (Turkey, Greece, the Balkans, Land of Israel)
- **North Africa** (Morocco, Tunisia, Algeria)
- **Netherlands** and **Italy**
- Eventually, the **Americas**

The Sephardi diaspora carried their distinctive customs, Ladino language, and sophisticated legal traditions. Major Sephardi centers emerged in:

- **Safed** (16th century), where Rabbi Yosef Karo wrote the Shulchan Aruch
- **Amsterdam**, the "Jerusalem of the West"
- **Salonica**, the "Mother of Israel"
- **Istanbul**, under Ottoman protection

### Mizrachi Communities: Continuous Presence

While Ashkenazi and Sephardi communities represent migrations and exiles, Mizrachi Jews maintained continuous presence in the Middle East and North Africa since antiquity.

**Babylonian Jewry** (Iraq):
- The Babylonian Talmud was compiled here (3rd-6th centuries)
- Maintained prestigious academies (Sura and Pumbedita) through the Geonic period
- Produced leaders like the Ben Ish Chai (Rabbi Yosef Chaim, 1835-1909)

**Yemenite Jewry:**
- Isolated from other communities for centuries, they preserved ancient traditions
- Known for meticulous Torah scrolls and unique pronunciation of Hebrew

**Persian Jewry (Iran):**
- Dating to the Babylonian exile (6th century BCE)
- Produced scholars and maintained distinct customs

**North African Communities:**
- **Moroccan Jewry** had both indigenous (Toshavim) and Sephardi refugee populations
- **Egyptian Jewry** maintained ancient communities alongside Sephardi arrivals

## Differences in Custom (Minhag)

The concept of **minhag** (custom) is central to understanding Jewish diversity. The principle is summarized in the Talmudic phrase: "*Minhag avoteinu b'yadeinu*" — "The custom of our ancestors is in our hands."

While all Jewish communities follow the same Torah and Halachah (Jewish law), different communities developed distinct customs in areas where Jewish law permits variation.

### Prayer Liturgy: Nusach (Rite)

Perhaps the most noticeable difference is in prayer texts and order:

**Nusach Ashkenaz:**
- Based on the customs of medieval German and French communities
- Influenced by Rabbi Yaakov ben Moshe Levi Moelin (Maharil, 1360-1427)
- Used by most non-Hasidic Ashkenazi communities

**Nusach Sfard:**
- Despite the name, this is a Hasidic nusach (not Sephardi!)
- Developed by the Baal Shem Tov and early Hasidic masters
- Incorporates Kabbalistic elements from Rabbi Yitzchak Luria (the Arizal)
- Used by most Hasidic groups

**Nusach Sephardi:**
- Based on Spanish and North African traditions
- Follows the Shulchan Aruch of Rabbi Yosef Karo
- Variations exist (Syrian, Moroccan, Yemenite, etc.)

**Edot HaMizrach (Eastern Communities):**
- Each Mizrachi community (Iraqi, Yemenite, Persian) has distinct variations
- Often preserve very ancient liturgical traditions

Differences include:
- Order of prayers and blessings
- Additional or omitted piyyutim (liturgical poems)
- Melodies and cantillation
- Wording of certain blessings (e.g., "אשר יצר" vs. "אשר צר")

### Pronunciation of Hebrew

Different communities pronounce Hebrew differently:

**Ashkenazi Pronunciation:**
- T (תו) and S (סו) are different; T (תו) and Sav (תו without dagesh) sound alike
- Cholam (ֹ) and Kamatz (ָ) sound the same ("oh")
- The "ת" without dagesh becomes "s" in some traditions (Yiddish influence)

**Sephardi/Mizrachi Pronunciation:**
- Closer to modern Israeli Hebrew
- Kamatz = "ah," Cholam = "oh" (distinction preserved)
- Ayin (ע) often pronounced as a guttural (preserved from Arabic)

**Yemenite (Temani) Pronunciation:**
- Most conservative, preserving ancient distinctions
- Pronounces all letters with subtle differences

### Food Traditions

Food customs vary significantly, especially regarding **Pesach (Passover)**:

**Kitniyot (Legumes):**
- **Ashkenazi custom:** Prohibit kitniyot on Pesach (rice, beans, corn, peas, lentils)
- **Sephardi/Mizrachi custom:** Kitniyot are permitted
- This difference dates to the 13th century; Ashkenazi rabbis worried about confusion with chametz (leavened products)

**Shabbat and Holiday Foods:**
- **Ashkenazi:** Gefilte fish, cholent (slow-cooked Shabbat stew), kugel, challah (braided)
- **Sephardi:** Fish with chraimeh (spicy tomato sauce), dafina or hamin (Shabbat stew), burekas, pan pita
- **Mizrachi:** Kubbeh (Iraqi dumplings), jachnun (Yemenite overnight pastry), sabich (Iraqi street food)

**Tu BiShvat:**
- All communities eat fruits, but Sephardi communities have elaborate customs involving fruit platters arranged symbolically

### Music and Melodies (Niggunim)

Musical traditions differ dramatically:

**Ashkenazi Music:**
- Influenced by Eastern European folk melodies
- Cantorial (chazzanut) tradition with elaborate vocal embellishments
- Hasidic niggunim (wordless melodies) for inspiration and joy

**Sephardi Music:**
- Influenced by Spanish, Ottoman, and Arab musical traditions
- Pizmonim (liturgical hymns) with complex poetry
- Maqam tradition (Middle Eastern musical modes) in Syrian communities

**Yemenite Music:**
- Ancient melodic traditions
- Distinctive cantillation (ta'amim) system

### Lifecycle and Holiday Customs

**Bar/Bat Mitzvah:**
- Ashkenazi: typically at age 13 for boys, 12 for girls
- Some Sephardi communities: additional celebration at age 13 and one day

**Weddings:**
- Ashkenazi: Bride circles groom (3, 7, or no times depending on custom)
- Sephardi: Elaborate henna ceremonies (pre-wedding)
- Both break glass; different melodies for "Im eshkachech Yerushalayim"

**Shavuot:**
- Ashkenazi: Eating dairy foods (cheesecake!)
- Sephardi: Also dairy, plus reading Ketubah (wedding contract between God and Israel)

## Famous Figures from Each Community

### Ashkenazi Luminaries

**Medieval Period:**
- **Rashi** (1040-1105): Indispensable Torah and Talmud commentator
- **Tosafists**: Franco-German Talmudic scholars
- **Maharam of Rothenburg** (1215-1293): Martyr and legal authority

**Early Modern:**
- **Rabbi Moshe Isserles (Rema)** (1530-1572): Author of glosses on Shulchan Aruch for Ashkenazi custom
- **Vilna Gaon** (1720-1797): Lithuanian Talmudic genius

**Hasidic Masters:**
- **Baal Shem Tov** (1698-1760): Founder of Hasidism
- **Rabbi Schneur Zalman of Liadi** (1745-1812): Founder of Chabad
- **Rabbi Nachman of Breslov** (1772-1810): Mystical storyteller

**Modern Era:**
- **Chofetz Chaim** (1839-1933): Ethical and legal authority
- **Rabbi Samson Raphael Hirsch** (1808-1888): Founder of Torah im Derech Eretz movement
- **Rabbi Abraham Isaac Kook** (1865-1935): First Chief Rabbi of pre-state Israel, mystic and philosopher

### Sephardi and Mizrachi Luminaries

**Medieval Golden Age:**
- **Maimonides (Rambam)** (1138-1204): Philosopher, physician, codifier
- **Rabbi Yehuda Halevi** (1075-1141): Poet and philosopher
- **Nachmanides (Ramban)** (1194-1270): Spanish mystic and commentator

**Post-Expulsion:**
- **Rabbi Yosef Karo** (1488-1575): Author of Shulchan Aruch (Code of Jewish Law)
- **Rabbi Yitzchak Luria (Arizal)** (1534-1572): Kabbalist who revolutionized Jewish mysticism
- **Rabbi Chaim Yosef David Azulai (Chida)** (1724-1806): Sephardi scholar and traveler

**Mizrachi Figures:**
- **Ben Ish Chai** (Rabbi Yosef Chaim, 1835-1909): Iraqi authority on law and Kabbalah
- **Baba Sali** (Rabbi Yisrael Abuhatzeira, 1890-1984): Moroccan kabbalist and miracle worker

### Bridge Figures

Some figures transcend communal boundaries:
- **Rabbi Menachem Mendel Schneerson** (Lubavitcher Rebbe, 1902-1994): Hasidic leader who reached across all communities
- **Rabbi Adin Steinsaltz** (1937-2020): Talmud translator and educator who made learning accessible

## Unity in Diversity: One Torah, Many Customs

The diversity of Jewish custom beautifully illustrates a profound principle: **unity does not require uniformity**.

All Jewish communities:
- Study the same Torah (Five Books of Moses)
- Follow the same 613 mitzvot (commandments)
- Observe the same Shabbat and holidays
- Share the same fundamental beliefs
- Pray to the same God
- Long for the same redemption

The differences are in style, not substance; in melody, not message.

### The Halachic Principle

The Talmud teaches: "*Lo plug rabbanan*" — "The rabbis did not make distinctions [where the law is ambiguous]." But where legitimate variation exists, "*minhag avoteinu b'yadeinu*" — we preserve ancestral custom.

Rabbi Moshe Isserles (Rema) wrote glosses on Rabbi Yosef Karo's Shulchan Aruch specifically to note where Ashkenazi custom differs. His opening words set the tone: "I have come to be the voice of Ashkenazi custom." This wasn't contradiction—it was completion.

### Israel: The Meeting Place

Modern Israel is the first place in 2,000 years where all Jewish communities live together in significant numbers. Walk through Israeli cities and you'll find:
- Ashkenazi, Sephardi, Yemenite, Ethiopian, and Indian synagogues
- Restaurants serving gefilte fish and kubbeh
- Schools teaching different pronunciation systems
- Weddings blending customs

The Chief Rabbinate structure includes both an Ashkenazi and Sephardi Chief Rabbi, honoring both traditions.

### Cross-Communal Respect

Great rabbis have always emphasized respect for other customs:

Rabbi Ovadia Yosef (Sephardi) frequently ruled on Ashkenazi questions and encouraged mutual learning.

The Lubavitcher Rebbe (Ashkenazi) sent emissaries to Sephardi and Mizrachi communities, honoring their traditions.

The principle: "*Eilu v'eilu divrei Elokim chaim*" — "These and these are the words of the living God" (Talmud Eruvin 13b). Multiple valid approaches exist within the framework of Halachah.

## Which Customs to Follow: Guidance for Converts and Newcomers

This question arises frequently for:
- Converts to Judaism (geirim)
- Jews reconnecting with observance (baalei teshuvah)
- Children of intermarriage
- Those who don't know their family tradition

### General Principles

**For Converts:**

The traditional ruling is that converts may choose the customs of the community where they converted or the community where they live. Ideally:
1. **Follow the community where you will live permanently** — if you're settling in an Ashkenazi neighborhood, adopt Ashkenazi customs
2. **Be consistent** — don't pick and choose "best of both"; consistency is important for Halachic reasons
3. **Consult your sponsoring rabbi** — they can guide you based on your specific situation

**For Baalei Teshuvah (Those Returning to Observance):**

If you know your family's tradition:
- **Follow your ancestral custom** when possible; it's your heritage
- If you genuinely don't know, follow the community where you pray and live

**For Mixed Heritage:**

Some people have both Ashkenazi and Sephardi ancestry. Generally:
- **Follow the father's tradition** (traditional Halachic principle for certain customs)
- OR **follow the more stringent custom** in areas of conflict
- OR **consistently follow one side**

**Practical Advice:**

1. **Choose one rabbi/community as your primary guide** — don't jump between authorities
2. **Learn the "why"** — understanding the reasoning behind customs helps you appreciate them
3. **Be patient** — you don't have to master everything immediately
4. **Ask questions** — every community welcomes sincere questions
5. **Attend different synagogues** — experience various communities before deciding

### When Moving Between Communities

If an Ashkenazi Jew moves to a Sephardi community (or vice versa):
- **Private customs** (like kitniyot on Pesach) typically stay with the individual
- **Public customs** (like prayer melodies in synagogue) typically adapt to local practice
- **Consult a rabbi** for specific situations

### Children's Education

Parents from different backgrounds should:
- Agree on one consistent practice for the household
- Teach children about both heritages
- Explain that both are equally valid

## Conclusion: A Tapestry of Tradition

Imagine the Jewish people as a magnificent tapestry, woven with threads from every corner of the world. Ashkenazi threads in deep blues and grays of Eastern European winters; Sephardi threads in vibrant Mediterranean colors; Mizrachi threads in desert golds and oasis greens.

Each thread is distinct, yet all are part of the same picture—the eternal story of the Jewish people, the covenant at Sinai, and the mission to be a light unto the nations.

The differences between Ashkenazi, Sephardi, and Mizrachi communities are not divisions—they are dimensions. They show that Torah is not rigid but dynamic, not monolithic but musical, not uniform but universal.

In synagogues around the world, the same words are chanted in different melodies. At Shabbat tables, different foods are blessed with the same blessings. In homes across continents, the same candles are lit, the same prayers are said, the same hope lives on.

As Jews return to Israel from exile, as communities blend and learn from each other, we are witnessing a beautiful reunion. The scattered branches of the Jewish family are coming home, bringing their unique gifts to contribute to the whole.

So whether you pray Nusach Ashkenaz or Sephardi, whether you eat kitniyot on Pesach or abstain, whether your ancestors came from Vilna or Salonika, from Baghdad or Casablanca—you are part of this eternal people.

Different melodies, one song.  
Different customs, one Torah.  
Different communities, one people.

*Am Yisrael Chai* — The People of Israel Lives.
