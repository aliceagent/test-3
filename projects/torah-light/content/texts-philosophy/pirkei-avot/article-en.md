# Pirkei Avot: Ethics of the Fathers

## Introduction: The Timeless Wisdom of Our Sages

Pirkei Avot (פִּרְקֵי אָבוֹת), literally "Chapters of the Fathers," is one of the most beloved and widely studied texts in Jewish literature. Unlike other tractates of the Mishnah that deal with ritual law and legal disputes, Pirkei Avot is entirely dedicated to ethics, moral teachings, and life wisdom. It presents a chain of Jewish wisdom passed down through generations, from Moses at Mount Sinai to the great rabbis of the Mishnaic period.

This unique tractate consists of six chapters filled with pithy sayings, profound insights, and practical guidance for living a meaningful life. The teachings address fundamental questions: How should we treat others? What is the purpose of learning? How do we balance worldly pursuits with spiritual growth? What defines a good person?

## Why Study Pirkei Avot?

Pirkei Avot serves multiple purposes in Jewish life:

**Personal Development**: The mishnayot provide a roadmap for character refinement (middot). Each saying challenges us to examine our behavior, motivations, and relationships.

**Accessible Wisdom**: Unlike complex legal discussions, Pirkei Avot speaks in clear, memorable language. A child can understand "Who is wise? One who learns from every person," while a scholar can spend a lifetime plumbing its depths.

**Universal Appeal**: While rooted in Jewish tradition, the ethical teachings resonate across cultures. The emphasis on humility, justice, kindness, and integrity transcends religious boundaries.

**Connection to Tradition**: Studying Pirkei Avot links us to an unbroken chain of transmission. We hear the voices of our ancestors, their struggles and insights echoing across millennia.

## When is Pirkei Avot Studied?

The custom of studying Pirkei Avot follows specific times in the Jewish calendar:

**Between Pesach and Shavuot**: The seven weeks of the Omer period are particularly associated with Pirkei Avot. As we count from physical freedom (Exodus from Egypt) toward spiritual readiness (receiving the Torah at Sinai), we study ethics to prepare ourselves morally and spiritually.

**Shabbat Afternoons**: Many communities have the tradition of studying one chapter each Shabbat afternoon during spring and summer months. This creates a rhythm of weekly ethical reflection, pausing from the week's activities to contemplate how we should live.

**Year-Round Study**: While these are the traditional times, Pirkei Avot is studied throughout the year. Its timeless wisdom makes it relevant every day, in every season.

## Chapter 1: The Chain of Tradition

The first chapter establishes the transmission of Torah wisdom and introduces fundamental ethical principles.

### The Unbroken Chain

**Hebrew**: משֶׁה קִבֵּל תּוֹרָה מִסִּינַי, וּמְסָרָהּ לִיהוֹשֻׁעַ, וִיהוֹשֻׁעַ לִזְקֵנִים, וּזְקֵנִים לִנְבִיאִים, וּנְבִיאִים מְסָרוּהָ לְאַנְשֵׁי כְנֶסֶת הַגְּדוֹלָה

**Transliteration**: Moshe kibel Torah mi-Sinai, u-mesarah li-Yehoshua, vi-Yehoshua li-zkenim, u-zkenim li-nevi'im, u-nevi'im mesaruha l'anshei knesset ha-gedolah.

**Translation**: "Moses received the Torah from Sinai and transmitted it to Joshua, Joshua to the elders, the elders to the prophets, and the prophets transmitted it to the Men of the Great Assembly." (1:1)

This opening mishna does more than recount history—it establishes the legitimacy and continuity of Jewish wisdom. Every teaching in Pirkei Avot connects to this chain, linking our contemporary ethical struggles to divine revelation.

### The Three Pillars

**Hebrew**: עַל שְׁלשָׁה דְבָרִים הָעוֹלָם עוֹמֵד, עַל הַתּוֹרָה וְעַל הָעֲבוֹדָה וְעַל גְּמִילוּת חֲסָדִים

**Transliteration**: Al shlosha devarim ha-olam omed: al ha-Torah, ve-al ha-avodah, ve-al gemilut chasadim.

**Translation**: "The world stands on three things: on Torah, on service [of God], and on acts of loving-kindness." (1:2)

Shimon the Righteous teaches that civilization requires this three-legged foundation. Torah represents wisdom and learning; avodah originally meant Temple service but now refers to prayer and spiritual devotion; gemilut chasadim encompasses all acts of kindness and compassion. Remove any one pillar, and society collapses.

### Antignos: Learning for Its Own Sake

**Hebrew**: אַל תִּהְיוּ כַעֲבָדִים הַמְשַׁמְּשִׁין אֶת הָרַב עַל מְנָת לְקַבֵּל פְּרָס, אֶלָּא הֱווּ כַעֲבָדִים הַמְשַׁמְּשִׁין אֶת הָרַב שֶׁלֹּא עַל מְנָת לְקַבֵּל פְּרָס

**Transliteration**: Al tihyu ka-avadim ha-meshamshim et ha-rav al menat lekabel pras, ela hevu ka-avadim ha-meshamshim et ha-rav she-lo al menat lekabel pras.

**Translation**: "Do not be like servants who serve the master in order to receive a reward, but be like servants who serve the master not for the sake of receiving a reward." (1:3)

Antignos of Socho challenges the transactional approach to ethics and spirituality. True service—whether to God, to others, or to truth—must be intrinsically motivated. This teaching laid groundwork for the concept of Torah lishma, learning for its own sake, not for status or material gain.

### Yose ben Yoezer: Your House as a Meeting Place

**Hebrew**: יְהִי בֵיתְךָ בֵית וַעַד לַחֲכָמִים, וֶהֱוֵי מִתְאַבֵּק בַּעֲפַר רַגְלֵיהֶם, וֶהֱוֵי שׁוֹתֶה בְצָמָא אֶת דִּבְרֵיהֶם

**Transliteration**: Yehi veitcha beit va'ad la-chachamim, ve-hevei mit'abek ba-afar ragleihem, ve-hevei shoteh be-tzama et divreihem.

**Translation**: "Let your house be a meeting place for the wise, sit in the dust of their feet, and drink their words with thirst." (1:4)

This mishna advocates creating spaces for learning and intellectual exchange. "Sitting in the dust of their feet" expresses both humility and eagerness—positioning yourself as a student, humble enough to learn from anyone.

### Hillel's Wisdom: If Not Now, When?

**Hebrew**: אִם אֵין אֲנִי לִי, מִי לִי? וּכְשֶׁאֲנִי לְעַצְמִי, מָה אֲנִי? וְאִם לֹא עַכְשָׁיו, אֵימָתָי?

**Transliteration**: Im ein ani li, mi li? U-kh'she-ani le-atzmi, mah ani? Ve-im lo achshav, eimatai?

**Translation**: "If I am not for myself, who will be for me? But if I am only for myself, what am I? And if not now, when?" (1:14)

Hillel's three questions form perhaps the most famous teaching in Pirkei Avot. They balance self-care with concern for others, and both with urgency. We must take responsibility for ourselves (no one else will do it for us), yet never become purely selfish (or we lose our humanity). And we must act now—tomorrow is promised to no one.

**Hebrew**: הִלֵּל אוֹמֵר, אַל תִּפְרֹשׁ מִן הַצִּבּוּר, וְאַל תַּאֲמִין בְּעַצְמְךָ עַד יוֹם מוֹתְךָ, וְאַל תָּדִין אֶת חֲבֵרְךָ עַד שֶׁתַּגִּיעַ לִמְקוֹמוֹ

**Transliteration**: Hillel omer: al tifrosh min ha-tzibur, ve-al ta'amin be-atzmecha ad yom motech, ve-al tadin et chavercha ad she-tagia limkomo.

**Translation**: "Do not separate yourself from the community; do not be sure of yourself until the day of your death; and do not judge your fellow until you have reached his place." (2:4)

Hillel offers three more crucial lessons: Stay connected to community (isolation breeds extremism and self-deception). Never become overconfident in your righteousness (we are all capable of falling). Don't judge others until you've walked in their shoes (empathy requires imagination and humility).

### Hillel and Shammai: The Golden Rule

**Hebrew**: הִלֵּל אוֹמֵר, הֱוֵי מִתַּלְמִידָיו שֶׁל אַהֲרֹן, אוֹהֵב שָׁלוֹם וְרוֹדֵף שָׁלוֹם, אוֹהֵב אֶת הַבְּרִיּוֹת וּמְקָרְבָן לַתּוֹרָה

**Transliteration**: Hillel omer: hevei mi-talmidav shel Aharon, ohev shalom ve-rodef shalom, ohev et ha-briyot u-mekarvan la-Torah.

**Translation**: "Be of the disciples of Aaron: loving peace and pursuing peace, loving people and bringing them closer to Torah." (1:12)

Hillel models himself after Aaron the High Priest, known for making peace between people. Note the distinction: love peace (appreciate it when it exists) AND pursue peace (actively work to create it where it doesn't). Love people—not just the righteous or learned, but all people—and through that love, draw them toward wisdom.

**Hebrew**: שַׁמַּאי אוֹמֵר, עֲשֵׂה תוֹרָתְךָ קֶבַע, אֱמֹר מְעַט וַעֲשֵׂה הַרְבֵּה, וֶהֱוֵי מְקַבֵּל אֶת כָּל הָאָדָם בְּסֵבֶר פָּנִים יָפוֹת

**Transliteration**: Shammai omer: aseh Toratcha keva, emor me'at va-aseh harbeh, ve-hevei mekabel et kol ha-adam be-sever panim yafot.

**Translation**: "Make your Torah study a fixed practice; say little and do much; and receive every person with a cheerful countenance." (1:15)

Shammai, often portrayed as Hillel's strict counterpart, teaches discipline (make learning a fixed habit), integrity (underpromise and overdeliver), and warmth (greet everyone with a pleasant face). The last teaching is particularly touching—even the "strict" Shammai recognizes that a warm welcome can transform someone's day.

## Chapter 2: Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai and His Students

Chapter 2 shifts focus to Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai, who saved Judaism after the Temple's destruction, and his five outstanding students.

### The Right Path

**Hebrew**: רַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי קִבֵּל מֵהִלֵּל וּמִשַּׁמָּאי. הוּא הָיָה אוֹמֵר, אִם לָמַדְתָּ תּוֹרָה הַרְבֵּה, אַל תַּחֲזִיק טוֹבָה לְעַצְמְךָ, כִּי לְכָךְ נוֹצַרְתָּ

**Transliteration**: Raban Yochanan ben Zakkai kibel me-Hillel u-mi-Shammai. Hu haya omer: im lamadta Torah harbeh, al tachazik tovah le-atzmecha, ki le-chach notzarta.

**Translation**: "Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai received [the tradition] from Hillel and Shammai. He used to say: If you have learned much Torah, do not claim credit for yourself, for this is why you were created." (2:8)

Learning Torah isn't a personal achievement to boast about—it's the fulfillment of your purpose. This deflates intellectual arrogance while elevating the importance of study.

### Five Students, Five Paths

Rabban Yochanan asked his five students: "What is the good path to which a person should cling?" Their answers reveal different ethical priorities:

**Rabbi Eliezer**: "A good eye" (generosity of spirit, seeing the good in others)

**Rabbi Joshua**: "A good friend" (the importance of companionship)

**Rabbi Yose**: "A good neighbor" (harmonious community relations)

**Rabbi Shimon**: "One who foresees consequences" (wisdom and prudence)

**Rabbi Elazar**: "A good heart" (the source of all good traits)

Rabban Yochanan declared Rabbi Elazar's answer superior, "for your words include all of theirs." A good heart generates a good eye, attracts good friends, makes you a good neighbor, and enables wise foresight.

### The Evil Path

He then asked the opposite: "What is the evil path from which a person should distance himself?"

**Rabbi Eliezer**: "An evil eye" (jealousy, stinginess, seeing the worst in others)

**Rabbi Joshua**: "A bad friend" (toxic relationships)

**Rabbi Yose**: "A bad neighbor" (discord in community)

**Rabbi Shimon**: "One who borrows and does not repay" (irresponsibility, broken trust)

**Rabbi Elazar**: "An evil heart" (the root of all bad traits)

Again, Rabban Yochanan affirmed Rabbi Elazar's answer as encompassing all the others.

### Rabbi Elazar: Weigh the Loss Against the Gain

**Hebrew**: רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר, הֱוֵי שָׁקוּד לִלְמֹד תּוֹרָה, וְדַע מָה שֶׁתָּשִׁיב לְאֶפִּיקוֹרוֹס, וְדַע לִפְנֵי מִי אַתָּה עָמֵל, וְנֶאֱמָן הוּא בַעַל מְלַאכְתְּךָ שֶׁיְּשַׁלֵּם לְךָ שְׂכַר פְּעֻלָּתֶךָ

**Transliteration**: Rabbi Elazar omer: hevei shakud lilmod Torah, ve-da mah she-tashiv le-apikoros, ve-da lifnei mi atah amel, ve-ne'eman hu ba'al melachtecha she-yeshalem lecha sechar pe'ulatecha.

**Translation**: "Rabbi Elazar said: Be diligent in learning Torah, know what to answer a heretic, know before Whom you toil, and faithful is your Employer to pay you the reward of your labor." (2:14)

This teaching emphasizes preparation (be diligent), intellectual integrity (know how to defend truth), consciousness (remember before Whom you work), and trust (God is a faithful employer).

### Ben Azzai: Don't Despise Any Person

**Hebrew**: בֶּן עַזַּאי אוֹמֵר, אַל תְּהִי בָז לְכָל אָדָם, וְאַל תְּהִי מַפְלִיג לְכָל דָּבָר, שֶׁאֵין לְךָ אָדָם שֶׁאֵין לוֹ שָׁעָה, וְאֵין לְךָ דָבָר שֶׁאֵין לוֹ מָקוֹם

**Transliteration**: Ben Azzai omer: al tehi vaz le-chol adam, ve-al tehi maflig le-chol davar, she-ein lecha adam she-ein lo sha'ah, ve-ein lecha davar she-ein lo makom.

**Translation**: "Ben Azzai said: Do not be scornful of any person, and do not be dismissive of anything, for there is no person who does not have their hour, and there is no thing that does not have its place." (4:3)

Everyone has their moment of importance; everything has its purpose. This teaching cultivates humility and openness. The person you dismiss today might be the one who saves you tomorrow. The teaching you ignore might be exactly what you need years from now.

## Chapter 3: Wisdom of Various Rabbis

Chapter 3 presents teachings from numerous sages, covering a wide range of ethical and spiritual topics.

### Akavya ben Mahalalel: Know Your Origins

**Hebrew**: עֲקַבְיָה בֶן מַהֲלַלְאֵל אוֹמֵר, הִסְתַּכֵּל בִּשְׁלשָׁה דְבָרִים וְאִי אַתָּה בָא לִידֵי עֲבֵרָה: דַּע מֵאַיִן בָּאתָ, וּלְאָן אַתָּה הוֹלֵךְ, וְלִפְנֵי מִי אַתָּה עָתִיד לִתֵּן דִּין וְחֶשְׁבּוֹן

**Transliteration**: Akavya ben Mahalalel omer: histakel bi-shlosha devarim ve-i atah ba lidei aveirah: da mei-ayin bata, u-le-an atah holech, ve-lifnei mi atah atid liten din ve-cheshbon.

**Translation**: "Akavya ben Mahalalel said: Reflect on three things and you will not come to sin: Know from where you came, where you are going, and before Whom you will give account and reckoning." (3:1)

Contemplate your humble origins (from a drop), your inevitable end (to dust and worms), and your ultimate accountability (before the King of Kings). This meditation on mortality isn't morbid—it's clarifying. When you remember that life is short and you'll answer for your choices, petty concerns fall away.

### Rabbi Chanina ben Dosa: Wisdom and Fear of Sin

**Hebrew**: רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן דּוֹסָא אוֹמֵר, כָּל שֶׁיִּרְאַת חֶטְאוֹ קוֹדֶמֶת לְחָכְמָתוֹ, חָכְמָתוֹ מִתְקַיֶּמֶת. וְכָל שֶׁחָכְמָתוֹ קוֹדֶמֶת לְיִרְאַת חֶטְאוֹ, אֵין חָכְמָתוֹ מִתְקַיֶּמֶת

**Transliteration**: Rabbi Chanina ben Dosa omer: kol she-yirat chet'o kodemet le-chochmato, chochmato mitkayemet. Ve-chol she-chochmato kodemet le-yirat chet'o, ein chochmato mitkayemet.

**Translation**: "Rabbi Chanina ben Dosa said: Anyone whose fear of sin precedes their wisdom, their wisdom will endure. But anyone whose wisdom precedes their fear of sin, their wisdom will not endure." (3:9)

Moral foundation must precede intellectual achievement. Without ethical grounding, knowledge becomes dangerous—a tool for manipulation rather than enlightenment. But wisdom built on moral awareness creates lasting impact.

### Rabbi Chanina ben Dosa: Deeds Over Words

**Hebrew**: הוּא הָיָה אוֹמֵר, כָּל שֶׁמַּעֲשָׂיו מְרֻבִּין מֵחָכְמָתוֹ, חָכְמָתוֹ מִתְקַיֶּמֶת. וְכָל שֶׁחָכְמָתוֹ מְרֻבָּה מִמַּעֲשָׂיו, אֵין חָכְמָתוֹ מִתְקַיֶּמֶת

**Transliteration**: Hu haya omer: kol she-ma'asav merubim me-chochmato, chochmato mitkayemet. Ve-chol she-chochmato merubah mi-ma'asav, ein chochmato mitkayemet.

**Translation**: "He used to say: Anyone whose deeds exceed their wisdom, their wisdom will endure. But anyone whose wisdom exceeds their deeds, their wisdom will not endure." (3:9)

Actions speak louder than words. Knowledge without implementation is sterile. A person who lives their values, even with limited learning, surpasses the scholar who studies but doesn't practice.

### Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah: No Torah, No Worldly Occupation

**Hebrew**: רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה אוֹמֵר, אִם אֵין תּוֹרָה, אֵין דֶּרֶךְ אֶרֶץ. אִם אֵין דֶּרֶךְ אֶרֶץ, אֵין תּוֹרָה

**Transliteration**: Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah omer: im ein Torah, ein derech eretz. Im ein derech eretz, ein Torah.

**Translation**: "Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah said: If there is no Torah, there is no worldly occupation. If there is no worldly occupation, there is no Torah." (3:17)

Torah and derech eretz (literally "way of the land," meaning proper conduct, earning a living, engaging with the world) depend on each other. Spiritual study without worldly engagement becomes disconnected and impractical. Worldly work without spiritual dimension becomes meaningless and soul-crushing. We need both.

## Chapter 4: Ben Zoma, Ben Azzai, and the Path to Honor

Chapter 4 continues with wisdom about character, honor, and the pursuit of wisdom.

### Ben Zoma: Who Is Wise? Who Is Strong?

**Hebrew**: בֶּן זוֹמָא אוֹמֵר, אֵיזֶהוּ חָכָם? הַלּוֹמֵד מִכָּל אָדָם... אֵיזֶהוּ גִבּוֹר? הַכּוֹבֵשׁ אֶת יִצְרוֹ... אֵיזֶהוּ עָשִׁיר? הַשָּׂמֵחַ בְּחֶלְקוֹ... אֵיזֶהוּ מְכֻבָּד? הַמְכַבֵּד אֶת הַבְּרִיּוֹת

**Transliteration**: Ben Zoma omer: eizehu chacham? Ha-lomed mi-kol adam... Eizehu gibor? Ha-kovesh et yitzro... Eizehu ashir? Ha-same'ach be-chelko... Eizehu mechubad? Ha-mechabed et ha-briyot.

**Translation**: "Ben Zoma said: Who is wise? One who learns from every person... Who is strong? One who conquers their inclination... Who is rich? One who is satisfied with their portion... Who is honored? One who honors others." (4:1)

This mishna redefines success. True wisdom isn't knowing everything—it's being humble enough to learn from anyone. True strength isn't physical power—it's self-control. True wealth isn't accumulation—it's contentment. True honor isn't receiving respect—it's giving it.

### Rabbi Levitas: Be Exceedingly Humble

**Hebrew**: רַבִּי לְוִיטָס אִישׁ יַבְנֶה אוֹמֵר, מְאֹד מְאֹד הֱוֵי שְׁפַל רוּחַ, שֶׁתִּקְוַת אֱנוֹשׁ רִמָּה

**Transliteration**: Rabbi Levitas ish Yavneh omer: me'od me'od hevei shefal ruach, she-tikvat enosh rimah.

**Translation**: "Rabbi Levitas of Yavneh said: Be exceedingly, exceedingly humble, for the hope of mortals is worms." (4:4)

The double emphasis ("exceedingly, exceedingly") stresses the importance of profound humility. Our ultimate destiny is the grave—a sobering reminder that deflates ego and pretension.

### Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka: Honor of Heaven and Honor of Others

**Hebrew**: רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָה אוֹמֵר, כָּל הַמְחַלֵּל שֵׁם שָׁמַיִם בַּסֵּתֶר, נִפְרָעִין מִמֶּנּוּ בְגָלוּי

**Transliteration**: Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka omer: kol ha-mechalel shem shamayim ba-seter, nifra'in mimenu ba-galuy.

**Translation**: "Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka said: Whoever desecrates God's name in secret will be punished publicly." (4:4)

Private misdeeds have public consequences. This isn't about divine punishment as much as the inevitable revelation of hidden corruption. What we do in secret shapes our character, and character eventually shows itself.

### Rabbi Yishmael: Learn to Teach, Teach to Do

**Hebrew**: רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּנוֹ אוֹמֵר, הַלּוֹמֵד עַל מְנָת לְלַמֵּד, מַסְפִּיקִין בְּיָדוֹ לִלְמֹד וּלְלַמֵּד. וְהַלּוֹמֵד עַל מְנָת לַעֲשׂוֹת, מַסְפִּיקִין בְּיָדוֹ לִלְמֹד וּלְלַמֵּד לִשְׁמֹר וְלַעֲשׂוֹת

**Transliteration**: Rabbi Yishmael beno omer: ha-lomed al menat lelamed, maspikin be-yado lilmod u-lelamed. Ve-ha-lomed al menat la'asot, maspikin be-yado lilmod u-lelamed lishmor ve-la'asot.

**Translation**: "Rabbi Yishmael his son said: One who learns in order to teach will be given the means to learn and to teach. But one who learns in order to do will be given the means to learn, teach, observe, and do." (4:5)

Intention matters. Learning with the goal of teaching is noble—you'll gain knowledge and the ability to transmit it. But learning with the intention to practice and implement is even higher—you'll gain everything: understanding, teaching ability, and the power to actually live your values.

### Rabbi Tzadok: Don't Make Torah a Crown

**Hebrew**: רַבִּי צָדוֹק אוֹמֵר, אַל תַּעַשׂ מֵהֶם עֲטָרָה לְהִתְגַּדֵּל בָּהֶם, וְלֹא קַרְדֹּם לַחְפֹּר בָּהֶם

**Transliteration**: Rabbi Tzadok omer: al ta'as mehem atarah lehitgadel bahem, ve-lo kardom lachfor bahem.

**Translation**: "Rabbi Tzadok said: Do not make [words of Torah] a crown with which to glorify yourself, nor a spade with which to dig." (4:5)

Don't use Torah learning for personal aggrandizement or material gain. Study for its own sake, not to impress others or advance your career. This teaching has guided Jewish scholars for centuries, many of whom maintained regular professions rather than commercialize their learning.

## Chapter 5: Numerical Teachings and Types of People

Chapter 5 is distinctive for its numerical lists and typologies that categorize human behavior.

### Ten Things Created at Twilight

**Hebrew**: עֲשָׂרָה דְבָרִים נִבְרְאוּ בְעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת בֵּין הַשְּׁמָשׁוֹת

**Transliteration**: Asarah devarim nivr'u be-erev Shabbat bein ha-shemashot.

**Translation**: "Ten things were created on the eve of Shabbat at twilight." (5:6)

This mishna lists miraculous phenomena created at the border between the sixth day and Shabbat: the mouth of the earth that swallowed Korach, Miriam's well, Balaam's talking donkey, the rainbow, the manna, Moses's staff, the shamir worm, the letters, the writing, and the tablets. The twilight timing suggests these miracles were built into creation from the start, not supernatural interventions. The universe was designed to allow for wonder.

### Four Types of Students

**Hebrew**: אַרְבַּע מִדּוֹת בַּיּוֹשְׁבִים לִפְנֵי חֲכָמִים: סְפוֹג, וּמַשְׁפֵּךְ, מְשַׁמֶּרֶת, וְנָפָה

**Transliteration**: Arba midot ba-yoshvim lifnei chachamim: sfog, u-mashpech, meshemeret, ve-nafah.

**Translation**: "There are four types among those who sit before the sages: the sponge, the funnel, the strainer, and the sieve." (5:15)

**The sponge** absorbs everything—good and bad, relevant and irrelevant.

**The funnel** takes in from one end and immediately releases from the other—nothing is retained.

**The strainer** lets the wine flow out and retains the sediment—keeps the worthless, loses the valuable.

**The sieve** lets the flour dust pass through and retains the fine flour—discards the worthless and retains the valuable.

The ideal student is the sieve: discerning, filtering, keeping what's valuable and releasing what isn't.

### Four Types of Character

**Hebrew**: אַרְבַּע מִדּוֹת בָּאָדָם: נוֹחַ לִכְעֹס וְנוֹחַ לִרְצוֹת, יָצָא שְׂכָרוֹ בְהֶפְסֵדוֹ. קָשֶׁה לִכְעֹס וְקָשֶׁה לִרְצוֹת, יָצָא הֶפְסֵדוֹ בִשְׂכָרוֹ. קָשֶׁה לִכְעֹס וְנוֹחַ לִרְצוֹת, חָסִיד. נוֹחַ לִכְעֹס וְקָשֶׁה לִרְצוֹת, רָשָׁע

**Transliteration**: Arba midot ba-adam: noach lich'os ve-noach lirtzot, yatza secharo be-hefsedo. Kasheh lich'os ve-kasheh lirtzot, yatza hefsedo bi-secharo. Kasheh lich'os ve-noach lirtzot, chasid. Noach lich'os ve-kasheh lirtzot, rasha.

**Translation**: "There are four types of temperaments: Easy to anger and easy to appease—their gain is canceled by their loss. Hard to anger and hard to appease—their loss is canceled by their gain. Hard to anger and easy to appease—pious. Easy to anger and hard to appease—wicked." (5:11)

This teaching maps the terrain of temperament. The ideal person is slow to anger (patient, giving others benefit of the doubt) but quick to forgive (doesn't hold grudges). The worst is the opposite: quick-tempered and unforgiving.

### Four Types of Charity Givers

**Hebrew**: אַרְבַּע מִדּוֹת בְּנוֹתְנֵי צְדָקָה: הָרוֹצֶה שֶׁיִּתֵּן וְלֹא יִתְּנוּ אֲחֵרִים, עֵינוֹ רָעָה בְּשֶׁל אֲחֵרִים. יִתְּנוּ אֲחֵרִים וְהוּא לֹא יִתֵּן, עֵינוֹ רָעָה בְּשֶׁלּוֹ. יִתֵּן וְיִתְּנוּ אֲחֵרִים, חָסִיד. לֹא יִתֵּן וְלֹא יִתְּנוּ אֲחֵרִים, רָשָׁע

**Transliteration**: Arba midot be-notnei tzedakah: ha-rotzeh she-yiten ve-lo yitnu acherim, eino ra'ah be-shel acherim. Yitnu acherim ve-hu lo yiten, eino ra'ah be-shelo. Yiten ve-yitnu acherim, chasid. Lo yiten ve-lo yitnu acherim, rasha.

**Translation**: "There are four types among givers of charity: One who wants to give but doesn't want others to give—has an evil eye toward others. Wants others to give but won't give himself—has an evil eye toward himself. Gives and wants others to give—pious. Doesn't give and doesn't want others to give—wicked." (5:13)

This dissects the psychology of generosity. The best person gives generously and encourages others to do the same. The worst is miserly and wants everyone else to be too. Between these extremes are the person who gives but resents others' generosity (competitive charity) and the person who praises others' giving while making excuses for themselves (hypocritical morality).

### Yehudah ben Teima: Bold as a Leopard

**Hebrew**: יְהוּדָה בֶן תֵּימָא אוֹמֵר, הֱוֵי עַז כַּנָּמֵר, וְקַל כַּנֶּשֶׁר, וְרָץ כַּצְּבִי, וְגִבּוֹר כָּאֲרִי, לַעֲשׂוֹת רְצוֹן אָבִיךָ שֶׁבַּשָּׁמָיִם

**Transliteration**: Yehudah ben Teima omer: hevei az ka-namer, ve-kal ka-nesher, ve-ratz ka-tzvi, ve-gibor ka-ari, la'asot retzon Avicha she-ba-shamayim.

**Translation**: "Yehudah ben Teima said: Be bold as a leopard, light as an eagle, swift as a deer, and strong as a lion to do the will of your Father in Heaven." (5:20)

Each animal represents a quality needed for spiritual service: the leopard's boldness (don't be embarrassed to do what's right), the eagle's lightness (rise above earthly concerns), the deer's speed (act quickly on opportunities for good), the lion's strength (overcome obstacles and opposition). Together, they form the profile of a spiritual warrior.

## Chapter 6: Kinyan Torah (Acquiring Torah)

The sixth chapter, technically a later addition called Baraita de-Rabbi Meir, focuses on the acquisition of Torah and its supreme value.

### Torah Greater Than Priesthood and Kingship

**Hebrew**: גְדוֹלָה תוֹרָה יוֹתֵר מִן הַכְּהֻנָּה וּמִן הַמַּלְכוּת, שֶׁהַמַּלְכוּת נִקְנֵית בִּשְׁלשִׁים מַעֲלוֹת, וְהַכְּהֻנָּה בְּעֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבַּע, וְהַתּוֹרָה נִקְנֵית בְּאַרְבָּעִים וּשְׁמוֹנָה דְּבָרִים

**Transliteration**: Gedolah Torah yoter min ha-kehunah u-min ha-malchut, she-ha-malchut nikneit bi-shloshim ma'alot, ve-ha-kehunah be-esrim ve-arba, ve-ha-Torah nikneit be-arba'im u-shmonah devarim.

**Translation**: "Torah is greater than the priesthood and than kingship, for kingship is acquired with thirty virtues, the priesthood with twenty-four, and Torah is acquired with forty-eight things." (6:5)

The chapter then lists the forty-eight ways Torah is acquired: through study, listening, articulating, understanding, awe, fear, humility, joy, serving scholars, companionship with colleagues, debate with students, calmness, knowledge of Scripture and Mishnah, minimal business activity, minimal socialization, minimal pleasure, minimal sleep, minimal conversation, minimal laughter, patience, a good heart, faith in the sages, acceptance of suffering... and many more.

### The Crown of Torah

**Hebrew**: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר, שְׁלשָׁה כְתָרִים הֵם: כֶּתֶר תּוֹרָה, וְכֶתֶר כְּהֻנָּה, וְכֶתֶר מַלְכוּת. וְכֶתֶר שֵׁם טוֹב עוֹלֶה עַל גַּבֵּיהֶן

**Transliteration**: Rabbi Shimon omer: shloshah ketarim hem: keter Torah, ve-keter kehunah, ve-keter malchut. Ve-keter shem tov oleh al gabeihen.

**Translation**: "Rabbi Shimon said: There are three crowns: the crown of Torah, the crown of priesthood, and the crown of kingship. But the crown of a good name surpasses them all." (4:13, also referenced in Chapter 6)

While priesthood and kingship are hereditary, Torah is open to all. But above even Torah scholarship stands the crown of a good name—earned through ethical living, integrity, and kindness. Reputation built on character outlasts all other achievements.

### The Reward of Torah Study

**Hebrew**: רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר, כָּל הָעוֹסֵק בַּתּוֹרָה לִשְׁמָהּ, זוֹכֶה לִדְבָרִים הַרְבֵּה

**Transliteration**: Rabbi Meir omer: kol ha-osek ba-Torah lishmah, zocheh li-devarim harbeh.

**Translation**: "Rabbi Meir said: Whoever engages in Torah study for its own sake merits many things." (6:1)

The mishna goes on to list the rewards: the entire world is worthwhile for their sake, they're called friend and beloved, they love God and people, they bring joy to God and people, Torah clothes them with humility and fear of God, prepares them to be righteous, pious, upright, and faithful, distances them from sin and brings them close to merit.

### Loving Rebuke

**Hebrew**: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר, הָעוֹשֶׂה מִצְוָה אַחַת, קוֹנֶה לוֹ פְרַקְלִיט אֶחָד. וְהָעוֹבֵר עֲבֵרָה אַחַת, קוֹנֶה לוֹ קַטֵּיגוֹר אֶחָד

**Transliteration**: Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov omer: ha-oseh mitzvah achat, koneh lo prakleet echad. Ve-ha-over aveirah achat, koneh lo kategor echad.

**Translation**: "Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov said: One who performs one mitzvah acquires for himself one advocate. One who commits one transgression acquires for himself one accuser." (4:11, echoed in Chapter 6)

Every deed has lasting impact. Good actions create advocates pleading our case; misdeeds create accusers. We're building our own jury through our daily choices.

## Pirkei Avot and Confucian Wisdom: A Dialogue Across Traditions

Remarkably, many themes in Pirkei Avot echo teachings from Confucius's Analects (論語), composed centuries earlier in a completely different cultural context. This parallelism suggests that certain ethical truths are universal, emerging independently across civilizations.

### On Learning and Teaching

**Confucius** (Analects 7:21): "學而不厭，誨人不倦" - "Learning without satiety, teaching without weariness."

**Pirkei Avot** (6:6): Lists among the forty-eight ways of acquiring Torah: "teaching" and "increasing one's students."

Both traditions emphasize lifelong learning and the duty to transmit wisdom to the next generation.

### On Humility and Self-Cultivation

**Confucius** (Analects 7:22): "I am not one who was born with knowledge; I am fond of antiquity and earnest in seeking it."

**Ben Zoma** (4:1): "Who is wise? One who learns from every person."

Both reject the arrogant notion that we can know everything. True wisdom lies in recognizing our limitations and remaining perpetually open to learning.

### On Speech and Action

**Confucius** (Analects 14:27): "One who is not trustworthy in speech cannot be esteemed."

**Shammai** (1:15): "Say little and do much."

Both traditions value integrity of word and deed. Empty promises and grandiose talk without action undermine trust and character.

### On Friendship

**Confucius** (Analects 1:8): "The Master said: 'If the gentleman is not serious, he will not inspire awe, and his learning will lack firmness. Make loyalty and faithfulness your guiding principles. Have no friends who are not as good as yourself. When you make a mistake, don't be afraid to correct it.'"

**Pirkei Avot** (1:6): "Acquire for yourself a friend, judge every person favorably."

Both emphasize choosing companions wisely, as friendship shapes character. However, while Confucius warns against inferior friends, Pirkei Avot adds the dimension of judging others favorably—giving everyone the benefit of the doubt.

### On Governing Oneself

**Confucius** (Analects 12:1): "克己復禮為仁" - "Restraining oneself and returning to propriety constitutes benevolence."

**Ben Zoma** (4:1): "Who is strong? One who conquers their inclination."

Both identify self-mastery as the highest strength. External power pales before the ability to govern one's desires and impulses.

### On Contentment

**Confucius** (Analects 1:15): "貧而樂，富而好禮" - "Poor yet delighting in the Way, wealthy yet a student of the rites."

**Ben Zoma** (4:1): "Who is rich? One who is satisfied with their portion."

Both reject materialism as the measure of wealth. True riches lie in spiritual contentment and moral cultivation, not possessions.

### On Serving Others

**Confucius** (Analects 15:24): "己所不欲，勿施於人" - "What you do not wish for yourself, do not impose on others."

**Hillel** (famous teaching outside Pirkei Avot but in the same tradition): "What is hateful to you, do not do to your fellow. This is the whole Torah; the rest is commentary."

This is the negative formulation of the Golden Rule, found in both traditions. It establishes empathy as the foundation of ethics.

### On Timing and Action

**Confucius** (Analects 4:17): "見賢思齊，見不賢而內自省" - "When you see someone worthy, think of equaling them; when you see someone unworthy, examine yourself."

**Hillel** (1:14): "If not now, when?"

Both emphasize the urgency of moral action. Self-improvement cannot be postponed. The time to grow is always now.

### Differences: Individual vs. Social Harmony

Despite these parallels, important differences exist. Confucianism places stronger emphasis on **social harmony** and **hierarchical relationships** (the Five Relationships: ruler-subject, father-son, husband-wife, older-younger siblings, friend-friend). Proper conduct within these structured relationships maintains social order.

Pirkei Avot, while certainly valuing community ("Do not separate yourself from the community"), places more emphasis on the **individual's relationship with God** and **personal responsibility** for ethical behavior regardless of social position. Every person, regardless of status, stands equally before God.

Confucianism focuses on **ritual propriety** (禮, li) and **social roles** as the path to virtue. Pirkei Avot emphasizes **Torah study**, **fear of God**, and **loving-kindness** as the foundations.

Yet both traditions ultimately seek the same goal: cultivating human excellence, building just societies, and transmitting wisdom across generations. The dialogue between them enriches both, showing that the human quest for meaning and morality transcends cultural boundaries.

## Conclusion: Living Pirkei Avot Today

Pirkei Avot remains intensely relevant in the modern world. Its questions are our questions:

- How do we balance personal ambition with service to others?
- How do we navigate moral complexity without clear-cut rules?
- How do we cultivate wisdom in an age of information overload?
- How do we remain humble while asserting our own worth?
- How do we build community while respecting individuality?

The sages of Pirkei Avot don't offer simple answers. Instead, they model a way of thinking—dialectical, nuanced, attentive to paradox. They teach us to hold multiple truths in tension: be bold, yet humble; learn constantly, yet teach others; care for yourself, yet serve the community; act urgently, yet patiently.

Perhaps most importantly, Pirkei Avot reminds us that ethics isn't abstract philosophy—it's practical wisdom for daily living. The measure of our learning isn't what we know but how we live. As Rabbi Hanina ben Dosa taught: "Anyone whose deeds exceed their wisdom, their wisdom will endure."

May we study these timeless teachings, internalize their wisdom, and most of all, live them—transforming ourselves and the world one mindful, compassionate action at a time.
