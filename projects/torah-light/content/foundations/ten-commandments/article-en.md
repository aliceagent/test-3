# The Ten Commandments: Foundation of Divine Covenant

## The Revelation at Sinai

The giving of the Ten Commandments at Mount Sinai stands as the defining moment in Jewish history—the direct revelation of God's will to an entire nation. Fifty days after the Exodus from Egypt, the Israelites stood at the foot of Mount Sinai, witnesses to the only mass revelation in human history.

The Torah describes the scene with vivid intensity: thunder and lightning, the sound of the shofar growing ever louder, the mountain enveloped in smoke and fire. The entire nation—according to tradition, some 600,000 men plus women and children—heard the voice of God speaking the Ten Commandments. This wasn't revelation to a single prophet or mystic; this was God addressing an entire people, establishing a covenant that would endure for all generations.

The Talmud teaches that at Sinai, God held the mountain over the people "like a barrel" (Shabbat 88a), creating a moment of absolute choice. Yet the Midrash also tells us that the souls of all future Jews were present at Sinai, binding every generation to this covenant. The revelation wasn't just historical—it was eternal, transcending time and space.

## The Two Tablets: Structure and Significance

The Ten Commandments were inscribed on two stone tablets (luchot), written by the finger of God Himself. But why two tablets? Jewish tradition offers profound insights into this division.

The classic interpretation divides the commandments into two categories: the first tablet contains the five commandments governing the relationship between humans and God (bein adam l'Makom), while the second tablet holds the five commandments governing relationships between people (bein adam l'chaveiro). This structure teaches a fundamental principle: you cannot properly serve God while mistreating your fellow human beings. The two tablets are inseparable, just as love of God and love of humanity must be integrated.

The Talmud describes a remarkable detail: the tablets were written through and through, yet the letters were readable from both sides—a physical impossibility that testified to their miraculous nature. The stone itself, according to tradition, was sapphire, quarried from beneath God's throne of glory.

## Why These Ten? The Foundation of All

While the Torah contains 613 commandments, why were these ten singled out for direct divine proclamation? The answer lies in their foundational nature.

Rabbi Saadia Gaon (882-942 CE) explained that these ten principles serve as categories under which all 613 commandments can be organized. They represent the pillars upon which the entire structure of Jewish law rests. Maimonides emphasized that these commandments combine both ethical and ritual law, showing their inseparability in Jewish thought.

The number ten itself holds significance. Just as God created the world with ten utterances ("And God said..." appears ten times in the creation narrative), He gave ten commandments to structure the moral universe. The Ten Commandments are thus the moral parallel to the physical creation, establishing how humans should live in the world God created.

The Zohar teaches that the Ten Commandments correspond to the ten sefirot (divine emanations), connecting earthly law to cosmic structure. Living by these commandments aligns human behavior with the very fabric of reality.

## The Two Versions: Exodus and Deuteronomy

The Ten Commandments appear twice in the Torah—in Exodus 20:1-14 and Deuteronomy 5:6-18. While largely identical, there are significant differences, particularly in the Fourth Commandment regarding the Sabbath.

In Exodus, the Sabbath commandment begins with "**Zachor**" (זָכוֹר)—"Remember the Sabbath day." In Deuteronomy, it begins with "**Shamor**" (שָׁמוֹר)—"Observe the Sabbath day." The Talmud (Shevuot 20b) teaches that God miraculously spoke both words simultaneously—something impossible for human speech but not for divine revelation. This teaches that remembering and observing are two aspects of one reality: we must remember the Sabbath's spiritual meaning while observing its practical laws.

Another difference: Exodus roots the Sabbath in creation ("For in six days God made heaven and earth"), while Deuteronomy connects it to the Exodus ("Remember that you were a slave in Egypt"). The rabbis explain that the Sabbath serves dual purposes—testifying to God as Creator and to God as Redeemer. Both truths matter.

These variations teach a crucial lesson: the Torah contains layers of meaning, and apparent contradictions reveal deeper harmonies.

## The Ten Commandments: Detailed Analysis

### First Commandment: Divine Identity

**Hebrew:** אָנֹכִי יְהוָה אֱלֹהֶיךָ, אֲשֶׁר הוֹצֵאתִיךָ מֵאֶרֶץ מִצְרַיִם, מִבֵּית עֲבָדִים  
**Transliteration:** *Anochi Adonai Elohecha, asher hotzeticha mei-eretz Mitzrayim, mi-beit avadim*  
**Translation:** "I am the LORD your God, who brought you out of the land of Egypt, out of the house of bondage."

**Interpretation:**  
Is this actually a commandment, or merely a statement? Maimonides counts it as the first commandment—the obligation to believe in God's existence and His role in history. Nachmanides disagreed, seeing it as the preamble establishing God's authority to command.

The commandment doesn't just say "I am God" but "I am YOUR God, who brought you out of Egypt." God doesn't introduce Himself as Creator of the universe but as the Redeemer of Israel. This is personal, historical, relational. The God of the Ten Commandments isn't an abstract philosophical principle but the God who intervenes in history, who hears cries, who liberates slaves.

**Practical Application:**  
- Cultivate personal relationship with God, not just abstract belief
- Remember that Jewish faith is rooted in historical experience, not just philosophy
- See God as actively involved in your life, not distant or detached
- Study Jewish history to understand God's ongoing relationship with the Jewish people

**Related Halachot:**  
- The obligation to believe in God's existence (the first of Maimonides' 13 Principles)
- The mitzvah to know that God is One
- Requirements to study Torah to deepen understanding of God

### Second Commandment: No Other Gods

**Hebrew:** לֹא-יִהְיֶה לְךָ אֱלֹהִים אֲחֵרִים, עַל-פָּנָי  
**Transliteration:** *Lo yihiyeh lecha Elohim acherim al-panai*  
**Translation:** "You shall have no other gods before Me."

**Extended:** The commandment continues to prohibit making or worshiping graven images.

**Interpretation:**  
This commandment establishes pure monotheism—not just that God is supreme among many gods (henotheism), but that no other gods exist or deserve recognition. The phrase "al-panai" (before My face) means "while I exist"—which is always. It's not about geographical priority but absolute exclusivity.

The prohibition extends to graven images because idolatry begins when we try to represent or limit the infinite God. Even making an image of the true God is forbidden, as it reduces the transcendent to the tangible. The rabbis teach that this includes any intermediary we place between ourselves and God—even good things can become idols if they displace God from our hearts.

**Practical Application:**  
- Reject all forms of idolatry, including modern equivalents (money, power, fame as ultimate values)
- Avoid even visual representations of God in Jewish practice
- Don't use intermediaries in prayer—approach God directly
- Be wary of any person, ideology, or thing claiming absolute loyalty

**Related Halachot:**  
- Prohibition against bowing to idols or making offerings to them
- Requirement to destroy idols and not derive benefit from them
- Laws against even appearing to worship idols (mar'it ayin)
- The three cardinal sins a Jew must die rather than transgress: idolatry, murder, and sexual immorality

### Third Commandment: God's Name

**Hebrew:** לֹא תִשָּׂא אֶת-שֵׁם-יְהוָה אֱלֹהֶיךָ, לַשָּׁוְא  
**Transliteration:** *Lo tisa et-shem Adonai Elohecha lashav*  
**Translation:** "You shall not take the name of the LORD your God in vain."

**Interpretation:**  
This commandment protects the sanctity of God's name. "Lashav" (in vain) encompasses several prohibitions:

1. **False oaths:** Swearing by God's name to something untrue
2. **Unnecessary oaths:** Using God's name when no oath is needed
3. **Unfulfilled vows:** Making promises in God's name and not keeping them
4. **Blasphemy:** Cursing or showing contempt for God's name

The Talmud teaches that this sin is so severe that God does not easily forgive it, even on Yom Kippur. Why? Because it treats the sacred carelessly, using God's name as a mere tool for human purposes.

Jewish law extends this by avoiding pronouncing God's explicit name (the Tetragrammaton) except in specific sacred contexts. Even substitute names like "Adonai" are used cautiously. Written instances of God's name must be handled with respect and buried rather than discarded.

**Practical Application:**  
- Avoid casual oaths and swearing
- Keep your word, especially promises made seriously
- Treat God's name with reverence in speech and writing
- Don't make vows or promises lightly
- Understand that your actions as a Jew reflect on God's name—behave accordingly

**Related Halachot:**  
- Laws of oaths and vows (nedarim and shevuot)
- Prohibition against erasing or destroying God's written name
- Requirements for disposing of holy texts (genizah)
- The obligation to sanctify God's name (Kiddush Hashem) and avoid desecrating it (Chillul Hashem)

### Fourth Commandment: The Sabbath

**Hebrew (Exodus):** זָכוֹר אֶת-יוֹם הַשַּׁבָּת, לְקַדְּשׁוֹ  
**Transliteration:** *Zachor et-yom haShabbat, l'kadesho*  
**Translation:** "Remember the Sabbath day, to keep it holy."

**Hebrew (Deuteronomy):** שָׁמוֹר אֶת-יוֹם הַשַּׁבָּת, לְקַדְּשׁוֹ  
**Transliteration:** *Shamor et-yom haShabbat, l'kadesho*  
**Translation:** "Observe the Sabbath day, to keep it holy."

**Interpretation:**  
The Sabbath commandment is unique—it's the longest of the Ten Commandments and the only one that is both a positive commandment (rest and make it holy) and a negative commandment (don't work).

The dual language—"remember" and "observe"—teaches that Shabbat requires both mental/spiritual engagement and physical action. Remembering includes making Kiddush (sanctification over wine), thinking about Shabbat's meaning, and preparing for it. Observing includes the prohibition of the 39 categories of creative work (melachot) and the obligation to rest.

The Sabbath serves as:
- **Weekly testimony** to God as Creator
- **Remembrance** of the Exodus and freedom from slavery
- **Preview** of the World to Come (olam haba)
- **Sign** of the covenant between God and Israel

The rabbis call Shabbat "a taste of the World to Come"—a weekly island of peace, holiness, and rest in time.

**Practical Application:**  
- Cease creative work from Friday sunset to Saturday nightfall
- Make Shabbat special through ritual (candles, Kiddush, special meals, havdalah)
- Dedicate time to prayer, study, and family
- Experience rest as sacred, not merely as absence of work
- Prepare during the week so Shabbat can be truly restful

**Related Halachot:**  
- The 39 categories of prohibited work (melachot)
- Laws of muktzeh (items not handled on Shabbat)
- Requirements for lighting Shabbat candles
- Laws of cooking and food preparation before Shabbat
- Obligations of Kiddush and havdalah
- Permitted activities for saving life (pikuach nefesh) on Shabbat

### Fifth Commandment: Honor Parents

**Hebrew:** כַּבֵּד אֶת-אָבִיךָ, וְאֶת-אִמֶּךָ  
**Transliteration:** *Kabed et-avicha v'et-imecha*  
**Translation:** "Honor your father and your mother."

**Extended:** "...that your days may be long upon the land which the LORD your God gives you."

**Interpretation:**  
This commandment bridges the two tablets—it's the last on the first tablet (according to most traditions), connecting reverence for parents to reverence for God. The Talmud teaches that when a person honors their parents, God considers it as if they honored God Himself, for there are three partners in the creation of a person: God, father, and mother.

"Honor" (kavod) includes providing for parents' physical needs—food, clothing, shelter—and treating them with respect in speech and action. The parallel commandment to "fear" (yirah) parents, found in Leviticus 19:3, adds requirements like not sitting in a parent's seat, not contradicting them directly, and showing deference.

This is the only commandment with an explicit reward: long life. The rabbis explain that honoring parents creates chain of respect across generations, building stable families and societies.

**Practical Application:**  
- Provide for aging parents' material needs
- Speak respectfully to and about parents
- Don't contradict or embarrass parents publicly
- Visit and care for parents, especially in old age
- Honor parents even if they were imperfect or difficult
- The obligation continues even after parents pass away (honoring their memory)

**Related Halachot:**  
- Children are obligated to honor parents even at personal expense
- The obligation to stand in a parent's presence
- If parents' commands conflict with Torah law, Torah takes precedence
- A married woman's obligation to her husband takes priority, but she should still honor parents when possible
- Honoring in-laws as a sign of respect

### Sixth Commandment: Do Not Murder

**Hebrew:** לֹא תִּרְצָח  
**Transliteration:** *Lo tirtzach*  
**Translation:** "You shall not murder."

**Interpretation:**  
This is often mistranslated as "You shall not kill," but the Hebrew word "tirtzach" specifically refers to unlawful killing—murder. Jewish law recognizes that some killing is permitted (self-defense, judicial execution, warfare under strict conditions), but murder—the intentional, unlawful taking of innocent life—is absolutely forbidden.

Murder is one of the three cardinal sins for which a Jew must allow themselves to be killed rather than transgress. The rabbis teach that one who murders is considered as if they destroyed an entire world, for that person might have had descendants who are now never born.

The commandment extends beyond physical murder. The Talmud discusses "verbal murder" (embarrassing someone publicly, which "drains the blood from their face"), the sin of hatred in the heart, and even causing someone indirect harm through negligence.

**Practical Application:**  
- Never take innocent human life
- Avoid violence and seek peaceful conflict resolution
- Don't embarrass others publicly
- Don't speak in ways that could drive someone to self-harm
- Maintain safe conditions to prevent accidental death
- Support life-affirming values and oppose cultures of death

**Related Halachot:**  
- The prohibition extends to causing indirect death through negligence
- The requirement to build safety features (like railings) to prevent accidental death
- Laws of self-defense (rodef)—you may kill an active pursuer to save an innocent life
- The obligation to save life (pikuach nefesh) overrides almost all other commandments
- Capital punishment in Jewish law (rarely applied due to strict evidentiary requirements)

### Seventh Commandment: Do Not Commit Adultery

**Hebrew:** לֹא תִּנְאָף  
**Transliteration:** *Lo tin'af*  
**Translation:** "You shall not commit adultery."

**Interpretation:**  
The prohibition of adultery is another cardinal sin—one of the three for which a Jew must die rather than transgress. In biblical law, adultery specifically refers to sexual relations between a married woman and a man who is not her husband. (Biblical law treats a married man's relations with an unmarried woman differently, though rabbinic law strongly condemns this as well.)

Why this severity? Adultery violates the fundamental covenant of marriage, creates confusion about paternity and inheritance, and destroys the trust essential to family life. The rabbis saw marriage as a microcosm of the covenant between God and Israel—adultery parallels idolatry, the violation of the divine covenant.

Jewish sexual ethics extend far beyond this prohibition. The Torah commands comprehensive laws of sexual morality (arayot), and rabbinic law adds protective measures like yichud (not being secluded with someone who is forbidden to you) and tzniut (modest dress and behavior).

**Practical Application:**  
- Maintain absolute fidelity in marriage
- Avoid situations that could lead to temptation (yichud)
- Dress and behave modestly
- Guard your thoughts—the rabbis teach that "thought of sin is worse than sin itself" in some ways
- Respect marriage boundaries—your own and others'
- Create societies that honor marriage and family stability

**Related Halachot:**  
- Laws of yichud (seclusion)
- Requirements for modest dress (tzniut)
- Prohibited relationships (arayot) detailed in Leviticus 18 and 20
- Laws of niddah (family purity)
- The requirement for a proper Jewish marriage (kiddushin)
- Laws regarding divorce (get) when a marriage cannot continue

### Eighth Commandment: Do Not Steal

**Hebrew:** לֹא תִּגְנֹב  
**Transliteration:** *Lo tignov*  
**Translation:** "You shall not steal."

**Interpretation:**  
The simple language masks profound scope. What kind of stealing does this prohibit? The rabbis debate: some say it refers to kidnapping (stealing a person), given its placement among capital crimes like murder and adultery. Others say it refers to ordinary theft of property.

The consensus: both are prohibited, but in the context of the Ten Commandments—brief, universal principles—this likely refers primarily to kidnapping, with property theft covered elsewhere in the Torah (Leviticus 19:11). Either way, the principle is clear: respecting others' property and personhood.

Jewish law extensively develops theft laws, including:
- **Gezel:** Robbery (taking by force)
- **Geneivah:** Theft (taking secretly)
- **Ona'ah:** Fraud or deception in business
- **Geneivat da'at:** "Stealing the mind"—deception that creates false impressions

Even causing financial loss through indirect means is often prohibited. The obligation extends beyond not stealing to actively returning lost property and not benefiting from stolen goods.

**Practical Application:**  
- Never take what isn't yours—whether property, ideas, or credit for work
- Don't keep lost items; make effort to return them
- Pay workers fairly and on time
- Don't deceive in business dealings
- Avoid even creating false impressions (geneivat da'at)
- Don't use others' intellectual property without permission
- Respect copyrights and licensing (according to dina d'malchuta dina—law of the land)

**Related Halachot:**  
- Laws of hashavas aveidah (returning lost property)
- Prohibition against moving boundary markers
- Laws of ona'ah (overcharging or underpaying)
- Requirements for honest weights and measures
- Prohibition against withholding wages
- Laws regarding found property and its return

### Ninth Commandment: Do Not Bear False Witness

**Hebrew:** לֹא-תַעֲנֶה בְרֵעֲךָ עֵד שָׁקֶר  
**Transliteration:** *Lo ta'aneh v're'acha ed shaker*  
**Translation:** "You shall not bear false witness against your neighbor."

**Interpretation:**  
This commandment specifically addresses testimony in judicial proceedings—don't give false testimony in court. The Torah imposes severe penalties on false witnesses: they receive the punishment their testimony would have imposed on the accused (measure for measure).

But the principle extends beyond courtrooms. The prohibition includes:
- **Lying** in general (though false testimony is more severe)
- **Slander** (lashon hara)—speaking true but damaging information unnecessarily
- **Gossip** (rechilut)—carrying tales between people
- **Flattery**—insincere praise that misleads

Truth is a foundational value in Jewish ethics. God's seal is truth (emet), and the Talmud teaches that one who changes their word is as if they worship idols.

**Practical Application:**  
- Tell the truth, especially in official or important contexts
- Don't testify in court unless you have direct knowledge
- Avoid gossip and tale-bearing
- Don't slander others, even with truth if sharing serves no constructive purpose
- Be careful not to mislead even without explicit lying
- Keep your word—let your "yes" be yes and "no" be no

**Related Halachot:**  
- Laws of eidut (testimony) and witness requirements
- The prohibition of lashon hara (speaking negatively about others)
- Laws of rechilut (talebearing)
- The requirement that two witnesses are needed for most judicial matters
- Witnesses must see the act directly, not rely on hearsay
- False witnesses receive the punishment they sought to impose

### Tenth Commandment: Do Not Covet

**Hebrew:** לֹא תַחְמֹד בֵּית רֵעֶךָ; לֹא-תַחְמֹד אֵשֶׁת רֵעֶךָ, וְעַבְדּוֹ וַאֲמָתוֹ וְשׁוֹרוֹ וַחֲמֹרוֹ, וְכֹל, אֲשֶׁר לְרֵעֶךָ  
**Transliteration:** *Lo tachmod beit re'echa; lo tachmod eshet re'echa, v'avdo va'amato v'shoro va'chamoro, v'chol asher l're'echa*  
**Translation:** "You shall not covet your neighbor's house; you shall not covet your neighbor's wife, nor his male servant, nor his female servant, nor his ox, nor his donkey, nor anything that is your neighbor's."

**Interpretation:**  
This final commandment is unique—it governs not action but desire itself. How can the law regulate thoughts and feelings? The rabbis distinguish between fleeting jealous thoughts (which are human nature) and active coveting that leads to scheming and action.

"Lo tachmod" prohibits taking concrete steps to acquire what belongs to another, even through legal means, when driven by covetousness. Deuteronomy adds "lo titaveh" (do not desire), which some authorities see as a separate prohibition against the mere desire itself, even without action.

The placement of this commandment is deliberate. It concludes the Ten Commandments by addressing the root of many sins: unchecked desire. Murder, adultery, theft—these often begin with coveting. By mastering our desires, we prevent future transgressions.

**Practical Application:**  
- Cultivate contentment with what you have
- Don't scheme to obtain what belongs to others
- Practice gratitude for your blessings
- Recognize that everyone's portion is divinely ordained
- Channel competitive feelings into self-improvement, not acquisitiveness
- Understand that happiness comes from wanting what you have, not having what you want

**Related Halachot:**  
- The distinction between chamad (coveting leading to action) and taavah (mere desire)
- The prohibition extends to pressuring someone to sell even if you pay full price
- Cultivation of contentment (histapkut) as a virtue
- The midah (character trait) of being same'ach b'chelko (happy with one's portion)

## The Enduring Significance

The Ten Commandments remain as relevant today as they were at Sinai. They address timeless human challenges: our relationship with the divine, our responsibility to family, our obligations to society, and our inner moral life.

In synagogue ritual, the congregation stands when the Ten Commandments are read during the Torah service, re-enacting the moment at Sinai when the people stood to receive them. The two tablets are a universal symbol of Judaism, displayed in synagogues and depicted in Jewish art worldwide.

These ten brief principles contain the seeds of entire legal systems, ethical philosophies, and spiritual traditions. They are simultaneously simple enough for a child to understand and deep enough to occupy scholars for lifetimes. They are given to the Jewish people but contain universal truths applicable to all humanity.

The Ten Commandments remind us that law and love are not opposites—God gives these laws precisely because He loves us and wants us to live well. They provide structure for freedom, showing that true liberty comes not from the absence of boundaries but from living within righteous limits.

As the prophet Micah summarized: "He has told you, O human, what is good, and what the LORD requires of you: only to do justice, to love kindness, and to walk humbly with your God" (Micah 6:8). The Ten Commandments show us how.
