# The Five Megillot: Sacred Scrolls of the Jewish Calendar

## Introduction

The Five Megillot (חמש מגילות, *Chamesh Megillot*) are five biblical books traditionally read aloud during specific Jewish holidays throughout the year. The term "megillah" means "scroll" in Hebrew, and these five scrolls hold a special place in Jewish liturgy and tradition. Each megillah is associated with a particular festival, and their themes resonate deeply with the spiritual essence of their corresponding holidays.

The Five Megillot are:
1. **Shir HaShirim** (Song of Songs) - read on Pesach (Passover)
2. **Ruth** - read on Shavuot (Feast of Weeks)
3. **Eicha** (Lamentations) - read on Tisha B'Av (Ninth of Av)
4. **Kohelet** (Ecclesiastes) - read on Sukkot (Feast of Tabernacles)
5. **Esther** - read on Purim (Feast of Lots)

These scrolls appear in the Ketuvim (Writings), the third section of the Hebrew Bible, and together they form a rich tapestry of Jewish thought, encompassing themes of love, loyalty, mourning, wisdom, and redemption.

## 1. Shir HaShirim (Song of Songs) - Pesach

### Authorship and Background

The Song of Songs, known in Hebrew as *Shir HaShirim*, is traditionally attributed to King Solomon (*Shlomo HaMelech*). The opening verse states: **שִׁיר הַשִּׁירִים אֲשֶׁר לִשְׁלֹמֹה** (*Shir HaShirim asher l'Shlomo*) - "The Song of Songs, which is Solomon's" (Song of Songs 1:1). Solomon, renowned for his wisdom and poetry, is said to have composed this work in his youth, though some scholars debate whether he wrote it himself or whether it was dedicated to him.

### When and Why It Is Read

Shir HaShirim is traditionally read on the Sabbath of Passover (Shabbat Chol HaMoed Pesach), though many communities read it on the eve of Passover or throughout the festival. The connection to Passover might seem puzzling at first—the text appears to be a collection of passionate love poems between a man and a woman. However, the Jewish interpretive tradition understands the book on multiple levels.

### Allegorical Meaning

The rabbis of the Talmud interpreted Shir HaShirim not as a simple love poem but as an allegory of the relationship between God and the Jewish people. Rabbi Akiva famously declared: "The whole world is not worth the day on which the Song of Songs was given to Israel, for all the Writings are holy, but the Song of Songs is the Holy of Holies" (Mishnah Yadayim 3:5).

In this interpretation, the passionate love between the lovers represents the covenant between God and Israel, established during the Exodus from Egypt—the very event celebrated on Passover. The redemption from Egypt is seen as a "honeymoon period" in the divine-human relationship, when God took Israel as His bride at Mount Sinai.

### Key Verses and Themes

One of the most famous passages captures the intensity of divine love:

**שִׂימֵנִי כַחוֹתָם עַל־לִבֶּךָ כַּחוֹתָם עַל־זְרוֹעֶךָ כִּי־עַזָּה כַמָּוֶת אַהֲבָה**
*"Simeini kachotam al-libecha kachotam al-zro'echa ki-azah kamavet ahavah"*
"Set me as a seal upon your heart, as a seal upon your arm; for love is strong as death" (Song of Songs 8:6)

This verse speaks to the unbreakable bond between the lovers—between God and Israel—a love that cannot be extinguished even by the trials of exile and persecution.

Another powerful verse proclaims:

**אֲנִי לְדוֹדִי וְדוֹדִי לִי**
*"Ani l'dodi v'dodi li"*
"I am my beloved's and my beloved is mine" (Song of Songs 6:3)

This mutual declaration of belonging reflects the reciprocal nature of the covenant relationship, where God chooses Israel and Israel chooses God.

### Main Themes and Lessons

- **Divine Love**: The primary theme is the passionate, enduring love between God and the Jewish people.
- **Redemption and Freedom**: Read on Passover, it celebrates the freedom from bondage and the beginning of the eternal relationship between God and Israel.
- **Beauty of Devotion**: The poetic imagery celebrates the beauty of spiritual devotion and religious commitment.
- **Longing and Seeking**: The beloved's search for her lover reflects the soul's yearning for connection with the Divine.

## 2. Ruth - Shavuot

### Story Summary

The Book of Ruth tells the story of a Moabite woman who, after the death of her husband (an Israelite), chooses to stay with her mother-in-law Naomi and embrace the Jewish faith. The story takes place during the time of the Judges, a period of instability in ancient Israel.

Ruth's declaration to Naomi is one of the most moving expressions of loyalty in the Bible:

**כִּי אֶל־אֲשֶׁר תֵּלְכִי אֵלֵךְ וּבַאֲשֶׁר תָּלִינִי אָלִין עַמֵּךְ עַמִּי וֵאלֹהַיִךְ אֱלֹהָי**
*"Ki el-asher telchi elech uva'asher talini alin amech ami vElohayich Elohai"*
"Where you go, I will go; where you lodge, I will lodge; your people shall be my people, and your God my God" (Ruth 1:16)

Ruth and Naomi return to Bethlehem poor and destitute. Ruth goes to glean in the fields, where she meets Boaz, a wealthy relative of Naomi's late husband. Boaz, impressed by Ruth's devotion to Naomi and her virtuous character, acts as her *goel* (redeemer), marrying her according to the laws of levirate marriage. Their union produces a son, Obed, who becomes the grandfather of King David.

### When and Why It Is Read

The Book of Ruth is read on Shavuot, the festival celebrating the giving of the Torah at Mount Sinai. Several connections link Ruth to Shavuot:

1. **Conversion and Torah Acceptance**: Ruth's voluntary acceptance of the Jewish faith mirrors Israel's acceptance of the Torah at Sinai.
2. **Harvest Season**: The story takes place during the barley and wheat harvests, which coincide with the season of Shavuot.
3. **David's Ancestry**: King David, whose lineage is traced through Ruth, was born and died on Shavuot according to tradition.

### Conversion and David's Ancestry

Ruth's story is the paradigmatic tale of conversion (*giyur*) in Jewish tradition. Despite coming from Moab—a nation traditionally hostile to Israel—Ruth's sincere devotion and commitment earned her a place not only among the Jewish people but in the royal lineage leading to the Messiah.

The book ends with a genealogy:

**וְאוֹבֵד הוֹלִיד אֶת־יִשָׁי וְיִשַׁי הוֹלִיד אֶת־דָּוִד**
*"V'Oved holid et-Yishai v'Yishai holid et-David"*
"Obed begot Jesse, and Jesse begot David" (Ruth 4:22)

This lineage demonstrates that genuine devotion to God and His people transcends ethnic boundaries. Even the Messiah will descend from a Moabite convert, teaching that righteousness, not blood, determines one's place in the divine plan.

### Themes of Chesed (Loving-Kindness)

The Hebrew word *chesed* (חסד) appears throughout the book, referring to acts of loving-kindness, loyalty, and grace. Ruth demonstrates *chesed* toward Naomi by refusing to abandon her. Boaz shows *chesed* toward Ruth by allowing her to glean in his fields and ultimately marrying her. God's *chesed* is evident in orchestrating the entire story, bringing redemption to a destitute widow and stranger.

### Main Themes and Lessons

- **Loyalty and Devotion**: Ruth's unwavering commitment to Naomi and to God.
- **Conversion**: The power of sincere spiritual transformation.
- **Divine Providence**: How God works through ordinary events to bring about redemption.
- **Social Justice**: The laws of gleaning and treatment of the poor and stranger.
- **Chesed**: The transformative power of loving-kindness in human relationships.

## 3. Eicha (Lamentations) - Tisha B'Av

### Authorship

Eicha (איכה), meaning "How" or "Alas," is traditionally attributed to the prophet Jeremiah (*Yirmiyahu*). Jeremiah witnessed the destruction of Jerusalem and the First Temple by the Babylonians in 586 BCE. His authorship is supported by 2 Chronicles 35:25, which mentions that Jeremiah composed lamentations.

### When and Why It Is Read

Eicha is read on Tisha B'Av, the Ninth of Av, the saddest day in the Jewish calendar. This day commemorates multiple tragedies in Jewish history, most notably the destruction of both the First and Second Temples. The book is chanted in a mournful melody in synagogues around the world, often while sitting on the floor or on low stools, in keeping with the mourning practices of the day.

### Structure: Acrostic Poetry

Lamentations is structured as five poems, most of which are *acrostics*—each verse begins with a successive letter of the Hebrew alphabet. This structure serves multiple purposes:
- It creates a sense of completeness, moving from aleph (א) to tav (ת), representing the full spectrum of suffering.
- The alphabetical structure may help with memorization and liturgical recitation.
- It imposes order on chaos, suggesting that even in destruction, there is divine structure and meaning.

The third chapter is particularly elaborate, with three verses for each letter of the alphabet, creating 66 verses total.

### Mourning the Temple

The book opens with a stark image of Jerusalem's desolation:

**אֵיכָה יָשְׁבָה בָדָד הָעִיר רַבָּתִי עָם הָיְתָה כְּאַלְמָנָה**
*"Eicha yashvah vadad ha'ir rabati am hayetah k'almanah"*
"How lonely sits the city that was full of people! She has become like a widow who was once great among nations" (Lamentations 1:1)

The personification of Jerusalem as a widow captures the depth of loss—not just physical destruction but the severing of a relationship. The Temple, where God's presence dwelt among His people, is gone.

Yet amid the despair, there is a glimmer of hope:

**חַסְדֵי יְהוָה כִּי לֹא־תָמְנוּ כִּי לֹא־כָלוּ רַחֲמָיו חֲדָשִׁים לַבְּקָרִים רַבָּה אֱמוּנָתֶךָ**
*"Chasdei Adonai ki lo-tamnu ki lo-chalu rachamav chadashim labekarim rabah emunatecha"*
"The steadfast love of the LORD never ceases; His mercies never come to an end; they are new every morning; great is Your faithfulness" (Lamentations 3:22-23)

This passage, placed at the center of the book, reminds the mourners that God's mercy endures even in exile, and that renewal is possible.

### Main Themes and Lessons

- **Collective Mourning**: The importance of communal grief and remembrance.
- **Consequences of Sin**: The destruction is understood as a result of the nation's sins and abandonment of God's covenant.
- **Hope in Despair**: Even in the darkest moments, faith in God's mercy persists.
- **Memory and Identity**: Remembering tragedy preserves identity and prevents repetition.
- **Divine Justice and Mercy**: Wrestling with how a loving God allows such suffering.

## 4. Kohelet (Ecclesiastes) - Sukkot

### Authorship

Kohelet (קֹהֶלֶת), known in English as Ecclesiastes, is traditionally attributed to King Solomon in his old age. The opening verse identifies the author as:

**דִּבְרֵי קֹהֶלֶת בֶּן־דָּוִד מֶלֶךְ בִּירוּשָׁלָיִם**
*"Divrei Kohelet ben-David melech b'Yerushalayim"*
"The words of Kohelet, son of David, king in Jerusalem" (Ecclesiastes 1:1)

The name "Kohelet" comes from the root *kahal* (קהל), meaning "to gather" or "assemble," suggesting one who gathers wisdom or addresses an assembly. According to tradition, Solomon wrote Song of Songs in his youth, Proverbs in his middle years, and Ecclesiastes in his old age, reflecting the accumulated wisdom of a lifetime.

### When and Why It Is Read

Kohelet is read on Sukkot, the autumn harvest festival when Jews dwell in temporary booths (*sukkot*) for seven days. The connection might seem paradoxical—Sukkot is called *Zman Simchateinu*, "the time of our rejoicing," yet Kohelet is often read as pessimistic or melancholic. However, this juxtaposition is deliberate:

1. **Impermanence**: Dwelling in temporary structures reminds us of life's transience, a central theme of Kohelet.
2. **True Joy**: After celebrating material blessings during the harvest, Kohelet reminds us that true joy comes from fearing God, not accumulating possessions.
3. **Wisdom and Reflection**: The festival's joy is deepened by contemplating life's meaning.

### "Vanity of Vanities"

The book's most famous phrase appears at the beginning and end:

**הֲבֵל הֲבָלִים אָמַר קֹהֶלֶת הֲבֵל הֲבָלִים הַכֹּל הָבֶל**
*"Havel havalim amar Kohelet havel havalim hakol havel"*
"Vanity of vanities, says Kohelet, vanity of vanities; all is vanity" (Ecclesiastes 1:2)

The Hebrew word *hevel* (הבל) literally means "breath" or "vapor"—something insubstantial and fleeting. Kohelet uses this word 38 times, emphasizing the transitory nature of human endeavors, pleasure, wealth, and even wisdom itself when pursued as ends in themselves.

### Life's Meaning

Despite its seemingly pessimistic tone, Kohelet ultimately affirms meaning in life through several key insights:

**לַכֹּל זְמָן וְעֵת לְכָל־חֵפֶץ תַּחַת הַשָּׁמָיִם**
*"Lakol zeman v'et l'chol-chefetz tachat hashamayim"*
"For everything there is a season, and a time for every purpose under heaven" (Ecclesiastes 3:1)

This famous passage reminds us that life's rhythms have meaning within a larger divine order.

The book concludes with practical wisdom:

**סוֹף דָּבָר הַכֹּל נִשְׁמָע אֶת־הָאֱלֹהִים יְרָא וְאֶת־מִצְוֹתָיו שְׁמוֹר כִּי־זֶה כָּל־הָאָדָם**
*"Sof davar hakol nishma et-haElohim yera v'et-mitzvotav sh'mor ki-zeh kol-ha'adam"*
"The end of the matter, all has been heard: Fear God and keep His commandments, for this is the whole of man" (Ecclesiastes 12:13)

Here, Kohelet resolves the tension: life's meaning is found not in chasing after fleeting pleasures or accumulating wealth, but in reverence for God and obedience to His commands.

### Main Themes and Lessons

- **Mortality and Impermanence**: Recognition that all earthly things pass away.
- **Search for Meaning**: The human quest to find purpose beyond material success.
- **Enjoyment of Simple Pleasures**: Finding joy in daily life as a gift from God.
- **Wisdom's Limits**: Even wisdom cannot answer all of life's mysteries.
- **Fear of God**: The foundation of true wisdom and meaningful existence.
- **Balance**: The importance of moderation and recognizing life's seasons.

## 5. Esther - Purim

### The Story

The Book of Esther tells the dramatic story of how the Jewish people in the Persian Empire were saved from genocide through the courage of Queen Esther and the wisdom of her cousin Mordechai. Set during the reign of King Ahasuerus (often identified with Xerxes I, 486-465 BCE), the narrative unfolds as follows:

King Ahasuerus holds a lavish banquet and, when his queen Vashti refuses to appear, deposes her. A search for a new queen leads to Esther, a Jewish orphan raised by her cousin Mordechai, who becomes queen without revealing her Jewish identity.

Meanwhile, Haman, the king's chief minister, becomes enraged when Mordechai refuses to bow to him. Learning that Mordechai is Jewish, Haman plots to destroy all Jews in the empire. He casts lots (*purim*) to determine the date for the genocide and convinces the king to issue an irrevocable decree ordering the annihilation of the Jews.

Mordechai urges Esther to intercede with the king, delivering this famous challenge:

**וּמִי יוֹדֵעַ אִם־לְעֵת כָּזֹאת הִגַּעַתְּ לַמַּלְכוּת**
*"Umi yodea im-l'et kazot higat lamalchut"*
"And who knows whether you have not attained royalty for such a time as this?" (Esther 4:14)

Esther risks her life by approaching the king uninvited, famously declaring:

**וְכַאֲשֶׁר אָבַדְתִּי אָבָדְתִּי**
*"V'ka'asher avadeti avadeti"*
"And if I perish, I perish" (Esther 4:16)

Through a series of dramatic events—including Esther's banquets, the king's discovery of Mordechai's past service, and Haman's downfall—the Jews are saved. A new decree allows them to defend themselves, and they triumph over their enemies. Haman is executed on the very gallows he prepared for Mordechai.

### Characters

- **Esther (Hadassah)**: The heroine who risks her life to save her people. Her name in Hebrew, Hadassah, means "myrtle."
- **Mordechai**: Esther's cousin and guardian, a wise leader who refuses to bow to Haman.
- **Ahasuerus**: The Persian king, portrayed as impressionable and easily manipulated.
- **Haman**: The villain, descended from Agag, king of Amalek (Israel's ancient enemy).
- **Vashti**: The deposed queen whose refusal sets the story in motion.
- **Harbona**: The eunuch who points out Haman's gallows at the crucial moment.

### Hidden Miracles

Uniquely among biblical books, Esther never explicitly mentions God's name. This absence is significant: the story demonstrates God's hidden presence (*hester panim*) working through natural events and human choices. Every "coincidence"—from Esther becoming queen to the king's sleepless night—reveals divine orchestration.

The name "Esther" itself is related to the Hebrew word *hester* (הסתר), meaning "hidden," emphasizing this theme. God's hiddenness in Esther reflects the experience of exile, where divine providence works behind the scenes rather than through open miracles like the splitting of the Red Sea.

### When It Is Read and Customs

The Megillat Esther is read twice on Purim—once in the evening and once in the morning. The reading is accompanied by unique customs:

1. **Noise-making**: Whenever Haman's name is mentioned, congregants make noise with *graggers* (noisemakers) to "blot out" his name, fulfilling the commandment to obliterate the memory of Amalek.

2. **Costumes**: Many people, especially children, dress in costumes, reflecting the theme of hidden identities in the story.

3. **Mishloach Manot**: Sending gifts of food to friends, promoting joy and community.

4. **Matanot La'evyonim**: Giving charity to the poor, ensuring everyone can celebrate.

5. **Seudah**: A festive meal, often including wine, celebrating the miraculous salvation.

6. **Reading with melody**: The scroll is chanted with special *trope* (cantillation marks), creating a dramatic oral performance.

### Main Themes and Lessons

- **Hidden Providence**: God's presence even when not explicitly mentioned.
- **Courage and Identity**: Esther's willingness to reveal her identity and risk her life.
- **Reversal of Fortune**: How circumstances can completely reverse (*ve'nahafoch hu*).
- **Jewish Unity**: The importance of communal solidarity in times of danger.
- **Obligation to Act**: Mordechai's reminder that privilege comes with responsibility.
- **Celebrating Deliverance**: The importance of remembering and celebrating redemption.

## Conclusion: The Five Megillot in Jewish Life

The Five Megillot form a literary and spiritual arc through the Jewish year, each scroll illuminating a different aspect of the Jewish experience and the human condition:

- **Shir HaShirim** celebrates divine love and redemption at Passover's springtime.
- **Ruth** embodies loyalty, conversion, and divine providence during the Shavuot harvest.
- **Eicha** confronts national tragedy and exile on Tisha B'Av's summer mourning.
- **Kohelet** contemplates life's meaning amid Sukkot's autumn harvest joy.
- **Esther** reveals hidden miracles and deliverance during Purim's late-winter celebration.

Together, these scrolls encompass the full range of Jewish experience—love and loss, joy and sorrow, exile and redemption. They teach that every season of life, every emotion, and every challenge has its place in the spiritual journey. By reading these texts at appointed times, the Jewish people re-live their collective memory and find wisdom for each generation's unique circumstances.

The Megillot remind us that sacred literature is not meant to be confined to academic study but should be encountered as living texts that speak to the rhythms of life and the cycle of the year. They invite us to see our own stories within the ancient narratives, to find God in both revelation and hiddenness, and to discover meaning in both celebration and mourning.

As Rabbi Akiva taught about Shir HaShirim, these texts are not merely holy—they are the "Holy of Holies," sacred spaces where heaven and earth meet, where the human and divine embrace, and where each generation discovers anew the eternal wisdom that sustains the Jewish soul.
