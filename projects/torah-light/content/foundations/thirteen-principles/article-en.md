# The Thirteen Principles of Faith (Shlosha Asar Ikkarim)

## Introduction: Rambam and the Foundation of Jewish Belief

Rabbi Moshe ben Maimon (1138-1204), known as Maimonides or by his Hebrew acronym Rambam, stands as one of the most influential Jewish scholars in history. Born in Córdoba, Spain, Rambam was a philosopher, physician, and Torah authority whose works continue to shape Jewish thought and practice today.

Living during the Golden Age of Spanish Jewry and later fleeing persecution to Morocco and eventually Egypt, Rambam witnessed firsthand the challenges facing Jewish communities who struggled to maintain their identity and faith under external pressures. As both a master of Jewish law (authoring the monumental *Mishneh Torah*) and a philosopher bridging Jewish tradition with Aristotelian thought (in his *Guide for the Perplexed*), Rambam possessed a unique ability to articulate the essential elements of Jewish belief.

### Why Thirteen Principles?

Before Rambam, Judaism had no formal creed. While Christianity and Islam had explicit statements of faith, Judaism focused primarily on practice and interpretation rather than dogmatic belief. This changed when Rambam formulated his Thirteen Principles of Faith (*Shlosha Asar Ikkarim*) in his commentary on the Mishnah, specifically in the introduction to Chapter 10 of Tractate Sanhedrin.

Rambam's purpose was threefold:

1. **To define the boundaries of Jewish belief** - establishing what distinguishes authentic Jewish faith from heresy
2. **To provide intellectual clarity** - giving philosophical structure to traditional beliefs
3. **To strengthen Jewish identity** - offering Jews a clear statement of what they believe, especially important during times of persecution and forced conversion

These principles became so central to Jewish consciousness that they were eventually incorporated into the liturgy through two poetic compositions: the **Ani Ma'amin** prayer (which begins each principle with "I believe with perfect faith") and the **Yigdal** hymn, which is sung at the conclusion of Shabbat and holiday services in many communities.

## The Thirteen Principles

### Principle 1: Existence of the Creator

**Hebrew:** אֲנִי מַאֲמִין בֶּאֱמוּנָה שְׁלֵמָה, שֶׁהַבּוֹרֵא יִתְבָּרַךְ שְׁמוֹ, הוּא בוֹרֵא וּמַנְהִיג לְכָל הַבְּרוּאִים

**Transliteration:** Ani ma'amin be'emunah sheleimah, she'haBoreh yitbarach shemo, hu borei u'manhig l'chol habru'im

**Translation:** I believe with perfect faith that the Creator, blessed be His name, is the Creator and Guide of all created beings.

**Explanation:**

This foundational principle establishes that God exists as the singular Creator and Sustainer of all reality. Unlike philosophical concepts of an impersonal force or abstract principle, this principle affirms a personal God who actively creates and continuously guides the universe.

The Hebrew word *Boreh* (Creator) implies ongoing creation rather than a one-time event. Jewish tradition understands God as constantly renewing creation, as expressed in the daily morning prayers: "He renews in His goodness every day, perpetually, the work of creation."

The addition of *manhig* (Guide) emphasizes Divine providence—God doesn't merely create and abandon the world but remains intimately involved with His creation. This concept, known as *hashgachah pratit* (Divine providence), teaches that nothing occurs by pure chance; God's wisdom guides all events, even if we cannot comprehend His ways.

This principle stands in contrast to:
- Atheism (denying God's existence)
- Deism (acknowledging a Creator who doesn't guide)
- Pantheism (equating God with nature itself)
- Dualism (claiming multiple divine powers)

**Scriptural Basis:**

The very first verse of the Torah establishes this principle: "In the beginning, God created the heavens and the earth" (Genesis 1:1). The prophets repeatedly affirm this truth: "I am the LORD, who made all things, who alone stretched out the heavens, who spread out the earth by Myself" (Isaiah 44:24).

**Daily Application:**

Recognizing God as Creator and Guide transforms our perspective on life. Every challenge becomes an opportunity for growth, every blessing a gift to acknowledge. When we understand that nothing is random—that the same Intelligence that designed galaxies also orchestrates the details of our lives—we can face uncertainty with confidence. This principle calls us to begin each day with gratitude (the *Modeh Ani* prayer) and to recognize God's hand in both the extraordinary and the mundane.

### Principle 2: God's Unity

**Hebrew:** אֲנִי מַאֲמִין בֶּאֱמוּנָה שְׁלֵמָה, שֶׁהַבּוֹרֵא יִתְבָּרַךְ שְׁמוֹ, הוּא יָחִיד

**Transliteration:** Ani ma'amin be'emunah sheleimah, she'haBoreh yitbarach shemo, hu yachid

**Translation:** I believe with perfect faith that the Creator, blessed be His name, is One.

**Explanation:**

The Hebrew word *yachid* denotes absolute, indivisible unity—not merely numerical oneness but a complete lack of any multiplicity, parts, or division. This principle, derived from the *Shema* ("Hear O Israel, the LORD our God, the LORD is One"), represents Judaism's central declaration.

God's unity means several things:

1. **Numerical unity** - There is only one God, not many
2. **Absolute simplicity** - God has no parts, no body, no physical form
3. **Conceptual unity** - All of God's attributes (mercy, justice, wisdom) are unified in His essence
4. **Temporal unity** - God's existence is unchanging and eternal

This stands in stark opposition to polytheism, but also to any theology that divides God into parts or persons. The absolute unity of God means that when we speak of God's "attributes" (mercy, justice, power), these are not separate characteristics but human attempts to understand different manifestations of a singular, indivisible reality.

**Scriptural Basis:**

The *Shema* (Deuteronomy 6:4) is the central declaration: "Hear O Israel, the LORD our God, the LORD is One." The prophet Isaiah emphasizes this: "Before Me no god was formed, nor shall there be after Me. I, I am the LORD, and besides Me there is no savior" (Isaiah 43:10-11).

**Daily Application:**

We recite the *Shema* twice daily, declaring God's absolute unity. This principle teaches us to see the underlying unity in all existence—that despite apparent contradictions and conflicts in the world, everything ultimately serves a singular divine purpose. In relationships, it reminds us that people who appear different or opposed may be united in pursuing truth. In personal struggles, it assures us that the God of mercy is identical to the God of justice; there is no contradiction in His nature.

### Principle 3: God's Incorporeality

**Hebrew:** אֲנִי מַאֲמִין בֶּאֱמוּנָה שְׁלֵמָה, שֶׁהַבּוֹרֵא יִתְבָּרַךְ שְׁמוֹ, אֵינוֹ גוּף

**Transliteration:** Ani ma'amin be'emunah sheleimah, she'haBoreh yitbarach shemo, eino guf

**Translation:** I believe with perfect faith that the Creator, blessed be His name, has no body.

**Explanation:**

This principle affirms that God is completely non-physical and cannot be imagined or depicted in any physical form. All anthropomorphic descriptions in Scripture—God's "hand," "eyes," "sitting on a throne"—are metaphors accommodating human understanding, not literal descriptions.

The prohibition against idolatry stems from this principle. If God has no physical form, any attempt to represent Him visually is fundamentally false and leads to worshiping a human creation rather than the Creator. This is why the second commandment forbids making "graven images."

Rambam emphasized this principle strongly because physical conceptions of God had crept into popular Jewish belief. Some Jews, influenced by literal readings of biblical anthropomorphisms, imagined God as having a body. Rambam considered such beliefs heretical because they limit God and equate Him with created beings.

God's incorporeality means:
- He has no location (though He is present everywhere)
- He undergoes no change (physical beings change)
- He cannot be perceived by physical senses
- He is completely beyond spatial dimensions

**Scriptural Basis:**

"God is not a man" (Numbers 23:19). "To whom will you liken Me, that I should be similar?" (Isaiah 46:5). The Torah explicitly prohibits making any image: "You saw no form on the day God spoke to you at Horeb" (Deuteronomy 4:15).

**Daily Application:**

This principle guards us against reducing God to our own image—whether physical or conceptual. It humbles our understanding, reminding us that our greatest thoughts about God are still inadequate. Practically, it prevents superstition and magical thinking, keeping our worship focused on God's true nature rather than on objects or images. It also teaches us that the highest form of spiritual connection is not through physical senses but through understanding, moral action, and study.

### Principle 4: God's Eternity

**Hebrew:** אֲנִי מַאֲמִין בֶּאֱמוּנָה שְׁלֵמָה, שֶׁהַבּוֹרֵא יִתְבָּרַךְ שְׁמוֹ, הוּא רִאשׁוֹן וְהוּא אַחֲרוֹן

**Transliteration:** Ani ma'amin be'emunah sheleimah, she'haBoreh yitbarach shemo, hu rishon v'hu acharon

**Translation:** I believe with perfect faith that the Creator, blessed be His name, is First and Last.

**Explanation:**

God exists outside of time. He has no beginning and no end. While everything in creation exists within the framework of time—with a past, present, and future—God transcends temporal limitations entirely.

The terms "first" and "last" (taken from Isaiah 44:6) don't mean God is the first in a sequence and will be the last remaining; rather, they indicate that God exists before time began and after time will end. More accurately, God exists in an eternal present where past, future, and present are equally accessible to His consciousness.

This principle has profound philosophical implications:
- God didn't "begin" to exist, nor can He cease to exist
- He is not subject to aging, decay, or change
- He exists independently of creation; even if nothing existed, He would still be
- Time itself is a creation; God is its Author, not its subject

Understanding God's eternity helps us grasp why He is the necessary foundation of all existence. Everything else in the universe could potentially not exist—it's *contingent*. But God's existence is *necessary*; He cannot not exist.

**Scriptural Basis:**

"Before the mountains were born, before You formed the earth and the world, from everlasting to everlasting, You are God" (Psalms 90:2). "I am the First and I am the Last, and besides Me there is no God" (Isaiah 44:6).

**Daily Application:**

When we grasp God's eternity, our temporal problems find perspective. What feels overwhelming today is a momentary ripple against the backdrop of eternity. This doesn't diminish our challenges but contextualizes them. Moreover, it assures us that God's promises are reliable—He who exists beyond time can be trusted to fulfill commitments that span generations. In prayer, we can bring concerns about both immediate needs and distant futures to the One who holds all time in His eternal present.

### Principle 5: God Alone is to be Worshiped

**Hebrew:** אֲנִי מַאֲמִין בֶּאֱמוּנָה שְׁלֵמָה, שֶׁהַבּוֹרֵא יִתְבָּרַךְ שְׁמוֹ, לוֹ לְבַדוֹ רָאוּי לְהִתְפַּלֵּל

**Transliteration:** Ani ma'amin be'emunah sheleimah, she'haBoreh yitbarach shemo, lo levado ra'ui l'hitpalel

**Translation:** I believe with perfect faith that the Creator, blessed be His name, to Him alone is it proper to pray.

**Explanation:**

This principle forbids worshiping, praying to, or serving any being or entity besides God Himself. No intermediaries, angels, saints, or spiritual forces may receive the worship due to God alone. Even righteous individuals or heavenly beings who might possess great spiritual power are still creations and cannot be objects of worship.

This principle has several important dimensions:

1. **No intermediary prayer** - We don't pray *through* angels or righteous people; we pray directly to God
2. **No divided loyalties** - Worship cannot be split between God and other forces
3. **No spiritual hierarchies requiring propitiation** - We need not appease lesser deities or spirits
4. **Personal relationship** - Every person has direct access to God without requiring human or angelic mediators

Judaism does recognize that righteous individuals and angels can *advocate* for us and that we can request prayers from living people. However, the crucial distinction is that ultimate prayer and worship are directed solely to God. When we ask for a righteous person's blessing, we're requesting they pray to God on our behalf—not that they themselves grant our request.

**Scriptural Basis:**

The first two commandments establish this: "I am the LORD your God... You shall have no other gods before Me" (Exodus 20:2-3). "You shall worship the LORD your God, and Him alone shall you serve" (Deuteronomy 6:13).

**Daily Application:**

This principle keeps our spiritual focus clear and our relationship with God direct. In times of trouble, we turn to God Himself, not to intermediaries. It prevents superstition and the multiplication of spiritual obligations. Practically, it means we can speak to God anywhere, anytime, without rituals to access Him through other beings. It also protects us from spiritual exploitation—no person can claim to be a necessary intermediary between us and God.

### Principle 6: Prophecy

**Hebrew:** אֲנִי מַאֲמִין בֶּאֱמוּנָה שְׁלֵמָה, שֶׁכָּל דִּבְרֵי נְבִיאִים אֱמֶת

**Transliteration:** Ani ma'amin be'emunah sheleimah, she'kol divrei nevi'im emet

**Translation:** I believe with perfect faith that all the words of the prophets are true.

**Explanation:**

God communicates with humanity through prophecy—direct Divine revelation to chosen individuals. The prophets didn't invent their messages or rely on their own wisdom; they received authentic communication from God and transmitted it faithfully.

Rambam understood prophecy as a natural overflow that occurs when a person perfects their intellect and moral character to such a degree that they become a suitable vessel for Divine communication. Prophecy requires:
- Intellectual perfection (deep wisdom and understanding)
- Moral perfection (exemplary character and conduct)
- Imaginative perfection (ability to receive and convey Divine visions)
- Divine will (ultimately, God chooses whom to address)

The Hebrew Bible records the messages of dozens of prophets, from Abraham and Moses through the later prophets like Isaiah, Jeremiah, and Malachi. Their prophecies contained moral teachings, predictions of future events, calls to repentance, and revelations about God's will.

This principle affirms that authentic prophecy existed, that the biblical prophets genuinely experienced Divine communication, and that their recorded words are true and authoritative. It stands against those who dismiss prophecy as delusion, hallucination, or clever invention.

**Scriptural Basis:**

"The LORD God does nothing without revealing His counsel to His servants the prophets" (Amos 3:7). The Torah repeatedly introduces prophetic messages with "Thus says the LORD" or "The word of the LORD came to [prophet's name]."

**Daily Application:**

Recognizing the truth of prophecy means taking seriously the moral teachings and insights of the prophets. Their words—calling for justice, mercy, humility, and faithfulness—remain relevant guidance for living righteously. When we read the prophetic books, we're not reading ancient literature but accessing Divine wisdom. This principle also reminds us that God desires communication with humanity; He doesn't remain distant and silent but reaches out to guide us. While prophecy as experienced by the biblical prophets has ceased (according to tradition, ending around the time of Malachi), the prophetic vision of a just, peaceful world remains our mandate.

### Principle 7: Moses's Prophecy is Supreme

**Hebrew:** אֲנִי מַאֲמִין בֶּאֱמוּנָה שְׁלֵמָה, שֶׁנְּבוּאַת מֹשֶׁה רַבֵּנוּ עָלָיו הַשָּׁלוֹם הָיְתָה אֲמִתִּית

**Transliteration:** Ani ma'amin be'emunah sheleimah, she'nevuat Moshe Rabbeinu alav hashalom hayetah amitit

**Translation:** I believe with perfect faith that the prophecy of Moses our teacher, peace be upon him, was true.

**Explanation:**

While all biblical prophets received authentic Divine communication, Moses's prophecy was unique and supreme. The Torah itself distinguishes Moses from all other prophets: "Never again did there arise in Israel a prophet like Moses, whom the LORD knew face to face" (Deuteronomy 34:10).

Moses's prophecy differed from all others in four crucial ways:

1. **Direct communication** - Other prophets received visions and dreams; Moses spoke with God "face to face" in direct, clear communication
2. **No intermediary** - Angels delivered messages to other prophets; God spoke directly to Moses
3. **Conscious awareness** - Other prophets often received prophecy in trance-like states; Moses remained fully conscious and aware
4. **At will** - Other prophets waited for prophecy to come; Moses could initiate prophetic communication whenever necessary

Because of Moses's unique prophetic level, the Torah he transmitted has absolute authority. No other prophet can contradict or alter Moses's Torah. Future prophets might apply the Torah to new situations, but they cannot change its fundamental teachings or commandments.

This principle establishes the Torah's reliability. Since Moses achieved the highest possible level of prophecy and transmitted the Torah with perfect accuracy, we can trust that the Torah represents God's authentic will.

**Scriptural Basis:**

"The LORD would speak to Moses face to face, as a man speaks to his friend" (Exodus 33:11). "With him I speak mouth to mouth, in a vision and not in riddles, and he beholds the likeness of the LORD" (Numbers 12:8).

**Daily Application:**

This principle gives us confidence in the Torah's teachings. When we study Torah, we're accessing the most reliable Divine communication possible. It also teaches humility—even the greatest later prophets and sages acknowledged Moses's supreme authority. In religious matters, the Torah remains the foundational standard against which all claims and innovations are measured. This prevents spiritual drift and ensures continuity across generations. When we face moral questions, we can turn to Moses's Torah knowing we're consulting the highest revelation.

### Principle 8: Torah's Divine Origin

**Hebrew:** אֲנִי מַאֲמִין בֶּאֱמוּנָה שְׁלֵמָה, שֶׁכָּל הַתּוֹרָה הַמְצוּיָה עַתָּה בְּיָדֵינוּ הִיא הַנְּתוּנָה לְמֹשֶׁה רַבֵּנוּ עָלָיו הַשָּׁלוֹם

**Transliteration:** Ani ma'amin be'emunah sheleimah, she'kol haTorah hametzuyah atah beyadenu hi hanetunah l'Moshe Rabbeinu alav hashalom

**Translation:** I believe with perfect faith that the entire Torah now in our hands is the same that was given to Moses our teacher, peace be upon him.

**Explanation:**

This principle affirms that the Torah—specifically the Five Books of Moses (Pentateuch)—is of Divine origin. Every word, indeed every letter, was communicated by God to Moses. The Torah we possess today is identical to what Moses received and recorded.

This belief encompasses several claims:

1. **Divine authorship** - God is the ultimate author; Moses was the scribe
2. **Complete accuracy** - The entire text, from Genesis through Deuteronomy, is God's word
3. **Perfect transmission** - The text has been preserved without error through generations
4. **Uniform sanctity** - No passage is more or less holy than another; all are equally Divine

Traditional Judaism holds that Moses wrote the Torah at God's dictation (with the possible exception of the last eight verses describing his death, which Joshua may have written). This includes not only the laws and commandments but also the narratives—stories of Creation, the patriarchs, the Exodus, and the wilderness wanderings.

This principle has been challenged by modern biblical criticism, which proposes that the Torah was composed by multiple human authors over centuries. Rambam's principle rejects such theories, maintaining that the Torah's unity and Divine origin are fundamental to Jewish faith.

**Scriptural Basis:**

"Moses wrote this Torah and gave it to the priests" (Deuteronomy 31:9). The Torah repeatedly states "The LORD spoke to Moses, saying..." and "Moses wrote down all the words of the LORD" (Exodus 24:4).

**Daily Application:**

Believing in the Torah's Divine origin means approaching it with reverence and expecting to find profound wisdom in every detail. When studying Torah, we're not analyzing an ancient human document but encountering God's eternal wisdom. This transforms study from academic exercise to spiritual practice. It also means we take the Torah's commandments seriously—they're not human customs or antiquated rules but Divine instructions for living. Even difficult passages demand careful interpretation rather than dismissal, because we trust that behind every word lies Divine wisdom.

### Principle 9: Torah's Immutability

**Hebrew:** אֲנִי מַאֲמִין בֶּאֱמוּנָה שְׁלֵמָה, שֶׁזֹּאת הַתּוֹרָה לֹא תְהֵא מֻחְלֶפֶת

**Transliteration:** Ani ma'amin be'emunah sheleimah, she'zot haTorah lo tehei muchlefet

**Translation:** I believe with perfect faith that this Torah will not be changed.

**Explanation:**

The Torah is eternal and unchanging. It will never be replaced by another Torah, and its commandments will never be abolished or substituted. While God could theoretically give a new Torah, this principle affirms that He will not do so.

This principle has several important implications:

1. **No new Torah** - No prophet can introduce a "new testament" or "replacement covenant"
2. **No abrogation** - Commandments remain in force forever (with the exception of those that applied only to specific historical circumstances, like Temple rituals when there is no Temple)
3. **Eternal relevance** - The Torah's teachings remain applicable in every generation
4. **Consistency of Divine will** - God's instructions don't change based on circumstances or eras

This principle particularly addresses claims by other religions that their scriptures supersede or replace the Torah. From a Jewish perspective, God's covenant with Israel and the commandments given through Moses remain eternally binding.

Rambam noted that even the Messiah, when he comes, will not alter the Torah or introduce new commandments. The Messianic era will be characterized by perfect observance of the existing Torah, not by its replacement.

Some philosophical debate exists about whether this immutability is absolute or whether God *could* theoretically change the Torah but has promised not to. Most traditional authorities maintain that God's eternal nature means His will, expressed in Torah, is itself eternal and unchanging.

**Scriptural Basis:**

"Everything I command you, you shall be careful to do; you shall not add to it or take away from it" (Deuteronomy 13:1). "The grass withers, the flower fades, but the word of our God endures forever" (Isaiah 40:8).

**Daily Application:**

This principle gives stability and continuity to Jewish practice. We need not worry that commandments will become obsolete or that new prophets will overturn our traditions. It creates confidence for raising children in Jewish values—what we teach them will remain true for their children and grandchildren. It also means we can study ancient texts and find contemporary relevance; the wisdom of the Torah doesn't age. In times of rapid change, this principle provides an anchor, reminding us that while societies transform, fundamental moral truths remain constant.

### Principle 10: God Knows Human Actions

**Hebrew:** אֲנִי מַאֲמִין בֶּאֱמוּנָה שְׁלֵמָה, שֶׁהַבּוֹרֵא יִתְבָּרַךְ שְׁמוֹ יוֹדֵעַ כָּל מַעֲשֵׂה בְנֵי אָדָם וְכָל מַחְשְׁבוֹתָם

**Transliteration:** Ani ma'amin be'emunah sheleimah, she'haBoreh yitbarach shemo yode'a kol ma'aseh bnei adam v'chol machshevotam

**Translation:** I believe with perfect faith that the Creator, blessed be His name, knows all the deeds of human beings and all their thoughts.

**Explanation:**

God possesses complete knowledge of everything that occurs in creation, including every human action and thought. Nothing is hidden from Divine awareness. This principle affirms God's omniscience—His unlimited, perfect knowledge.

This creates a profound theological question: If God knows everything, including our future choices, do we truly have free will? This paradox has occupied Jewish philosophers for centuries. Rambam's resolution was that God's knowledge operates differently from human knowledge. God exists outside time, so His "foreknowledge" isn't really "fore-" at all—He sees all of time simultaneously. From our temporal perspective, we genuinely choose; from God's eternal perspective, our choices are known. Both are true from their respective frames of reference.

This principle has several practical implications:

1. **Moral accountability** - Every action matters because God observes all
2. **No hiding** - Even private thoughts and secret actions are known
3. **Divine providence** - God's involvement in the world is informed and intentional
4. **Justice assurance** - God's knowledge ensures perfect justice; nothing escapes His notice

The knowledge that God knows our thoughts should inspire honesty and integrity even in private. It should also provide comfort—when we're misunderstood or falsely accused, God knows the truth.

**Scriptural Basis:**

"The eyes of the LORD are in every place, observing the evil and the good" (Proverbs 15:3). "Even before a word is on my tongue, behold, O LORD, You know it altogether" (Psalms 139:4). "I the LORD search the heart and test the mind" (Jeremiah 17:10).

**Daily Application:**

Living with awareness of God's knowledge transforms behavior. We strive for integrity not only in public but in private, knowing that God observes all. This prevents hypocrisy—acting righteous publicly while behaving differently when alone. It also offers comfort when we're struggling internally; God knows our struggles, our intentions, our hearts. We don't need to explain ourselves to Him; He already understands. In prayer, this means we can be completely honest—God knows our true thoughts anyway, so authentic expression strengthens rather than threatens our relationship with Him.

### Principle 11: Reward and Punishment

**Hebrew:** אֲנִי מַאֲמִין בֶּאֱמוּנָה שְׁלֵמָה, שֶׁהַבּוֹרֵא יִתְבָּרַךְ שְׁמוֹ גּוֹמֵל טוֹב לְשׁוֹמְרֵי מִצְוֹתָיו, וּמֵשִׂים עֹנֶשׁ לְעוֹבְרֵי מִצְוֹתָיו

**Transliteration:** Ani ma'amin be'emunah sheleimah, she'haBoreh yitbarach shemo gomeil tov l'shomrei mitzvotav, u'maisim onesh l'overei mitzvotav

**Translation:** I believe with perfect faith that the Creator, blessed be His name, rewards those who keep His commandments and punishes those who transgress them.

**Explanation:**

The universe operates according to moral justice. Righteous actions receive reward, and transgressions receive punishment. This principle doesn't necessarily mean immediate, obvious consequences (the righteous sometimes suffer and the wicked sometimes prosper in this world), but it affirms that ultimate justice prevails.

Jewish tradition understands reward and punishment operating on multiple levels:

1. **Natural consequences** - Many mitzvot have built-in benefits (honesty builds trust, charity creates community)
2. **This-worldly** - Some rewards and punishments occur in our lifetime
3. **Collective** - Nations and communities experience consequences for their moral character
4. **The World to Come** - Ultimate reward and punishment occur in the afterlife (*Olam Haba*)

The Talmud teaches that the primary reward for mitzvot is in the World to Come, where spiritual reality is fully experienced. In this world, the righteous may face suffering as a test, refinement, or to increase their ultimate reward. Similarly, the wicked may prosper temporarily, receiving their limited reward in this world while forfeiting eternal reward.

This principle doesn't reduce religious observance to a transaction ("I'll obey if You reward me"). Rather, it affirms that the universe is just—that morality matters cosmically, not merely socially. We obey God out of love and duty, trusting that His justice ensures meaningful consequences for our choices.

**Scriptural Basis:**

"If you walk in My statutes and keep My commandments and do them, then I will give you rains in their season" (Leviticus 26:3-4). "The soul that sins shall die... but if a man is righteous and does what is just and right... he shall surely live" (Ezekiel 18:4-9).

**Daily Application:**

This principle motivates righteous behavior even when inconvenient or costly. Knowing that God sees and judges fairly encourages persistence in doing good even without immediate visible results. It also provides comfort when facing injustice—ultimate accountability exists, even if not immediately apparent. However, we must balance this with serving God out of love, not merely fear of punishment or desire for reward. As the Mishna teaches: "Do not be like servants who serve their master for the sake of receiving reward; rather, be like servants who serve their master not for the sake of receiving reward" (Avot 1:3).

### Principle 12: The Coming of the Messiah

**Hebrew:** אֲנִי מַאֲמִין בֶּאֱמוּנָה שְׁלֵמָה בְּבִיאַת הַמָּשִׁיחַ, וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁיִּתְמַהְמֵהַּ, עִם כָּל זֶה אֲחַכֶּה לּוֹ בְּכָל יוֹם שֶׁיָּבוֹא

**Transliteration:** Ani ma'amin be'emunah sheleimah be'viat haMashiach, v'af al pi she'yitmameha, im kol zeh achakeh lo b'chol yom she'yavo

**Translation:** I believe with perfect faith in the coming of the Messiah, and even though he may delay, nevertheless I await him every day.

**Explanation:**

A descendant of King David will arise to restore Jewish sovereignty, rebuild the Temple, gather the exiles, and usher in an era of universal peace and Divine knowledge. This figure, the Messiah (*Mashiach*, meaning "anointed one"), will be a human being—not divine or supernatural—who accomplishes these tasks through wisdom, leadership, and Divine assistance.

The Messianic era will be characterized by:
- End of war and oppression
- Universal recognition of God
- Return of all Jews to the Land of Israel
- Rebuilding of the Holy Temple in Jerusalem
- Renewed prophecy
- Prosperity and peace
- Universal study of Torah

Importantly, Jewish messianism is this-worldly, not purely spiritual. The Messiah will bring tangible political, social, and physical redemption. However, human nature will remain essentially unchanged—people will still have free will and must still choose good over evil.

The phrase "even though he may delay" acknowledges the long wait. Jews have anticipated the Messiah for millennia. Yet this principle demands that hope remain alive. Every day, we should live as if the Messiah might arrive that very day, maintaining readiness for redemption.

False messiahs have appeared throughout history, causing disappointment and sometimes catastrophe. Rambam therefore established clear criteria: the true Messiah must succeed in specific tasks (rebuilding the Temple, gathering exiles, etc.). Anyone who fails or dies before completing these tasks was not the Messiah.

**Scriptural Basis:**

"A shoot shall grow from the stump of Jesse... and he shall judge the poor with righteousness" (Isaiah 11:1-4). "I will raise up for David a righteous branch, and he shall reign as king and deal wisely" (Jeremiah 23:5). "They shall beat their swords into plowshares... nation shall not lift up sword against nation" (Isaiah 2:4).

**Daily Application:**

Believing in the Messiah means maintaining hope even in dark times. It prevents despair, reminding us that history has a positive trajectory and purpose. Practically, it means we should work toward the Messianic vision—pursuing peace, justice, and knowledge—as if we're preparing the world for his arrival. When we act charitably, study Torah, and promote understanding, we're helping bring the Messianic age closer. This belief also provides perspective on temporary setbacks; we're part of a long story that will ultimately resolve positively.

### Principle 13: Resurrection of the Dead

**Hebrew:** אֲנִי מַאֲמִין בֶּאֱמוּנָה שְׁלֵמָה שֶׁתִּהְיֶה תְּחִיַּת הַמֵּתִים בְּעֵת שֶׁיַּעֲלֶה רָצוֹן מֵאֵת הַבּוֹרֵא יִתְבָּרַךְ שְׁמוֹ וְיִתְעַלֶּה זִכְרוֹ לָעַד וּלְנֵצַח נְצָחִים

**Transliteration:** Ani ma'amin be'emunah sheleimah she'tiheyeh techiyat hametim b'et she'ya'aleh ratzon mei'et haBoreh yitbarach shemo v'yit'aleh zichro la'ad ul'netzach netzachim

**Translation:** I believe with perfect faith that there will be resurrection of the dead at the time when it will be the Creator's will, blessed be His name and exalted be His remembrance forever and ever.

**Explanation:**

At some future time determined by God, the dead will be brought back to life. This bodily resurrection (*techiyat hametim*) represents the ultimate affirmation of the body's value and the completeness of Divine redemption.

Resurrection differs from the immortality of the soul (which Judaism also affirms). The soul's survival after death means consciousness continues in a spiritual realm (*Olam Haba*, the World to Come). Resurrection means that at a future time, souls will be reunited with physical bodies, and people will live again in a perfected physical world.

Several aspects of this principle:

1. **Bodily resurrection** - Not merely spiritual immortality but physical restoration
2. **Divine timing** - God will determine when this occurs
3. **Complete redemption** - Affirms that the physical world is good and will be perfected
4. **Justice fulfilled** - Allows the righteous who suffered in life to experience ultimate reward bodily

The exact details of resurrection remain mysterious. Will everyone be resurrected, or only the righteous? At what age will people return? Will they eat, marry, and eventually die again, or will it be a transformed physical existence? Different sages have offered various opinions. What's essential is the core belief: God will restore life to the dead.

This principle affirms God's absolute power over life and death and the ultimate value of physical existence. The body is not a prison for the soul but a partner in serving God. The goal isn't to escape physicality but to perfect and sanctify it.

**Scriptural Basis:**

"Your dead shall live; their bodies shall rise" (Isaiah 26:19). "Many who sleep in the dust of the earth shall awake, some to eternal life and some to shame and eternal contempt" (Daniel 12:2). Ezekiel's vision of the dry bones coming back to life (Ezekiel 37) is understood as a prophecy of resurrection.

**Daily Application:**

Belief in resurrection affirms that life has ultimate meaning and purpose. Our actions in this physical world matter eternally because this world and our bodies are not temporary illusions but sacred realities that will be restored and perfected. It also provides comfort in the face of death—our loved ones are not lost forever; we will be reunited. This belief encourages us to care for our bodies (they're sacred vessels) while not becoming obsessed with physical perfection (since ultimate bodily existence transcends current limitations). It teaches that the culmination of human destiny includes both spiritual and physical dimensions.

## The Ani Ma'amin Prayer

The **Ani Ma'amin** ("I believe") is a liturgical poem that distills each of the Thirteen Principles into a single sentence beginning with the phrase "I believe with perfect faith..." These declarations became particularly significant during the Holocaust, when Jews recited them even on their way to death camps, affirming their faith in the face of unspeakable evil.

The Ani Ma'amin is not recited daily in most liturgies but is found in prayer books and is studied as a summary of essential Jewish beliefs. Each line corresponds to one of Rambam's principles, making the complex philosophical concepts accessible through simple declaration.

The historical power of the Ani Ma'amin lies in its transformation of abstract theology into personal testimony. Rather than third-person descriptions ("God is one"), it uses first-person declaration ("I believe that God is one"), making faith active and personal.

---

## The Yigdal Hymn

**Yigdal** ("May He be magnified") is a liturgical poem composed in the 13th or 14th century by Daniel ben Judah Dayyan of Rome. It poetically expresses all Thirteen Principles in thirteen verses, each corresponding to one principle.

Unlike the simple declarations of Ani Ma'amin, Yigdal uses poetic Hebrew with sophisticated rhyme and meter. It is traditionally sung at the conclusion of Shabbat and holiday evening services and at the beginning of morning services in many communities.

The opening verse sets the tone:

**יִגְדַּל אֱלֹהִים חַי וְיִשְׁתַּבַּח, נִמְצָא וְאֵין עֵת אֶל מְצִיאוּתוֹ**

*Yigdal Elohim chai v'yishtabach, nimtza v'ein et el metzi'uto*

"Exalted be the Living God and praised; He exists, and His existence has no time."

This verse combines Principles 1 (God's existence), 3 (incorporeality), and 4 (eternity) in beautiful poetic form.

Yigdal has become beloved in Jewish communities worldwide, with different musical traditions developing various melodies. Its role in the liturgy makes the Thirteen Principles part of regular worship, ensuring these foundational beliefs remain in Jewish consciousness.

## Connections to Chinese Philosophy

For readers familiar with Chinese philosophical traditions, several interesting parallels and contrasts emerge:

### The Dao and God's Unity

The concept of God's absolute unity resonates with Daoist teaching about the Dao as the singular source and principle of reality. However, while the Dao is often described as impersonal and beyond attributes, the Jewish God is personal and relational, entering into covenant with humanity.

### Heaven's Mandate and Divine Providence

The principle that God guides all creation parallels the Chinese concept of "Tian" (天, Heaven) overseeing human affairs. The "Mandate of Heaven" (tianming) and Jewish Divine providence both affirm that ultimate authority rests with a transcendent reality that holds leaders and nations accountable.

### Filial Piety and Honoring Torah

The Confucian emphasis on filial piety and respect for tradition finds a parallel in the Jewish reverence for Torah and the chain of transmission from Moses through the generations. Both traditions emphasize that wisdom is received and transmitted, not merely invented.

### Virtue and Mitzvot

The Confucian cultivation of virtues like *ren* (仁, benevolence) and *yi* (義, righteousness) shares common ground with the Jewish practice of mitzvot aimed at character development. Both traditions believe moral perfection is achieved through practice, not merely intellectual assent.

### The Messianic Age and Great Harmony

The Jewish vision of the Messianic era—universal peace, prosperity, and knowledge of God—parallels the Confucian ideal of "Great Harmony" (*datong* 大同), where society achieves perfect order and well-being.

### Key Differences

Where the traditions diverge significantly:
- **Personal vs. Impersonal Ultimate** - Judaism's God is intensely personal; Chinese philosophy often describes ultimate reality in impersonal terms
- **Revelation vs. Discovery** - Judaism emphasizes revealed truth through prophecy; Chinese philosophy generally focuses on discovering truth through observation and reflection
- **Linear vs. Cyclical** - Jewish thought sees history as linear, moving toward redemption; much Chinese thought sees cyclical patterns
- **Transcendence vs. Immanence** - Judaism emphasizes God's transcendence beyond creation; Chinese philosophy often emphasizes the ultimate as immanent within nature

## Living the Principles Today

These Thirteen Principles are not merely intellectual propositions to affirm but foundations for living. Each principle should shape how we think, feel, and act:

When we recognize **God as Creator** (Principle 1), we treat the world with respect as His handiwork and see other people as His creations, our equals in dignity.

When we affirm **God's unity** (Principle 2), we see ultimate purpose and meaning unifying all existence, preventing nihilism and despair.

When we acknowledge **God's incorporeality** (Principle 3), we resist idolatry in all its forms—whether worshiping physical objects, political leaders, or abstract ideologies.

When we grasp **God's eternity** (Principle 4), we gain perspective on our temporal struggles and confidence in His eternal promises.

When we commit to **worshiping God alone** (Principle 5), we maintain spiritual focus and avoid the anxiety of trying to please multiple demanding forces.

When we accept **prophecy's truth** (Principle 6), we take seriously the moral teachings and visions of the prophets.

When we recognize **Moses's supreme prophecy** (Principle 7) and **Torah's Divine origin** (Principle 8), we approach Torah study as encountering God's wisdom, not merely ancient literature.

When we affirm **Torah's immutability** (Principle 9), we have confidence in timeless moral truth amid changing circumstances.

When we remember **God knows our actions** (Principle 10), we maintain integrity in private as well as public.

When we trust in **Divine justice** (Principle 11), we persist in righteousness even without immediate reward and comfort ourselves when facing injustice.

When we await **the Messiah** (Principle 12), we maintain hope and work toward the Messianic vision of peace and justice.

When we believe in **resurrection** (Principle 13), we affirm that physical existence matters, our choices have eternal significance, and death is not the end.

## Conclusion

The Thirteen Principles of Faith represent Rambam's attempt to articulate the essential beliefs distinguishing authentic Jewish faith. While not without controversy in their time (some sages questioned whether Judaism needed a formal creed), these principles have become deeply embedded in Jewish consciousness through liturgy, study, and communal memory.

They have sustained Jewish faith through centuries of persecution, exile, and challenge. During the Holocaust, Jews went to their deaths declaring these principles. In times of prosperity, they prevented spiritual complacency. In intellectual contexts, they provided a framework for sophisticated theological reflection.

Today, these principles continue to offer clarity about Jewish belief, connecting contemporary Jews to millennia of tradition while addressing timeless questions about God, revelation, morality, and human destiny. They remind us that Judaism is not merely a cultural identity or set of rituals but a comprehensive worldview grounded in fundamental convictions about reality, meaning, and purpose.

Whether recited in prayer, studied in depth, or simply reflected upon, the Thirteen Principles invite us to consider what we believe, why we believe it, and how our beliefs should shape our lives. In doing so, they fulfill Rambam's vision of providing intellectual clarity, spiritual strength, and communal unity for the Jewish people across all generations.
