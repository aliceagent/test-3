## Introduction

Imagine a day each week when all your devices are turned off, your work is set aside, and your entire focus shifts to rest, family, study, and connection with God. For over three thousand years — since the time corresponding to China's Shang Dynasty (商朝, ~1600-1046 BCE) — Jews have observed such a day: **Shabbat** (שַׁבָּת), the Sabbath.

Shabbat is not merely a day off from work. It is a weekly sanctuary in time, a taste of the World to Come, and one of Judaism's most transformative gifts to the world. From Friday evening at sunset until Saturday night when three stars appear, observant Jews refrain from creative labor, kindle candles, share festive meals, pray, study Torah, and spend unhurried time with family and community.

For someone exploring Judaism — especially in modern China, where the pace of life can be relentless — Shabbat offers something radical: permission to stop. Not because you've finished everything, but because holiness has a schedule, and rest is a divine commandment.

This comprehensive guide will walk you through everything you need to know about Shabbat: its theological meaning, how to prepare for it, what happens during its 25 hours, and how Chinese families can adapt this ancient tradition to their own context.

## What is Shabbat? — Theological Meaning

### The Connection to Creation

Shabbat is rooted in the very fabric of creation. The Torah (Genesis 2:1-3) tells us:

> "The heavens and the earth were completed, and all their hosts. On the seventh day God completed His work which He had done, and He rested on the seventh day from all His work which He had done. And God blessed the seventh day and made it holy, because on it He rested from all His work which He had created to do."

God did not rest because He was tired — the Creator of the universe does not grow weary. Rather, God's "rest" means that He **ceased creating**. He stepped back, looked at the world He had made, and declared it complete. By resting on the seventh day, God established a pattern: creation requires both action and cessation, both work and rest.

When we observe Shabbat, we imitate God. We testify that just as God created the world in six days and rested on the seventh, we too acknowledge that ultimate creative power belongs to God alone. Our rest is an act of faith.

### The Fourth of the Ten Commandments

Shabbat appears twice in the Ten Commandments, with slightly different wording. In Exodus 20:8-11, the commandment reads:

> "**Remember** the Sabbath day to keep it holy. Six days you shall labor and do all your work, but the seventh day is a Sabbath to the Lord your God... For in six days the Lord made the heavens and the earth, the sea and all that is in them, and He rested on the seventh day."

In Deuteronomy 5:12-15, it says:

> "**Observe** the Sabbath day to keep it holy... Remember that you were slaves in the land of Egypt, and the Lord your God brought you out from there with a mighty hand and an outstretched arm; therefore the Lord your God commanded you to observe the Sabbat day."

The dual language — "remember" and "observe" — teaches us that Shabbat has both a spiritual dimension (remembrance of creation) and a practical one (actually refraining from work). The second version adds another layer: Shabbat is also about freedom. Slaves cannot choose when to rest. Free people can.

### A Sign Between God and Israel

The Torah describes Shabbat as an **eternal sign** (ot olam) between God and the Jewish people (Exodus 31:16-17):

> "The children of Israel shall keep the Sabbath, to observe the Sabbath throughout their generations as an everlasting covenant. It is a sign between Me and the children of Israel forever, that in six days the Lord made the heavens and the earth, and on the seventh day He ceased and rested."

This "sign" functions like a wedding ring. It is visible, recurring, and identifies who we are. Each week, by observing Shabbat, we renew our covenant with God.

## Preparing for Shabbat

Shabbat doesn't just happen — it requires preparation, both physical and spiritual. The transformation from the ordinary weekday to the sacred day of rest begins long before sunset on Friday.

### Cleaning and Organizing the Home

Many Jewish families spend Thursday evening and all day Friday cleaning and organizing their homes. This isn't ordinary housekeeping — it's an act of honor, like preparing for royalty to visit. The Talmud (Shabbat 119a) teaches that Shabbat should be greeted as a queen or a bride entering your home.

**Chinese parallel:** Just as families in China thoroughly clean their homes before Chinese New Year (春节) to sweep away bad luck and welcome good fortune, Jewish families prepare their homes to welcome the sacred "Shabbat Queen."

Common preparations include:
- Sweeping, mopping, and tidying all rooms
- Changing to fresh bed linens and towels
- Polishing candlesticks and the kiddush cup
- Setting the table with a white tablecloth, nice dishes, and flowers

### Cooking Shabbat Meals

Since cooking is forbidden on Shabbat itself, all food must be prepared in advance. This includes:
- **Friday night dinner:** Usually the most elaborate meal, featuring challah (braided bread), fish, soup, chicken or meat, side dishes, and dessert
- **Shabbat lunch:** Another full meal, often featuring cholent (a slow-cooked stew that sits on a warming plate overnight)
- **Seudah shlishit:** A third, lighter meal eaten late Saturday afternoon

Traditional Ashkenazi Shabbat dishes include gefilte fish, chicken soup with matzo balls, roasted chicken, and kugel (noodle or potato casserole). Sephardic traditions might feature spiced fish, couscous, and slow-cooked stews like dafina or hamin.

**For Chinese families:** Many families adapt Shabbat meals to include familiar flavors — braised meats, stir-fried vegetables (prepared Friday), steamed fish, and rice. The key is preparing everything before Shabbat begins.

### Candle Lighting Times

Shabbat begins at sunset on Friday, but the tradition is to **light candles 18 minutes before sunset**. This ensures that we welcome Shabbat while it is still daylight and do not accidentally violate the prohibition on kindling fire after Shabbat has begun.

Candle lighting times vary by location and season. You can find exact times for your city at:
- [Chabad.org Candle Lighting Times](https://www.chabad.org/calendar/candlelighting_cdo/aid/6226/jewish/Candle-Lighting-Times.htm)
- [MyZmanim](https://www.myzmanim.com/)
- [Hebcal](https://www.hebcal.com/)

For example:
- **Beijing in summer:** Candles might be lit around 7:30 PM
- **Beijing in winter:** Candles might be lit around 4:45 PM

## Friday Night: Welcoming the Shabbat Queen

### 1. Candle Lighting (הַדְלָקַת נֵרוֹת, *Hadlakat Nerot*)

Traditionally, the woman of the household lights at least two Shabbat candles, though many light one candle for each family member. The candles represent the dual language of the Fourth Commandment: "Remember" and "Observe."

**How to light Shabbat candles:**

1. Light the candles
2. Circle your hands around the flames three times, drawing the light toward yourself
3. Cover your eyes with your hands
4. Recite the blessing:

**בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה ה׳ אֱלֹהֵינוּ מֶלֶךְ הָעוֹלָם, אֲשֶׁר קִדְּשָׁנוּ בְּמִצְוֹתָיו וְצִוָּנוּ לְהַדְלִיק נֵר שֶׁל שַׁבָּת.**

*Baruch atah Adonai, Eloheinu melech ha'olam, asher kid'shanu b'mitzvotav v'tzivanu l'hadlik ner shel Shabbat.*

"Blessed are You, Lord our God, King of the universe, who has sanctified us with His commandments and commanded us to kindle the Shabbat light."

4. Uncover your eyes and gaze at the candles
5. This is a special moment for personal prayer — many women offer private prayers for their families and loved ones

**Why cover the eyes?** Normally, blessings are recited *before* performing a mitzvah. But once the candles are lit, Shabbat has begun for the woman lighting them, and she can no longer kindle fire. The solution: light first, cover the eyes (so she hasn't yet "seen" or benefited from the light), recite the blessing, and then uncover the eyes. In this way, the blessing still precedes the fulfillment of the mitzvah.

Once the candles are lit, Shabbat has officially begun for the household.

### 2. Shalom Aleichem — Greeting the Sabbath Angels

When the family returns from synagogue (or at home if not attending synagogue), they sing **Shalom Aleichem** (שָׁלוֹם עֲלֵיכֶם, "Peace be upon you"), a beautiful song welcoming the ministering angels who accompany each Jew home on Friday night.

**Full text and transliteration:**

**שָׁלוֹם עֲלֵיכֶם, מַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת, מַלְאֲכֵי עֶלְיוֹן,  
מִמֶּלֶךְ מַלְכֵי הַמְּלָכִים, הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא.**

*Shalom aleichem, malachei ha-shareit, malachei elyon,  
mi-melech malchei ha-melachim, ha-kadosh baruch hu.*

"Peace be upon you, ministering angels, angels of the Most High,  
from the King of Kings of Kings, the Holy One, blessed be He."

**בּוֹאֲכֶם לְשָׁלוֹם, מַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁלוֹם, מַלְאֲכֵי עֶלְיוֹן,  
מִמֶּלֶךְ מַלְכֵי הַמְּלָכִים, הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא.**

*Bo'achem l'shalom, malachei ha-shalom, malachei elyon,  
mi-melech malchei ha-melachim, ha-kadosh baruch hu.*

"May your coming be in peace, angels of peace, angels of the Most High,  
from the King of Kings of Kings, the Holy One, blessed be He."

**בָּרְכוּנִי לְשָׁלוֹם, מַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁלוֹם, מַלְאֲכֵי עֶלְיוֹן,  
מִמֶּלֶךְ מַלְכֵי הַמְּלָכִים, הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא.**

*Barchuni l'shalom, malachei ha-shalom, malachei elyon,  
mi-melech malchei ha-melachim, ha-kadosh baruch hu.*

"Bless me with peace, angels of peace, angels of the Most High,  
from the King of Kings of Kings, the Holy One, blessed be He."

**צֵאתְכֶם לְשָׁלוֹם, מַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁלוֹם, מַלְאֲכֵי עֶלְיוֹן,  
מִמֶּלֶךְ מַלְכֵי הַמְּלָכִים, הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא.**

*Tzeitchem l'shalom, malachei ha-shalom, malachei elyon,  
mi-melech malchei ha-melachim, ha-kadosh baruch hu.*

"May your departure be in peace, angels of peace, angels of the Most High,  
from the King of Kings of Kings, the Holy One, blessed be He."

### 3. Eishet Chayil — A Woman of Valor

Many families then recite or sing **Eishet Chayil** (אֵשֶׁת חַיִל, "A Woman of Valor"), the final 22 verses of Proverbs 31. This is traditionally sung by a husband to his wife, honoring her role in creating the Shabbat atmosphere.

**Key verses (abbreviated):**

**אֵשֶׁת חַיִל מִי יִמְצָא, וְרָחֹק מִפְּנִינִים מִכְרָהּ.**  
*Eishet chayil mi yimtza, v'rachok mi-pninim michrah.*  
"A woman of valor, who can find? Her worth is far beyond pearls."

**בָּטַח בָּהּ לֵב בַּעְלָהּ, וְשָׁלָל לֹא יֶחְסָר.**  
*Batach bah leiv ba'lah, v'shalal lo yechsar.*  
"Her husband's heart trusts in her, and he lacks no gain."

The passage continues, praising the woman's wisdom, kindness, industriousness, and fear of God.

### 4. Friday Night Kiddush (קִדּוּשׁ)

The centerpiece of Friday night is **Kiddush**, the blessing over wine that sanctifies the day. The head of the household (or anyone present) holds a full cup of wine or grape juice and recites the following:

**Full Kiddush text:**

*(Quietly:)*  
**וַיְהִי עֶרֶב וַיְהִי בֹקֶר, יוֹם הַשִּׁשִּׁי.**

*Vayehi erev vayehi voker, yom ha-shishi.*  
"And there was evening and there was morning, the sixth day."

*(Aloud:)*  
**וַיְכֻלּוּ הַשָּׁמַיִם וְהָאָרֶץ וְכָל צְבָאָם.  
וַיְכַל אֱלֹהִים בַּיּוֹם הַשְּׁבִיעִי מְלַאכְתּוֹ אֲשֶׁר עָשָׂה,  
וַיִּשְׁבֹּת בַּיּוֹם הַשְּׁבִיעִי מִכָּל מְלַאכְתּוֹ אֲשֶׁר עָשָׂה.  
וַיְבָרֶךְ אֱלֹהִים אֶת יוֹם הַשְּׁבִיעִי וַיְקַדֵּשׁ אֹתוֹ,  
כִּי בוֹ שָׁבַת מִכָּל מְלַאכְתּוֹ אֲשֶׁר בָּרָא אֱלֹהִים לַעֲשׂוֹת.**

*Vayechulu ha-shamayim v'ha-aretz v'chol tzeva'am.  
Vayechal Elohim bayom ha-shvi'i melachto asher asah,  
vayishbot bayom ha-shvi'i mikol melachto asher asah.  
Vayevarech Elohim et yom ha-shvi'i vayekadesh oto,  
ki vo shavat mikol melachto asher bara Elohim la'asot.*

"The heavens and the earth were completed, and all their hosts. On the seventh day God completed His work which He had done, and He rested on the seventh day from all His work which He had done. And God blessed the seventh day and made it holy, because on it He rested from all His work which He had created to do." (Genesis 2:1-3)

**בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה ה׳ אֱלֹהֵינוּ מֶלֶךְ הָעוֹלָם, בּוֹרֵא פְּרִי הַגָּפֶן.**

*Baruch atah Adonai, Eloheinu melech ha-olam, borei p'ri ha-gafen.*

"Blessed are You, Lord our God, King of the universe, who creates the fruit of the vine."

**בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה ה׳ אֱלֹהֵינוּ מֶלֶךְ הָעוֹלָם, אֲשֶׁר קִדְּשָׁנוּ בְּמִצְוֹתָיו וְרָצָה בָנוּ,  
וְשַׁבַּת קָדְשׁוֹ בְּאַהֲבָה וּבְרָצוֹן הִנְחִילָנוּ,  
זִכָּרוֹן לְמַעֲשֵׂה בְרֵאשִׁית.  
כִּי הוּא יוֹם תְּחִלָּה לְמִקְרָאֵי קֹדֶשׁ, זֵכֶר לִיצִיאַת מִצְרָיִם.  
כִּי בָנוּ בָחַרְתָּ וְאוֹתָנוּ קִדַּשְׁתָּ מִכָּל הָעַמִּים,  
וְשַׁבַּת קָדְשְׁךָ בְּאַהֲבָה וּבְרָצוֹן הִנְחַלְתָּנוּ.  
בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה ה׳, מְקַדֵּשׁ הַשַּׁבָּת.**

*Baruch atah Adonai, Eloheinu melech ha-olam, asher kid'shanu b'mitzvotav v'ratza vanu,  
v'Shabbat kodsho b'ahavah uv'ratzon hinchilanu,  
zikaron l'ma'aseh v'reishit.  
Ki hu yom t'chila l'mikra'ei kodesh, zecher litziyat Mitzrayim.  
Ki vanu vacharta v'otanu kidashta mikol ha'amim,  
v'Shabbat kodsh'cha b'ahava uv'ratzon hinchaltanu.  
Baruch atah Adonai, m'kadesh ha-Shabbat.*

"Blessed are You, Lord our God, King of the universe, who has sanctified us with His commandments and has been pleased with us, and His holy Sabbath, in love and favor, has He given us as a heritage, a memorial of the work of Creation. For it is the first among the holy festivals, a remembrance of the Exodus from Egypt. For You have chosen us and sanctified us from among all the nations, and Your holy Sabbath, in love and favor, You have given us as a heritage. Blessed are You, Lord, who sanctifies the Sabbath."

The leader drinks from the cup, and then pours a bit for everyone at the table to drink.

### 5. Washing Hands (נְטִילַת יָדַיִם, *Netilat Yadayim*)

Before eating bread, we ritually wash our hands. This is done by pouring water from a cup over each hand, alternating three times (right, left, right, left, right, left).

After washing, recite:

**בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה ה׳ אֱלֹהֵינוּ מֶלֶךְ הָעוֹלָם, אֲשֶׁר קִדְּשָׁנוּ בְּמִצְוֹתָיו וְצִוָּנוּ עַל נְטִילַת יָדָיִם.**

*Baruch atah Adonai, Eloheinu melech ha-olam, asher kid'shanu b'mitzvotav v'tzivanu al n'tilat yadayim.*

"Blessed are You, Lord our God, King of the universe, who has sanctified us with His commandments and commanded us concerning the washing of hands."

From this point until eating the challah, no talking is permitted.

### 6. HaMotzi — Blessing Over Challah

The head of the household uncovers the two challah loaves (representing the double portion of manna that fell in the desert on Fridays), holds them together, and recites:

**בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה ה׳ אֱלֹהֵינוּ מֶלֶךְ הָעוֹלָם, הַמּוֹצִיא לֶחֶם מִן הָאָרֶץ.**

*Baruch atah Adonai, Eloheinu melech ha-olam, ha-motzi lechem min ha-aretz.*

"Blessed are You, Lord our God, King of the universe, who brings forth bread from the earth."

The challah is then sliced or torn, dipped in salt (a reminder of the salt placed on sacrifices in the Temple), and distributed to everyone at the table.

**Why two loaves?** When the Israelites wandered in the desert after the Exodus from Egypt — during what corresponds to China's late Shang Dynasty (商朝晚期, ~13th century BCE) — God provided manna (miraculous bread) each morning. On Fridays, a double portion fell so that the people would not need to gather food on Shabbat. We commemorate this by having two loaves on our Shabbat table.

### 7. The Festive Meal

The Friday night meal is a time of joy, song, and connection. Families eat slowly, sing traditional Shabbat songs (*zemiros*), discuss the weekly Torah portion, and enjoy one another's company without the distractions of phones, work, or schedules.

Traditional *zemiros* include:
- **Yom Zeh L'Yisrael** ("This day is for Israel")
- **Mah Yedidut** ("How beloved is Your rest")
- **Yom Shabbaton** ("The day of rest")

**Chinese parallel:** Just as Chinese families gather for reunion dinners (团圆饭) during festivals, treating the meal as sacred family time, Shabbat meals create a weekly moment of togetherness and intentional presence.

## Shabbat Day: Morning, Afternoon, and Evening

### 1. Shabbat Morning Prayers

Shabbat morning services at the synagogue are longer and more elaborate than weekday prayers. The service includes:
- **Shacharit** (morning prayers)
- **Torah reading** (a portion from the Five Books of Moses, divided so that the entire Torah is read over the course of one year)
- **Haftarah** (a selection from the Prophets related to the weekly Torah portion)
- **Musaf** (additional prayers commemorating the special Temple sacrifices offered on Shabbat)

If attending synagogue is not possible, individuals can pray at home, though some prayers (like the public Torah reading) require a *minyan* (quorum of ten adult Jewish men in Orthodox tradition; ten adults in egalitarian communities).

### 2. Shabbat Lunch and Kiddush

After services, families return home for a second festive meal. This begins with **Kiddush**, a shorter blessing than the Friday night version:

**Full Shabbat day Kiddush:**

**וְשָׁמְרוּ בְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל אֶת הַשַּׁבָּת,  
לַעֲשׂוֹת אֶת הַשַּׁבָּת לְדֹרֹתָם בְּרִית עוֹלָם.  
בֵּינִי וּבֵין בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל אוֹת הִיא לְעֹלָם,  
כִּי שֵׁשֶׁת יָמִים עָשָׂה ה׳ אֶת הַשָּׁמַיִם וְאֶת הָאָרֶץ,  
וּבַיּוֹם הַשְּׁבִיעִי שָׁבַת וַיִּנָּפַשׁ.**

*V'shamru v'nei Yisrael et ha-Shabbat,  
la'asot et ha-Shabbat l'dorotam brit olam.  
Beini uvein b'nei Yisrael ot hi l'olam,  
ki sheishet yamim asah Adonai et ha-shamayim v'et ha-aretz,  
u'vayom ha-shvi'i shavat vayinafash.*

"The children of Israel shall keep the Sabbath, to observe the Sabbath throughout their generations as an everlasting covenant. It is a sign between Me and the children of Israel forever, that in six days the Lord made the heavens and the earth, and on the seventh day He ceased and rested." (Exodus 31:16-17)

**בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה ה׳ אֱלֹהֵינוּ מֶלֶךְ הָעוֹלָם, בּוֹרֵא פְּרִי הַגָּפֶן.**

*Baruch atah Adonai, Eloheinu melech ha-olam, borei p'ri ha-gafen.*

"Blessed are You, Lord our God, King of the universe, who creates the fruit of the vine."

Then proceed with hand-washing, HaMotzi over challah, and the meal.

### 3. What is "Melacha"? — Understanding Shabbat Prohibitions

The Torah commands us to "do no work" (melacha, מְלָאכָה) on Shabbat. But what exactly is "work"?

In Jewish law, melacha does not simply mean "effort" or "difficult tasks." Rather, it refers to **creative labor** — actions that demonstrate human mastery over the natural world, the kinds of activities involved in building the Tabernacle (Mishkan) in the desert.

The Talmud (Shabbat 73a) identifies **39 categories of creative work** that were used in constructing the Tabernacle. These are the 39 *melachot* (plural of melacha) forbidden on Shabbat.

### 4. The 39 Categories of Melacha — Overview

The 39 categories are grouped into broader themes:

#### **Making Bread (11 activities):**
1. Plowing
2. Sowing
3. Reaping
4. Binding sheaves
5. Threshing
6. Winnowing
7. Selecting/sorting
8. Grinding
9. Sifting
10. Kneading
11. Baking

#### **Making Cloth (13 activities):**
12. Shearing
13. Bleaching
14. Combing raw materials
15. Dyeing
16. Spinning
17. Warping
18. Making loops
19. Weaving
20. Separating threads
21. Tying a knot
22. Untying a knot
23. Sewing
24. Tearing

#### **Leatherwork and Writing (9 activities):**
25. Trapping/hunting
26. Slaughtering
27. Skinning
28. Tanning
29. Smoothing
30. Marking/measuring
31. Cutting to shape
32. Writing
33. Erasing

#### **Building and Creating (5 activities):**
34. Building
35. Demolishing
36. Kindling a fire
37. Extinguishing a fire
38. Finishing/completing an object
39. Carrying/transferring between domains

Each of these categories has evolved over millennia to include modern applications. For example:
- **Kindling a fire** includes turning on electronic devices, because completing an electrical circuit generates heat and light
- **Writing** includes typing, texting, or drawing
- **Building** includes assembling furniture or toys

The purpose of these prohibitions is not to make Shabbat burdensome, but to create a space where we step back from controlling, altering, or "fixing" the world. On Shabbat, we accept the world as it is and rest within it.

### 5. Practical Examples: What is Forbidden on Shabbat?

- **No cooking or baking** (but food cooked before Shabbat can be kept warm on a hotplate or *blech* — a metal sheet covering the stove burners)
- **No kindling fire or electricity** (no turning lights on/off, no using phones, computers, or electronic devices)
- **No writing or erasing** (including typing, texting, or drawing)
- **No driving** (operating machinery, igniting fuel)
- **No carrying in public domains** (unless within an *eruv*, a ritual boundary that encloses a neighborhood)
- **No monetary transactions** (no buying, selling, or handling money)
- **No gardening or agricultural work**

### 6. What is Permitted and Encouraged on Shabbat?

- **Eating, drinking, and enjoying festive meals**
- **Studying Torah**
- **Prayer and meditation**
- **Singing Shabbat songs**
- **Walking and enjoying nature** (within the limits of the *eruv* or private property)
- **Spending time with family and friends**
- **Resting, napping, and relaxing**
- **Discussing the Torah portion or spiritual matters**

The goal of Shabbat is not deprivation — it's joy, peace, and connection.

### 7. Seudah Shlishit — The Third Meal

Late Saturday afternoon, before sunset, families gather for **Seudah Shlishit** (סְעוּדָה שְׁלִישִׁית, "the third meal"), a lighter meal that often includes bread, salads, fish, or fruit.

This meal has a unique spiritual quality. The afternoon hours of Shabbat are considered a time of special divine favor, when prayers are particularly powerful. Many communities sing slow, soulful melodies during Seudah Shlishit, creating a contemplative atmosphere as the day draws to a close.

Some recite Psalm 23 before this meal:

**מִזְמוֹר לְדָוִד, ה׳ רֹעִי לֹא אֶחְסָר...**  
*Mizmor l'David, Adonai ro'i lo echsar...*  
"A Psalm of David. The Lord is my shepherd, I shall not want..."

## Havdalah: Ending Shabbat Beautifully

Shabbat concludes on Saturday night when **three medium-sized stars are visible in the sky** (approximately 42-72 minutes after sunset, depending on location and custom).

The ceremony marking the end of Shabbat is called **Havdalah** (הַבְדָּלָה, "separation"). It distinguishes between the sacred time of Shabbat and the ordinary time of the week ahead.

### What You'll Need for Havdalah:

1. **A cup of wine or grape juice**
2. **Sweet-smelling spices** (usually whole cloves, kept in a decorative spice box)
3. **A braided candle** with at least two wicks
4. **A plate** to catch wine drips and extinguish the candle

### Full Havdalah Ceremony:

**Step 1: Introductory verses** (from Isaiah and Psalms):

**הִנֵּה אֵל יְשׁוּעָתִי, אֶבְטַח וְלֹא אֶפְחָד...**  
*Hinei El yeshuati, evtach v'lo efchad...*

"Behold, God is my salvation; I will trust and not be afraid..."

**Step 2: Blessing over wine:**

**בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה ה׳ אֱלֹהֵינוּ מֶלֶךְ הָעוֹלָם, בּוֹרֵא פְּרִי הַגָּפֶן.**

*Baruch atah Adonai, Eloheinu melech ha-olam, borei p'ri ha-gafen.*

"Blessed are You, Lord our God, King of the universe, who creates the fruit of the vine."

(Do not drink yet.)

**Step 3: Blessing over spices:**

**בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה ה׳ אֱלֹהֵינוּ מֶלֶךְ הָעוֹלָם, בּוֹרֵא מִינֵי בְשָׂמִים.**

*Baruch atah Adonai, Eloheinu melech ha-olam, borei minei v'samim.*

"Blessed are You, Lord our God, King of the universe, who creates various kinds of spices."

(Pass the spice box around; everyone smells the spices.)

**Step 4: Blessing over fire:**

(Light the braided candle. Look at your fingernails in the candlelight.)

**בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה ה׳ אֱלֹהֵינוּ מֶלֶךְ הָעוֹלָם, בּוֹרֵא מְאוֹרֵי הָאֵשׁ.**

*Baruch atah Adonai, Eloheinu melech ha-olam, borei m'orei ha-esh.*

"Blessed are You, Lord our God, King of the universe, who creates the lights of fire."

**Step 5: The Havdalah blessing:**

**בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה ה׳ אֱלֹהֵינוּ מֶלֶךְ הָעוֹלָם,  
הַמַּבְדִּיל בֵּין קֹדֶשׁ לְחוֹל,  
בֵּין אוֹר לְחֹשֶׁךְ,  
בֵּין יִשְׂרָאֵל לָעַמִּים,  
בֵּין יוֹם הַשְּׁבִיעִי לְשֵׁשֶׁת יְמֵי הַמַּעֲשֶׂה.  
בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה ה׳, הַמַּבְדִּיל בֵּין קֹדֶשׁ לְחוֹל.**

*Baruch atah Adonai, Eloheinu melech ha-olam,  
ha-mavdil bein kodesh l'chol,  
bein or l'choshech,  
bein Yisrael la'amim,  
bein yom ha-shvi'i l'sheishet yemei ha-ma'aseh.  
Baruch atah Adonai, ha-mavdil bein kodesh l'chol.*

"Blessed are You, Lord our God, King of the universe,  
who distinguishes between the sacred and the mundane,  
between light and darkness,  
between Israel and the nations,  
between the seventh day and the six days of work.  
Blessed are You, Lord, who distinguishes between the sacred and the mundane."

**Step 6: Drink the wine and extinguish the candle**

The leader drinks most of the wine, then pours a little onto the plate. The candle is extinguished in the wine, producing a hissing sound that marks the official end of Shabbat.

Many people then dip their fingers in the remaining wine and touch their pockets or eyelids, symbolically carrying the blessing of Shabbat into the new week.

**Why spices?** The Talmud teaches that on Shabbat, every Jew receives an "extra soul" (*neshamah yeteirah*) — a heightened spiritual capacity. When Shabbat ends, this extra soul departs, and we feel a subtle sadness. The sweet fragrance revives and comforts us.

## Practical Guide for Beginners

### Starting Small

If you're new to Shabbat observance, you don't have to adopt everything at once. Here are some gentle starting points:

**Level 1: Light candles and have a special meal**
- Light candles 18 minutes before sunset Friday
- Prepare a nicer-than-usual dinner
- Turn off your phone for a few hours
- Say a simple blessing over bread and wine (even in English, if you don't know Hebrew yet)

**Level 2: Add structure and intentionality**
- Attend a Shabbat service (in person or online)
- Unplug from all electronics Friday night through Saturday night
- Invite friends or family to join you
- Read the weekly Torah portion

**Level 3: Full traditional observance**
- Refrain from all 39 categories of creative work
- Attend both Friday night and Saturday morning services
- Celebrate all three Shabbat meals
- Study Torah on Shabbat afternoon
- Perform Havdalah with all the traditional elements

### Common Beginner Questions

**Q: What if I live alone?**  
A: Many communities have Shabbat hospitality programs where you can be invited to someone's home for meals. You can also observe Shabbat beautifully on your own — lighting candles, making Kiddush, and spending the day in prayer, study, and rest.

**Q: What if I can't afford fancy Shabbat items?**  
A: You don't need expensive silver candlesticks or kiddush cups. Simple glass candleholders and any cup will do. Challah can be homemade or store-bought. The essence of Shabbat is not luxury, but intention and holiness.

**Q: What if my family isn't Jewish?**  
A: Many people observe Shabbat even when their families don't. You can explain it as a personal spiritual practice, similar to how someone might observe vegetarianism or daily meditation. Over time, your family may come to appreciate the peace and togetherness that Shabbat brings.

**Q: Can I use a timer to turn lights on and off?**  
A: Yes. Lights can be set on automatic timers before Shabbat begins. This allows you to have lights go on and off without violating the prohibition on kindling or extinguishing fire.

## Shabbat for Chinese Families: Adapting Traditions

### Cultural Bridges

Many aspects of Shabbat resonate deeply with traditional Chinese values:

**Family Togetherness (家庭团聚):**  
Just as Chinese culture emphasizes family meals during festivals, Shabbat creates a weekly reunion dinner without phones or distractions.

**Respect for Elders:**  
Jewish tradition includes blessing children on Friday night and honoring parents. This mirrors the Confucian value of filial piety (孝, *xiào*).

**Cyclical Time:**  
The Chinese lunar calendar and the Jewish weekly cycle both sanctify time, creating rhythm and meaning in life.

**Food as Love:**  
In both cultures, preparing and sharing elaborate meals is an expression of care, honor, and celebration.

### Adapting Shabbat Meals

Chinese-Jewish families often incorporate familiar flavors into Shabbat meals:
- **Braised soy sauce chicken** instead of roasted chicken
- **Steamed fish with ginger and scallions** (prepared Friday, kept warm)
- **Vegetable stir-fries** (cooked before Shabbat)
- **Rice or noodles** as accompaniments
- **Sweet red bean soup** or fruit for dessert

**Challah variations:** Some families braid challah in shapes reminiscent of Chinese knots (中国结), symbolizing unity and continuity.

### Language and Blessings

If you're more comfortable in Mandarin, you can recite blessings in Chinese while learning Hebrew. Many prayer books now include transliteration and translation. Over time, the Hebrew will become familiar.

What matters most is **intention and understanding**, not perfect pronunciation.

### Community in China

Jewish communities exist in major Chinese cities, including:
- **Shanghai:** Chabad of Shanghai, Kehillat Shanghai
- **Beijing:** Chabad of Beijing
- **Hong Kong:** Multiple Orthodox and progressive synagogues

These communities often host Shabbat meals and are welcoming to newcomers, whether Jewish by birth, conversion, or exploration.

## Key Takeaways

- **Shabbat is a weekly 25-hour period of rest**, from Friday sunset to Saturday nightfall, rooted in God's rest on the seventh day of creation
- **Preparation is essential**: Clean your home, cook all meals in advance, and light candles 18 minutes before sunset
- **Friday night includes**: Candle lighting, Shalom Aleichem, Eishet Chayil (optional), Kiddush, hand-washing, HaMotzi over challah, and a festive meal
- **Shabbat day includes**: Morning prayers and Torah reading, a second Kiddush and meal, rest and study, and Seudah Shlishit (third meal) in the afternoon
- **The 39 melachot (creative labors)** are forbidden, including cooking, writing, kindling fire, and using electronics
- **Havdalah marks the end of Shabbat** with blessings over wine, spices, and fire, symbolizing the separation between sacred and mundane time
- **Start small and build gradually** — even lighting candles and unplugging for one evening is a meaningful beginning
- **Chinese families can adapt Shabbat** by incorporating familiar foods, honoring family values, and connecting with local Jewish communities
