# Relations with Non-Jews: A Torah Perspective on Universal Brotherhood

## Introduction: A Light Unto the Nations

The relationship between Jews and non-Jews is one of the most important yet delicate topics in Jewish life. For those of us living in China and other diaspora communities, it's not merely theoretical—it shapes our daily interactions, business relationships, friendships, and our children's social worlds.

At the heart of the Jewish approach to non-Jewish relations stands a profound truth: every human being is created in the image of God (Genesis 1:27). This fundamental principle informs everything from how we conduct business to how we celebrate—or don't celebrate—our neighbors' holidays.

The Torah envisions the Jewish people as an "or la'goyim" (light unto the nations), a phrase from the prophet Isaiah that captures our role in the world. We are not meant to isolate ourselves from humanity, nor to dissolve into it. Rather, we walk a careful path: maintaining our unique identity and mission while contributing positively to the societies in which we live.

## The Foundation: All Humanity Under One Creator

### The Noahide Laws: Universal Moral Code

Long before the giving of the Torah at Sinai, God established a covenant with all humanity through Noah. The seven Noahide Laws represent the basic moral framework that Judaism believes applies to all people:

1. **Prohibition of idolatry**: Recognizing one God and rejecting false worship
2. **Prohibition of blasphemy**: Respecting God's name and the sacred
3. **Prohibition of murder**: The sanctity of human life
4. **Prohibition of theft**: Respecting others' property and rights
5. **Prohibition of sexual immorality**: Maintaining family structure and sexual ethics
6. **Prohibition of eating flesh from a living animal**: Basic compassion for creatures
7. **Requirement to establish courts of justice**: Creating fair legal systems

These laws reveal something crucial: Judaism does not believe non-Jews must convert to be righteous. The Talmud explicitly states, "The righteous of all nations have a share in the world to come" (Sanhedrin 105a). A non-Jew who lives ethically according to these principles is considered righteous in God's eyes.

This framework gives us tremendous respect for the moral achievements of non-Jewish civilizations. When we see justice, kindness, and wisdom in Chinese philosophy, Western democracy, or any ethical system, we can appreciate it as reflecting universal divine truth.

### Our Unique Role, Their Equal Dignity

Judaism maintains a paradox: Jews have a unique covenant and mission, yet this does not make us inherently superior as human beings. Every person possesses infinite worth as a creation of God. The difference lies in responsibility, not value.

Think of it like a family. A father might give one child the family business to run while another pursues art. Different roles, equal love. Similarly, Jews received specific commandments (613 mitzvot) while non-Jews have their own path (the seven Noahide Laws). Neither is superior; both are divinely ordained.

This understanding prevents both arrogance and false humility. We need not apologize for our distinctive practices, nor look down on those who follow different paths.

## Darkei Shalom: The Ways of Peace

### A Guiding Principle

One of the most important concepts governing Jewish-Gentile relations is "mipnei darkei shalom"—for the sake of peaceful relations. The Talmud uses this principle to permit and even require actions that promote harmony in mixed societies.

For example, the Talmud teaches that we should:
- Visit non-Jewish sick people alongside Jewish ones
- Support their poor alongside our own
- Offer condolences to non-Jewish mourners
- Greet non-Jews warmly in the marketplace

These aren't merely permitted—they're encouraged. Why? Because promoting peace, reducing hostility, and demonstrating Jewish ethical excellence serve both practical and spiritual purposes.

However, darkei shalom is not about hiding who we are or compromising core principles. It's about presenting Judaism's face in the most attractive light possible, demonstrating that our tradition produces mensches—decent, ethical, warm human beings.

### Kiddush Hashem: Sanctifying God's Name

Every interaction a Jew has with a non-Jew carries the potential for kiddush Hashem (sanctifying God's name) or, God forbid, its opposite—chillul Hashem (desecrating God's name).

When Jews act with exceptional honesty in business, show unusual kindness to neighbors, or display remarkable integrity under pressure, non-Jews often remark, "That's because they're religious." This is kiddush Hashem—making God's name beloved through our actions.

Conversely, when Jews behave unethically, the reaction is often, "And they claim to be religious!" This chillul Hashem is considered among the gravest sins because it pushes people away from God and Torah.

Living in China as a tiny minority, we are constantly "on stage." Our Chinese colleagues, neighbors, and business partners form their impressions of Judaism largely through us. This is both a responsibility and an opportunity.

## Practical Interactions: Navigating Daily Life

### Business Partnerships

Judaism has a long, proud tradition of commercial interaction with non-Jews. The Talmud discusses business partnerships between Jews and Gentiles extensively, assuming they are normal and acceptable.

**Key principles:**
- **Absolute honesty**: The prohibition against deception applies equally to Jewish and non-Jewish customers. Some authorities argue it's even more stringent with non-Jews because of the chillul Hashem factor.
- **Fair dealing**: Price gouging, false weights, misleading advertising—all forbidden regardless of the customer's religion.
- **Keeping commitments**: Breaking your word to a non-Jewish partner is a serious violation, both of business ethics and of kiddush Hashem.
- **Sabbath considerations**: Partnerships may require careful structuring to avoid Sabbath violations, but such arrangements are definitely possible.

The medieval Jewish communities of Spain, Germany, and Poland often had extensive business relationships with their Christian neighbors. Today, Jewish businesspeople worldwide maintain partnerships with people of all faiths. The key is conducting yourself in a way that would make your partner say, "I trust him because he's a religious Jew," not "despite being a religious Jew."

### Friendships

Can Jews have genuine friendships with non-Jews? Absolutely. Throughout Jewish history, from Joseph's relationship with Pharaoh's courtiers to Maimonides' correspondence with Muslim scholars, we see examples of respectful, even warm relationships.

**What this looks like:**
- **Authenticity**: You can be yourself—explaining when you can't eat certain foods or when you need to leave early for Shabbat. True friends respect differences.
- **Shared values**: Friendship often emerges from common interests—work projects, children's schools, shared hobbies. The Torah values truth, kindness, and justice—values that transcend religious boundaries.
- **Appropriate boundaries**: Some activities traditionally practiced by observant Jews only with other Jews (like Torah study partnerships or serving as a witness) aren't signs of prejudice but of our distinct covenant.

Living in China, many of us have warm relationships with Chinese colleagues and neighbors. We celebrate their achievements, commiserate over their challenges, and build genuine connection—all while maintaining our Jewish identity.

### Attending Non-Jewish Events

This is where many people feel most uncertain. What about weddings, funerals, and other lifecycle events?

**Weddings:**
The general principle: you may attend the ceremony and reception of a non-Jewish wedding (or an interfaith wedding) to show respect and maintain relationships, with some caveats:
- You should not attend if it takes place in a church or involves active participation in religious ritual (like taking communion).
- Civil ceremonies or cultural weddings are generally acceptable.
- Be mindful of food—you'll need to politely stick to items you can eat or eat beforehand.
- Your presence shows you value the relationship, even if you can't participate in every element.

**Funerals:**
Attending a non-Jewish funeral to pay respects is generally permitted and often encouraged under darkei shalom. You are showing compassion to the mourners, which is a great mitzvah.
- You may attend the service and burial.
- You need not participate in ritual acts foreign to Judaism (like making the sign of the cross), but respectful presence is appropriate.
- Offering condolences and practical help to mourners is a kindness Judaism values highly.

**Other events:**
- Work celebrations, retirement parties, cultural festivals: generally fine to attend.
- Religious celebrations in churches or temples: usually avoided, though circumstances vary.
- Community service events, charity dinners, educational programs: typically encouraged.

The general rule: you can show up for people without showing up for theological beliefs you don't hold.

### Holiday Greetings

May you wish a Christian colleague "Merry Christmas" or a Muslim coworker "Ramadan Mubarak"?

Most contemporary authorities permit—even encourage—such greetings:
- You're acknowledging their celebration, not affirming their theology.
- It promotes workplace harmony and shows basic human courtesy.
- It's an opportunity for kiddush Hashem, showing that religious Jews are warm and respectful.

Some prefer more neutral formulations like "Happy Holidays" or "Enjoy your celebration," which is also perfectly fine.

What you typically shouldn't do is actively celebrate their religious holidays—putting up a Christmas tree or Easter decorations in your home crosses a line from courtesy into participation.

## Food Considerations: The Kosher Question

Food is where Jewish distinctiveness becomes most visible—and sometimes most socially awkward.

### Eating with Non-Jews

The Torah's dietary laws (kashrut) create natural boundaries around Jewish eating, but they don't prohibit socializing with non-Jews. Here's how to navigate it:

**In their homes:**
- You can attend a dinner party and politely eat only items you're comfortable with (fruit, raw vegetables, certain packaged items with kosher certification).
- Explain your dietary needs graciously: "I keep kosher, which means I'm limited in what I can eat. I appreciate the invitation and I'm happy to come and enjoy the company!"
- Many hosts appreciate knowing in advance so they can accommodate—buying kosher wine or certified products.
- Consider eating a meal before you go so you're not hungry and can participate socially.

**In restaurants:**
- Eating in non-kosher restaurants ranges from prohibited to complex depending on your level of observance and the specific situation.
- Some observant Jews will order only fish or vegetarian dishes (avoiding bishul akum concerns through specific orders).
- Many prefer to suggest coffee shops or certified kosher establishments.
- In China, this can be particularly challenging, but many cities now have kosher restaurants or at least options observant Jews can navigate.

### Bishul Akum and Pas Akum

These concepts—restrictions on food cooked or baked by non-Jews—reflect deeper issues of Jewish-Gentile separation in ancient times. Today, they're applied with considerable nuance:

**Bishul akum** (food cooked by non-Jews): Generally applies only to foods that could be served at royal banquets and require cooking. A Jew must participate in the cooking process (even just lighting the flame) for such foods to be permissible.

**Pas akum** (bread baked by non-Jews): Many communities are lenient about commercial bakery bread (pas palter) when Jewish-baked bread isn't available, though some are stricter.

These laws can feel like barriers, but they've also preserved Jewish identity across millennia. The key is navigating them with both integrity and sensitivity—explaining graciously when necessary but never in a way that suggests moral superiority.

## The Intermarriage Question: Love, Identity, and Continuity

This is perhaps the most emotionally charged issue in modern Jewish-Gentile relations. How can we maintain respect for all people while strongly discouraging intermarriage?

### Why Judaism Opposes Intermarriage

The Torah explicitly forbids marriage between Jews and the seven Canaanite nations (Deuteronomy 7:3-4), and the rabbis extended this to all non-Jews. But why?

**It's not about racism or superiority**. Three main reasons emerge:

1. **Covenant continuity**: The Jewish people have a unique covenant with God, passed down through generations. Intermarriage drastically reduces the likelihood that children will identify as Jewish and live Jewishly.

2. **Shared life foundations**: Marriage requires shared values, rhythms, and goals. When one partner observes Shabbat and the other doesn't, or one keeps kosher and the other doesn't, daily life becomes a constant negotiation. While marriages can work across many differences, religious practice affects everything from what you eat to how you raise children.

3. **Historical experience**: Intermarriage has always been the fastest path to Jewish assimilation. Communities that permitted it largely disappeared within a generation or two.

### Addressing This with Compassion

If someone you love is dating or married to a non-Jew, how do you respond?

**What doesn't work:**
- Cutting off the relationship
- Insults or condemnation
- Treating the non-Jewish partner as a problem to be solved
- Guilt and manipulation

**What might work:**
- Maintaining the relationship with love
- Being honest about your sadness and concern for Jewish continuity
- Welcoming the non-Jewish partner and showing them the beauty of Judaism
- Encouraging conversion if the partner is genuinely interested
- Recognizing that choice remains with the couple while you model authentic Jewish life

This is genuinely difficult. We affirm both that intermarriage is deeply problematic from a Jewish continuity perspective AND that individual people in such relationships deserve respect and love.

### Living in China's Context

For Jewish families in China, this issue has unique dimensions:
- Small Jewish communities mean fewer potential Jewish partners
- Chinese culture's emphasis on family continuity resonates with Jewish concerns
- The language barrier and cultural difference can actually make the shared Jewish piece even more important
- Some families have found creative solutions: spending summers in larger Jewish communities, connecting children online with Jewish peers, or relocating for critical years

## Righteous Gentiles: Honoring Our Helpers

Judaism has tremendous respect for non-Jews who demonstrate exceptional morality, particularly those who help Jews at personal risk.

### The Righteous Among the Nations

Yad Vashem in Jerusalem honors "Righteous Among the Nations"—non-Jews who rescued Jews during the Holocaust. This recognition embodies a key Jewish principle: goodness deserves honor regardless of the good person's religion.

But this concept extends beyond the Holocaust:
- Non-Jewish business partners who acted with integrity when they could have cheated
- Gentile neighbors who protected Jewish property during pogroms
- Government officials who stood up for Jewish rights
- Teachers, doctors, and friends who showed exceptional kindness

The Talmud teaches that "the righteous of all nations have a share in the world to come." This isn't a grudging concession—it's a celebration of universal human potential for goodness.

### Gratitude as a Jewish Value

Judaism requires hakarat hatov—recognizing and acknowledging good done to us. When a non-Jew helps you:
- Express gratitude sincerely and publicly when appropriate
- Look for ways to reciprocate
- Honor their memory
- Teach your children about them

This attitude combats any tendency toward insularity or ingratitude. We remember that throughout history, individual gentiles often showed courage and compassion even when broader societies did not.

## Living in China: A Special Case Study

For the growing Jewish community in China—whether long-term residents, expats, or Chinese Jews by heritage—the relationship with non-Jewish Chinese neighbors presents unique opportunities and challenges.

### Maintaining Identity While Integrating

Chinese culture values both community harmony (he) and respect for distinct traditions. This actually aligns well with Jewish approaches:

**What integration looks like:**
- Learning Chinese language and customs
- Participating in community life
- Contributing to society through business, education, and innovation
- Showing respect for Chinese holidays and traditions
- Building genuine friendships with Chinese colleagues and neighbors

**What identity preservation looks like:**
- Creating Jewish community infrastructure (schools, synagogues, social groups)
- Celebrating Jewish holidays fully
- Maintaining kosher standards to the extent possible
- Teaching children Hebrew and Jewish texts
- Traveling to Israel regularly to reinforce connection

These aren't contradictory—they're complementary. The Chinese Jews of Kaifeng maintained their identity for centuries through exactly this balance.

### The Chinese Philosophical Resonance

Interestingly, several Chinese philosophical concepts resonate with Jewish ideas:
- **Ren** (仁, benevolence/humaneness) parallels Jewish chesed (loving-kindness)
- **Li** (礼, ritual propriety) echoes Jewish emphasis on mitzvot and structure
- **Tian** (天, Heaven) shares some conceptual space with Jewish understanding of divine providence
- **Xiao** (孝, filial piety) strongly aligns with the Jewish commandment to honor parents

These parallels create natural bridges. When explaining Judaism to Chinese friends, you might note: "We also believe in honoring parents deeply—it's one of our Ten Commandments" or "Jewish law has detailed ritual prescriptions, somewhat like li in Confucian thought."

### Practical Tips for Jewish Life in China

**Community:**
- Connect with local Jewish communities (Beijing, Shanghai, Hong Kong all have active communities)
- Use technology to stay connected with Jewish family and friends globally
- Create Jewish moments at home even when community is far

**Food:**
- Learn which Chinese restaurants understand dietary restrictions
- Find Chinese ingredients that are naturally kosher
- Connect with kosher food delivery services if available in your city
- Cook more at home, exploring Jewish recipes adapted with Chinese ingredients

**Education:**
- Use online Jewish educational resources
- Connect children with Jewish peers through video calls
- Visit Israel and other Jewish communities during holidays and summer
- Bring Jewish educators in for intensive periods if resources permit

**Cultural navigation:**
- Be open about your practices: "I don't eat pork because I'm Jewish" is usually met with understanding
- Draw parallels: "Shabbat is our day of rest, like your family time on Sunday"
- Invite Chinese friends to experience Jewish holidays—most find Shabbat dinner or Passover seders fascinating

### Contributing to Chinese Society

Part of being a light unto the nations is contributing positively:
- Jewish expertise in education, technology, medicine, and business has much to offer Chinese development
- Jewish emphasis on tzedakah (charity) can inspire social service initiatives
- Cultural exchange programs deepen mutual understanding
- Simply being a good neighbor, honest businessperson, and engaged citizen creates kiddush Hashem

Remember: Jews have thrived in diaspora for millennia by being both proudly distinct and genuinely contributing members of their host societies. China is no exception.

## Common Questions Addressed

### "Isn't this ethnocentric? Don't all religions think they're special?"

Yes and no. Judaism does claim a unique covenant, but it explicitly affirms that non-Jews can be equally righteous. We don't proselytize because we don't believe everyone needs to be Jewish. That's actually less ethnocentric than universalizing religions that believe everyone must convert.

### "Can I be friends with someone who eats pork in front of me?"

Absolutely. Your friend's eating choices don't affect your kashrut. You can sit together, talk, enjoy each other's company. You simply eat what you eat, and they eat what they eat.

### "What if my child's best friend is non-Jewish? Should I discourage it?"

No. Childhood friendships are developmentally important and teach valuable social skills. As your child grows, ensure they also have strong Jewish friendships and identity. The issue isn't non-Jewish friends; it's having *only* non-Jewish friends with no Jewish social connection.

### "How do I explain to my Chinese in-laws why we can't intermarry?"

With both honesty and sensitivity: "Our people have maintained our identity for 4,000 years through our traditions, including marrying within the faith. It's not about superiority—it's about continuity. We deeply respect your family and culture, and we hope you can respect our need to preserve ours."

### "Is it okay to celebrate Chinese New Year?"

Cultural celebrations are generally fine—the dragon dances, family gatherings, special foods (that are kosher). It's the religious aspects (offerings to ancestors, temple visits) that observant Jews would avoid. You can participate in the cultural joy while maintaining your religious boundaries.

### "My boss wants me to attend a business dinner at a temple. What do I do?"

Explain politely: "I appreciate the invitation. However, my religious practice prevents me from entering temples of other religions. Could I perhaps meet you before or after at a restaurant, or attend a different venue?" Most people respect clearly stated religious boundaries.

### "Why can't I just convert my non-Jewish spouse? Problem solved."

Conversion to Judaism must be motivated by genuine belief and commitment to observant Jewish life—not by romantic considerations. Rabbis won't perform conversions primarily to enable a marriage. If your partner is genuinely drawn to Judaism and willing to embrace full observance, conversion is possible, but it's a serious, lengthy process.

## Conclusion: Walking the Balance

Relations with non-Jews require constant balance—living in the world without being entirely of it, contributing to society while maintaining distinctiveness, showing universal respect while preserving particular practices.

This balance isn't always comfortable. You'll have moments of social awkwardness when you can't eat what's offered. You'll field questions about "strange" practices. You'll navigate the tension between fitting in and standing out.

But this discomfort is part of the Jewish mission. We are meant to be distinct—not isolated, not superior, but recognizably different in ways that inspire questions and model alternatives.

When done right, your non-Jewish neighbors should think:
- "They're different, but I respect them."
- "Their traditions seem meaningful and beautiful."
- "They're honest, kind, and contribute to our community."
- "I'd like to learn more about what makes them tick."

That's being an "or la'goyim"—a light unto the nations. Not through preaching or proselytizing, but through living so well that others are drawn to the Source of that goodness.

In China, in America, in Israel, or anywhere Jews live, this remains our calling: to be distinctly, beautifully, contributively Jewish—honoring all humans while maintaining the covenant that makes us who we are.

May we merit to reflect God's image in all our interactions, creating kiddush Hashem wherever we go.
