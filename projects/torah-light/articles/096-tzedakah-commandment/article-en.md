## Introduction: More Than Generosity

In most cultures, giving to the poor is considered a virtue — an act of kindness that earns you moral credit. In Judaism, it's something else entirely: a *commandment*. The Hebrew word for charitable giving isn't "charity" at all — it's *tzedakah* (צדקה), which comes from the root *tzedek* (צדק), meaning "justice" or "righteousness."

This subtle distinction changes everything. When you give tzedakah, you're not doing someone a *favor*. You're fulfilling an *obligation*. The money you give isn't truly yours to begin with — it belongs to God, and you're merely the custodian. The poor have a *right* to a portion of what you possess, just as you have a right to what remains.

This article explores why Judaism treats giving as justice rather than generosity, how much you're obligated to give, and Maimonides' famous eight levels of tzedakah — a ladder that leads from reluctant giving all the way to helping someone become self-sufficient.

## The Biblical Foundation

The Torah doesn't suggest giving to the poor — it commands it repeatedly:

**"You shall surely open your hand to your brother, to the poor and needy in your land."** (Deuteronomy 15:8)

**"When you reap the harvest of your land, you shall not reap to the very edges of your field... you shall leave them for the poor and the stranger."** (Leviticus 19:9-10)

These aren't recommendations. They're *mitzvot* (commandments) with the same binding force as keeping kosher or observing Shabbat. A Jew who refuses to give tzedakah is in violation of Torah law.

Why does the Torah command this? Because wealth inequality was a fact of life even during the era of the Shang Dynasty (商朝, ~1600-1046 BCE) when the patriarchs lived, and God designed a society where those with resources would be legally obligated to share them.

The Talmud goes further: "Tzedakah is equal in weight to all the other commandments combined" (Bava Batra 9a). This isn't hyperbole. Jewish law holds that someone who refuses to give tzedakah can be *compelled* by a beit din (Jewish court) to do so, and if they still refuse, the court may even seize their assets to distribute to the poor.

## Tzedakah vs. Charity: A Crucial Difference

The English word "charity" comes from the Latin *caritas*, meaning "dear" or "beloved" — implying an act of love or kindness. It's voluntary, praiseworthy, and makes the giver feel good about themselves.

*Tzedakah* is different. It's:

- **Obligatory, not voluntary:** Every Jew who has the means must give. It's not a choice.
- **Justice, not mercy:** The poor aren't begging for your kindness; they're claiming what is rightfully theirs.
- **Anonymous when possible:** The highest forms of tzedakah involve the giver not knowing the recipient and vice versa, precisely to avoid the patronizing dynamic of "look at my generosity."

In Confucian thought, the concept of *ren* (仁, benevolence) includes caring for others as an ethical imperative, not merely an optional virtue. Judaism's approach to tzedakah similarly frames giving as a fundamental duty that maintains social harmony and reflects divine justice.

## How Much Must You Give?

Jewish law is remarkably specific about giving:

### The Minimum: 10% (Maaser)
Every Jew is obligated to give at least one-tenth of their income to tzedakah. This is called *maaser* (מעשר), literally "a tenth." The source is Genesis 28:22, where Jacob vows, "Of all that You give me, I will surely give a tenth to You."

Some authorities consider this a Torah-level obligation, while others view it as a rabbinic enactment. Either way, it's binding. If you earn $50,000 per year, at least $5,000 must go to tzedakah.

### The Maximum: 20%
The Talmud teaches that while you should give at least 10%, you shouldn't give more than 20% unless you're very wealthy (Ketubot 50a). Why? Because you might impoverish yourself and become dependent on others — defeating the purpose of tzedakah.

There is one exception: if you're wealthy enough that giving 20% won't affect your lifestyle, you may give more. But even then, many rabbis counsel against giving everything away, because the ideal is to remain financially stable while supporting others.

### For Those Who Can't Afford 10%
If you're poor yourself, you're still obligated to give *something* — even if it's a tiny amount. The Talmud teaches, "Even a poor person who lives entirely from tzedakah must give tzedakah to another poor person" (Gittin 7b).

Why? To preserve the dignity and connection to community that comes from being a giver, not just a receiver. Even a single coin given with the right intention fulfills the mitzvah.

## The Eight Levels of Tzedakah: Maimonides' Ladder

The Rambam (Rabbi Moshe ben Maimon, also called Maimonides, 1138-1204 CE, during China's Song Dynasty 宋朝) outlined eight levels of giving, arranged from lowest to highest. This isn't theoretical — it's practical guidance for how to improve the *quality* of your giving:

### Level 8 (Lowest): Giving Reluctantly
You give because you're asked or pressured, but your heart isn't in it. You hand over the money with obvious reluctance, perhaps with a sigh or a frown. The poor person receives help, but also receives humiliation.

**Example:** You give to a homeless person but make a face or comment about how they should "get a job."

### Level 7: Giving Less Than You Should, But Cheerfully
You give less than the appropriate amount, but you do so with a smile and kind words. The warmth of your demeanor partially compensates for the inadequate sum.

**Example:** You should give $100 based on your income, but you give $50 — yet you do it graciously.

### Level 6: Giving After Being Asked
You don't seek out opportunities to give; you wait until someone asks you directly. But when they do, you respond generously.

**Example:** You ignore charity emails but when a friend organizing a fundraiser personally asks you, you contribute.

### Level 5: Giving Before Being Asked
You proactively give without waiting to be solicited. You see a need and meet it.

**Example:** When you hear a family in your community is struggling, you discreetly arrange for groceries to be delivered to their home.

### Level 4: The Recipient Knows the Donor, But the Donor Doesn't Know the Recipient
You give publicly or put your name on a donation, so the poor person knows who helped them. This preserves your connection to the community but allows the recipient some privacy.

**Example:** You sponsor a community Shabbat meal and your name is listed as the sponsor.

### Level 3: The Donor Knows the Recipient, But the Recipient Doesn't Know the Donor
You know who you're helping, but you give anonymously so they don't feel indebted to you.

**Example:** You hear that a coworker's family is in crisis, and you anonymously pay one month of their rent through a third party.

### Level 2: Both Donor and Recipient Are Anonymous to Each Other
Neither party knows the other. In ancient times, this was accomplished through a special chamber in the Temple where people would leave money and the poor would take what they needed without seeing who gave. Today, reputable charities often serve this function — you give to an organization, and they distribute funds to people you'll never meet.

**Example:** You give to a properly run tzedakah fund that carefully vets recipients and distributes money based on genuine need.

### Level 1 (Highest): Enabling Self-Sufficiency
You help someone become financially independent so they no longer need tzedakah at all. This might mean:

- Giving them a job
- Teaching them a trade or skill
- Providing a business loan (interest-free, according to Jewish law)
- Entering into a business partnership with them

**Example:** Instead of just giving money to someone unemployed, you hire them or help them start a small business, or you connect them with job training programs.

This is the highest level because you're not just alleviating poverty temporarily — you're *ending* it. As the Talmud teaches: "Make your Shabbat like a weekday [eat simple food] rather than be dependent on other people" (Pesachim 113a). Self-sufficiency is dignity.

## Who Should Receive Your Tzedakah?

If everyone needs your help and you can't help everyone, whom should you prioritize?

The Talmud and later codes provide clear guidelines:

1. **Family first:** Your own relatives (parents, siblings, children) take precedence. "Charity begins at home" is literally Torah law.

2. **Your city before other cities:** People in your local community have first claim on your resources.

3. **Torah scholars and teachers:** Those who dedicate their lives to teaching Torah deserve special support.

4. **The Torah-observant poor:** Those who are poor while trying to keep mitzvot have priority over those who are indifferent to observance.

5. **Orphans and widows:** Those with no family support are especially vulnerable.

6. **Marrying off orphans:** Helping a poor bride or groom get married is one of the highest forms of tzedakah.

However, these are priorities, not exclusions. You may still give to secular charities, non-Jewish causes, or international relief — and many poskim (halachic authorities) encourage supporting worthy causes outside the Jewish community as well, especially for *kiddush Hashem* (sanctifying God's name by showing the world Jewish values).

## Practical Ways to Give Tzedakah

How can you integrate tzedakah into daily life?

### 1. Automate It
Set up automatic monthly transfers from your checking account to a tzedakah fund. Calculate 10% of your monthly income and schedule the transfer to happen right after you receive your paycheck. This ensures you fulfill the mitzvah consistently without relying on willpower.

### 2. Keep a Pushka (Charity Box)
Many Jewish homes keep a small box or tin for collecting coins. Before lighting Shabbat candles, it's customary to put money in the pushka. Even small amounts add up over time.

### 3. Give Before Praying
The Talmud teaches: "Whoever gives even a small coin to a poor person before praying is worthy of beholding the Divine Presence" (Bava Batra 10a). Many synagogues have charity boxes near the entrance for this reason.

### 4. Volunteer Your Time and Skills
Tzedakah isn't only money. If you're a doctor, providing free medical care is tzedakah. If you're a teacher, tutoring students who can't afford it is tzedakah. If you're a plumber, fixing a poor family's broken pipes for free is tzedakah.

The Rambam writes: "If someone cannot afford to give money, they should give time and effort" (Mishneh Torah, Hilchot Matanot Aniyim 10:16).

### 5. Combine Tzedakah with Celebrations
When something good happens — a birthday, a promotion, recovering from illness — give tzedakah in gratitude. Many Jews give chai ($18, numerically equivalent to the Hebrew word for "life") for celebrations.

### 6. Use Tzedakah Apps
In the digital age, there are apps like *Zehut* and *Matbia* that let you track your tzedakah giving, ensure maaser compliance, and discover verified charities.

## Tzedakah for Jews Living in Asia

If you're exploring Judaism in China or elsewhere in Asia, you may wonder: *Whom do I give to when I'm far from a Jewish community?*

Good news: you have options!

- **Online Tzedakah Platforms:** Websites like [Colel Chabad](https://www.colelchabad.org), [Kupat Ha'ir](https://kupat-hair.co.il/en), and [Chabad.org's charity directory](https://www.chabad.org/tools/tzedakah/default.htm) accept online donations and distribute them to verified recipients in Israel and around the world.

- **Local Jewish Institutions:** If there's a Chabad House in your city (Beijing, Shanghai, Hong Kong, etc.), they often have a tzedakah fund for helping local Jews in need, supporting education, or assisting travelers.

- **Support Your Conversion/Learning:** If you're studying for conversion or working with a rabbi long-distance, supporting Jewish education (including paying your rabbi or funding your own learning materials) can be a form of tzedakah.

- **Non-Jewish Causes:** While prioritizing Jewish recipients is the halachic ideal, giving to secular charities (disaster relief, medical research, poverty alleviation) is still valuable and fulfills the broader ethical imperative of *tikkun olam* (repairing the world).

## The Spiritual Dimension: Why Giving Makes You Wealthy

This might sound paradoxical: how does giving money away make you wealthier?

The Talmud promises: "One does not become poor from giving tzedakah" (Gittin 7b). And the prophet Malachi challenges: "Test Me, please, in this... [and see] if I will not open for you the windows of heaven and pour out for you blessing without end" (Malachi 3:10).

This is the *only* mitzvah where God explicitly invites us to test Him. Why?

Because tzedakah is about recognizing the true source of wealth. When you give, you're declaring: "This isn't mine; it's God's. I'm merely the channel through which His blessing flows." That recognition transforms you from a hoarder into a conduit.

Jewish mysticism (Kabbalah) explains it differently: every soul is part of one unified spiritual organism. When you help another Jew (or another human being), you're actually helping yourself, because at the soul level, you're all connected. Giving opens spiritual channels that allow divine abundance to flow more freely into your life.

This doesn't mean tzedakah is a get-rich-quick scheme. But it does mean that generosity rewires your relationship with money from scarcity and fear to abundance and trust.

## Common Questions

**Q: What if I have debt? Do I still have to give tzedakah?**  
A: It depends. If your debt is manageable and you have income, you should still give at least something (even if it's less than 10%). If you're drowning in debt and barely surviving, focus on achieving stability first. But even then, giving a small amount — even $5 — keeps you connected to the mitzvah.

**Q: Can I give tzedakah to non-Jewish causes?**  
A: Yes, especially for universal needs like disaster relief, medical research, or fighting hunger. The Talmud teaches *darchei shalom* (ways of peace) — that we support non-Jewish poor as well. However, prioritize Jewish causes first if resources are limited.

**Q: What if I don't trust the charity? What if the money is misused?**  
A: Give to reputable organizations with transparent accounting. In Jewish law, a charity that misuses funds violates the trust placed in it and can be held accountable. Research before giving.

**Q: Can I fulfill maaser (10%) by giving to family members who are struggling?**  
A: Yes! Supporting your own family absolutely counts as tzedakah. In fact, it's a *higher priority* than giving to strangers.

**Q: Should I give to people begging on the street?**  
A: Jewish law obligates you to give something to anyone who asks, but you're not required to give a lot. A small amount is sufficient. If you're concerned about how the money will be used, consider carrying granola bars or gift cards to give instead of cash.

## Conclusion: From Obligation to Transformation

Tzedakah begins as an obligation — 10% of your income, no excuses, no exceptions. It's not about feeling generous. It's about fulfilling a commandment, just like putting on tefillin or lighting Shabbat candles.

But somewhere along the way, something shifts. What started as obligation becomes joy. You stop thinking of tzedakah as losing money and start experiencing it as participating in God's work of sustaining the world.

You realize that every time you give, you're declaring: "I trust that there will be enough. I trust that God provides. I trust that my purpose is bigger than accumulating wealth for myself."

The Talmud tells a story: Once Rabbi Tarfon inherited a large sum of money, and people urged him to buy property. He responded: "I've already invested it in something better — I gave it all to tzedakah. My 'land' is now stored up in Heaven, where it can never be stolen, where it lasts forever" (Jerusalem Talmud, Pe'ah 1:1).

This is the ultimate truth about tzedakah: the only wealth you truly keep is the wealth you give away.

In a world obsessed with accumulation, tzedakah is a radical act. It says that meaning isn't found in what you own, but in what you share. It says that your money has a purpose beyond your comfort. It says that justice isn't something governments do — it's something *you* do, one act of giving at a time.

Ten percent of your income. Every month. Without fail. Not because it feels good (though it will). Not because it makes you look generous (ideally, no one should even know). But because it's *right*. Because it's *tzedek*. Because you're not just making the world better — you're making yourself better.

As Maimonides wrote: "We are obligated to be more scrupulous in the mitzvah of tzedakah than in any other positive commandment, because tzedakah is the sign of a righteous person" (Mishneh Torah, Hilchot Matanot Aniyim 10:1).

That sign — the sign of someone who recognizes that what they have isn't theirs alone — is what marks a true child of Abraham, a true servant of God, a true Jew.

Give. Not because you must, but because you were given to first.
