## Introduction

If you've ever felt like an outsider looking in — perhaps the only person in your city interested in Judaism, wondering if you truly belong — the story of Ruth is for you. Nearly three thousand years ago (during what corresponds to China's Western Zhou Dynasty 西周, ~1020–771 BCE), a young Moabite princess made a choice that would change Jewish history forever. She left behind her royal palace, her family, her gods, and her entire world to follow her Jewish mother-in-law into poverty and uncertainty.

Ruth's declaration — "Your people shall be my people, and your God my God" — became the timeless expression of sincere conversion. Her great-grandson would be King David, ancestor of the Messiah. But Ruth's greatness wasn't in her royal descendants. It was in her unwavering commitment to truth, kindness, and the God of Israel.

## The Story Begins: Tragedy in Moab

The Book of Ruth opens during the time of the Judges (about 1000 years before the Common Era), when "a famine occurred in the land" of Israel. A wealthy man named Elimelech, from Bethlehem in Judah, made a controversial decision: instead of staying to help his community through the famine, he fled with his wife Naomi and their two sons to the neighboring land of Moab.

Moab was not a friendly place for Jews. The Moabites were descendants of Lot (Abraham's nephew) through an incestuous relationship. They worshipped the cruel god Moloch, to whom they sacrificed children in fire. When the Israelites had fled Egypt and passed near Moab centuries earlier, the Moabites had refused them bread and water. The Torah explicitly forbids marriage between Jews and Moabites because of this historic cruelty.

Yet Elimelech's two sons — Mahlon and Chilion — married Moabite women: Orpah and Ruth. According to tradition, these were no ordinary women. They were daughters of Eglon, king of Moab — princesses who lived in the royal palace.

Then disaster struck. First Elimelech died. Then, after about ten years, both sons died as well, leaving three widows: Naomi, Orpah, and Ruth.

## The Turning Point: Ruth's Declaration

Naomi, heartbroken and destitute, decided to return to Bethlehem. Word had reached her that the famine in Israel had ended. Both her daughters-in-law began the journey with her, but on the road, Naomi urged them to turn back:

"Return, each of you, to your mother's house. May the Lord deal kindly with you, as you have dealt with the dead and with me."

The women wept. Orpah kissed her mother-in-law goodbye and returned to Moab. But Ruth refused.

What happened next became one of the most famous passages in the entire Hebrew Bible:

> **"Do not urge me to leave you, to turn back and not follow you. For wherever you go, I will go; wherever you lodge, I will lodge; your people shall be my people, and your God my God. Where you die, I will die, and there I will be buried. Thus and more may the Lord do to me if anything but death parts me from you."** (Ruth 1:16-17)

This wasn't a spur-of-the-moment emotional declaration. According to the Talmud, Naomi had tried three times to discourage Ruth, explaining the difficulties of Jewish life: Shabbat restrictions, the laws of modesty, the prohibition against idolatry. Each time Ruth insisted: "I know. I accept. I am committed."

The sages teach that these words contain the essence of conversion: accepting the Jewish people (community), accepting God (theology), and committing to live and die as a Jew (permanence).

## Arriving in Bethlehem: Poverty and Dignity

When Naomi and Ruth arrived in Bethlehem, the entire town was stirred. People hardly recognized the once-wealthy Naomi. She told them, "Do not call me Naomi ('Pleasant'). Call me Mara ('Bitter'), for God has made my lot very bitter."

They arrived at the beginning of the barley harvest — significant timing, as we'll see. Ruth, though a princess, insisted on going to the fields to glean leftover grain. This was her right under Torah law: the poor and the stranger were permitted to collect *leket* (fallen stalks), *shikchah* (forgotten sheaves), and *pe'ah* (corners of the field deliberately left unharvested).

Ruth "happened" to come to a field belonging to Boaz, a wealthy and righteous man who was a relative of Elimelech. The text uses the phrase *"vayiker mikreha"* — "it happened by chance" — but the sages note: there is no chance in the Book of Ruth. Everything was guided by Divine Providence.

## Boaz: Kindness and Recognition

Boaz noticed Ruth immediately. He asked his workers, "Whose girl is that?" They told him: "She is the Moabite who came back with Naomi."

Boaz's response reveals his character. Instead of scorning her as a foreigner, he blessed her:

> "May the Lord reward your deeds. May you have a full recompense from the Lord, the God of Israel, under whose wings you have sought refuge." (Ruth 2:12)

Boaz ordered his workers to treat Ruth with respect, to let her glean even among the sheaves (not just the leftovers), and to deliberately drop extra grain for her. He invited her to eat with the harvesters and gave her roasted grain.

Ruth returned to Naomi with an *ephah* of barley — about 30-40 pounds, far more than any ordinary gleaner would collect in a day. Naomi immediately understood: Boaz was showing Ruth extraordinary kindness.

## The Midnight Encounter: Faith and Courage

On Naomi's advice, Ruth went to the threshing floor where Boaz was winnowing barley. After he had eaten and drunk and lay down to sleep, Ruth uncovered his feet and lay down at his feet.

This may sound scandalous to modern ears, but it was actually a formal, symbolic act. By lying at his feet and asking him to "spread your robe over your maidservant," Ruth was formally requesting that Boaz fulfill the role of *go'el* (redeemer) — a close relative who was obligated to marry the widow of a deceased kinsman and redeem his property.

Boaz was deeply moved. He called Ruth an *eshet chayil* — "a woman of valor" — the highest praise in Jewish tradition. He promised to fulfill the role of redeemer, though he noted there was one relative closer in line who had first rights.

## The Public Redemption: Becoming Part of Israel

The next morning, Boaz went to the city gate, where legal matters were decided. He gathered ten elders as witnesses. The closer relative initially agreed to redeem Elimelech's land, but when Boaz explained that this would also require marrying Ruth the Moabite, the man declined.

According to the sages, the man's concern was halachic (legal): Could a Moabite woman truly marry into the Jewish people? The Torah forbids "a Moabite" from entering the congregation of Israel. But the rabbis explain that the prohibition applies only to Moabite *men*, not women, because it was the men who refused to provide bread and water. Moabite *women* could convert and marry Jews.

Boaz thus lawfully married Ruth before the entire community. The elders blessed them: "May the Lord make the woman who is coming into your house like Rachel and Leah, both of whom built up the House of Israel!"

## The Legacy: From Ruth to David to Messiah

God blessed Ruth and Boaz with a son named Obed. The women of Bethlehem said to Naomi: "Blessed be the Lord, who has not withheld a redeemer from you today! He will renew your life and sustain your old age; for he is born of your daughter-in-law, who loves you and is better to you than seven sons."

That last line is revolutionary: in a society where sons were everything, Ruth — a convert, a foreigner, a daughter-in-law — was praised as worth more than seven sons.

Obed became the father of Jesse, who became the father of David, the greatest king of Israel. And from David's line will come the Messiah.

The Book of Ruth ends with a genealogy that makes this point explicit:

> "Obed begot Jesse, and Jesse begot David." (Ruth 4:22)

## Why We Read Ruth on Shavuot

The Book of Ruth is traditionally read on Shavuot, the festival celebrating the giving of the Torah at Mount Sinai. Why this connection?

- **Conversion and Torah:** Just as Ruth chose to accept the Torah and become part of the Jewish people, Shavuot celebrates the moment when all of Israel stood at Sinai and said "Na'aseh v'nishma" — "We will do and we will listen." Both are moments of total commitment.

- **Harvest Season:** Ruth's story takes place during the grain harvest, which corresponds to the season of Shavuot.

- **King David's Yahrtzeit:** According to tradition, King David was born and died on Shavuot. The book that tells of his great-grandmother is thus especially appropriate.

- **Chesed (Kindness):** The Book of Ruth overflows with *chesed* — Naomi's kindness to Ruth, Ruth's loyalty to Naomi, Boaz's generosity to Ruth, and ultimately God's kindness to all of them. The Torah itself is an expression of God's *chesed* to humanity.

## Lessons for Converts and All Jews

Ruth's story offers profound lessons:

### 1. Conversion Is About Commitment, Not Convenience

Ruth didn't convert for marriage (she had already been widowed). She didn't convert for material benefit (she was walking into poverty). She converted because she genuinely came to believe in the God of Israel and wanted to be part of His people. This is the model of sincere *geirus* (conversion).

### 2. Your Past Doesn't Define Your Future

Ruth came from Moab, from a people known for cruelty and immorality. Her own ancestor Lot had committed incest. Yet through her own choices — her *chesed*, her modesty, her commitment — she became the ancestor of King David and the Messiah. Judaism teaches that who you *choose to become* matters more than who you were born.

### 3. A Convert Can Become Greater Than a Born Jew

The Talmud teaches: "A convert who comes to Judaism is dearer to God than all the Israelites who stood at Mount Sinai. For they saw the thunder and lightning and accepted the Torah; Ruth saw none of this, yet she accepted it all."

### 4. God Guides Those Who Seek Him

The phrase "it happened by chance" (*vayiker mikreha*) that Ruth came to Boaz's field teaches us: what seems like coincidence is often Divine Providence. When we take steps toward what is right, God opens doors we didn't know existed.

### 5. Kindness Has Eternal Consequences

Boaz's simple acts of kindness toward a poor foreigner led to the birth of the Davidic dynasty and the lineage of Messiah. We never know how far the ripples of our *chesed* will extend.

## A Message for Jews in China and Asia

If you are exploring Judaism in China, in a city where you may be the only Jew — or the only *potential* Jew — for hundreds of miles, Ruth's story is your story. Like her, you may have left behind familiar gods (perhaps atheism or folk religion), familiar customs, familiar family expectations. Like her, you may be gleaning knowledge from whatever sources you can find, studying alone, practicing Shabbat alone, wondering if you truly belong.

Ruth teaches you: Yes. You belong. Your commitment, your *neshama* (soul), your choice to say "Your people shall be my people, your God my God" — these matter more than where you were born or what language you speak.

Just as Ruth had only Naomi to teach her, you may have only books, websites, or occasional visits to a Chabad house. It's enough. God sees your effort. The Jewish people welcome you.

In Chinese culture, loyalty to family (*xiao* 孝) is paramount. Ruth exemplifies this perfectly — her loyalty to Naomi was so complete that she gave up everything for her mother-in-law's sake. In doing so, she also fulfilled the deepest Jewish value of *chesed*.

## Practical Takeaways

If Ruth's story inspires you:

- **Study the Book of Ruth:** Read it in full (it's only four chapters). Read it in Hebrew if you can, or with commentaries from Rashi or the Malbim.

- **Reach Out:** Contact your nearest Chabad house or an Orthodox rabbi. If you're in China, there are Chabad centers in Beijing, Shanghai, Hong Kong, and other cities. Even if you're far away, many rabbis offer guidance via email or video call.

- **Learn Gradually:** Ruth didn't become righteous overnight. She asked questions, listened to Naomi, and took one step at a time. Do the same. Start with Shabbat candle-lighting, learn one prayer, keep one mitzvah.

- **Don't Be Discouraged:** Naomi tried to dissuade Ruth three times. This isn't cruelty — it's to ensure converts are truly committed. If a rabbi seems to discourage you, understand that this is part of the process. Persistence shows sincerity.

- **Remember You're Not Alone:** Throughout history, countless people have walked Ruth's path. You are part of an ancient and honorable tradition of those who *chose* to be Jewish.

## Conclusion: Ruth's Eternal Voice

Ruth could have stayed in Moab. She could have remarried a Moabite man, returned to her family's palace, lived in comfort. Instead, she walked a dusty road to an uncertain future, driven by something deeper than logic or safety: a call of the soul.

That call is still heard today. Wherever there is someone who says, "I want to learn more about Judaism," "I want to keep Shabbat," "I want to be part of the Jewish people," Ruth's voice echoes across the millennia:

**"Wherever you go, I will go."**

Her courage is your courage. Her faith is your faith. Her place in the Jewish story can be your place too.

Welcome home.
