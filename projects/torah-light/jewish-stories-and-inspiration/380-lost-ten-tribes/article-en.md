## Introduction

In the year 722 BCE — during what corresponds to China's Spring and Autumn Period (春秋时代, 770-476 BCE), when Confucius would be born two centuries later — a catastrophic event reshaped the destiny of the Jewish people. The mighty Assyrian Empire, sweeping down from Mesopotamia, conquered the Northern Kingdom of Israel and scattered ten of the twelve tribes of Israel into exile.

These were not small clans. They were ten of Jacob's sons' descendants: **Reuben, Simeon, Dan, Naphtali, Gad, Asher, Issachar, Zebulun, Manasseh, and Ephraim** — millions of people with distinct identities, traditions, and spiritual inheritance. The Assyrians, unlike the later Babylonians who exiled the Kingdom of Judah, pursued a policy of forced resettlement and cultural assimilation. They deliberately spread the Israelites across their vast empire — from Media and Mesopotamia to the edges of Central Asia — with the explicit goal of erasing their collective identity.

And it worked. Or did it?

The tribes vanished from recorded history. They became known as the **Ten Lost Tribes** — one of the most enduring mysteries in human history. But "lost" does not necessarily mean "gone." For over 2,700 years, communities around the world have claimed descent from these scattered Israelites. From the highlands of Ethiopia to the mountains of Afghanistan, from remote villages in India to the ancient Silk Road cities of China, the search for the Lost Tribes has captivated explorers, scholars, and dreamers.

This is not just ancient history. It's a story that continues today — a story of memory, identity, exile, and return. It's also a story that resonates deeply across cultures. Just as the Chinese people endured countless invasions, migrations, and diaspora experiences throughout their long history — yet preserved their cultural identity across continents — so too have scattered peoples maintained fragments of ancient Israelite tradition in the most unexpected places.

Let us explore the historical record, the theories, and the living communities who may carry the legacy of Israel's Lost Tribes.

## The Assyrian Conquest: What Actually Happened?

To understand the mystery, we must first understand the catastrophe.

### The Divided Kingdom

After the death of King Solomon in 931 BCE, the unified Kingdom of Israel split into two:

- **The Northern Kingdom of Israel** — Ten tribes under the rule of Jeroboam, with its capital eventually in Samaria (שומרון, Shomron)
- **The Southern Kingdom of Judah** — Two tribes (Judah and Benjamin, plus many Levites) under the Davidic dynasty, with its capital in Jerusalem

The Northern Kingdom was larger, wealthier, and more populous. But it was also politically unstable and spiritually wayward. The prophets — Elijah, Elisha, Amos, Hosea — thundered warnings that the people's abandonment of God would lead to disaster.

### The Assyrian Invasion

The disaster came in waves. The Assyrian Empire, one of the most brutal military machines in ancient history, began encroaching on Israelite territory in the 740s BCE. By 722 BCE, after a three-year siege, the capital city of Samaria fell to the Assyrian king Shalmaneser V and his successor Sargon II.

The Bible records the event in 2 Kings 17:5-6:

> *"The king of Assyria marched against the whole land and came to Samaria; he besieged it for three years. In the ninth year of Hoshea, the king of Assyria captured Samaria. He deported the Israelites to Assyria and settled them in Halah and Habor, on the river Gozan, and in the cities of the Medes."*

Assyrian records confirm this. Sargon II boasted in his annals:

> *"I besieged and conquered Samaria, led away as booty 27,290 inhabitants of it."*

But this number — around 27,000 — represents only a fraction of the population. Scholars estimate that hundreds of thousands, perhaps over a million Israelites, were deported or scattered.

### The Assyrian Strategy: Forced Assimilation

Unlike the later Babylonian exile of Judah (586 BCE), which kept the Jewish people together and allowed them to maintain their identity, the Assyrian policy was **deliberate cultural genocide**. They didn't just relocate populations — they fragmented and scattered them across vast distances, mixing them with other conquered peoples, imposing new languages, new gods, new identities.

The Assyrian Empire stretched from Egypt to the Persian Gulf, from Anatolia to the borders of India. Deportees were resettled in:

- **Media** (modern-day northwestern Iran and Kurdistan)
- **Mesopotamia** (modern Iraq)
- **Halah and Habor** (along the Khabur River in Syria)
- **The cities of the Medes** (northern Iran)

This policy of scattering and mixing is why the Ten Tribes "vanished." They were meant to disappear.

But human memory is stubborn. Cultural identity, even under immense pressure, can persist for generations — sometimes for millennia.

## Did Any Israelites Remain?

Before we explore where the tribes went, it's important to note: **not everyone was exiled**.

Archaeological and biblical evidence suggests that many Israelites fled south to the Kingdom of Judah before and after the Assyrian conquest. Jerusalem's population exploded in the 8th century BCE, likely absorbing refugees from the north. Thus, descendants of all twelve tribes may have been preserved within the Jewish people who survived the later Babylonian exile and returned to build the Second Temple.

Additionally, some Israelites remained in the land. The Assyrians repopulated Samaria with foreign settlers (the origin of the Samaritans), but a remnant of Israelites stayed, intermingling and adapting. This remnant maintained a connection to Israelite tradition, though modified and contested.

So the "Lost" Tribes are not entirely lost — some were absorbed into Judah, some remained in the land, and some... well, that's where the story gets fascinating.

## Theories and Claimant Communities

Over the centuries, dozens of communities around the world have been identified — or have self-identified — as descendants of the Lost Tribes. Some theories are based on oral tradition, some on linguistic or cultural parallels, some on recent DNA evidence, and some on pure speculation.

Let's explore the most compelling cases.

### 1. **The Bnei Menashe: India's Children of Manasseh**

In the remote northeastern states of India — Manipur and Mizoram, near the borders of Myanmar and Bangladesh — live a people called the **Bnei Menashe** (בני מנשה, "Children of Manasseh"). They number around 10,000 and practice a form of Judaism, claiming descent from the tribe of Manasseh.

#### The Tradition

According to their oral history, their ancestors were exiled from Israel by the Assyrians, traveled eastward through Persia and Afghanistan, crossed into Central Asia, and eventually reached the mountains of China. Over many centuries, they migrated through Tibet, Burma, and finally settled in northeastern India, where they became the Kuki-Chin-Mizo peoples.

They preserved traditions remarkably similar to ancient Israelite practices:

- **Animal sacrifice** similar to Temple practices
- **Circumcision** on the eighth day
- **Levirate marriage** (a man marrying his deceased brother's widow)
- **Tzitzit**-like fringes on garments
- Oral legends of a homeland to the west, across many rivers

In the 19th century, Christian missionaries arrived and were astonished by these customs. Some Bnei Menashe began returning to Jewish practice, and in the late 20th century, Rabbi Eliyahu Avichail of the organization **Amishav** championed their cause.

#### Modern Return

Since the 1990s, thousands of Bnei Menashe have undergone formal conversion to Judaism (to satisfy rabbinic authorities about their status) and made *aliyah* (עלייה, immigration) to Israel. They now serve in the Israeli Defense Forces, live in communities across Israel, and have reunited with the Jewish people after nearly 2,700 years.

Their story is one of the most remarkable in the Lost Tribes saga — not because their connection is definitively proven, but because they have *chosen* to reconnect with the Jewish people and rebuild their Israelite identity.

#### A Chinese Parallel

The Bnei Menashe story resonates with Chinese history in a particular way. Just as many Chinese communities migrated across vast distances — the Hakka (客家人) people, for example, who migrated south over centuries and preserved distinct dialects and customs — so too did the Bnei Menashe preserve a fragmented memory of origin through millennia of migration.

### 2. **Beta Israel: The Ethiopian Jews**

Perhaps the most famous claimant community is **Beta Israel** (ቤተ እስራኤል in Ge'ez, "House of Israel"), the Jewish community of Ethiopia, often called "Falasha" (though this term is considered derogatory by many).

#### Ancient Origins

The Beta Israel maintained for centuries that they were descendants of the tribe of **Dan**, one of the Lost Tribes. Their own traditions, however, also include a legend connecting them to the time of King Solomon and the Queen of Sheba — suggesting they may predate the Assyrian exile entirely.

What is certain is that the Beta Israel practiced a unique form of Judaism:

- They observed the Torah (the Five Books of Moses) but had no knowledge of the Talmud or later rabbinic law, suggesting their separation from mainstream Judaism occurred very early.
- They observed Shabbat, kashrut (כשרות, dietary laws), and the biblical festivals.
- They practiced ritual purity laws with great strictness.
- They had their own religious leaders called *kessim* (priests) rather than rabbis.
- Their language of prayer was Ge'ez, the ancient liturgical language of Ethiopia, not Hebrew.

For centuries, they lived in isolation in the mountains of northern Ethiopia (around Gondar), facing periodic persecution, forced conversions, and marginalization.

#### The Great Return

In the 1980s and 1990s, Israel conducted two dramatic airlifts — **Operation Moses** (1984) and **Operation Solomon** (1991) — bringing tens of thousands of Beta Israel to Israel, rescuing them from famine, civil war, and persecution. Today, over 150,000 Ethiopian Jews live in Israel.

#### DNA Evidence

Recent genetic studies have shown that Ethiopian Jews carry **Middle Eastern genetic markers** alongside their predominantly African ancestry, suggesting ancient admixture with Jewish populations. While DNA alone cannot definitively prove descent from the Lost Tribes, it does confirm that Beta Israel have ancient Jewish ancestry that predates their isolation in Ethiopia.

Their integration into Israeli society has been complex — facing both acceptance and discrimination, celebrating their heritage while adapting to modern Jewish life. But their return represents one of the most moving chapters in Jewish history: a lost community, separated for millennia, coming home.

### 3. **The Pashtuns: Afghanistan's Sons of Israel?**

One of the most controversial and fascinating theories concerns the **Pashtuns** (also called Pathans), the largest ethnic group in Afghanistan and a significant population in Pakistan.

#### The "Bani Israel" Tradition

Many Pashtun tribes have an oral tradition claiming descent from the **Bani Israel** (بني إسرائيل, "Children of Israel"). According to this tradition:

- They are descended from King Saul (Talut in Islamic tradition) and the tribes of Israel.
- After the Assyrian exile, their ancestors migrated eastward through Persia and settled in the region that is now Afghanistan and Pakistan.
- They were later converted to Islam but retained memory of their Israelite origin.

Several pieces of cultural evidence are cited:

- **Tribal names**: Some Pashtun tribes bear names reminiscent of Israelite origins (e.g., Yusufzai = "sons of Joseph," Afridi = possibly related to Ephraim, Levani = possibly Levites).
- **Customs**: Lighting candles on Friday night (though this could be Islamic practice), circumcision on the eighth day, levirate marriage, wearing fringes on garments, and a strong emphasis on hospitality and honor codes similar to biblical law.
- **Physical appearance**: Some Pashtuns have lighter skin, hair, and eyes than surrounding populations, though this likely reflects Central Asian and Persian ancestry rather than Israelite origin.

#### Skepticism and Scholarly Debate

Most historians are skeptical. The Pashtun tribal code, **Pashtunwali**, is deeply rooted in local pre-Islamic and Islamic traditions. The tribal names might be coincidental or adapted over time. And DNA studies have not found clear genetic links to Middle Eastern Jewish populations (though such studies are complicated by millennia of intermarriage and migration).

Nonetheless, the tradition persists. Some Pashtun elders still tell their grandchildren: "We are Pashtun, and Pashtuns are the Children of Israel."

For Jews and Pashtuns alike, the question remains open — a tantalizing mystery at the crossroads of history, faith, and identity.

### 4. **The Kaifeng Jews and the Silk Road Connection**

Here we arrive at one of the most intriguing connections to Chinese history: the possibility that some Lost Tribes reached **China** via the ancient Silk Road.

#### The Jews of Kaifeng (开封)

The most documented Jewish community in Chinese history is the **Kaifeng Jewish community** (开封犹太人). Historical records show that Jews arrived in Kaifeng — then the capital of the Song Dynasty (宋朝, 960-1279 CE) — likely as merchants traveling along the Silk Road from Persia, Central Asia, or India.

By the 12th century, the Kaifeng Jews had built a synagogue and established a thriving community. They spoke Chinese, adopted Chinese names, dressed in traditional Chinese clothing, and integrated deeply into society. They were known as the *Tiao Jin Jiao* (挑筋教, "those who remove the sinew" — a reference to the Jewish practice of removing the sciatic nerve from meat, as commanded in Genesis 32:32).

Over the centuries, the community gradually assimilated. The synagogue was destroyed and rebuilt several times (floods, fires, and the Yellow River's course changes devastated Kaifeng repeatedly). By the 19th century, the community had largely disappeared as a distinct entity, though descendants remain and some have reconnected with Jewish identity in recent decades.

#### Were They Lost Tribesmen?

**Some theories suggest** that the Kaifeng Jews — or other Jewish communities that once existed along the Silk Road — might have included descendants of the Lost Tribes who migrated eastward after the Assyrian exile.

The reasoning:

- **Timing**: The Assyrian deportations (722 BCE) placed Israelites in Media and points east — precisely the regions through which the Silk Road would later pass.
- **Migration patterns**: Displaced populations often followed trade routes. If Israelites were resettled in areas that became part of Silk Road networks, some could have migrated eastward over centuries.
- **The Bnei Menashe connection**: If the Bnei Menashe tradition of traveling from Israel through Central Asia to China (and then to India) is accurate, it suggests that Silk Road migration was indeed a route taken by ancient Israelite exiles.

However, most scholars believe the Kaifeng Jews were **Persian or Central Asian Jewish merchants** who arrived in China during the Song Dynasty — long after the Lost Tribes period but still representing the Jewish diaspora's reach.

Still, the possibility remains tantalizing. Could some of the Lost Tribes have traveled as far as the Middle Kingdom? Could fragments of Israelite identity have been preserved among Chinese Jewish communities before they assimilated?

#### A Reflection on Diaspora

The Kaifeng Jewish story mirrors a theme deeply familiar to Chinese culture: **the tension between preserving identity and adapting to a dominant culture**. Just as overseas Chinese communities (华侨) throughout Southeast Asia, the Americas, and beyond have navigated the balance between maintaining Chinese language, customs, and values while integrating into new societies, so too did the Kaifeng Jews navigate their dual identity.

In both cases, the question arises: When does adaptation become assimilation? When does a community cease to be what it was and become something new? The Kaifeng Jews ultimately dissolved into the broader Chinese population — but their memory, their gravestones, and their descendants remain, a testament to a people who once lived between two worlds.

### 5. **Other Claimants: From Japan to the Americas**

The search for the Lost Tribes has generated countless theories, some scholarly and some wildly speculative:

- **The Lemba of Southern Africa**: A Bantu-speaking people in Zimbabwe and South Africa who practice circumcision, dietary restrictions, and other customs resembling Jewish law. DNA testing has revealed a **high frequency of the Cohen Modal Haplotype** (a genetic marker found among Jewish priestly families) among Lemba men, suggesting ancient Jewish ancestry, though the connection to the Lost Tribes specifically is uncertain.

- **Japanese-Israelite Theory**: Some fringe theorists have claimed that the Japanese people, or certain Japanese customs (such as Shinto rituals), derive from ancient Israelite origins. This theory lacks credible evidence and is not taken seriously by mainstream scholars, but it reflects the global fascination with the Lost Tribes mystery.

- **Native American Theories**: In the 19th century, some Christian groups (notably the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints) taught that Native Americans were descendants of Israelites. Modern DNA evidence has disproven this claim — Native Americans descended from East Asian populations who migrated across the Bering land bridge.

- **The Igbo of Nigeria**: Some Igbo people in Nigeria claim descent from the tribe of Gad and observe practices they believe are Israelite in origin. A small number have converted to Judaism and emigrated to Israel.

The proliferation of such claims reflects something profound: the **enduring power of the Lost Tribes narrative**. It speaks to universal human experiences — exile, loss, the search for belonging, and the hope of return.

## What Does "Lost" Really Mean?

The question "Where did the Lost Tribes go?" assumes that they went *somewhere* as intact, identifiable communities. But the Assyrian strategy was precisely to prevent this. They scattered, mixed, and assimilated the Israelites.

So what does "lost" mean?

### Three Possibilities

1. **They assimilated completely.** Over 27 centuries, the vast majority of the exiled Israelites likely intermarried with local populations, adopted local languages and religions (first paganism, later Christianity and Islam), and ceased to see themselves as Israelites. Their descendants are alive today — but they don't know who they are. They might be Kurds, Iranians, Turks, Arabs, or any of dozens of ethnic groups across the Middle East and Central Asia. In this sense, the tribes are "lost" because their identity is lost, even though their bloodlines continue.

2. **Fragments of identity survived in isolated communities.** Groups like the Bnei Menashe, Beta Israel, and possibly others preserved oral traditions, religious practices, or cultural memories that maintained a thread of connection to ancient Israel. These communities are "found" when they reconnect with the Jewish people, but they represent only a tiny remnant of the original exiles.

3. **They were absorbed into Judah and the Jewish people.** As mentioned earlier, many northern Israelites fled to Judah before or after the Assyrian conquest. Their descendants became part of the Jewish people — meaning that modern Jews carry the legacy of all twelve tribes, not just Judah and Benjamin.

### The Kabbalistic View: Nothing Is Ever Lost

Jewish mystical tradition (Kabbalah) teaches that nothing in the spiritual realm is ever truly lost. The souls of the Lost Tribes, and the sparks of holiness they carried, are scattered throughout the world. The task of every generation is to "gather the exiles" (קיבוץ גלויות, *kibbutz galuyot*) — not just physically, but spiritually.

In this view, whenever a person from any background discovers their Jewish roots and returns to the covenant, they are potentially a descendant of the Lost Tribes coming home. The Bnei Menashe, Beta Israel, and others are fulfilling this ancient promise.

## The Lessons: Memory, Identity, and Return

What can we learn from the story of the Lost Ten Tribes?

### 1. **Memory is Resilient**

Even after 2,700 years — 27 centuries of war, migration, forced conversion, and cultural erasure — fragments of memory survived. Communities preserved stories of a distant homeland, of practices they didn't fully understand, of an identity that whispered through generations.

This resilience of memory is something Chinese culture knows intimately. Despite the upheavals of dynasties, invasions, revolutions, and modernization, core elements of Chinese culture — the Confucian values, the written language, the reverence for ancestors — have endured for millennia. Memory, when embedded in ritual, story, and family, can outlast empires.

### 2. **Identity is Both Fixed and Fluid**

The Beta Israel are genetically and historically Jewish, yet their Judaism evolved in isolation and looks different from rabbinic Judaism. The Bnei Menashe preserved fragments of Israelite practice but needed to undergo formal conversion to rejoin the Jewish people. The Kaifeng Jews were undeniably Jewish, yet they assimilated into Chinese society and ceased to be a distinct community.

What does it mean to "be" part of a people? Is it blood? Practice? Belief? Self-identification? Community recognition?

The Lost Tribes challenge us to think deeply about identity — how it is constructed, how it is maintained, and how it can be reclaimed.

### 3. **Exile and Return are Universal Themes**

The Jewish experience of exile (גלות, *galut*) and return (שיבה, *shiva*) resonates across cultures. The Chinese diaspora knows what it means to leave the homeland and preserve identity across generations. Indigenous peoples worldwide know what it means to be displaced and to fight for the right to return.

The Lost Tribes are not just a Jewish story. They are a human story — a story about what happens when peoples are torn from their homes, scattered across the earth, and forced to choose between remembering and forgetting.

---

## Conclusion: The Search Continues

Nearly three millennia after the Assyrian conquest, the mystery of the Lost Ten Tribes remains unsolved. We may never know with certainty where all the exiles went, how many communities preserved their identity, or how many bloodlines quietly continued in populations that forgot their origins.

But the search continues.

Communities around the world still explore their connections to ancient Israel. DNA technology offers new tools to trace ancient migrations. And the State of Israel, through organizations like **Shavei Israel** (which works with "lost" Jewish communities worldwide), continues to welcome home those who wish to reconnect.

Perhaps the deepest truth of the Lost Tribes is this: **they are only as lost as we allow them to be.** Every act of welcoming, every effort to investigate a tradition or honor a claim, every person who chooses to reconnect with Jewish identity — these are acts of gathering the exiles, of reversing the ancient scattering, of healing the wound that the Assyrians inflicted 2,700 years ago.

In a sense, the Lost Tribes are still coming home.

And perhaps that's the ultimate lesson: **the journey of return is not just about reaching a destination. It's about the courage to remember, the willingness to explore forgotten paths, and the faith that even what seems lost forever can, one day, be found.**

*May the scattered be gathered, and may all who seek return find their way home.*
