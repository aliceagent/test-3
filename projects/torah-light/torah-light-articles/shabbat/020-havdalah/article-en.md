## Introduction

As the first three stars appear in the Saturday evening sky, Jewish families around the world gather for one of the most poignant and beautiful ceremonies in Jewish life: Havdalah. The word "Havdalah" (הַבְדָּלָה) comes from the Hebrew root meaning "to distinguish" or "to separate." This brief but profound ritual marks the end of Shabbat and the beginning of the new week.

If Kiddush welcomes Shabbat like greeting an honored guest, Havdalah is the bittersweet farewell—acknowledging the departure of something precious while carrying its light forward into the week ahead. The ceremony engages all five senses: we taste wine, smell fragrant spices, see the braided candle's flame, hear the blessings, and feel the cup in our hands.

For someone experiencing Havdalah for the first time, it might seem like a simple ritual with wine, spices, and a candle. But it contains deep wisdom about transitions, distinctions, and how to carry spiritual elevation into everyday life. This article provides the complete Havdalah text in Hebrew with full English and Chinese translations, explains the meaning behind each element, and offers practical guidance for performing this beautiful ceremony.

## What is Havdalah?

Havdalah is the ceremony that formally ends Shabbat and separates the holy day from the weekdays that follow. Just as Kiddush sanctifies the beginning of Shabbat over wine, Havdalah marks its conclusion with blessings over wine, aromatic spices, and a multi-wicked candle.

The ceremony consists of four blessings:
1. **Wine (יין)** — Symbol of joy and spiritual accomplishment
2. **Spices (בשמים, besamim)** — To comfort the soul at Shabbat's departure
3. **Candle (נר)** — Representing light, wisdom, and new beginnings
4. **The Havdalah blessing itself** — Distinguishing sacred from ordinary

These four elements form the acrostic **יבנה (Yavneh)**, which also means "it will be built" — a hopeful message that the week ahead will be constructive and meaningful.

### Historical Context

The practice of Havdalah dates back to the time of the Second Temple period and the early rabbinic era—during what corresponds to China's Han Dynasty (汉朝, 206 BCE – 220 CE). The Talmud (Pesachim 106a) records that the Men of the Great Assembly first established the requirement to recite Havdalah over wine, and the sages of the Mishnaic period codified its precise format.

The tradition teaches that when the first Shabbat ended at the beginning of creation, Adam became frightened as darkness fell. God then gave him the gift of fire—the ability to create light and transform the world through human action. This is why we bless fire specifically on Saturday night at Havdalah.

## The Elements of Havdalah: Symbolism and Meaning

### Wine: Celebrating What We've Accomplished

We begin Havdalah with wine (or grape juice), just as we began Shabbat. Wine symbolizes joy and transformation—simple grapes transformed into something refined and precious. 

It's customary to fill the cup until it overflows slightly, symbolizing our wish for blessings to overflow into the coming week. After the blessings, the one reciting Havdalah drinks at least 2 ounces (about 59ml) of the wine.

**Cultural Connection:** In Chinese tea ceremony tradition (茶道), the careful pouring and sharing of refined beverages also marks transitions and expresses wishes for abundance. Both traditions use transformed agricultural products (grapes→wine, tea leaves→tea) to elevate ordinary moments into sacred ones.

### Spices: Comforting the Departing Soul

According to Jewish mystical tradition, we receive an extra soul (*neshama yeteira*) during Shabbat—a heightened spiritual sensitivity and capacity for holiness. As Shabbat departs, so does this extra soul, leaving us feeling somewhat diminished.

The sweet-smelling spices (usually cloves, cinnamon, or fragrant herbs) are like "spiritual smelling salts" that revive and comfort our soul at this loss. We pass around the spice box so everyone can inhale the fragrance, carrying the sweetness of Shabbat into the week.

**Historical Note:** Sephardic and Mizrahi communities often use fresh aromatic plants like myrtle or mint, while Ashkenazi tradition typically uses dried cloves. Either fulfills the mitzvah beautifully.

### The Braided Candle: Light and Darkness, Wisdom and Confusion

The Havdalah candle must have at least two wicks (or two separate flames held together), creating a larger, intertwined flame. This braided flame symbolizes several profound ideas:

- **Unity in diversity**: Multiple wicks coming together to create one greater light
- **The gift of fire**: Commemorating God giving Adam fire on the first Saturday night
- **Light versus darkness**: The ability to distinguish between wisdom and confusion, good and evil, truth and falsehood

After blessing the candle, we hold our hands up to the light and look at our fingernails, using the candle's light to see the difference between the bright side of our hands and the shadows. This physical act represents our spiritual task: to develop the ability to distinguish and discern in all areas of life.

**Cultural Parallel:** The Confucian concept of *辨 (biàn)* — the ability to distinguish and discern — is central to moral development. Mencius taught that wisdom begins with the ability to distinguish right from wrong, appropriate from inappropriate. Havdalah's emphasis on distinction (*havdalah* literally means separation/distinction) resonates with this fundamental principle of Chinese philosophy.

### The Havdalah Blessing: Making Distinctions

The climactic blessing praises God who distinguishes between:
- **Sacred and ordinary** (*kodesh* and *chol*)
- **Light and darkness**
- **Israel and the nations**
- **The seventh day and the six working days**

The ability to distinguish is perhaps the greatest tool we have for appreciating anything. When we see things as unique and distinct, they stand out as special. Without distinctions, everything blurs together into sameness.

Shabbat is special precisely because it's *different* from the rest of the week. By marking its ending with Havdalah, we acknowledge this difference and commit to carrying Shabbat's light—its peace, holiness, and elevated consciousness—into our weekday lives.

## The Complete Havdalah Text

### Introductory Verses (הנה אל)

These biblical verses (from Isaiah, Psalms, and Esther) express trust in God's salvation and the joy that came to the Jewish people:

**Hebrew:**

הִנֵּה אֵל יְשׁוּעָתִי, אֶבְטַח וְלֹא אֶפְחָד, כִּי עָזִּי וְזִמְרָת יָהּ ה', וַיְהִי לִי לִישׁוּעָה. וּשְׁאַבְתֶּם מַיִם בְּשָׂשׂוֹן, מִמַּעַיְנֵי הַיְשׁוּעָה. לַה' הַיְשׁוּעָה, עַל עַמְּךָ בִרְכָתֶךָ סֶלָה. ה' צְבָאוֹת עִמָּנוּ, מִשְׂגָּב לָנוּ אֱלֹהֵי יַעֲקֹב סֶלָה. ה' צְבָאוֹת, אַשְׁרֵי אָדָם בֹּטֵחַ בָּךְ. ה' הוֹשִׁיעָה, הַמֶּלֶךְ יַעֲנֵנוּ בְיוֹם קָרְאֵנוּ. לַיְּהוּדִים הָיְתָה אוֹרָה וְשִׂמְחָה וְשָׂשׂוֹן וִיקָר. כֵּן תִּהְיֶה לָּנוּ. כּוֹס יְשׁוּעוֹת אֶשָּׂא, וּבְשֵׁם ה' אֶקְרָא.

**Transliteration:**

*Hinei El yeshuati, evtach velo efchad, ki ozi vezimrat Yah, Adonai vayehi li liyeshuah. Ushavtem mayim besason mimainei hayeshuah. La'Adonai hayeshuah, al amcha virchatecha, selah. Adonai Tzeva'ot imanu, misgav lanu, Elohei Ya'akov, selah. Adonai Tzeva'ot, ashrei adam boteach bach. Adonai hoshiah, hamelech ya'aneinu veyom kareinu. Layehudim hayetah orah vesimchah vesason vikar. Kein tiheyeh lanu. Kos yeshuot esa uvesheim Adonai ekra.*

**English Translation:**

"Behold, God is my salvation, I will trust and not be afraid, for my strength and song is God, and He has become my salvation. You will draw water with joy from the wellsprings of salvation. Salvation belongs to God; may Your blessing rest upon Your people, Selah. The Lord of Hosts is with us, the God of Jacob is a fortress protecting us, Selah. Lord of Hosts, happy is the one who trusts in You. God, save us; may the King answer us on the day we call. The Jews had light, happiness, joy, and honor; so may it be for us. I will raise the cup of salvation and call out in the name of God."

**Chinese Translation (中文翻译):**

看哪，上帝是我的拯救，我将信靠而不害怕，因为我的力量和颂歌是上帝，祂成为了我的救恩。你们将欢欣地从救恩的泉源中汲水。救恩属于上帝；愿祢的祝福临到祢的子民，细拉。万军之主与我们同在，雅各的上帝是我们的堡垒，细拉。万军之主啊，信靠祢的人是有福的。上帝啊，求祢拯救；愿君王在我们呼求的日子应允我们。犹太人曾拥有光明、喜乐、欢欣和尊荣；愿我们也如此。我要举起救恩的杯，奉上帝的名呼求。

### Blessing Over Wine (על היין)

**Hebrew:**

בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה ה', אֱלֹהֵינוּ מֶלֶךְ הָעוֹלָם, בּוֹרֵא פְּרִי הַגָּפֶן.

**Transliteration:**

*Baruch atah Adonai, Eloheinu melech ha'olam, borei pri hagafen.*

**English Translation:**

"Blessed are You, Lord our God, King of the universe, who creates the fruit of the vine."

**Chinese Translation:**

我们的上帝，宇宙的君王，您是应当称颂的，您创造了葡萄树的果实。

**Hebrew:**

### The Havdalah Blessing (ברכת ההבדלה)

**Hebrew:**

בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה ה', אֱלֹהֵינוּ מֶלֶךְ הָעוֹלָם, הַמַּבְדִּיל בֵּין קֹדֶשׁ לְחוֹל, בֵּין אוֹר לְחֹשֶׁךְ, בֵּין יִשְׂרָאֵל לָעַמִּים, בֵּין יוֹם הַשְּׁבִיעִי לְשֵׁשֶׁת יְמֵי הַמַּעֲשֶׂה. בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה ה', הַמַּבְדִּיל בֵּין קֹדֶשׁ לְחוֹל.

**Transliteration:**

*Baruch atah Adonai, Eloheinu melech ha'olam, ha-mavdil bein kodesh lechol, bein or lechoshech, bein Yisrael la'amim, bein yom hashvi'i lesheshet yemei hama'aseh. Baruch atah Adonai, ha-mavdil bein kodesh lechol.*

**English Translation:**

"Blessed are You, Lord our God, King of the universe, who distinguishes between sacred and ordinary, between light and darkness, between Israel and the nations, between the seventh day and the six working days. Blessed are You, Lord, who distinguishes between sacred and ordinary."

**Chinese Translation:**

我们的上帝，宇宙的君王，您是应当称颂的，您区分了神圣与世俗，光明与黑暗，以色列与列国，第七日与六个工作日。我们的上帝，您是应当称颂的，您区分了神圣与世俗。

*(The leader drinks at least 2 ounces of wine, then the candle is extinguished in the leftover wine)*

## Practical Guide: How to Perform Havdalah

### What You'll Need

1. **Wine or grape juice** — Preferably red wine; fill a kiddush cup
2. **A spice box** — Filled with cloves, cinnamon, or fragrant herbs
3. **A Havdalah candle** — A braided candle with multiple wicks (or hold two candles together)
4. **A small plate or dish** — To catch overflow and extinguish the candle

### When to Perform Havdalah

Shabbat officially ends when three medium-sized stars are visible in the evening sky. This varies by location and season.

**Quick calculation for North America:** Add 1 hour and 10 minutes to Friday candle lighting time. For example, if candles were lit at 6:00 PM Friday, Havdalah is approximately 7:10 PM Saturday.

For precise times for your city, use: **www.hebcal.com** or **www.chabad.org/shabbat**

### Step-by-Step Procedure

**1. Preparation**
- Fill the kiddush cup with wine until it overflows slightly onto the plate
- Light the Havdalah candle (someone other than the person reciting should hold it)
- Have the spice box ready

**2. Stand or Sit**
You may stand or sit for Havdalah. Once you begin, do not speak until the ceremony is completed.

**3. Recite the Blessings in Order**

- **First:** Introductory verses (*Hinei El*)
- **Second:** Blessing over wine (*borei pri hagafen*) — but don't drink yet!
- **Third:** Blessing over spices (*borei minei vesamim*) — pass the spice box around for everyone to smell
- **Fourth:** Blessing over the candle (*borei me'orei ha'esh*) — hold hands up to see fingernails in the light
- **Fifth:** The Havdalah blessing (*ha-mavdil*) — the final blessing

**4. Drink the Wine**
The person who recited the blessings now drinks at least 2 ounces (about 59ml) of the wine.

**5. Extinguish the Candle**
Pour some of the leftover wine into the dish and immerse the candle flame in it until it's extinguished. The sizzling sound symbolizes the reluctant departure of Shabbat.

**6. Optional Customs**
- Some dip their fingers in the leftover wine and touch their eyelids or pockets (based on Psalms 19:9, "The commandment of God is clear, enlightening the eyes")
- Some light two new candles from the Havdalah candle before extinguishing it, to "extend the light of Shabbat" into the week

**7. After Havdalah**
It's traditional to sing "**Eliyahu HaNavi**" (Elijah the Prophet) — expressing hope that Elijah will soon herald the coming of the Messiah and the ultimate redemption. Then wish everyone "**Shavua Tov!**" (Have a good week!) or in Yiddish, "**Gut Voch!**"

### If You're Alone

If performing Havdalah by yourself:
- Place the candle in a holder instead of having someone hold it
- You can still smell the spices yourself
- All blessings remain the same
- The ceremony is equally valid and meaningful

### If You Forget or Can't Do It Saturday Night

If you forget or are unable to recite Havdalah on Saturday night, it can be performed as late as Tuesday sunset. However:
- The blessings over spices and candle are **omitted**
- Only the blessing on wine and the Havdalah blessing itself are recited

Before doing any "weekday" activity after Shabbat ends, you should at minimum say: "**Baruch ha-mavdil bein kodesh lechol**" (Blessed is the One who separates between sacred and ordinary). This brief declaration permits you to do weekday activities, though you're still obligated to perform the full Havdalah ceremony over wine later.

## The Deeper Meaning: Living With Distinctions

Havdalah is not merely a ritual marking time; it's a philosophical statement about how we should live our lives.

### The Power of Distinction

The greatest tool we have for appreciating anything is the ability to distinguish and differentiate. When we blur everything together, life becomes monotonous and meaningless. But when we cultivate the ability to see distinctions — between sacred and ordinary, between wisdom and folly, between what truly matters and what doesn't — we gain clarity and purpose.

**Confucian Parallel:** The concept of *礼 (lǐ)* in Confucianism emphasizes proper distinctions and appropriate behavior in different contexts. Just as Confucius taught that a noble person (*junzi*, 君子) must distinguish between different relationships and contexts to act appropriately, Havdalah trains us to recognize and honor different domains of existence.

### Carrying Shabbat Into the Week

Havdalah is bittersweet: we're sad to see Shabbat go, yet we're energized to begin the week ahead. The ceremony doesn't simply end Shabbat; it helps us *carry Shabbat forward*.

The wine represents the spiritual accomplishments we've made during Shabbat — the time spent with family, the Torah study, the prayers, the rest. We hope these "overflow" into our week.

The spices comfort us, reminding us that even though the extra soul departs, the sweetness of Shabbat can linger.

The candle's light — created by bringing multiple flames together — reminds us that the unity and peace we experienced on Shabbat can illuminate our weekday work and relationships.

### A Weekly Opportunity for Growth

Every Saturday night, Havdalah offers us a fresh start. We distinguish between what was (Shabbat) and what can be (the week ahead). We acknowledge that the week is different from Shabbat, yet we commit to infusing it with Shabbat's holiness.

This weekly rhythm of elevation (Shabbat) and application (the week) creates a spiral of growth. Each Shabbat, we rise to a higher spiritual level. Each week, we bring that elevation into our daily lives. Over time, we transform not just Shabbat, but ourselves and our world.

## Cultural Connection: Transitions and Tea Ceremonies

In Chinese tradition, particularly in *Daoist* philosophy and tea ceremony practice, transitions between states are considered significant moments worthy of attention and ritual.

The *道 (Dao)* emphasizes awareness of change and transition. Just as the *Yi Jing* (易经, Book of Changes) teaches that understanding transitions is key to wisdom, Havdalah sanctifies the transition from rest to work, from elevation to application.

The Chinese tea ceremony (茶道, *chadao*) shares with Havdalah an emphasis on:
- **Mindful presence** during transitions
- **Engaging the senses** to deepen awareness
- **Marking significance** through deliberate ritual
- **Community sharing** of the experience
- **Carrying forward** the tranquility achieved

Both traditions recognize that how we end one state and begin another shapes the quality of what follows.

## Common Questions

### Can women recite Havdalah?

Yes. While traditionally the male head of household recited Havdalah, women are fully obligated in this mitzvah and may recite it. In contemporary Orthodox practice, women commonly make Havdalah for themselves and others.

### What if I don't have grape juice or wine?

Preferably use wine or grape juice. If absolutely unavailable, you can use other important beverages (called *chamar hamedina*) such as beer or tea, though some authorities disagree on this. Consult a rabbi for guidance in specific situations.

### What spices should I use?

- **Ashkenazi tradition:** Cloves (most common), cinnamon, or a mixture
- **Sephardic tradition:** Fresh aromatic plants like myrtle (*hadas*), mint, or rosemary
- Any fragrant spice that has a pleasant smell fulfills the mitzvah

### Why do we look at our fingernails?

This custom serves multiple purposes:
1. **Practical:** We demonstrate that the candle produces enough light to actually use (distinguishing between the nail and the skin)
2. **Symbolic:** The contrast between light and shadow on our hands represents our task to distinguish between good and evil, wisdom and folly
3. **Traditional:** Some say looking at fingernails, which grow throughout Shabbat, reminds us of the blessing and growth we received

### Can I use a regular candle?

The candle should have at least two wicks creating one joined flame. You can:
- Use a special braided Havdalah candle (sold in Jewish stores or online)
- Hold two regular candles together with flames touching
- Twist two birthday candles together

The multi-wick candle symbolizes unity and teaches that multiple lights together create greater illumination than any single flame.

### Why do we extinguish the candle in wine?

This demonstrates that the candle was lit solely for the mitzvah of Havdalah, not for practical use. Once the mitzvah is complete, we extinguish it. The wine that fulfilled one mitzvah (drinking) now fulfills another (extinguishing the sacred flame).

## Practical Application: Making Havdalah Your Own

### Creating a Weekly Tradition

Even if you're far from a Jewish community, you can make Havdalah a beautiful weekly ritual:

- **Prepare ahead:** Have your Havdalah supplies ready in a designated spot
- **Invite friends:** Havdalah is wonderful to share with others, Jewish or non-Jewish
- **Sing together:** Learn the melodies for the blessings and "Eliyahu HaNavi"
- **Share intentions:** After Havdalah, each person can share one thing they'll carry from Shabbat into the week

### Havdalah in China: Practical Tips

**Where to get supplies:**
- **Wine:** Kosher wine can be ordered online or use any quality grape juice
- **Spices:** Cloves are available in any Chinese supermarket (丁香, dīngxiāng)
- **Candles:** Braided candles can be ordered online, or simply use two regular candles held together
- **Spice boxes:** Beautiful decorative containers can be found in Chinese markets and adapted for use

**Connecting with community:**
- Contact your nearest Chabad house (see Article #65 for directory)
- Join online Havdalah ceremonies via Zoom (many communities worldwide host them)
- Share photos and experiences with other Jewish learners on social media

### Deepening Your Experience

As you become familiar with the basic ceremony, consider:

- **Study the Hebrew:** Learn to recite at least the four blessings in Hebrew
- **Understand the verses:** Read the introductory passages from Isaiah and Psalms in English/Chinese to appreciate their meaning
- **Add melodies:** The traditional tunes for Havdalah are hauntingly beautiful and add emotional depth
- **Create family customs:** Some families dance with the Havdalah candle, or each person shares a blessing for the week ahead

## Key Takeaways

1. **Havdalah marks transitions** — It's a weekly ceremony that separates Shabbat from the weekdays, teaching us to honor both sacred rest and purposeful work.

2. **All five senses are engaged** — Wine (taste), spices (smell), candle (sight), blessings (hearing), and cup (touch) create a complete, embodied experience.

3. **Distinction is a spiritual skill** — The ability to distinguish between sacred and ordinary, wisdom and folly, what matters and what doesn't, is essential for living meaningfully.

4. **Carry Shabbat forward** — Havdalah isn't just an ending; it's a way to infuse the week ahead with Shabbat's peace, holiness, and elevated consciousness.

5. **Bittersweet beauty** — Like all meaningful farewells, Havdalah acknowledges loss (the departing Shabbat) while celebrating continuity (the light we carry forward).

6. **Anyone can do it** — Whether you're in Beijing, Shanghai, or a small town, whether alone or with community, you can perform this beautiful ceremony and connect with Jewish tradition.

7. **It's about transformation** — Just as grapes become wine and multiple flames become one light, we too are transformed by the weekly rhythm of Shabbat and Havdalah, gradually becoming better versions of ourselves.
