## Introduction

In a small study hall in Jerusalem, a young man sits across from an elderly rabbi. They've been meeting every Tuesday evening for three years. The rabbi doesn't just teach Jewish law — he guides his student through life decisions, helps him navigate doubts, and models how to live as a Jew. When the student's father passed away, it was this rabbi who sat with him through the night. When he got engaged, the rabbi officiated the wedding. This isn't just a teacher; this is a *rav* — a mentor, guide, and spiritual father rolled into one.

If you're familiar with Chinese culture, this relationship might remind you of the Confucian ideal of the teacher-student bond, captured in the saying "一日为师，终身为父" (yī rì wéi shī, zhōng shēn wéi fù) — "A teacher for a day is a father for a lifetime." Judaism has a remarkably similar concept. The relationship between a *rav* (רב, teacher/rabbi) and *talmid* (תלמיד, student) is one of the most sacred bonds in Jewish life — deeper than friendship, more personal than academic instruction, and lasting a lifetime.

This article explores how Jewish mentorship works, why it matters, and how you can find and cultivate this transformative relationship.

## The Biblical Foundation: Moses and Joshua

The archetypal teacher-student relationship in Judaism is Moses and Joshua. When we first meet Joshua, he's simply called "Moshe's attendant" (Exodus 33:11) — not his student, but his *servant*. This seems strange to modern ears. Why would a student serve his teacher?

The answer reveals something profound about Jewish learning: wisdom isn't transferred merely through lectures and study sessions. It's absorbed through proximity, imitation, and service. Joshua didn't just learn Torah from Moses; he learned how Moses prayed, how he led, how he made decisions, how he handled pressure. By serving Moses — attending to his daily needs — Joshua was granted intimate access to see Torah being *lived*, not just taught.

This is the origin of the Hebrew concept *shimush* (שימוש) — literally "usage" or "service," but in practice meaning the apprenticeship model where a student serves their teacher in order to observe how wisdom manifests in daily life.

Before Moses died, God commanded him to appoint Joshua as his successor: "Take Joshua son of Nun, a man in whom there is spirit, and lay your hand upon him" (Numbers 27:18). This laying on of hands symbolizes the transmission of authority and wisdom from teacher to student — a chain that continues unbroken to this day.

Every rabbi today can trace their ordination back through a chain of teachers to Moses at Sinai. This unbroken chain, called *mesorah* (מסורה, tradition), is the lifeblood of Judaism.

## Another Model: Elijah and Elisha

While Moses and Joshua show us the succession of communal leadership, the relationship between the prophets Elijah and Elisha demonstrates a more mystical dimension of mentorship.

Elisha first meets Elijah while plowing a field with twelve yoke of oxen — he's a wealthy farmer, not a scholar. But when Elijah throws his mantle over him (1 Kings 19:19), Elisha immediately abandons everything to follow him. He slaughters his oxen, burns his plowing equipment to cook them, and says goodbye to his parents. This total commitment is what Jewish mentorship sometimes requires — a willingness to reshape your entire life around the pursuit of wisdom and holiness.

For years, Elisha serves Elijah as his attendant, literally "pouring water on his hands" (2 Kings 3:11) — the ancient equivalent of being a personal assistant. When Elijah is about to be taken up to heaven in a whirlwind, Elisha refuses to leave his side. He clings to his teacher with such devotion that Elijah asks, "What can I do for you before I am taken?" Elisha responds with remarkable boldness: "Let me inherit a double portion of your spirit" (2 Kings 2:9).

This request — for a "double portion" — was the inheritance right of a firstborn son. Elisha is asking to be not just a student, but a spiritual heir. And he receives it. When Elijah ascends to heaven, his mantle falls to Elisha, who picks it up and performs the same miracle Elijah had just done — parting the Jordan River. The transmission is complete.

This story teaches that a true *rav-talmid* relationship isn't just about learning information; it's about inheriting a spiritual legacy.

## What Makes a Rav Different from a Teacher?

In English, we might call someone who teaches us math a "teacher" and someone who teaches us Torah a "teacher" too. But in Jewish thought, there's a critical distinction.

A teacher (*moreh*, מורה) imparts knowledge.  
A *rav* (רב) transforms a person.

The Talmud teaches: "I learned much from my teachers, more from my colleagues, and most from my students" (*Berakhot* 63b). This seemingly paradoxical statement — how can you learn more from your students than your teachers? — reveals the difference between information transfer and wisdom cultivation.

Your *rav* isn't just someone who teaches you Jewish law; they're someone who:

- **Models holiness**: You watch how they pray, how they treat their spouse, how they handle stress, how they respond to insults. Torah becomes real when you see it lived.

- **Provides personal guidance**: They help you make life decisions through the lens of Torah — career choices, relationships, ethical dilemmas.

- **Knows your soul**: Over time, a *rav* learns your strengths, weaknesses, spiritual challenges, and unique path in serving God. Their guidance becomes increasingly personalized.

- **Holds you accountable**: They call you out when you're rationalizing bad behavior. They push you to grow when you're comfortable.

- **Represents the chain of tradition**: Through them, you connect to a lineage stretching back 3,300 years to Mount Sinai.

## The Student's Responsibilities: Derech Eretz

The Mishnah in Pirkei Avot (Ethics of the Fathers) states clearly: "Acquire for yourself a teacher (*rav*), and acquire for yourself a friend" (1:6). Notice the active verb — *acquire*. You don't passively wait for a teacher to appear; you actively seek one out and commit to the relationship.

But what does it mean to be a good *talmid* (student)? Jewish law outlines specific obligations:

### 1. Show Respect

The Talmud teaches that "fear of your teacher should be like fear of Heaven" (*Pirkei Avot* 4:12). This doesn't mean cowering in terror, but rather recognizing that your teacher represents something greater than themselves — the Torah itself, and the Divine wisdom within it.

Practical expressions:
- Stand when your *rav* enters the room
- Don't sit in their designated seat
- Don't contradict them publicly
- Use respectful language
- Don't call them by their first name (unless they explicitly ask you to)

During China's Spring and Autumn Period (春秋时代, 770-476 BCE), Confucius taught similar respect for teachers. The *Analects* record that students would not sit on their teacher's mat or speak until spoken to. While styles differ across cultures, the underlying principle is the same: respect creates the proper vessel for receiving wisdom.

### 2. Serve Them Practically

Remember Joshua "attending" Moses, or Elisha "pouring water on Elijah's hands"? This tradition continues today in modified forms.

In traditional yeshivot (Jewish study academies), senior students might:
- Help their *rav* with administrative tasks
- Run errands for them
- Prepare the study hall before they arrive
- Ensure they have everything they need for teaching

This isn't servitude; it's *shimush* — learning through proximity and service. By being around your teacher in non-learning moments, you absorb lessons that can't be taught directly.

The Talmud states that serving Torah scholars is even greater than studying Torah (*Berakhot* 7b), because you learn not just what they know, but who they *are*.

### 3. Ask Real Questions

A good student doesn't just absorb passively; they engage actively. In the Talmud, we constantly see students challenging their teachers: "But rabbi, doesn't this contradict what you said earlier?" or "I don't understand — please explain again."

This isn't disrespectful; it's the highest form of respect. You honor your teacher by taking their words seriously enough to wrestle with them.

The Talmud records that when a student didn't understand something after their teacher explained it four times, the teacher was obligated to explain it a fifth time — and if the student still didn't understand, the teacher had to find a different teacher better suited to that student's learning style.

### 4. Implement Their Guidance

There's a concept in Judaism called *emunat chachamim* (אמונת חכמים) — faith in the sages. When your *rav* gives you guidance, especially on matters of Jewish law or spiritual practice, you're expected to follow it — even if you don't fully understand the reasoning.

This might seem contrary to the Western emphasis on personal autonomy. Why should I follow someone else's advice about my own life?

The answer lies in recognizing that Torah wisdom often transcends our immediate understanding. Your *rav* has decades of study, life experience, and connection to the chain of tradition. They see things you can't see yet. Trusting their guidance is how you grow beyond your current limitations.

That said, this isn't blind obedience. A *rav* who tells you to violate clear Jewish law isn't acting as a *rav* in that moment. The Talmud teaches: "If your teacher says to transgress even the most minor commandment, do not listen to him" (*Yevamot* 96a).

## The Teacher's Responsibilities: More Than Instruction

Just as students have obligations, so do teachers. The relationship is reciprocal, not one-sided.

### 1. Love Your Students

Rambam (Maimonides, 1138-1204 CE, during China's Southern Song Dynasty 南宋, 1127-1279 CE) writes in his legal code: "A teacher should relate to his students as to his own children — indeed, more so, for children bring you benefit in this world while students bring you benefit in the World to Come" (*Mishneh Torah*, Laws of Torah Study 4:1).

This isn't sentimental affection; it's deep, committed love — caring for the student's wellbeing, celebrating their growth, feeling pain at their struggles.

### 2. Tailor Your Teaching

Not every student learns the same way. The Passover Haggadah famously describes four types of children — wise, wicked, simple, and one who doesn't know how to ask — and instructs parents to teach each one differently.

A good *rav* recognizes that what works for one student may not work for another. Some need strict discipline; others need gentle encouragement. Some learn best through abstract principles; others through concrete examples.

The medieval commentator Rashi (1040-1105 CE, during China's Northern Song Dynasty 北宋, 960-1127 CE) was famous for explaining difficult Talmudic passages in simple, clear language — even using French words when Hebrew was unclear — because he understood that the goal wasn't to show off his erudition but to ensure his students actually *learned*.

### 3. Model, Don't Just Teach

The Talmud says: "Whoever teaches his friend's son Torah, it's as if he gave birth to him" (*Sanhedrin* 19b). This biological metaphor is striking — just as a parent shapes a child not primarily through lectures but through modeling behavior, a *rav* shapes a *talmid* through living Torah in front of them.

Students watch everything: How does my *rav* treat waiters? How do they respond when someone insults them? How do they handle financial pressure? Do they practice what they preach?

Hypocrisy destroys the *rav-talmid* relationship faster than anything else.

### 4. Know When to Let Go

Perhaps the hardest part of being a teacher is recognizing when your student has outgrown you. The goal isn't to create perpetual dependence but to cultivate independence.

Rabbi Akiva, one of the greatest sages in Jewish history, started learning Torah at age 40 — as an illiterate shepherd. His first teacher taught him the alphabet. Eventually, Rabbi Akiva surpassed all his teachers and became the teacher of a generation. His teachers celebrated this, not resented it.

The ultimate success of a *rav* is when their *talmid* becomes a *rav* to others, extending the chain.

## Chinese Parallels: The Confucian and Daoist Models

### Confucian Teacher-Student Bonds

The Confucian tradition has a remarkably similar view of the teacher-student relationship. Confucius said: "Among three people walking together, I will certainly find a teacher among them" (*Analects* 7:21) — echoing the Talmudic teaching that you can learn from everyone.

The *Analects* (论语) itself is structured like the Talmud — not as systematic lectures but as dialogues between Confucius and his students. We see students questioning, challenging, and being corrected by the Master. This back-and-forth dynamic is very similar to the Jewish learning model.

Moreover, the Confucian emphasis on *filial piety* (孝, xiào) extends explicitly to teachers. The *Book of Rites* (*Li Ji*, 礼记) states: "In teaching, the relationship between teacher and student is like that of father and son." This parallels the Jewish concept that a *rav* is a spiritual parent.

During the Han Dynasty (汉朝, 206 BCE - 220 CE, contemporary with the late Second Temple and early Talmudic period in Jewish history), Chinese scholars would travel hundreds of miles to study under a master, living in their household and serving them — precisely the *shimush* model we see in Judaism.

### Daoist Master-Disciple Transmission

Daoism offers another parallel — the concept of *shi fu* (师父), the master who transmits not just knowledge but spiritual power and realization to the disciple. A Daoist master doesn't just teach about the Dao; they embody it, and through extended proximity and practice, the student absorbs this embodiment.

This is very close to the Jewish concept of *kabbalah* (קבלה) — literally "receiving" — where mystical teachings are transmitted orally from master to disciple, not through books alone but through lived experience and spiritual practice.

## How to Find a Rav

This all sounds beautiful, you might be thinking, but how do I actually find a *rav* — especially if I live in China or another place with a small Jewish community?

### 1. Start With Who's Available

The Talmud teaches: "If you can't find bread, eat cake; if you can't find cake, eat whatever you can" (paraphrase of *Chullin* 27a). In other words, don't let the perfect be the enemy of the good.

Maybe you don't have access to a world-renowned Torah scholar. That's okay. Start with whoever you *do* have access to:
- A local Chabad rabbi
- An online Torah teacher
- A more knowledgeable friend
- Even recorded lectures from deceased rabbis

The key is to commit to *someone*. As the Mishnah says: "Acquire for yourself a *rav*" — you have to take initiative.

### 2. Online Rabbinic Relationships

In our digital age, *rav-talmid* relationships can flourish online. Organizations like Partners in Torah, Chabad's MyShliach program, and many independent rabbis offer one-on-one learning via video chat.

While in-person is ideal, a consistent online relationship is far better than no relationship at all. Schedule a regular time, prepare for your sessions, and treat them with the same seriousness you would an in-person meeting.

### 3. Visit Jewish Communities

If you live in China, consider visiting Hong Kong, Shanghai, or Beijing, which have established Jewish communities and Chabad Houses. Spend a Shabbat there, attend services, introduce yourself to the rabbi. Even a short personal connection can lay the groundwork for an ongoing relationship.

When you travel abroad — Israel, the United States, Europe — make time to meet rabbis. Attend lectures. Ask questions. Exchange contact information. You never know which connection might develop into a mentorship.

### 4. Trust Your Gut

Not every rabbi is the right *rav* for you. Some personalities click; others don't. This isn't a moral judgment — it's about finding the right match.

Look for someone who:
- Inspires you to grow
- Challenges you without crushing you
- Answers questions with depth, not dismissiveness
- Practices what they preach
- You can imagine calling when you have a life crisis

The Talmud says every person should choose a *rav* who is most suited to their particular soul.

### 5. Be Patient

Finding a *rav* is like finding a spouse — it takes time. Don't force it. Learn from multiple teachers, attend different lectures, and see who resonates.

And remember: even if you never find that one ultimate *rav* who guides you through everything, every teacher you learn from contributes to your spiritual growth. Rabbi Akiva reportedly learned from thousands of teachers. Each one gave him something.

## Practical Tips for Cultivating the Relationship

Once you've found a potential *rav*, how do you actually build the relationship?

### 1. Ask Permission

Don't just assume someone is your *rav*. Approach them respectfully: "Rabbi, I've been learning from you and have benefited greatly. Would you be willing to serve as my *rav* — to guide me in learning and answer my questions about Jewish practice?"

Most rabbis will be honored by this request. It also sets a clear framework for the relationship.

### 2. Schedule Regular Learning

Consistency is key. Whether it's 30 minutes weekly or an hour monthly, having a regular time slot ensures the relationship develops depth.

Come prepared. Have a text to study or specific questions to discuss. Respect their time.

### 3. Keep Them Updated

Don't only reach out when you have a question. Update them on your progress: "Rabbi, I've been keeping Shabbat for three months now." Let them celebrate your growth.

Also share your struggles: "Rabbi, I'm finding daily prayer really hard." Vulnerability deepens the bond.

### 4. Support Their Work

If your *rav* runs a synagogue or organization, volunteer. Donate if you're able. Attend their events. Show through action that you value what they do.

### 5. Introduce Others

When you meet other Jews seeking guidance, connect them with your *rav*. Helping your teacher reach more students is a form of honor and gratitude.

## Common Questions

**Q: What if I disagree with my rav's interpretation of something?**

Respectful disagreement is not only allowed but encouraged in Jewish learning. The Talmud is filled with debates. You can say: "Rabbi, I heard a different interpretation from Rabbi X. How do you understand that?"

What you shouldn't do is publicly contradict them in a way that undermines their authority, or ignore their guidance on practical matters while still claiming them as your *rav*.

**Q: Can I have more than one rav?**

Yes. Many Jews learn from multiple rabbis — one for Talmud, another for Jewish law, another for Chassidic philosophy. However, for practical life guidance (psak halacha), it's best to have one primary *rav* whose authority you recognize, to avoid confusion when different rabbis might give different advice.

**Q: What if my rav tells me to do something I think is wrong?**

If they're telling you to violate clear Jewish law, don't listen. If they're giving you guidance within the bounds of Jewish law but you don't understand why, ask questions. If it still seems wrong, you can seek a second opinion from another rabbi — but do so transparently, not behind your *rav*'s back.

**Q: How do I know if someone is qualified to be my rav?**

Look for someone who:
- Has studied with recognized Torah scholars
- Lives according to the values they teach
- Is part of an established Jewish community
- Can cite sources for their teachings
- Admits when they don't know something rather than making things up

Beware of self-appointed "rabbis" with no connection to traditional Jewish learning or community.

**Q: I'm a woman. Can I have a rav?**

Absolutely. While historically most rabbis were men, there are now many learned women teaching Torah, and any woman can and should seek a mentor to guide her Jewish growth. In Orthodox communities, a *rabbanit* (rabbi's wife) or female Torah scholar might be an appropriate choice. In non-Orthodox communities, there are ordained female rabbis.

The principles of the *rav-talmid* relationship apply regardless of gender.

## The Ultimate Goal: Becoming a Link in the Chain

The mystical rabbi known as the Baal Shem Tov (1698-1760, during China's Qing Dynasty 清朝, 1644-1912) taught: "Everyone should consider themselves a link in the chain connecting heaven to earth."

When you find a *rav* and become a *talmid*, you're not just pursuing personal growth. You're connecting yourself to a chain that stretches back 3,300 years — from you to your teacher, from your teacher to their teacher, all the way back to the giving of the Torah at Mount Sinai (circa 1313 BCE, during what corresponds to China's Xia Dynasty 夏朝, circa 2070-1600 BCE).

And you're also connecting forward. Someday, someone will learn from *you*. You'll pass on what you received. The chain continues.

This is the profound beauty of the *rav-talmid* relationship: it's not a transaction but a transmission. Not information but transformation. Not just learning about Judaism but becoming a Jew.

## Key Takeaways

- The *rav-talmid* (teacher-student) relationship is one of the most sacred bonds in Judaism, deeper than academic instruction
- Biblical models include Moses and Joshua (leadership succession) and Elijah and Elisha (mystical inheritance)
- A true *rav* doesn't just teach knowledge but transforms the person, modeling how to live Torah
- Students have responsibilities: show respect, serve practically, ask real questions, and implement guidance
- Teachers must love their students, tailor teaching, model behavior, and know when to let students surpass them
- Chinese parallels include Confucian teacher-student bonds ("一日为师，终身为父") and Daoist master-disciple transmission
- You can find a *rav* locally, online, or by visiting Jewish communities — start with who's available and be patient
- The relationship requires initiative, consistency, and mutual commitment
- Every *rav-talmid* relationship is a link in the unbroken chain from Sinai to today
