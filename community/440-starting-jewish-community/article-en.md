You're Jewish—or converting, or married to a Jew, or deeply connected to Judaism—and you live in a city with no organized Jewish community. Maybe you're in a tier-two Chinese city, a remote area of Southeast Asia, or anywhere the Jewish population is sparse or scattered. You feel isolated. You wonder: Can I maintain Jewish life here? Can I create community where none exists?

The answer is yes. Throughout history, small groups of Jews in unlikely places have built thriving communities from nothing. You can too. It requires vision, effort, and patience—but it's absolutely possible.

## The Precedent: Jews Everywhere

Jews have always been pioneers. From Kaifeng, China (a Jewish community dating to the Song Dynasty) to Suriname, South Africa, and Singapore, Jewish communities emerged wherever Jews settled.

These communities often started with one family, one trader, one refugee. They found each other, gathered for holidays, created minyanim, built synagogues, and established schools. What seemed impossible became normal through persistence.

You're continuing this tradition.

## Step 1: Find Other Jews

The foundation of community is connection. You're probably not alone—you just don't know each other yet.

### **Where to Look**

**1. Online searches and social media**: Search Facebook, LinkedIn, WeChat (in China), or local forums for Jewish groups or individuals in your city. Even if no formal group exists, individual Jews may be findable.

**2. Universities**: If your city has universities, contact international student offices or Middle Eastern studies departments. Jewish students and faculty might be there.

**3. International businesses**: Multinational corporations, especially Israeli companies, may employ Jewish professionals. Tech companies, finance firms, and startups are good places to start.

**4. Expat communities**: Check expat forums, international clubs, and embassy lists. Western expat communities often include some Jews.

**5. Israeli connections**: Contact the nearest Israeli consulate or embassy. They maintain lists of Israeli citizens and often know about Jewish residents.

**6. Jewish travel networks**: Websites like Jewish Travel Network or Chabad's worldwide directory list Jews in remote locations willing to connect with visitors—some may be local.

**7. Hospitals and international schools**: Jewish doctors, teachers, and NGO workers might be in your area.

**8. Local government or chambers of commerce**: Sometimes city governments maintain lists of religious communities for official purposes.

### **Making First Contact**

When you find someone, reach out warmly but respectfully:

"Hi, I'm [name], and I'm Jewish living in [city]. I'm trying to connect with other Jews here for holidays, community, and mutual support. Would you be interested in meeting?"

Keep it low-pressure. Some people will be enthusiastic; others may be uninterested or too busy. That's okay. Keep reaching out.

### **The Power of Two**

The Talmud teaches: "When two sit together and words of Torah are between them, the Divine Presence rests between them" (Pirkei Avot 3:2).

You don't need hundreds. Two families celebrating Shabbat together is a community. Three people studying Torah is a community. Five people gathering for Passover is a community.

Start small. Growth comes with time.

## Step 2: Establish Regular Gatherings

Community requires regularity. Occasional meetups are nice; consistent gatherings create community.

### **Start with High Holidays**

If you can't manage weekly or monthly events, prioritize the High Holidays—Rosh Hashanah, Yom Kippur, and Passover. These are when even less-observant Jews seek connection.

**Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur**: Rent a hotel conference room, community center, or gather in someone's home. Use a machzor (High Holiday prayer book) and lead services yourself if no one has experience—YouTube and online tutorials can guide you. The community will appreciate the effort even if it's imperfect.

**Passover Seder**: This is often the easiest starting point. A Seder is explicitly designed for home celebration. Invite all the Jews you've found, plus interested non-Jewish spouses and friends. Follow a Haggadah, share the story, eat symbolic foods, and connect.

One Seder can birth a community. People who attend often say, "We should do this more often!" That's your opening.

### **Monthly Shabbat Gatherings**

Once you have a core group, establish monthly (or quarterly) Shabbat dinners, rotating hosts.

**Why Shabbat dinners work**:
- Home-based (no venue needed)
- Flexible (can be fully religious or culturally Jewish)
- Welcoming (food brings people together)
- Scalable (start with three families, grow to ten)

Host rotation distributes the work and gives everyone ownership. Provide a basic structure—candle lighting, kiddush, motzi, meal, songs—but keep it welcoming and accessible.

### **Study Groups**

Not everyone is comfortable with religious services, but many Jews value learning. Start a monthly book club reading Jewish texts or topics:

- Pirkei Avot (Ethics of the Fathers)—accessible, short, wisdom-filled
- Modern Jewish authors (Abraham Joshua Heschel, Harold Kushner, etc.)
- Jewish history or Holocaust studies
- Israeli literature or politics

Learning creates bonds and deepens understanding.

### **Children's Programming**

If families with children exist, prioritize them. Parents desperately want Jewish experiences for their kids.

- Monthly "Jewish playgroup" for young children
- Occasional "Hebrew School" sessions teaching basics
- Bar/Bat Mitzvah preparation (even if you need to bring in a rabbi remotely)

Parents will commit to community when it benefits their children.

## Step 3: Create Structure (But Keep It Simple)

Once you have regular gatherings, light structure helps sustainability.

### **Designate Roles (Informally)**

You don't need a board of directors or formal nonprofit. But designating roles helps:

**Coordinator**: One person (or rotating) keeps the calendar, sends reminders, manages the contact list.

**Treasurer**: If you're collecting money for events (shared meals, renting space), someone should track it transparently.

**Education leader**: Someone interested in Jewish learning can coordinate study sessions or invite speakers.

**Children's coordinator**: If kids are involved, someone should plan age-appropriate activities.

These roles can be fluid and shared. The goal is distribution of labor so it doesn't all fall on one person.

### **Communication**

Create a simple communication channel:
- WhatsApp or WeChat group
- Email list
- Facebook group

Share calendar dates, Torah thoughts, articles, and community updates. This keeps people connected between gatherings.

### **Minimal Fundraising**

You'll need some money—prayer books, holiday supplies, maybe venue rental. Options:

**Dues**: Optional annual dues ($100-500 per household) create steady income. Make it voluntary; never turn anyone away for inability to pay.

**Event fees**: Charge modest amounts for expensive events (High Holiday services, Passover Seder) to cover costs.

**Donations**: Some members may donate generously. Accept gratefully, but never create class divisions based on giving.

Keep finances simple and transparent. Publish income and expenses annually.

## Step 4: Access Resources and Expertise

You don't need to figure everything out alone. External resources help enormously.

### **Connect with Established Communities**

Find the nearest established Jewish community (even if it's hundreds of miles away) and build a relationship.

- Visit occasionally to experience "real" services and community
- Invite their rabbi to visit you once a year for High Holidays or scholar-in-residence weekends
- Ask for used prayer books, educational materials, or holiday supplies
- Seek halachic guidance when questions arise

Many established communities gladly support emerging ones.

### **Partner with Chabad**

Chabad specializes in exactly this—creating Jewish life in remote places. Even if there's no Chabad House in your city, regional Chabad rabbis often visit outlying areas.

Contact the nearest Chabad and ask:
- Can they visit for High Holidays?
- Can they provide kosher wine and matzah?
- Can they answer halachic questions remotely?
- Can they support bar/bat mitzvah preparation?

Chabad rabbis are trained for outreach and often willing to help even from a distance.

### **Online Learning and Services**

The internet has revolutionized Jewish life in remote areas.

**Virtual learning**:
- Torah classes via Zoom from rabbis worldwide
- Online yeshivot and institutes offering courses
- YouTube tutorials on everything from Shabbat candle lighting to Torah chanting

**Virtual services**: While not ideal, livestreamed Shabbat and holiday services allow isolated Jews to participate in communal prayer. Major synagogues worldwide stream services.

**Apps and websites**: Resources like Sefaria (free Jewish texts), MyJewishLearning (educational articles), and Orthodox Union (holiday guides) provide unlimited free content.

### **Jewish Agency and Kulanu**

Organizations like the Jewish Agency for Israel and Kulanu specifically support emerging and isolated Jewish communities. They provide:
- Educational materials
- Financial support for programming
- Connections to other small communities
- Guidance on community development

Contact them even in early stages.

### **Visiting Rabbis and Educators**

Once or twice a year, bring in a rabbi or Jewish educator for an intensive weekend:
- Friday night and Shabbat services
- Study sessions
- Lifecycle events (baby namings, conversions, weddings)
- Q&A sessions

This provides professional expertise and energizes the community. Fundraise specifically for this; it's worth the investment.

## Step 5: Lifecycle Events

Community solidifies when it marks life's sacred moments together.

### **Births**

Celebrate baby namings even without a formal synagogue. A gathering at home with a short ceremony, sharing the Hebrew name's meaning, and welcoming the child into the community is meaningful.

### **Bar/Bat Mitzvah**

This is challenging without a Torah scroll or trained reader, but possible:
- Rent a Torah from a nearby community or buy a kosher used one (expensive but possible)
- Train the child using online resources or visiting tutors
- Hold a service with what you have; celebrate the milestone communally
- Some families travel to established communities for the actual service but hold local celebrations

The effort to provide this for one child often galvanizes the whole community.

### **Weddings**

Jewish weddings require a ketubah (marriage contract), chuppah (wedding canopy), and someone knowledgeable to conduct the ceremony. A visiting rabbi can officiate, or a knowledgeable layperson can lead with rabbinic guidance remotely.

The community's presence makes the wedding Jewish and joyful.

### **Funerals and Mourning**

This is perhaps the most critical lifecycle need. When death occurs, Jews need:
- Burial according to Jewish tradition
- Kaddish (requiring a minyan, or at least community presence)
- Shiva (seven days of mourning with community support)

If you can't form a chevra kadisha (burial society), connect with the nearest one. For shiva, gather daily if possible to comfort mourners. Even without a minyan for Kaddish, communal presence provides profound comfort.

### **Conversions**

Supporting someone converting to Judaism requires rabbinic supervision. Connect the candidate with a rabbi (remotely if necessary) who can guide the process. The community can support by providing learning opportunities, welcoming the candidate, and celebrating their conversion.

## Step 6: Physical Space

Communities eventually need physical space.

### **Start in Homes**

Begin with rotating home gatherings. This requires no money and creates intimacy.

### **Rent Space as Needed**

For High Holidays or large events, rent hotel conference rooms, community centers, or other neutral spaces.

### **Establish a Community Center**

If the community grows and stabilizes, consider renting permanent space:
- Office/meeting room
- Small library of Jewish books
- Storage for holiday supplies and ritual objects
- Space for services and events

This requires significant funding and commitment. Don't rush it.

### **Building a Synagogue**

This is a long-term goal (years or decades). Small communities can sometimes purchase or construct modest buildings, but only when membership and finances are stable.

Many successful small communities operate for decades without formal buildings. Spaces matter less than people.

## Step 7: Navigate Challenges

Building community from scratch is hard. Expect challenges:

### **Transience**

Expats and professionals rotate in and out. Just as you build relationships, people leave. Accept this. Welcome newcomers; bless those who leave; maintain connections with alumni who may return or send others.

### **Ideological Differences**

Your small group will likely include Orthodox, Conservative, Reform, and secular Jews. Disagreements will arise about how religious services should be, whether to count women in the minyan, what kashrut standards to follow, etc.

**Strategies**:
- Start pluralistic and welcoming
- Focus on what unites (holidays, lifecycle events, study)
- Avoid forcing consensus on divisive issues
- Offer multiple options when possible (separate Orthodox and egalitarian services, for example)
- Emphasize respect and coexistence

Small communities can't afford splits. Prioritize unity over uniformity.

### **Burnout**

Leading a startup community is exhausting. The same few people do everything. Prevent burnout:
- Rotate responsibilities
- Take breaks when needed
- Celebrate small victories
- Ask for help; delegate
- Lower expectations during busy seasons

Remember: slow progress is still progress.

### **Lack of Expertise**

You'll face questions you can't answer: How do we kasher a kitchen? What if we can't find a minyan for someone's yahrzeit? Can we bury someone here according to Jewish law?

Don't pretend to know. Seek expertise: email rabbis, consult books, post questions to online Jewish forums. The community will respect honesty more than false confidence.

### **Financial Limitations**

Small communities operate on shoestring budgets. Be creative:
- Make your own challah rather than buying it
- Create DIY Torah mantles and ritual objects
- Use free online resources instead of buying curricula
- Share rides to reduce travel costs for visiting speakers

Poverty of resources can create richness of creativity and community bonding.

## The Chinese Context: Specific Considerations

For Jews building community in China, additional factors apply:

### **Legal and Cultural Context**

Understand local regulations about religious gatherings. China's approach to religion is complex. Small private gatherings are typically fine; registering as a formal religious organization may be complicated or impossible.

Consult legal experts if you plan anything beyond home gatherings.

### **Cultural Bridge-Building**

Chinese society values community harmony and cultural exchange. Position your Jewish community as contributing positively:
- Participate in local charity and civic life
- Welcome Chinese neighbors to cultural events (Purim carnival, Hanukkah party)
- Offer Hebrew or Jewish studies classes open to interested non-Jews
- Build relationships with local universities and cultural institutions

This creates goodwill and integration.

### **Kosher Food**

China's major cities have some kosher resources; smaller cities have none. Strategies:
- Learn to kasher your own kitchen
- Focus on vegetarian and fish options
- Coordinate group purchases from kosher suppliers who ship
- Connect with Chabad for wine and matzah

Kashrut observance will vary widely in your group. Find an approach that works.

### **Hebrew and Education**

Teaching Hebrew and Jewish literacy is challenging without resources. Use:
- Online programs like Duolingo for Hebrew
- Virtual Hebrew schools offering classes to isolated students
- Recorded lessons and tutorials
- Visiting teachers during intensive periods

Even basic Hebrew literacy enriches Jewish life enormously.

### **Connection to Israel**

For Chinese Jews (or non-Jewish Chinese people exploring Judaism), connecting to Israel can be powerful. Encourage Birthright trips for eligible young adults, virtual tours, Israeli cultural events, and Hebrew media.

## Success Stories: Communities That Started from Nothing

**Abuja, Nigeria**: A single Israeli diplomat started gathering Jews in Nigeria's capital in the 1990s. Today, there's an established community with regular services, Hebrew school, and Jewish lifecycle events.

**Anchorage, Alaska**: Isolated geographically, Anchorage's small Jewish community maintained itself for decades through determination and creativity, eventually building a synagogue.

**Chengdu, China**: Even in western China's Sichuan province, small groups of Jews have gathered periodically, forming temporary communities around expat and business populations.

**Singapore**: In the early 19th century, a handful of Jewish traders from Baghdad established Singapore's first Jewish community. Today it's a thriving multicultural Jewish population with multiple synagogues and institutions.

None of these started with resources or infrastructure. They started with committed individuals who refused to let geography separate them from Jewish community.

## The Spiritual Dimension: Why It Matters

Building community isn't just logistical—it's spiritual. The Talmud teaches that when Jews gather, the Shekhinah (Divine Presence) dwells among them.

You're not just creating social programming. You're creating sacred space where:
- Children learn their heritage
- Mourners find comfort
- Joyous occasions are celebrated
- Torah is studied and lived
- Strangers become family
- Isolation transforms into belonging

This is holy work. It's challenging, sometimes frustrating, often exhausting—but profoundly meaningful.

Every time two Jews in a remote place light Shabbat candles together, they continue a chain stretching back thousands of years. Every time a child in an isolated community learns the Shema, Jewish continuity extends one more generation.

You're not just building community. You're sustaining the Jewish people.

## Final Encouragement: You Can Do This

If you're reading this in a city with no Jewish community, feeling alone and uncertain, hear this: You can do this.

Start small. Find one other Jew. Light Shabbat candles together. Celebrate one Passover Seder. Study one page of Talmud. These small acts are seeds that can grow into flourishing community.

You're not the first. Throughout history, Jews in unlikely places have created vibrant communities from nothing. You join a long tradition of pioneers.

Be patient. Building community takes years, not months. There will be setbacks and frustrations. Keep going.

Be flexible. Your community won't look like established ones in New York, London, or Tel Aviv. That's okay. It will look like what you need it to be.

Be courageous. Reaching out to strangers, leading services when you're uncertain, celebrating holidays without expertise—all require courage. Have it.

Be faithful. Trust that God's presence dwells wherever Jews gather sincerely. Trust that your efforts matter. Trust that others will join you.

The Talmud says: "It is not your responsibility to finish the work, but neither are you free to desist from it" (Pirkei Avot 2:16).

You don't have to build a complete, perfect community instantly. You just have to start. Do what you can. Others will join. Over time, something beautiful will grow.

Wherever you are—in a Chinese city, a remote Asian town, anywhere—you can create Jewish community. You just have to begin.

B'hatzlacha—may you succeed.
