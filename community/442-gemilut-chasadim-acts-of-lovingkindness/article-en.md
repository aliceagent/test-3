# Gemilut Chasadim: Acts of Lovingkindness That Build Community

In many cultures, helping others is considered praiseworthy but optional—a nice thing to do when you have extra time or resources. Judaism takes a radically different approach. Acts of lovingkindness, known in Hebrew as *gemilut chasadim* (גמילות חסדים), are not optional extras but fundamental obligations that sit at the very heart of what it means to live a Jewish life.

## More Important Than Sacrifice

The importance of *gemilut chasadim* cannot be overstated. The Talmud teaches that acts of lovingkindness are among the three pillars upon which the world stands, alongside Torah study and divine service (Pirkei Avot 1:2). In another passage, the sages go even further, stating that *gemilut chasadim* is greater than charity (*tzedakah*) in three ways:

1. Charity can only be given with money, but acts of lovingkindness can be performed with one's person or one's money
2. Charity is given only to the poor, but acts of lovingkindness can be done for both rich and poor
3. Charity is given only to the living, but acts of lovingkindness can be performed for both the living and the dead (Talmud, Sukkah 49b)

The prophet Hosea conveyed God's priorities clearly: "For I desire lovingkindness, not sacrifice" (Hosea 6:6). When the Temple stood, sacrifices were central to Jewish worship. Yet the prophet declares that acts of kindness are what God truly desires. After the Temple's destruction, when sacrifices were no longer possible, the rabbis taught that *gemilut chasadim* could serve in their place, atoning for sin and bringing humans closer to the Divine.

## The Core Obligations

While *gemilut chasadim* encompasses countless acts of kindness, Jewish tradition has identified certain obligations as particularly fundamental:

### Visiting the Sick (*Bikur Cholim*)

When someone is ill, their physical suffering is often compounded by loneliness, fear, and a sense of isolation. Visiting the sick addresses this emotional and spiritual pain. The Talmud teaches that visiting a sick person removes one-sixtieth of their suffering (Nedarim 39b).

But this mitzvah comes with important guidelines. Visitors should assess whether their presence is helpful or burdensome. The Talmud specifies that one should not visit during the first three hours of the day (when the patient may need care) or the last three (when their condition may worsen and visits would be distressing). The goal is to pray for the person, offer practical help, and provide companionship—not to satisfy one's own need to "do something" or to gossip about the patient's condition.

### Comforting Mourners (*Nichum Aveilim*)

Judaism recognizes that grief requires community support. During the seven-day mourning period after a close relative's death (*shiva*), the mourner stays home while the community comes to them. Visitors are instructed not to speak first but to wait for the mourner to initiate conversation, respecting their emotional state.

The tradition of bringing food to a house of mourning is not just about practical nourishment; it symbolizes the community's embrace of the bereaved. The first meal after the funeral, the *seudat havra'ah* (meal of consolation), traditionally includes round foods like eggs and lentils, symbolizing the cycle of life.

Significantly, the obligation to comfort mourners doesn't end after shiva. Jewish tradition recognizes that grief continues for thirty days (*sheloshim*) for most relatives and a full year for parents. Communities continue reaching out, checking in, and including mourners in activities as they gradually reenter normal life.

### Providing for the Poor (*Tzedakah*)

While *tzedakah* is sometimes translated as "charity," it literally means "righteousness" or "justice." Giving to those in need is not optional benevolence but a requirement of justice. Jewish law mandates that even someone who receives charity must also give, preserving the dignity that comes from being a giver as well as a receiver.

Maimonides outlined eight levels of charity, from lowest to highest:

8. Giving reluctantly
7. Giving less than appropriate but cheerfully
6. Giving directly when asked
5. Giving directly without being asked
4. Giving when the recipient knows the donor, but the donor doesn't know the recipient
3. Giving when the donor knows the recipient, but the recipient doesn't know the donor
2. Giving anonymously through a trustworthy intermediary
1. Helping someone become self-supporting through a loan, partnership, or employment

The highest form is enabling someone to support themselves, preserving their dignity and independence.

### Providing for Brides (*Hachnasat Kallah*)

Ensuring that a bride has what she needs for her wedding is considered so important that the Talmud says one should interrupt Torah study to join a wedding procession (Ketubot 17a). In traditional communities, this meant providing a dowry for poor brides, ensuring that poverty would not prevent marriage.

Today, this mitzvah extends to helping couples with wedding expenses, offering venues or services, or simply celebrating enthusiastically at the wedding to bring joy to the couple. The Talmud teaches that making a bride and groom happy is equivalent to rebuilding one of the ruins of Jerusalem.

### Welcoming Guests (*Hachnasat Orchim*)

Abraham's tent, according to tradition, was open on all four sides so he could see travelers from any direction and invite them in. Hospitality is so central to Jewish ethics that Abraham interrupted a conversation with God Himself to welcome three strangers (Genesis 18:1-8).

Welcoming guests means more than providing food and shelter; it includes making people feel comfortable, respected, and valued. In communities where Jews are scattered or isolated, hospitality ensures that travelers can maintain Jewish observance and not feel alone.

### Accompanying the Deceased (*Levayat HaMet*)

Attending a funeral and participating in burial is considered one of the truest acts of kindness because the deceased cannot repay you. It is an act of pure *chesed* (lovingkindness) with no expectation of return.

Jewish tradition places great importance on timely burial and requires the community to participate. The actual filling of the grave is considered such an important mitzvah that mourners sometimes encourage participants to take turns with the shovel, allowing many people to participate in this final act of kindness.

## Why These Obligations Matter

These acts of *gemilut chasadim* are not merely individual good deeds; they are the connective tissue of Jewish community. They ensure that no one faces life's challenges alone—not illness, grief, poverty, major celebrations, or death.

In Chinese culture, there is a similar emphasis on communal responsibility and mutual care, particularly within family and social networks. The concept of *guanxi* (关系) emphasizes relationships and reciprocal obligations. Judaism extends this framework to the entire community, making it a religious obligation rather than just a social expectation.

## The Ripple Effect

When *gemilut chasadim* becomes embedded in a community's culture, something remarkable happens. People who receive kindness in their time of need later extend that kindness to others. A person whose medical bills were helped by the community becomes someone who visits the sick. Someone whose wedding was supported by others later helps other couples.

The Talmud tells of Rabbi Yochanan, who would visit every sick person, regardless of whether they were Jewish or not (Jerusalem Talmud, Peah 1:1). His teaching was simple: acts of lovingkindness have no limit. There is no amount that fulfills the obligation, no point at which you can say "I've done enough."

## Practical Applications Today

In the modern world, these ancient obligations take new forms:

- **Visiting the sick** might include video calls to isolated patients, driving someone to medical appointments, or organizing meal deliveries during recovery
- **Comforting mourners** can happen through text messages, memorial donations, or simply being willing to listen
- **Tzedakah** extends to supporting food banks, homeless shelters, educational programs, and countless other causes
- **Helping couples** might mean offering photography skills, helping with wedding planning, or contributing to crowdfunding campaigns
- **Hospitality** includes welcoming newcomers to your synagogue, hosting Shabbat dinners for students far from home, or offering your home to Jewish travelers
- **Honoring the deceased** means attending funerals even when inconvenient and supporting mourners long after the immediate crisis has passed

## A Community Built on Kindness

What makes these obligations transformative is that they are obligations—not feelings-based volunteerism. You visit the sick even when it's uncomfortable. You attend funerals even when you're busy. You give charity even when money is tight.

This transforms a collection of individuals into a true community. Everyone knows that in their time of need, the community will show up. And everyone knows they must show up for others. This mutual obligation creates bonds stronger than shared ethnicity or even shared beliefs.

The Talmud teaches that Jerusalem was destroyed not because of cardinal sins but because people relied on strict justice rather than going beyond the letter of the law in kindness (Bava Metzia 30b). A community built only on rules and obligations cannot survive. It requires the oil of lovingkindness to make the gears turn smoothly.

*Gemilut chasadim* is not about grand gestures. It's about showing up, consistently and reliably, in the small moments that make up a life. It's about building a community where no one suffers alone, no one celebrates alone, and no one faces death alone.

This is what it means to be part of the Jewish people: to know you are responsible for others, and they for you.
