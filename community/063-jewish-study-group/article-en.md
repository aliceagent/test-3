## Introduction

You're learning Torah by yourself — perhaps online, maybe from books. The material is fascinating, but something feels incomplete. You read a profound commentary from Rashi and think, "I wonder what others would say about this." You encounter a challenging concept and wish you could discuss it with someone who's also grappling with the same ideas.

This loneliness in Jewish learning is especially acute if you live in a city without an established Jewish community — a reality for many people exploring Judaism in China, Southeast Asia, and beyond. But here's something remarkable: **you don't need to be an expert to start a Jewish study group**. In fact, some of the most vibrant Torah study happens when genuine seekers gather together, bringing their questions, insights, and curiosity to a shared text.

This article will guide you through creating and sustaining a Jewish study group in your city, even if you're just beginning your Jewish journey yourself.

## Why Study Torah in a Group?

### The Jewish Ideal: Chavruta Learning

Judaism has always emphasized study in partnership. The Talmud states: "Either study with a partner or find yourself a teacher" (Pirkei Avot 1:6). The traditional chavruta model — where two people study together, challenging and refining each other's understanding — has been the foundation of Torah education for thousands of years.

But group study offers something even richer. When multiple perspectives converge on a single text, each person becomes both student and teacher. Someone notices a pattern you missed. Another person asks a question that unlocks a new layer of meaning. The shy participant offers a quiet insight that transforms everyone's understanding.

### Cultural Parallel: Chinese Scholarly Circles

This may resonate with anyone familiar with Chinese intellectual traditions. During the Song Dynasty (宋朝, 960-1279 CE) — a period coinciding with the golden age of Rashi and other medieval Jewish commentators — Chinese scholars formed study societies where they would gather to discuss classical texts, poetry, and philosophy. These weren't formal classes with a single authority figure; they were communities of inquiry where participants learned through dialogue and debate.

Your Jewish study group can embody this same spirit: a gathering of sincere seekers committed to understanding wisdom together.

## Getting Started: The Practical Foundations

### Step 1: Define Your Purpose and Format

Before inviting anyone, clarify what kind of group you want to create. Here are some common models:

**Weekly Parsha Study Group**
- Meets once a week
- Studies the Torah portion (parsha) of that week
- Great for beginners because it provides a natural structure
- Recommended reading: ~15-30 verses plus 2-3 commentaries

**Pirkei Avot Ethics Circle**
- Studies Ethics of the Fathers (Pirkei Avot)
- Perfect for philosophical discussions
- Accessible to complete beginners
- Each mishnah (teaching) can spark 30-60 minutes of conversation

**Talmud Page Study**
- For more advanced learners
- Moves slowly through a tractate (one page might take several sessions)
- Requires Hebrew/Aramaic resources or good English translations
- Builds logical reasoning skills

**Topical Study Series**
- Focuses on specific themes: prayer, Shabbat laws, Jewish philosophy, etc.
- Flexible format that can adapt to group interests
- Can draw from various sources

**Mixed Format**
- Alternates between different types of texts
- Keeps engagement high through variety
- Good for maintaining long-term momentum

### Step 2: Choose Your Text and Resources

Start with something accessible. If everyone in your group is new to Jewish learning, don't begin with dense Talmudic passages. Instead, consider:

**For Beginners:**
- **Pirkei Avot** with English commentary (available free on Sefaria.org)
- **Weekly Parsha** with Rashi's commentary (also on Sefaria)
- **Tanya** Chapter 1-12 (fundamental Chassidic teachings, very beginner-friendly)
- **Selected stories from the Talmud** (Gemara with stories, not legal sections)

**For Intermediate Learners:**
- **Ein Yaakov** (narrative sections of the Talmud, without legal debates)
- **Mesillat Yesharim** (Rabbi Moshe Chaim Luzzatto's ethical masterpiece)
- **The Kuzari** (philosophical dialogue by Rabbi Yehuda HaLevi)

**Essential Resources:**
- **Sefaria.org** — free online library of Jewish texts with translations and commentaries
- **Chabad.org** — extensive Torah classes and articles
- **Aish.com** — contemporary explanations of Jewish concepts

**Language Note for Chinese Speakers:**
Sefaria has some Chinese translations, but they're limited. If your group includes native Mandarin speakers, consider assigning someone to translate key passages before each session. This also deepens that person's learning.

### Step 3: Find Your People

Where do you find others interested in Jewish study in a city with few or no Jews?

**Online Outreach:**
- Post in local expat WeChat groups (be respectful and clear about your group's purpose)
- Join Chinese Jewish learning groups on WeChat or QQ
- Use international platforms: Facebook groups for Jews in Asia, Reddit's r/Judaism
- Connect with Chabad Houses in nearby cities and ask them to spread the word

**Local Connections:**
- Universities often have students interested in comparative religion
- Check if there's a tiny existing Jewish community you haven't discovered yet
- Reach out to anyone you know who's expressed curiosity about Judaism
- Don't underestimate the power of one friend saying "I'm interested" — start with just two people

**Conversion Candidates and Distant Observers:**
Your initial group might include:
- People seriously exploring conversion to Judaism
- Partners of Jews learning about their spouse's heritage  
- Scholars of religion interested in comparative study
- Simply curious individuals drawn to Jewish wisdom

Be clear about the group's purpose: this is a learning circle, not a religious service or conversion prep class (though it can complement those things).

### Step 4: Set Clear Expectations

When inviting people, be explicit about:

**Time Commitment:**
- How long each session lasts (60-90 minutes is ideal)
- How often you meet (weekly is ideal; biweekly works too)
- Whether it's an ongoing group or a limited series

**Preparation:**
- Should people read the text in advance? (Recommended but not always required)
- Will you provide study sheets or questions ahead of time?

**Format:**
- Is this a structured class or an open discussion?
- Will someone lead, or is it rotating leadership?
- How much time for questions versus going through the text?

**Inclusivity:**
- Is the group open to non-Jews? (Usually yes for study groups)
- Are all Jewish movements welcome? (Define your denominational stance)
- What level of observance is expected outside the group? (Usually none)

## Running Your First Session

### Before People Arrive

**Set the Space:**
- Choose a quiet location: a home, café, library study room, or park
- Arrange seating in a circle or around a table (avoid classroom-style rows)
- Have physical copies of the text if possible, or ensure everyone can access it on devices
- Consider providing tea or light refreshments — food creates community

**Prepare Yourself:**
- Read the text you'll study multiple times beforehand
- Write down 3-5 discussion questions
- Look up any difficult words or concepts
- Be ready to say "I don't know, let's find out together"

### The Opening (10 minutes)

1. **Welcome everyone** — especially emphasize that questions are encouraged
2. **Brief context** — What book is this from? Who wrote it? When? Why does it matter?
3. **Set the intention** — "We're here to learn together, not to impress each other"

### Reading the Text (15-20 minutes)

- Read the passage aloud (someone can volunteer, or take turns)
- For Hebrew texts, read the English translation, then mention a key Hebrew word or two
- Go slowly — pause after each sentence if it's dense material
- Immediately clarify confusing words or phrases

### Discussion (30-40 minutes)

**Use these prompts to spark conversation:**
- "What stands out to you in this passage?"
- "Is there anything that surprises you or seems strange?"
- "How might this apply to our lives today?"
- "Does this remind you of anything from your own culture or experience?"

**Facilitate, don't lecture:**
- Your role isn't to be the expert, but to keep the conversation flowing
- When someone makes a point, ask "Can you say more about that?"
- If the group goes silent, that's okay — give people time to think
- Redirect respectfully if the conversation wanders too far off topic

**Handle disagreements gracefully:**
- Jewish tradition embraces debate — multiple interpretations can coexist
- Acknowledge different viewpoints: "That's a valid reading, and here's another way to see it..."
- Focus on the text's meaning, not personal opinions about unrelated topics

### Closing (5-10 minutes)

- Summarize the key insights that emerged
- Preview next week's text
- Thank everyone for coming
- Optional: End with a brief Jewish text or teaching related to learning together

## Keeping Momentum: Long-Term Sustainability

### Addressing Common Challenges

**"People stop coming after a few sessions"**
- This is normal — expect some attrition
- Core groups often stabilize at 3-6 regular attendees
- Quality matters more than quantity
- Check in with absent members personally; life gets busy

**"I don't feel qualified to teach"**
- You're not teaching, you're facilitating a shared learning experience
- Frame yourself as "the person who organizes" not "the rabbi"
- Invite guest speakers occasionally (even via video call from other cities)
- Embrace the beginner's mind — your questions are valuable

**"The group isn't deep enough / is too advanced"**
- Check in with participants: is the pace right?
- Consider splitting into two groups if interests diverge significantly
- Rotate between easier and harder material

**"We're geographically scattered"**
- Meet online via Zoom or WeChat video
- Hybrid model: some in person, some on screen
- Record sessions for absent members (with everyone's permission)

### Building Community Beyond Study

**The study group can become a hub for:**
- Shabbat dinners (rotating hosts)
- Holiday celebrations (especially if no synagogue nearby)
- Life cycle events (someone's conversion, a wedding, a baby naming)
- Support network during difficult times

**Consider starting:**
- A WhatsApp/WeChat group for daily inspiration or questions
- A shared folder of resources and notes
- Occasional social gatherings (not just study)

### Growing Without Losing Intimacy

If your group grows beyond 10-12 people, consider:
- Splitting into two groups (beginner vs. advanced)
- Having sub-groups that meet for different texts
- Maintaining one larger monthly gathering while weekly sessions stay small

## Special Considerations for Groups in Asia

### Language Challenges

If your group includes both English and Mandarin speakers:
- Prepare bilingual study sheets with key terms in both languages
- Budget extra time for translation during sessions
- Rotate who does the translating so it's not always the same person
- Consider teaching basic Hebrew letters so everyone can recognize words

### Cultural Bridge-Building

Judaism and Chinese culture have beautiful resonances:
- **Filial piety (孝)** and the commandment to honor parents
- **Study as spiritual practice** in both Confucian and Jewish tradition
- **Community responsibility** over radical individualism
- **This-world focus** rather than abstract afterlife speculation

Actively draw these connections — they help Chinese learners feel less like they're adopting something foreign and more like they're discovering a parallel wisdom tradition.

### Handling Curiosity vs. Commitment

In Asia, you may encounter people who are:
- Genuinely exploring conversion
- Academically interested but not religious
- Drawn to "Jewish secrets of success" (gently correct these stereotypes)
- Partners of Jews trying to understand their spouse's background

All are welcome in a study group, but be clear that Jewish learning isn't a self-help seminar or a business strategy. It's about engaging with sacred texts and living an examined life.

### Connecting with Distant Communities

Your group doesn't have to operate in isolation:
- Reach out to the nearest Chabad House (even if it's in another city) for guidance and resources
- Connect with other independent study groups via online Jewish learning networks
- Invite traveling rabbis or scholars to visit when they're in your region
- Consider an annual pilgrimage to a city with a larger Jewish community for Shabbat

## Resources and Discussion Questions to Get Started

### Sample Session Plan: Pirkei Avot 1:1

**Text:**
"Moses received the Torah at Sinai and transmitted it to Joshua, Joshua to the Elders, the Elders to the Prophets, and the Prophets transmitted it to the Men of the Great Assembly. They said three things: Be deliberate in judgment, raise up many disciples, and make a fence around the Torah."

**Opening Question:**
What does it mean that wisdom is "transmitted" rather than just written down and read?

**Discussion Prompts:**
1. The text emphasizes an unbroken chain of teacher-to-student transmission. Why is that important? How is it different from just reading a book alone?
2. "Be deliberate in judgment" — What's the danger of being too quick to judge?
3. "Raise up many disciples" — If you teach what you learn, does that make you a teacher even if you're still a student?
4. "Make a fence around the Torah" — This means creating extra protections so you don't accidentally violate a law. Is this a good idea or too strict?

**Cultural Connection:**
The Confucian concept of the transmission of the Way (道統) similarly emphasizes the importance of an unbroken chain from ancient sages to later scholars. Both traditions see wisdom as something alive, passed through personal relationship, not just preserved in books.

**Closing Reflection:**
"By studying together today, we've become part of this chain. What we learn tonight, we might teach someone else tomorrow — even if it's just sharing an insight with a friend. We're not just reading ancient words; we're keeping them alive."

## Conclusion: You're More Ready Than You Think

Here's the secret that intimidates many people away from starting a study group: **you don't need to be a scholar to facilitate one.** What you need is:

- **Curiosity** — a genuine desire to understand the text
- **Humility** — comfort with saying "I don't know, let's explore this together"
- **Consistency** — showing up week after week, even when only two people come
- **Generosity** — creating a welcoming space where every question is respected

Some of the most transformative Jewish learning happens not in prestigious yeshivas, but in small living rooms where a handful of seekers gather around a text. Your study group might start with just you and one other person. That's enough. The Talmud says, "When two people sit and words of Torah pass between them, the Divine Presence rests between them" (Pirkei Avot 3:2).

You don't need a rabbi. You don't need a synagogue. You don't even need a minyan (prayer quorum of ten). What you need is one other person who wants to learn, a text, and the willingness to begin.

So take out your calendar. Pick a day and time. Message that one friend who's shown interest. Find a text on Sefaria that intrigues you. Prepare three questions.

And start.

---

## Key Takeaways

- **You don't need to be an expert** — facilitating a study group is about creating space for shared learning, not lecturing
- **Start small and simple** — two or three people studying Pirkei Avot or the weekly parsha is enough to begin
- **Consistency matters more than size** — a group of three that meets every week for a year will learn far more than a group of twelve that fizzles out after a month
- **Use free online resources** — Sefaria.org and Chabad.org provide almost everything you need
- **Cultural connections deepen understanding** — especially for Chinese learners, drawing parallels to Confucian and Daoist traditions helps Jewish ideas feel less foreign
- **The group can grow beyond study** — what starts as a text discussion can become a community for Shabbat, holidays, and life's milestones
