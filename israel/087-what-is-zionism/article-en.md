## Introduction

For nearly two thousand years — from the destruction of the Second Temple in 70 CE (during China's Eastern Han Dynasty, 东汉, 25-220 CE) until the establishment of the State of Israel in 1948 — the Jewish people lived in diaspora, scattered across the globe. Yet every day, three times daily in their prayers, Jews turned toward Jerusalem and asked God to return them to their ancestral homeland. When a Jewish wedding ends with the groom breaking a glass, it's a reminder of the destroyed Temple. At the Passover Seder table, families conclude with the words "Next year in Jerusalem!" This wasn't metaphorical. For Jews, the Land of Israel — called Eretz Yisrael (ארץ ישראל) in Hebrew — has always been home, even when they couldn't live there.

Zionism is the movement that transformed this ancient yearning into modern political reality. But what exactly is Zionism? Is it a political movement, a religious obligation, or something else entirely? And why does it matter to someone learning about Judaism in China today? This article explores Zionism from a Jewish perspective — its roots in Torah, its historical development, and its theological significance.

## What Does "Zion" Mean?

The word "Zionism" comes from "Zion" (ציון, Tzion), one of the biblical names for Jerusalem. Mount Zion was the hill on which King David established his capital around 1000 BCE (during China's Western Zhou Dynasty, 西周, 1046-771 BCE). In Jewish liturgy and poetry, "Zion" became a poetic synonym for Jerusalem and, by extension, the entire Land of Israel.

When we speak of Zionism, we're talking about the Jewish people's connection to Zion — to Jerusalem, to the land promised to Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, to the place where the Temple stood, where prophecy flourished, and where, according to Jewish belief, the Messiah will one day arrive.

## The Biblical Roots: God's Promise to Abraham

The Jewish connection to the Land of Israel begins in the Torah, in the very first conversation God has with Abraham:

> "Go forth from your land, from your birthplace, and from your father's house, to the land that I will show you. And I will make you into a great nation..." (Genesis 12:1-2)

God doesn't just call Abraham to leave; He calls him **to** somewhere — to a specific land. A few verses later, God makes explicit what that land is:

> "To your descendants I will give this land." (Genesis 12:7)

Later, God formalizes this as an eternal covenant:

> "On that day, the Lord made a covenant with Abram, saying: To your descendants I have given this land, from the river of Egypt to the great river, the Euphrates River." (Genesis 15:18)

This covenant — the promise of the land — is repeated to Isaac, to Jacob, and reaffirmed throughout the Torah. In Jewish thought, the Land of Israel isn't just any land; it's the land chosen by God for the Jewish people, the place where the relationship between God and Israel is meant to unfold.

### The Land Flows with Holiness

The Torah describes the Land of Israel as "a land flowing with milk and honey" (Exodus 3:8), but it also makes clear that it's spiritually unique. Deuteronomy 11:12 says:

> "A land that the Lord your God seeks out; the eyes of the Lord your God are always upon it, from the beginning of the year to the end of the year."

According to Jewish tradition, the Land of Israel is the place where God's presence is most felt, where prophecy was possible, where the Temple once stood and will stand again. Many commandments in the Torah — agricultural laws, offerings, tithes — can only be fully observed in the Land of Israel. To live there is not merely a geographic choice; it's a religious act.

## A 2,000-Year Longing: Remembering Zion in Exile

When the Romans destroyed the Second Temple in 70 CE and exiled much of the Jewish population, the physical connection to the Land was severed — but the spiritual connection never was.

### Daily Prayers Point to Jerusalem

Three times a day, in the Amidah prayer (the silent standing prayer at the heart of every Jewish service), Jews pray:

> "Sound the great shofar for our freedom, and raise a banner to gather our exiles, and gather us together from the four corners of the earth. Blessed are You, God, who gathers the dispersed of His people Israel."

And:

> "Return in mercy to Jerusalem Your city, and dwell in it as You have spoken; rebuild it soon in our days as an eternal structure..."

These aren't ancient relics. These are prayers recited by Jews around the world — in Shanghai, Beijing, New York, Jerusalem — every single day, for nearly 2,000 years.

### "Next Year in Jerusalem"

At the end of the Passover Seder, after recounting the Exodus from Egypt, Jewish families say together: **"L'shanah haba'ah b'Yerushalayim!"** — "Next year in Jerusalem!" It's both a hope and a commitment. Even when living in exile was comfortable, the Jewish people never stopped longing to return.

### Mourning and Memory

Jewish tradition institutionalized the memory of Jerusalem's destruction:

- **Tisha B'Av**, the saddest day on the Jewish calendar, commemorates the destruction of both the First and Second Temples.
- At weddings, even in moments of joy, a glass is broken to remember the destroyed Temple.
- Traditional Jews leave a small section of wall unpainted in their homes as a reminder of the Temple's ruins.
- Psalms sung on Shabbat and holidays constantly reference Zion and Jerusalem.

One of the most famous psalms, Psalm 137, captures this sentiment:

> "By the rivers of Babylon, there we sat and wept when we remembered Zion... If I forget you, O Jerusalem, let my right hand forget its skill. Let my tongue cling to the roof of my mouth if I do not remember you, if I do not set Jerusalem above my highest joy." (Psalm 137:1, 5-6)

## The Modern Zionist Movement: Turning Prayer into Action

For centuries, the return to Zion was understood as something that would happen when the Messiah came. Jews prayed for it, longed for it, but didn't actively organize to make it happen politically. That changed in the late 19th century.

### The Birth of Political Zionism

In the 1880s and 1890s, Jews across Europe — especially in Eastern Europe and Russia — faced increasing persecution, pogroms, and legal restrictions. At the same time, nationalist movements were sweeping across Europe: Italians, Greeks, Poles, and others were asserting their right to self-determination and statehood. Jewish intellectuals began to ask: if every nation deserves its own homeland, why not the Jewish people?

The most famous figure in this movement was **Theodor Herzl** (1860-1904), an Austrian-Hungarian journalist. After witnessing the Dreyfus Affair in France — where a Jewish military officer was falsely convicted of treason amid widespread antisemitism — Herzl concluded that Jews would never be safe as minorities in other nations. In 1896, he published *Der Judenstaat* (*The Jewish State*), arguing that the Jewish people needed their own sovereign state.

In 1897, Herzl convened the **First Zionist Congress** in Basel, Switzerland, where he declared:

> "At Basel, I founded the Jewish State. If I said this out loud today, I would be greeted by universal laughter. In five years perhaps, and certainly in fifty years, everyone will perceive it."

He was off by one year. Fifty-one years later, in 1948, the State of Israel was established.

### Different Streams of Zionism

Zionism wasn't monolithic. From the start, there were different visions:

1. **Political Zionism** (Herzl): Focused on creating a legally recognized Jewish state through diplomacy and international support.

2. **Labor Zionism**: Emphasized building a socialist Jewish society in the land, with collective farms (kibbutzim) and worker cooperatives. Many early Israeli leaders, including David Ben-Gurion, came from this stream.

3. **Cultural Zionism** (Ahad Ha'am): Argued that the Land of Israel should first be a spiritual and cultural center for the Jewish people, reviving Hebrew language and culture, before focusing on statehood.

4. **Religious Zionism**: Saw the return to the land as the fulfillment of biblical prophecy and a religious commandment. Rabbi Abraham Isaac Kook (1865-1935), the first Ashkenazi Chief Rabbi of British Mandate Palestine, taught that the Zionist movement — even its secular members — was part of the divine plan for redemption.

### Chinese Parallel: The Fall of Imperial China and the Republic

To understand the context, consider: Modern Zionism emerged during the same period when China's Qing Dynasty (清朝, 1644-1912) was collapsing and Chinese intellectuals were debating how to modernize and save the nation. Just as Chinese reformers looked to restore China's sovereignty and dignity after the "Century of Humiliation," Zionists sought to restore Jewish sovereignty after centuries of exile and persecution. Both movements asked: How do we take an ancient people with a glorious past and rebuild them into a modern nation?

## The Religious Debate: Is Zionism a Mitzvah?

Among religious Jews, opinions on Zionism have varied:

### The Religious Zionist View

Many religious Jews, especially those influenced by Rabbi Kook, see Zionism as deeply holy:

- **The Mitzvah of Living in the Land**: The Torah commands Jews to settle and inherit the Land (Numbers 33:53). Ramban (Nachmanides, 1194-1270) counts living in the Land of Israel as one of the 613 commandments.

- **The Beginning of Redemption**: Religious Zionists view the establishment of the State of Israel as *Atchalta d'Geulah* (אתחלתא דגאולה) — "the beginning of the redemption." The return of Jews to the land after 2,000 years is seen as the start of the messianic process.

- **Ingathering of Exiles**: The prophecies speak of God gathering the Jewish people from the four corners of the earth (Isaiah 11:12, Ezekiel 37). Zionism fulfilled this literally — Jews came to Israel from over 100 countries.

### The Anti-Zionist Orthodox View

Some ultra-Orthodox groups, particularly Satmar Hasidim and Neturei Karta, oppose Zionism on religious grounds:

- **Waiting for the Messiah**: They argue that Jews should wait passively for God to send the Messiah, not try to "force the end" through human political action.

- **The Three Oaths**: A Talmudic passage (Ketubot 111a) speaks of three oaths: that Jews not "go up like a wall" (ascend en masse without divine permission), not rebel against the nations, and that the nations not oppress Israel too much. These groups interpret this as prohibiting mass return before the Messiah.

- **Secular Leadership**: They object to a Jewish state led by secular Jews who don't observe Torah law.

**Important Note**: These anti-Zionist groups represent a small minority of Orthodox Jews. The vast majority of religious Jews today, including the major rabbinical authorities in Israel and the diaspora, support the State of Israel, even if they have political disagreements with specific policies.

## What Zionism Means Today

In contemporary Jewish life, Zionism is multifaceted:

### 1. Support for Israel's Right to Exist

At its core, Zionism today means affirming the Jewish people's right to self-determination in their ancestral homeland. It means believing that Jews, like any other people, deserve a state where they can live freely and safely.

This is especially meaningful in the context of Jewish history. After the Holocaust — when six million Jews were murdered while most of the world's borders were closed to them — the need for a Jewish homeland became undeniable. Zionism means: "Never again will Jews be stateless, vulnerable, and without refuge."

### 2. Connection to the Land

For religious Jews, Zionism means maintaining the connection to the Land of Israel established in the Torah. It means the ability to pray at the Western Wall, walk where Abraham walked, study Torah in Jerusalem, and observe mitzvot that can only be done in the land.

### 3. Hebrew Revival

Zionism made an ancient miracle modern: Hebrew, the language of the Bible, became a living, spoken language again. Before Zionism, Hebrew was mostly a language of prayer and scholarship. Today, children in Israel argue about playground rules in the language of the prophets. This is unprecedented in history.

### 4. A Pluralistic Society

Modern Israel is complex. It includes:
- Secular Jews and ultra-Orthodox Jews
- Ashkenazi Jews from Europe and Sephardi Jews from the Middle East and North Africa
- Ethiopian Jews (Beta Israel), Indian Jews (Bnei Menashe), and Chinese Jews (Kaifeng)
- Russian-speaking immigrants, French speakers, English speakers
- Arab citizens of Israel, Druze, Bedouins

Zionism in practice means wrestling with what a Jewish democratic state looks like when "Jewish" and "democratic" sometimes pull in different directions.

## Zionism Is Not…

It's important to clarify what Zionism is **not**:

- **Not imperialism**: The Jewish people are indigenous to the Land of Israel; Zionism is a return, not colonization.
- **Not racism**: Zionism is about Jewish self-determination, not superiority over others.
- **Not the same as Israeli government policies**: One can be a Zionist and still disagree with specific actions of the Israeli government. Debate and criticism are core to Jewish tradition.

## Why This Matters to You

If you're studying Judaism in China, Zionism may seem distant. But understanding it is crucial because:

1. **It's Central to Jewish Identity**: The majority of Jews worldwide identify as Zionists. To understand Judaism today, you need to understand this connection to the land.

2. **It Shapes Jewish Prayer and Practice**: From the direction you face during prayer to the holidays you observe (especially connected to the agricultural cycle of Israel), the Land of Israel is woven throughout Jewish life.

3. **It's Part of the Covenant**: If you're considering conversion or deepening your Jewish learning, you're connecting to a people whose story is inseparable from this land.

4. **Chinese Jews Have Returned Too**: Some descendants of the Kaifeng Jewish community have made aliyah (immigrated to Israel) and reconnected with the global Jewish people. Your learning might one day lead you to walk the streets of Jerusalem yourself.

## Practical Application

If you want to connect with the Zionist spirit:

- **Add "Next year in Jerusalem" to your Passover Seder** (even if you celebrate it alone or with friends).
- **Face Jerusalem when you pray** (from China, that's west-southwest).
- **Learn modern Hebrew**: Many excellent apps like Duolingo now teach it.
- **Study Israel's history**: Read about the early pioneers (chalutzim), the kibbutz movement, and Israel's founding.
- **Visit Israel**: If possible, make a trip. Walking in Jerusalem, swimming in the Dead Sea, visiting Masada — experiencing the land transforms your understanding.

## Key Takeaways

- **Zionism is the belief that the Jewish people have the right to self-determination in their ancestral homeland, the Land of Israel.**
- **This connection is rooted in the Torah**: God promised the land to Abraham and his descendants.
- **For 2,000 years, Jews prayed daily to return** to Jerusalem and the Land of Israel.
- **Modern Zionism transformed this religious longing into a political movement** that led to the establishment of the State of Israel in 1948.
- **Religious Jews debate the theological implications**, but the majority see the return as divinely significant.
- **Zionism is central to contemporary Jewish identity**, and understanding it is essential to understanding Judaism today.
