## Introduction

Imagine a city that has been continuously inhabited for over 3,000 years, destroyed twice, besieged 23 times, attacked 52 times, and captured and recaptured 44 times. A city that was the capital of only two nations in all of recorded history — both of them Jewish. A city mentioned in the Bible 669 times, yet never once mentioned in the Quran. A city toward which Jews have prayed three times daily for over 2,500 years, no matter where in the world they lived.

This is **Jerusalem** — **Yerushalayim** (ירושלים) in Hebrew, the eternal capital of the Jewish people and the holiest city in Judaism.

For a Chinese reader, perhaps the closest parallel would be imagining Xi'an (西安) — ancient Chang'an — as the spiritual center of Chinese civilization. But even this comparison falls short, because Jerusalem's significance to Jews transcends politics, history, or culture. It represents the point where heaven and earth meet, where the Divine chose to rest His presence, and where the ultimate redemption of humanity will unfold.

This article traces Jerusalem's 3,000-year journey through Jewish history and explains why this city — and specifically this one spot on earth — holds such singular importance in Jewish consciousness, prayer, and hope.

## The Foundation: King David and the City of Peace

### David Conquers Jebus (~1000 BCE / Late Western Zhou Dynasty 西周晚期)

Nearly three thousand years ago — during what corresponds to China's late Western Zhou Dynasty (西周, ~1046-771 BCE) — Jerusalem was a small Jebusite fortress city called Jebus, perched on a narrow ridge between two valleys. It was militarily strong but relatively insignificant.

When David became king of the united tribes of Israel, he needed a capital that would belong equally to all twelve tribes — neutral ground that wouldn't favor the northern or southern kingdoms. Jerusalem, sitting on the border between the tribes of Judah and Benjamin, was perfect.

The Book of Samuel (II Samuel 5:6-10) describes how David conquered the city through strategic cunning, entering through the water shaft system. He renamed it "Ir David" (City of David) and made it the political capital of the united kingdom of Israel.

### The Ark Comes to Jerusalem

But David did something even more significant: he brought the **Ark of the Covenant** — the physical symbol of God's presence among the Jewish people — to Jerusalem (II Samuel 6). With this act, Jerusalem became not just a political capital, but the spiritual center of the nation.

The Ark contained the tablets of the Ten Commandments that Moses received at Mount Sinai. By placing the Ark in Jerusalem, David was saying: this is where God has chosen to dwell among His people. This is where heaven touches earth.

### "The City of David" — A Name That Echoes Through History

The Tanakh (Hebrew Bible) tells us that David danced before the Ark as it entered Jerusalem, so overcome with joy that he cast off his royal robes and danced in simple garments (II Samuel 6:14). This image — the king himself dancing with abandonment before God's presence — set the tone for Jerusalem's spiritual significance.

From that moment forward, Jerusalem would be known as "the City of David," and every Jewish prayer for Jerusalem's restoration invokes David's name. Even today, Jews pray daily: "And to Jerusalem, Your city, may You return in compassion... Rebuild it soon, in our days, as an eternal structure, and may You speedily establish the throne of David within it" (Amidah prayer).

## The Temple: Solomon and the Divine Dwelling

### Solomon Builds the First Temple (~957 BCE / Early Western Zhou 西周)

David's son Solomon fulfilled his father's dream and built the First Temple (Beit HaMikdash HaRishon) on Mount Moriah in Jerusalem — the very spot where, according to tradition, Abraham had bound Isaac centuries earlier (Genesis 22).

The building of the Temple (described in I Kings 6-8) was an unprecedented engineering and spiritual achievement. Solomon employed 150,000 workers and craftsmen. The structure was overlaid with gold, decorated with carved cherubim, and contained chambers for the priestly service. Most significantly, the Holy of Holies (Kodesh HaKodashim) — the innermost chamber — housed the Ark of the Covenant.

### The Shekhinah Descends

When the Temple was completed and the Ark placed in the Holy of Holies, something extraordinary occurred:

> "And when the priests came out of the Holy Place, a cloud filled the house of the Lord, so that the priests could not stand to minister because of the cloud, for the glory of the Lord filled the house of the Lord." (I Kings 8:10-11)

This moment — when the **Shekhinah** (Divine Presence) descended and filled the Temple — marked Jerusalem as the chosen dwelling place of God on earth. It wasn't just a symbol or metaphor; Jewish tradition teaches that this was a real, tangible manifestation of divine reality in the physical world.

From this moment until the Temple's destruction 410 years later, Jerusalem was **the** religious center of the Jewish world. Three times a year, during the pilgrimage festivals (Passover, Shavuot, and Sukkot), hundreds of thousands of Jews from across the Land of Israel would ascend to Jerusalem to worship at the Temple.

### Jerusalem as the "Navel of the World"

The Talmud and Midrashic literature describe Jerusalem — and specifically the Foundation Stone (Even HaShtiyah) in the Holy of Holies — as the "navel of the world," the point from which all creation emanated (Tanchuma, Kedoshim 10).

In Chinese cosmology, there's a similar concept of the axis mundi (世界之軸) or cosmic center. Jerusalem, in Jewish thought, is precisely this: the vertical axis connecting the earthly and heavenly realms.

## Destruction and Exile: The First Catastrophe

### The Babylonian Conquest (586 BCE / Spring and Autumn Period 春秋时代)

For over four centuries, the Temple stood and Jerusalem flourished. But in 586 BCE — during what corresponds to China's Spring and Autumn period (春秋时代, 770-476 BCE) — the Babylonian king Nebuchadnezzar laid siege to Jerusalem.

After a brutal siege that caused mass starvation, the Babylonians breached the walls, burned the Temple, and destroyed the city. The Book of Lamentations (Eicha), traditionally attributed to the prophet Jeremiah, describes the devastation:

> "How lonely sits the city that was full of people! How like a widow has she become, she who was great among the nations!" (Lamentations 1:1)

The Jewish people were exiled to Babylon. But even in exile, they never forgot Jerusalem.

### "If I Forget You, O Jerusalem"

Psalm 137, composed during the Babylonian exile, captures the Jewish relationship with Jerusalem in words that have echoed for 2,600 years:

> "By the rivers of Babylon, there we sat down, and we wept when we remembered Zion... If I forget you, O Jerusalem, let my right hand forget its skill! Let my tongue stick to the roof of my mouth if I do not remember you, if I do not set Jerusalem above my highest joy." (Psalm 137:1, 5-6)

This wasn't just poetic sentiment. It became — and remains — the defining feature of Jewish identity: no matter where Jews lived, Jerusalem was home. No matter how long the exile, Jerusalem was never forgotten.

Even today, at every Jewish wedding, a glass is broken and the words of Psalm 137 are recited — "If I forget you, O Jerusalem" — to remember that even in moments of greatest joy, we recall Jerusalem's destruction and pray for its restoration.

## Return and Renewal: The Second Temple

### The Return from Babylon (516 BCE / Late Spring and Autumn 春秋晚期)

Seventy years after the destruction — exactly as the prophet Jeremiah had foretold — the Persian king Cyrus issued a decree permitting the Jews to return to Jerusalem and rebuild the Temple (Ezra 1:1-4).

The returning exiles found the city in ruins. But led by Ezra and Nehemiah, they rebuilt the Temple (completed in 516 BCE, during China's late Spring and Autumn period) and restored the walls of Jerusalem.

The Second Temple (Beit HaMikdash HaSheni) stood for 586 years — longer than the First Temple. During this period, Jerusalem witnessed:

- The Hellenization crisis and the Maccabean revolt (167-160 BCE / Han Dynasty 汉朝)
- Herod's massive expansion of the Temple (20 BCE onward), creating one of the architectural wonders of the ancient world
- The ministry of Hillel and Shammai, the great sages who shaped the Oral Torah
- The birth of Rabbinic Judaism

### "Next Year in Jerusalem"

During the Second Temple period, the practice solidified of Jews everywhere praying toward Jerusalem. The prophet Daniel is described as praying three times daily facing Jerusalem (Daniel 6:11), a practice that became universal Jewish law.

Even more poignantly, the Passover Seder — which had been practiced for centuries even during the exile — ended with the words: **"L'shanah haba'ah b'Yerushalayim!"** ("Next year in Jerusalem!")

These words are still recited today at every Passover Seder, at the conclusion of Yom Kippur, and often at Jewish weddings. They capture the Jewish people's unbreakable hope: no matter how long the exile, we will return to Jerusalem.

## The Second Destruction: Mourning That Never Ended

### The Roman Siege (70 CE / Eastern Han Dynasty 东汉)

In 66 CE (during China's Eastern Han Dynasty, 东汉, 25-220 CE), a Jewish revolt against Roman occupation broke out. Four years later, the Roman general (and future emperor) Titus laid siege to Jerusalem during Passover, when the city was packed with pilgrims.

The siege was brutal. The historian Josephus, who witnessed it, describes mass starvation, internecine fighting among Jewish factions, and ultimately the burning of the Temple. On the 9th day of the Hebrew month of Av (Tisha B'Av) in the year 70 CE, the Second Temple was destroyed.

According to tradition, this was the exact same date — Tisha B'Av — on which the First Temple had been destroyed 656 years earlier.

### A Mourning That Continues

To this day — over 1,950 years later — Jews observe Tisha B'Av as a day of fasting and mourning. They read the Book of Lamentations, sit on low stools (as mourners do), and refrain from normal pleasures. Jerusalem's destruction is mourned as if it happened yesterday.

This is unprecedented in human history. No other people has mourned the loss of a city for nearly two millennia. No other religion has maintained such focused grief and longing for a specific geographical location.

Consider: if someone in modern China mourned the fall of a city from the Eastern Han Dynasty with the same intensity Jews mourn Jerusalem, it would mean weeping every year for something that happened in the year 220 CE — over 1,800 years ago. Yet Jews do exactly this for Jerusalem's destruction in 70 CE.

### Bar Kochba and the Final Desolation (135 CE / Eastern Han 东汉)

Sixty-five years after the Temple's destruction, another revolt broke out under the leadership of Simon Bar Kochba (132-135 CE). The revolt initially succeeded, and Jerusalem was briefly liberated.

But the Roman emperor Hadrian crushed the revolt with overwhelming force. He renamed Jerusalem "Aelia Capitolina," banned Jews from entering the city on pain of death (except one day per year — Tisha B'Av, to mourn at the ruins), and attempted to erase Jewish connection to the land by renaming Judea "Syria Palaestina."

From 135 CE until 1967 — a span of 1,832 years — Jerusalem was not under Jewish sovereignty.

## Jerusalem in Exile: The Heart of Jewish Prayer

### Facing Jerusalem: A Daily Compass

Even during the darkest periods of exile, Jews never ceased praying toward Jerusalem. Jewish law (halakha) is explicit:

- If you live outside the Land of Israel, you pray facing Israel.
- If you're in Israel, you pray facing Jerusalem.
- If you're in Jerusalem, you pray facing the Temple Mount.
- If you're on the Temple Mount, you pray facing the site of the Holy of Holies.

This created a spiritual geography: every Jew, no matter where they stood on earth, oriented themselves toward this one spot in Jerusalem when they prayed. Three times a day, millions of Jews across the world turned toward Jerusalem and prayed for its restoration.

### Jerusalem in Daily Prayer

Jerusalem is woven into the fabric of Jewish prayer:

- The **Amidah** (central prayer) includes a blessing: "And to Jerusalem, Your city, may You return in compassion... Rebuild it soon, in our days."
- **Grace After Meals** (Birkat HaMazon) includes: "Have mercy, Lord our God, on Israel Your people, on Jerusalem Your city, on Zion the abode of Your glory, on the monarchy of the house of David Your anointed."
- Every wedding ends with "If I forget you, O Jerusalem..."
- Every house of mourning hears the words: "May God comfort you among the mourners of Zion and Jerusalem."

A Jewish person who prays daily mentions Jerusalem dozens of times each day. Over a lifetime, this amounts to hundreds of thousands of utterances of Jerusalem's name — a continuous, millennia-long prayer.

### Medieval Poets and Jerusalem Longing

Throughout the Middle Ages, Jewish poets in Spain, North Africa, and the Middle East composed heartrending poems of longing for Jerusalem. One of the most famous is Rabbi Yehuda HaLevi's (1075-1141, during China's Song Dynasty 宋朝) "My Heart is in the East":

> "My heart is in the East, and I am at the edge of the West.  
> How can I taste what I eat, how can it please me?  
> How can I fulfill my vows and pledges  
> While Zion is in the domain of Edom, and I am in the bonds of Arabia?  
> It would be easy for me to leave behind all the goodness of Spain —  
> As it is precious for me to see the dust of the desolate Sanctuary."

Rabbi Yehuda HaLevi wasn't being metaphorical. In his old age, he actually left Spain and traveled to the Land of Israel, fulfilling his lifelong dream to see Jerusalem. According to legend, he was killed by an Arab horseman as he kissed the ground outside Jerusalem's gates — dying in the very moment of fulfillment.

## Under Foreign Rule: Byzantines, Muslims, Crusaders, Ottomans

### Byzantine and Early Islamic Periods (4th-7th centuries / 魏晋南北朝, 隋唐)

After the Bar Kochba revolt, Jerusalem remained largely closed to Jews under Byzantine Christian rule. Jews were permitted to visit once a year on Tisha B'Av to mourn at the ruins of the Temple.

In 637 CE (during China's early Tang Dynasty, 唐朝, 618-907), the Muslim caliph Umar conquered Jerusalem. Initially, Jewish access to the city improved somewhat under Islamic rule, though Jews still could not live freely in the city and had to pay special taxes.

### The Crusader Nightmare (1099-1187 / Northern Song 北宋)

In 1099 (during China's Northern Song Dynasty, 北宋, 960-1127), Christian Crusaders from Europe conquered Jerusalem and massacred most of its Jewish and Muslim inhabitants. The Crusader period was one of the darkest in Jerusalem's history for Jews, who were either killed, expelled, or forced to ransom their lives.

When the Muslim leader Salah ad-Din (Saladin) reconquered Jerusalem in 1187, he invited Jews to return to the city.

### Ottoman Jerusalem (1517-1917 / Ming-Qing Dynasties 明清)

Under Ottoman rule (corresponding to China's Ming and Qing dynasties), Jewish life in Jerusalem slowly revived. Communities of Sephardic Jews (expelled from Spain in 1492) joined Ashkenazi Jews and local Mizrahi communities.

By the 19th century, Jews once again formed a majority of Jerusalem's population — a majority they have maintained continuously since the 1860s until today.

### The Balfour Declaration and British Mandate (1917-1948 / Republican Era 民国时期)

When the British captured Jerusalem from the Ottomans in 1917, hopes were high for Jewish restoration. The Balfour Declaration (1917) promised British support for "the establishment in Palestine of a national home for the Jewish people."

However, the British Mandate period (1920-1948) was marked by increasing tensions between Jews and Arabs, violent riots, and British restrictions on Jewish immigration — even as Hitler was murdering six million Jews in Europe.

### Divided Jerusalem (1948-1967)

When Israel declared independence in 1948, Jordan immediately invaded and conquered East Jerusalem, including the Old City and the Western Wall. For the next 19 years, Jews were completely barred from their holiest sites. The Jordanians destroyed 58 synagogues in the Old City and used Jewish tombstones from the Mount of Olives to pave roads and build latrines.

This division was a wound in the heart of every Jew. The State of Israel existed, but its capital — the city Jews had prayed toward for 2,000 years — was cut in two, and the holiest site was unreachable.

## The Liberation: 1967 and the Reunification of Jerusalem

### The Six-Day War (June 1967)

In June 1967, Egypt, Syria, and Jordan mobilized to destroy Israel. In response, Israel launched a preemptive strike. Within six days, the course of Jewish history changed.

On June 7, 1967 — the third day of the war — Israeli paratroopers broke through Jordanian lines and reached the Old City. When they reached the Western Wall, the brigade commander, Motta Gur, radioed the words that electrified the Jewish world:

**"Har HaBayit b'yadeinu!"** ("The Temple Mount is in our hands!")

For the first time in 1,897 years, Jerusalem was under Jewish sovereignty. For the first time since 70 CE, Jews could freely pray at the Western Wall.

### The Iconic Photograph

One of the most famous photographs in Jewish history was taken that day: three young Israeli soldiers, exhausted and battle-worn, standing before the Western Wall moments after its liberation. Their faces show awe, tears, and disbelief. In their hands are rifles; on their faces is wonder.

This image captures the moment when 2,000 years of prayer and longing became reality.

### The Chief Rabbi Blows the Shofar

Chief Rabbi Shlomo Goren arrived at the Wall carrying a Torah scroll and a shofar (ram's horn). Surrounded by soldiers, many weeping openly, he blew the shofar and declared: "I proclaim to you the beginning of the Messianic Age!"

For observant Jews worldwide, the reunification of Jerusalem was seen as nothing less than a miracle — a clear sign of God's ongoing relationship with the Jewish people and the beginning of the redemptive process foretold by the prophets.

## Why Jerusalem Matters: Theological and Spiritual Dimensions

### The Place God Chose

Jerusalem isn't holy to Jews because it's beautiful, strategically important, or historically significant (though it is all these things). Jerusalem is holy because, according to Jewish tradition, **God chose it**.

The Torah repeatedly speaks of "the place that God will choose to place His name" (Deuteronomy 12:11). Jewish tradition identifies this place as Jerusalem, and specifically the Temple Mount.

This choice is described as eternal and irrevocable. Even though the Temple was destroyed, the holiness of the site remains. Even during 2,000 years of exile, Jerusalem's status as the holiest city never diminished.

### The Gate of Heaven

Jacob, after his dream of the ladder reaching heaven, declared: "This is none other than the house of God, and this is the gate of heaven" (Genesis 28:17). Jewish tradition identifies the place of Jacob's dream as the site of the future Temple in Jerusalem.

The image is powerful: Jerusalem — and specifically the Temple Mount — is the point where heaven and earth meet, where prayers ascend most directly, where the boundary between the physical and spiritual is thinnest.

### The Heart of Redemption

Jewish eschatology (end-of-days beliefs) places Jerusalem at the center of ultimate redemption. The prophets foretell that in the Messianic Age:

- The Temple will be rebuilt (Ezekiel 40-48)
- Jerusalem will be the spiritual capital of the world, and "out of Zion shall go forth Torah, and the word of the Lord from Jerusalem" (Isaiah 2:3)
- All nations will recognize the God of Israel and make pilgrimage to Jerusalem (Zechariah 14:16)

Every Jewish prayer for redemption — and Jews pray for redemption dozens of times daily — centers on Jerusalem. There is no conception of Jewish redemption that doesn't include Jerusalem's restoration.

## Living Jews and Jerusalem Today

### Jerusalem as Israel's Capital

Since 1967, Israel has declared Jerusalem its "eternal and indivisible capital." The Knesset (Israeli parliament), Supreme Court, and Prime Minister's residence are all in Jerusalem.

However, the international community largely does not recognize Israeli sovereignty over East Jerusalem, making Jerusalem's status one of the most contested political issues in the world.

### A City of Three Religions — But Only One People's Capital

Jerusalem is sacred to Judaism, Christianity, and Islam. However, it's crucial to note a historical distinction: Jerusalem has been the capital of a nation-state only twice in history — both times for Jewish kingdoms (the Kingdom of Israel under David and Solomon, and the modern State of Israel).

Jerusalem is never mentioned in the Quran. Mecca and Medina are Islam's holiest cities. For Christianity, Rome and the Vatican serve as the headquarters of the Catholic Church. But for Jews, there is no substitute for Jerusalem. It isn't one holy city among many; it is **the** holy city.

### The Demographics: A Jewish Majority

Today, Jerusalem's population is approximately 936,000, of which about 61% are Jewish and 37% are Arab (Muslim and Christian). The city has maintained a Jewish majority continuously since the 1860s — for over 160 years.

### Visiting Jerusalem Today

For Jews today, visiting Jerusalem is a pilgrimage that transcends tourism. The experience of seeing the Old City's walls, walking through the stone streets where prophets walked, praying at the Western Wall, and looking out over the city from the Mount of Olives is profoundly emotional.

Many Jews describe visiting Jerusalem for the first time as "coming home" — even if they've never been there before.

## Chinese Historical Parallels: Understanding Jerusalem's Significance

For Chinese readers trying to understand what Jerusalem means to Jews, consider these parallels:

### The Capital That Defines a Civilization

Jerusalem to Jews is somewhat like what **Chang'an (modern Xi'an)** was to ancient China during the Han and Tang dynasties — the capital during the culture's golden age, the center of learning, the place from which the civilization radiated.

But imagine if Chang'an had been destroyed, the Chinese people scattered across the world for 2,000 years, yet every Chinese person worldwide still prayed three times a day facing Xi'an, still said "Next year in Xi'an!" at major celebrations, and still mourned its fall every year as if it happened last week. This begins to approximate Jerusalem's place in Jewish consciousness.

### The Unbreakable Thread

Throughout Chinese history, even during periods of division (such as the Three Kingdoms period, the Northern and Southern Dynasties, or the period before reunification under the Sui), the idea of a unified China under Heaven's Mandate remained central to Chinese identity.

Similarly, throughout 2,000 years of Jewish dispersion, the idea of return to Jerusalem remained the unbreakable thread of Jewish identity. "Next year in Jerusalem" kept the Jewish people united across continents, languages, and centuries.

### The Sacred Mountain

In Chinese culture, mountains like **Mount Tai (泰山)** hold immense spiritual significance as points of connection between heaven and earth, places where emperors performed sacrifices to Heaven.

The Temple Mount in Jerusalem serves a similar function in Judaism — it's the axis mundi, the cosmic center, the mountain where heaven and earth meet. But for Jews, it isn't one sacred mountain among many; it's **the** sacred mountain, chosen by God Himself.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Jerusalem's uniqueness:** Jerusalem has been the capital of only two nation-states in history — both Jewish. It's mentioned 669 times in the Hebrew Bible but never in the Quran. For Jews, it isn't one holy city among many; it is the only holy city.

2. **Three thousand years of continuity:** From King David (~1000 BCE) to the modern State of Israel, Jewish connection to Jerusalem has been continuous and unwavering. Even during 2,000 years of exile, Jews prayed toward Jerusalem three times daily and never abandoned hope of return.

3. **The Temple site:** The holiness of Jerusalem centers on the Temple Mount, where the First and Second Temples stood and where Jewish tradition places the binding of Isaac, Jacob's dream, and the Foundation Stone of creation.

4. **Mourning and hope intertwined:** For 2,000 years, Jews have mourned Jerusalem's destruction on Tisha B'Av while simultaneously expressing hope for restoration in daily prayers and lifecycle events. The phrase "If I forget you, O Jerusalem" expresses an oath of eternal fidelity.

5. **1967 as a turning point:** The reunification of Jerusalem in 1967 after 1,897 years of non-Jewish rule was experienced by many Jews as a miracle and the beginning of messianic redemption.

6. **Religious and national identity fused:** For Jews, Jerusalem represents the inseparable fusion of religious and national identity. One cannot understand Judaism without understanding Jerusalem, and one cannot understand the Jewish people without understanding their relationship to this city.

7. **Contemporary significance:** Today, Jerusalem is Israel's capital, a city of nearly one million people, and the focal point of the Israeli-Palestinian conflict. Yet beneath all political complexities, Jerusalem's meaning for Jews remains what it has been for three millennia: the place God chose, the city of David, and the eternal capital of the Jewish people.

## Conclusion: "If I Forget You, O Jerusalem"

Three thousand years is a long time. Empires have risen and fallen. Languages have gone extinct. Entire civilizations have disappeared without a trace.

But the Jewish people's bond with Jerusalem has not frayed. If anything, it has strengthened through the centuries of exile and persecution. The very impossibility of Jewish survival — a small nation scattered across the globe for two millennia, speaking different languages, living under different rulers, yet remaining identifiably Jewish — is inseparable from Jerusalem.

Jerusalem kept the Jews alive. The memory of Jerusalem, the prayer toward Jerusalem, the hope of return to Jerusalem — these things prevented the Jewish people from simply dissolving into the nations around them.

And Jerusalem itself, the physical city, owes its sanctity and its survival to the Jewish people who never forgot it. Other ancient cities — Babylon, Nineveh, Carthage — are ruins or archaeological sites. Jerusalem lives because Jews never stopped praying for it, never stopped saying "Next year in Jerusalem," never stopped believing that someday, somehow, they would return.

When Israeli soldiers reached the Western Wall in 1967, they were closing a circle that began with King David 3,000 years earlier. In that moment, the words of Psalm 137 — "If I forget you, O Jerusalem, let my right hand forget its skill" — were answered. The Jewish people had not forgotten. And they had come home.

For Jews today, whether religious or secular, Israeli or diaspora, Jerusalem remains what it has always been: the city of longing, the city of prayer, the city where — according to the prophets — all humanity will one day recognize the One God and live in peace.

**Next year in Jerusalem.**  
**L'shanah haba'ah b'Yerushalayim.**  
**明年在耶路撒冷。**
