## Introduction

If you've ever seen photographs of Jerusalem, you've likely noticed something remarkable: people standing before a massive stone wall, their hands pressed against ancient blocks, their heads bowed in prayer. To an outside observer, it might seem puzzling — why would someone pray at a wall? What makes these particular stones so sacred that Jews from around the world travel thousands of miles just to touch them?

The Western Wall — known in Hebrew as the **Kotel** (הכותל) or **Kotel HaMa'aravi** (הכותל המערבי) — is the last visible remnant of the Second Temple complex that stood in Jerusalem nearly two thousand years ago. For Jews, this isn't just an archaeological site or a historical monument. It's the holiest place on earth where Jewish people can pray, the closest physical point to where God's presence was once manifest in the Temple's Holy of Holies.

This article will explore what the Western Wall is, why it holds such profound significance in Judaism, and what customs surround visiting this sacred site.

## The Temple and Its Walls

To understand the Western Wall, we must first understand the Temple that once stood behind it.

### The First and Second Temples

Nearly three thousand years ago — during what corresponds to China's Western Zhou Dynasty (西周, ~1046-771 BCE) — King Solomon built the First Temple in Jerusalem. This wasn't just a large synagogue; it was the **only** place in the world where the Jewish sacrificial service was performed, the dwelling place of the Divine Presence (Shekhinah), and the spiritual center of the Jewish nation.

The First Temple stood for approximately 410 years before it was destroyed by the Babylonians in 586 BCE (during China's Spring and Autumn period, 春秋时代).

Seventy years later, the Jews who had been exiled to Babylon returned and built the Second Temple on the same spot. This Temple was later expanded and beautified by King Herod around 20 BCE, creating the magnificent structure that stood during the time of Hillel, Shammai, and the early rabbis.

### The Retaining Walls

To create a level platform for the Temple complex on the top of Mount Moriah, massive retaining walls were built around the mountain. The Western Wall is part of this western retaining wall. The Temple building itself stood on the platform above and behind this wall.

The stones you see today — some weighing over 100 tons — were quarried, transported, and fitted together with such precision that no mortar was needed. Many of these stones have stood for over 2,000 years, surviving wars, earthquakes, and the passage of empires.

### The Destruction and What Remained

In 70 CE (during China's Eastern Han Dynasty, 东汉, 25-220 CE), the Romans destroyed the Second Temple after a devastating siege of Jerusalem. The Temple itself was burned, and most of the structures on the Temple Mount were demolished.

According to Jewish tradition, the retaining walls were left standing — not out of Roman mercy, but because even the Romans recognized that these foundations were too massive and expensive to demolish completely. The Talmud records that God promised: "I will never leave this Western Wall; it will remain forever" (Midrash Tanchuma, Shemot 10).

## Why the Western Wall is Sacred

### Closest Point to the Holy of Holies

In the innermost chamber of the Temple stood the Holy of Holies (Kodesh HaKodashim), where the Ark of the Covenant once rested and where the High Priest entered only once a year on Yom Kippur. This was considered the point of closest connection between heaven and earth.

When Jews pray at the Western Wall, they are praying at the closest accessible point to where the Holy of Holies once stood. Jewish law (halakha) specifies that when Jews pray anywhere in the world, they face Jerusalem; when in Jerusalem, they face the Temple Mount; and at the Wall itself, they face the spot where the Holy of Holies stood.

### The Divine Presence Never Departed

Jewish tradition teaches that although the Temple was destroyed, the Divine Presence (Shekhinah) never completely departed from the Western Wall. As the Midrash states: "The Divine Presence has never moved from the Western Wall" (Shemot Rabbah 2:2).

This belief gives the Wall a unique status among all holy sites in Judaism. While synagogues and study halls are called "mikdash me'at" (small sanctuaries), the Western Wall is considered the remnant of the original and ultimate sanctuary.

### The Wall That Weeps

Throughout Jewish history, especially during periods of exile and persecution, the Western Wall became known as the "Wailing Wall" — named for the tears shed by Jews mourning the destruction of the Temple and praying for redemption. The stones themselves seem to bear witness to Jewish suffering and hope across the generations.

Interestingly, this parallels a Chinese concept: just as ancient Chinese scholars believed that stones and landscapes could hold memory and emotion (as expressed in the scholar's rocks tradition, 供石), Jews believe these ancient stones hold within them the prayers and tears of countless generations.

## Historical Journey: The Wall Through the Ages

### Byzantine and Early Islamic Periods (4th-11th centuries)

After the Roman destruction, Jews were generally forbidden from living in Jerusalem or visiting the Temple Mount. However, at certain periods, Jews were permitted to visit the ruins, particularly on Tisha B'Av (the day commemorating the Temple's destruction).

When the Muslims conquered Jerusalem in 637 CE (during China's early Tang Dynasty, 唐朝, 618-907 CE), the status of Jewish access to holy sites fluctuated depending on the ruler. Some periods allowed relatively free access, while others imposed restrictions.

### Crusader Period (1099-1187)

During the Crusader conquest of Jerusalem, Jews were largely expelled from the city or massacred. Access to the Western Wall was severely restricted during this period.

### Mamluk and Ottoman Periods (13th-20th centuries)

Under Mamluk and later Ottoman rule (similar in time to China's Yuan, Ming, and Qing dynasties, 元明清), Jews gradually regained more consistent access to pray at the Western Wall. The area in front of the wall, however, was very narrow — just a small alley — and prayers often took place in cramped, difficult conditions.

### British Mandate and Jordanian Control (1917-1967)

After World War I, when Britain controlled Palestine, disputes arose between Jews and Muslims regarding rights at the Western Wall. In 1929, these tensions contributed to violent riots.

From 1948-1967, when Jordan controlled the Old City of Jerusalem, Jews were completely barred from accessing the Western Wall. Ancient synagogues in the Jewish Quarter were destroyed, and Jewish gravestones were desecrated.

### Liberation and Access Restored (1967-Present)

During the Six-Day War in 1967, Israeli forces reunited Jerusalem. The most emotional moment came when paratroopers reached the Western Wall and realized that, for the first time in nearly 2,000 years, Jews had sovereignty over their holiest site.

The famous radio transmission from that moment — "The Temple Mount is in our hands!" — and the subsequent photographs of hardened soldiers weeping and praying at the Wall became iconic images of modern Jewish history.

The narrow alley in front of the Wall was cleared (controversial at the time because it required demolishing structures built against it), and today a large plaza accommodates thousands of worshippers at once.

## Visiting the Western Wall: Customs and Practices

### The Approach

As you approach the Western Wall through the Old City's alleyways and descend toward the plaza, there's a palpable shift in atmosphere. Many visitors report feeling an emotional weight — a sense of history and holiness.

Before entering the plaza, men cover their heads (a kippah is required; free paper kippot are available at the entrance). Women should dress modestly (covered shoulders and knees).

### The Plaza is Divided

The Western Wall plaza is divided into two sections:
- **Men's section** (left side when facing the Wall) — larger area where men pray
- **Women's section** (right side) — separate area where women pray

This division follows Orthodox Jewish practice of separate prayer spaces for men and women.

### Prayer Customs

**Approaching the Wall:** It is customary to walk toward the Wall facing forward (not turning your back to it initially) and to walk backward when leaving, as a sign of respect.

**Touching the Wall:** Many people place their hands on the ancient stones as they pray. Some kiss the Wall or press their foreheads against it.

**Prayer Services:** Daily prayer services (Shacharit, Mincha, Maariv) take place at the Wall, and it's always possible to find a minyan (quorum of ten men) for prayer. On Shabbat, holidays, and Monday/Thursday mornings when the Torah is read, special celebrations occur.

**Bar Mitzvahs:** Many Jewish families from around the world celebrate their sons' bar mitzvahs at the Wall, considering it the most meaningful place to mark this coming-of-age milestone.

### The Notes in the Wall

One of the most famous customs at the Western Wall is placing written prayers (called "kvitlach" in Yiddish) into the cracks between the stones.

People write prayers for healing, success, marriage, children, or any other need, fold the paper, and insert it into the Wall's crevices. Twice a year (before Passover and Rosh Hashanah), these notes are collected and buried on the Mount of Olives with proper respect.

This custom has parallels in other cultures — for instance, Chinese temple-goers writing wishes on red papers or tying prayer cloths — but at the Western Wall, it has become a global phenomenon. People email prayers from around the world to be placed in the Wall on their behalf.

### What Happens Underground: The Western Wall Tunnels

Most visitors only see about 60 feet of the Western Wall at the plaza level. But the Wall extends much further — both below ground and northward along the Temple Mount.

The Western Wall Tunnels (discovered and excavated in recent decades) allow visitors to walk along the hidden sections of the Wall underground. This remarkable archaeological site reveals the full height and extent of the Herodian construction, including stones that are among the largest building blocks ever used in construction.

At one point in the tunnels, visitors can stand at the spot closest to where the Holy of Holies once stood. This small chamber has become an incredibly popular place for intense prayer.

## Theological Significance: What the Wall Represents

### Memory and Hope

The Western Wall embodies two seemingly contradictory ideas that are central to Judaism:

1. **Memory of Destruction:** The Wall is a remnant — a reminder of what was lost. Jewish tradition teaches that we should never be so comfortable in exile that we forget Jerusalem and the Temple. The Wall keeps that memory alive.

2. **Hope for Redemption:** At the same time, the Wall stands as testimony that not everything was destroyed. Its very existence is seen as a Divine promise that the Temple will be rebuilt and the Jewish people redeemed.

This dual nature — mourning and hope — is expressed in Jewish prayers like "Aneinu" and "Nahem" recited on fast days.

### The Gate of Heaven

In Genesis 28, Jacob dreams of a ladder connecting heaven and earth, with angels ascending and descending. When he wakes, he says: "This is none other than the house of God, and this is the gate of heaven" (Genesis 28:17).

Jewish tradition identifies this place with the Temple Mount — the point where heaven and earth meet, where prayers ascend most directly to God. The Western Wall, as the remnant of the Temple complex, is seen as preserving this function: it is still a "gate of heaven" where prayers have special power.

### A Place of Miracles

Throughout history, countless stories have been told of prayers answered at the Western Wall, of miraculous events, and of spiritual transformations that occurred there.

While Judaism generally doesn't promise that prayers will always be answered in the way we hope, there is a special tradition that "prayers from the Western Wall are never turned away empty" (various sources in Midrash and Kabbalah).

## Chinese Cultural Parallels

Several aspects of the Western Wall's significance may resonate with Chinese cultural concepts:

### Sacred Mountains and Pilgrimage Sites

Just as Mount Tai (泰山) holds profound significance in Chinese tradition as a sacred site where emperors performed sacrifices to Heaven and Earth, the Temple Mount (with the Western Wall at its base) is Judaism's most sacred site — the place where heaven and earth meet.

The custom of pilgrimage to the Western Wall parallels Chinese traditions of pilgrimage to sacred mountains and temples, where the journey itself is spiritually meaningful.

### Stones That Hold Memory

The Chinese tradition of scholar's rocks (供石) — appreciating stones for their form, age, and the energy they hold — has a parallel in Jewish reverence for the Western Wall's stones. These aren't just building materials; they are witnesses to history, imbued with the prayers and tears of generations.

### Continuity Across Dynasties

Just as the Great Wall represents Chinese continuity across multiple dynasties and thousands of years, the Western Wall represents Jewish continuity despite exile, persecution, and the destruction of the Temple. It's a physical anchor connecting modern Jews to their ancient origins.

## The Wall in Modern Jewish Life

### A National Symbol

Beyond its religious significance, the Western Wall has become a national symbol of the Jewish people. Israeli soldiers take their oath of service at the Wall. State ceremonies and commemorations often take place there.

When Israeli Prime Minister Menachem Begin signed the peace treaty with Egypt in 1979, he brought Egyptian President Anwar Sadat to visit the Western Wall as part of the historic reconciliation.

### 24-Hour Live Stream

In the digital age, the Western Wall is one of the most watched places on earth. A 24-hour live stream allows Jews (and others) around the world to see the Wall at any moment, to watch prayer services, and to feel connected to Jerusalem even from thousands of miles away.

### A Place of Unity

Despite denominational differences within Judaism (Orthodox, Conservative, Reform), the Western Wall is recognized by all Jewish movements as the holiest accessible site in Judaism. While debates continue about prayer practices at the Wall, its fundamental importance is universal among Jews.

## Visiting: Practical Information

### Location
The Western Wall is located in the Old City of Jerusalem, in the Jewish Quarter. It's accessible through several gates, most commonly through the Dung Gate (Sha'ar Ha'ashpot) or the Jaffa Gate.

### Hours
The Western Wall plaza is open 24 hours a day, 7 days a week. However, security checks are in place at all times.

### What to Bring
- **Head covering** (men) — kippah/yarmulke
- **Modest clothing** — shoulders and knees covered
- **Prayer book** (siddur) — available for free use at the site, or bring your own
- **Small note** — if you wish to place a prayer in the Wall

### What Not to Bring
- **Camera/phone on Shabbat/holidays** — photography is forbidden on these holy days
- **Non-modest clothing** — shorts, tank tops, or revealing clothing
- **Food and drink** — not permitted in the prayer area

### Tours
Guided tours of the Western Wall Tunnels are available (advance booking recommended). These provide fascinating historical and archaeological context to what you see at the plaza level.

## The Wall in Jewish Prayer and Song

The Western Wall appears in numerous Jewish prayers, poems, and songs. Perhaps most famous is Rabbi Yehuda Halevi's 12th-century poem (written while he was in Spain, longing for Jerusalem):

> "My heart is in the East, and I am at the ends of the West.  
> How can I taste what I eat, how can I enjoy it?  
> How can I fulfill my vows and pledges  
> While Zion is in the domain of Edom, and I am in the bonds of Arabia?"

Modern Israeli songs like "Jerusalem of Gold" by Naomi Shemer capture the longing for and joy of accessing the Wall, especially the verse written after 1967: "We have returned to the cisterns, to the market and the square. A shofar calls on the Temple Mount in the Old City."

## Conclusion: A Stone Connection to the Eternal

What is it that draws millions of people each year to touch these ancient stones? Why do hardened soldiers weep when they see the Wall? Why do people send prayers from across the globe to be placed in its cracks?

The Western Wall represents something fundamental in the Jewish soul: the belief that despite exile, suffering, and the passage of millennia, the connection between the Jewish people and God remains unbroken. These stones are a physical reminder that the covenant made at Sinai endures.

For Chinese readers approaching Judaism, the Western Wall offers a powerful entry point into understanding Jewish values: the importance of memory, the refusal to despair despite historical tragedy, the hope for redemption, and the belief in a sacred center that gives meaning to the periphery.

Whether you visit in person or see it through a screen, whether you pray there or simply observe, the Western Wall stands as a testimony to the enduring nature of faith, the power of sacred space, and the human longing for connection with the Divine.

As the Talmud teaches: "Ten measures of beauty were given to the world; nine were taken by Jerusalem" (Kiddushin 49b). The Western Wall, in all its weathered majesty, embodies that beauty — not merely physical, but spiritual and eternal.

## Key Takeaways

- The Western Wall is the last visible remnant of the Second Temple complex, destroyed in 70 CE, and is the holiest accessible site in Judaism
- Jews pray at the Wall because it's the closest point to where the Holy of Holies once stood, where God's presence was most manifest
- The Wall represents both mourning (for the destroyed Temple) and hope (for future redemption)
- Customs include placing written prayers in the Wall's cracks, walking backward when leaving, and separate prayer sections for men and women
- The Western Wall Tunnels reveal the full extent of the ancient retaining wall underground
- Throughout 2,000 years of exile, the Wall remained a focal point of Jewish longing and prayer

- The Wall serves as both a religious site and a national symbol of Jewish continuity and resilience
