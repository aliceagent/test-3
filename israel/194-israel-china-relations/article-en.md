# Israel-China Relations: A Jewish Perspective

The connection between the Jewish people and China spans centuries, weaving together threads of refuge, cultural exchange, economic partnership, and diplomatic complexity. For those exploring Judaism from a Chinese background, understanding this relationship offers a unique window into how Jewish values of *chesed* (loving-kindness), historical memory, and pragmatic survival have shaped Israel's engagement with the world's most populous nation.

## Ancient Echoes: The Kaifeng Jews

Long before modern diplomacy, Jewish traders traveled the Silk Road, establishing communities across Asia. The most remarkable of these was in Kaifeng, the capital of China's Northern Song Dynasty. Arriving as early as the 10th century, these Jewish merchants—likely from Persia and India—brought their Torah scrolls, dietary laws, and Sabbath observance to the heart of China.

The Kaifeng Jewish community thrived for centuries, building a magnificent synagogue and maintaining Jewish practice even as they adopted Chinese names, language, and customs. They called themselves *Tiao jin jiao* (挑筋教), "the religion that removes the sinew," referring to the kosher practice of removing the sciatic nerve from meat—a law stemming from Jacob's wrestling with the angel (Genesis 32:32).

What's remarkable is how these Jews balanced particularism with integration. They served as government officials, scholars, and military officers while maintaining their distinct religious identity. Chinese emperors recognized them as one of several religious communities contributing to the empire's diversity. By the 19th century, however, without access to Jewish learning from the outside world, the community gradually assimilated. Today, descendants of Kaifeng Jews are rediscovering their heritage, some even making *aliyah* (immigration) to Israel—a powerful testament to the endurance of Jewish identity.

**Lesson**: The Kaifeng experience teaches that Jewish survival requires both rootedness in tradition and creative adaptation to local circumstances. Like bamboo that bends in the wind but doesn't break, the Jewish people have always sought this balance.

## Sanctuary in Shanghai: The WWII Refugee Story

When the world closed its doors to Jews fleeing Nazi persecution in the 1930s, Shanghai became an unexpected haven. Unlike most countries that imposed strict immigration quotas, Shanghai—then under various foreign concessions—required no visa for entry. Between 1938 and 1941, approximately 20,000 European Jews found refuge in this bustling port city.

The refugees, mostly from Germany and Austria, arrived with little more than their lives. They settled primarily in the Hongkou district, creating a "Little Vienna" with cafés, newspapers, schools, and synagogues. Despite poverty, overcrowding, and the hardships of war, the Jewish community established institutions that maintained their cultural and religious life.

What made this period extraordinary was the relationship between Jewish refugees and local Chinese residents. Facing their own suffering under Japanese occupation, Chinese neighbors showed remarkable compassion. They shared food during shortages, protected Jews during periods of danger, and developed genuine friendships across profound cultural differences. This mutual aid reflected the Confucian value of *rén* (仁, benevolence)—strikingly similar to the Jewish concept of *rachamim* (mercy) and *chesed*.

**Cultural Parallel**: Just as Chinese culture emphasizes *bào* (报)—reciprocating kindness—Jewish tradition teaches *hakarat hatov* (הכרת הטוב), recognizing and remembering goodness shown to you. Israeli leaders and ordinary Jews have never forgotten Shanghai's sanctuary.

After the war, most Shanghai Jews emigrated to Israel, the United States, and elsewhere. But they carried with them deep gratitude and enduring connections to China. The Shanghai Jewish Refugees Museum stands today as a memorial to this chapter, visited by Israeli officials and tourists as a pilgrimage site of remembrance.

## Modern Diplomatic Dance: Recognition and Relations

Israel and China established formal diplomatic relations on January 24, 1992—relatively late compared to other nations. China's delay reflected its complex position in the Cold War, relationships with Arab states, and internal political considerations.

Since normalization, the relationship has grown dramatically. China is now one of Israel's largest trading partners in Asia, with bilateral trade exceeding $18 billion annually. Israeli innovation in agriculture, water technology, cybersecurity, and medical devices has found eager Chinese partners. Chinese investment has flowed into Israeli startups, infrastructure projects, and real estate.

For Israel, China represents:
- **Economic opportunity**: A massive market hungry for Israeli technology
- **Diplomatic balance**: Diversifying beyond dependence on Western allies
- **Strategic value**: A global power whose friendship matters

For China, Israel offers:
- **Cutting-edge technology**: Particularly in agriculture (drip irrigation, desert farming), cybersecurity, and medical innovation
- **Gateway to innovation**: Access to Israeli R&D and startup culture
- **Middle East presence**: Economic ties that don't require choosing sides in regional conflicts

Yet tensions simmer beneath the surface. The United States, Israel's closest ally, views Chinese investment in Israeli infrastructure and technology with concern—particularly regarding security implications. Israel has had to carefully balance Chinese economic engagement with American security interests, sometimes canceling deals under U.S. pressure.

**Torah Perspective**: The book of Proverbs teaches, "Many are the plans in a person's heart, but it is God's purpose that prevails" (Proverbs 19:21). Israel's foreign policy reflects this wisdom—making strategic plans while recognizing that ultimate outcomes rest with the divine.

## Cultural Bridges: Shared Values Across Civilizations

Despite vast differences, Jewish and Chinese civilizations share surprising commonalities:

**Emphasis on Education**: Both cultures place extraordinary value on learning. The Chinese tradition of *xiùcái* (秀才, cultivated talent through study) mirrors the Jewish ideal of the *talmid chacham* (wise student). Both peoples have historically honored scholars above warriors or merchants.

**Family Centrality**: Confucian *xiào* (孝, filial piety) resonates deeply with the Fifth Commandment: "Honor your father and mother" (Exodus 20:12). Both traditions view the family as the foundation of society and emphasize intergenerational obligation.

**Historical Memory**: Chinese culture's long historical consciousness—maintaining records spanning millennia—parallels the Jewish commitment to remembering our past. "Remember the days of old; consider the generations long past" (Deuteronomy 32:7). Both peoples see themselves as part of ancient, continuous civilizations.

**Practical Wisdom**: Jewish *chochmah* (wisdom) literature, especially Proverbs and Ecclesiastes, shares thematic similarities with Confucian and Daoist texts—emphasizing proper conduct, balance, timing, and the limits of human understanding.

**Resilience Through Adversity**: Both peoples have survived catastrophic periods—the Chinese through dynastic collapses, invasions, and modern upheavals; the Jews through exile, persecution, and the Holocaust. Both developed survival strategies balancing adaptation with core identity preservation.

## Technology Partnerships: Innovation Meets Scale

One of the most vibrant aspects of Israel-China relations is technological collaboration. Israel's startup ecosystem—producing the most startups per capita globally—finds in China the manufacturing capacity, market size, and investment capital to scale innovations.

Examples include:
- **Agriculture**: Israeli drip irrigation and greenhouse technology helping China address water scarcity and food security
- **Water management**: Desalination and wastewater treatment technologies addressing China's water crisis
- **Healthcare**: Israeli medical devices and digital health platforms expanding in Chinese markets
- **Cybersecurity**: Israeli expertise protecting Chinese digital infrastructure (though security concerns have limited this sector)

From a Jewish perspective, these partnerships reflect the value of *tikkun olam*—repairing the world. When Israeli agricultural technology helps feed millions, when water purification systems provide clean drinking water, the Jewish imperative to "choose life" (Deuteronomy 30:19) finds practical expression.

**Contemporary Relevance**: Young Chinese professionals studying in Israel, attending Hebrew University or Technion, return home as bridges between cultures. Israeli entrepreneurs learning Mandarin and building relationships in Beijing and Shanghai create networks that transcend politics.

## Challenges and Complexities

The relationship faces ongoing challenges:

**Geopolitical Pressure**: U.S. concerns about Chinese access to Israeli military technology and critical infrastructure have forced Israel to reject or cancel several major Chinese investment deals, including the privatization of Israel's largest port and telecommunication upgrades.

**Human Rights Questions**: Israel, built on the memory of persecution and genocide, faces difficult questions about engaging economically with a government accused of human rights violations, particularly regarding Uyghur Muslims in Xinjiang. Jewish ethical tradition demands we not be "silent in the face of bloodshed" (Leviticus 19:16), creating moral dilemmas for policymakers.

**The Palestinian Question**: China officially supports Palestinian statehood and criticizes Israeli settlement policies, though it maintains pragmatic economic ties. This creates diplomatic awkwardness that both sides manage through careful compartmentalization.

**Technology Theft Concerns**: Like many nations, Israel worries about intellectual property protection and technology transfer, requiring vigilance in partnerships.

## Looking Forward: Opportunity and Responsibility

As Israel-China relations continue evolving, several principles from Jewish thought offer guidance:

**Balance** (*emtza*): Maimonides taught the "golden mean"—avoiding extremes. Israel must balance economic opportunity with security concerns, Chinese partnership with Western alliance, short-term gain with long-term strategic thinking.

**Discernment** (*binah*): The wisdom to distinguish between what benefits Israel's core interests and what merely appears attractive. Not every deal should be made, not every investment accepted.

**Gratitude** (*hakarat hatov*): Never forgetting Shanghai's refuge or the kindness shown by ordinary Chinese to desperate Jews. This historical debt influences Israeli attitudes, even when contemporary interests conflict.

**Hope** (*tikvah*): The Jewish people have always believed that peaceful coexistence across differences is possible. Israel's engagement with China reflects this optimism—that mutual benefit can bridge vast cultural, political, and geographic gaps.

## Practical Takeaways

For those exploring Judaism from a Chinese background:

1. **Appreciate Historical Connections**: The Kaifeng Jews and Shanghai refugees show that Chinese-Jewish interaction predates modern politics, rooted in deeper human values of hospitality and survival.

2. **Recognize Shared Values**: Don't be surprised by resonances between Confucian ethics and Jewish teaching—both ancient civilizations developed profound wisdom about human flourishing.

3. **Understand Complexity**: Israel's foreign policy, like all countries', involves difficult trade-offs between values and interests, idealism and pragmatism. Jewish tradition has always acknowledged this complexity.

4. **See Personal Bridges**: Your own journey learning about Judaism creates a living connection between these civilizations. You carry forward the Kaifeng legacy in a new form.

5. **Engage Critically**: Both civilizations value questioning and thoughtful analysis. Bringing that critical lens to understanding Israel-China relations enriches both traditions.

## Conclusion

The relationship between Israel and China cannot be reduced to simple narratives. It encompasses millennium-old encounters, wartime rescue, modern economic partnership, technological collaboration, diplomatic complexity, and ongoing challenges. Through it all runs a thread of mutual respect between two ancient peoples who have survived against tremendous odds.

As you continue your Jewish journey, remember that Judaism has always engaged with the wider world—sometimes through conflict, often through creative exchange. The Israel-China relationship exemplifies this dynamic: two civilizations, deeply rooted in their own traditions, finding ways to learn from each other, benefit together, and navigate differences.

The Talmud teaches that "the seal of the Holy One, blessed be He, is truth" (*Shabbat 55a*). Truth in international relations means acknowledging both the profound possibilities and real challenges, maintaining gratitude for past kindness while clear-eyed about present complexity. This balanced vision—hopeful yet realistic, grateful yet discerning—captures the Jewish perspective on Israel's relationship with the Middle Kingdom.
