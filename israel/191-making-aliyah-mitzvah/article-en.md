# Making Aliyah: The Mitzvah of Living in Israel

---

The word *aliyah* (עלייה) literally means "going up" or "ascending." In Jewish life, it has several meanings: being called up to the Torah in synagogue, immigrating to Israel, or even spiritual elevation. But when Jews speak about "making aliyah," they're referring to something profound: returning home to the Land of Israel.

For thousands of years, Jews in exile have prayed three times daily facing Jerusalem, concluded their Passover Seders with the words "Next year in Jerusalem," and broken a glass at weddings to remember the destruction of the Temple. This deep connection to the Land of Israel isn't merely sentimental—it's rooted in Torah law, Jewish history, and the very identity of the Jewish people.

## The Torah's Promise of the Land

The connection between the Jewish people and the Land of Israel begins with God's first words to Abraham: "Go forth from your land, from your birthplace, and from your father's house, to the land that I will show you" (Genesis 12:1). This wasn't just a geographical relocation—it was the foundational moment of Jewish history.

God later specifies this promise: "To your descendants I have given this land, from the river of Egypt to the great river, the Euphrates River" (Genesis 15:18). This covenant is reaffirmed to Isaac and Jacob, establishing an eternal connection between the Jewish people and their ancestral homeland.

The Torah describes the Land of Israel in unique terms—as a land that God "continuously watches over; the eyes of the Lord your God are upon it, from the beginning of the year until the end of the year" (Deuteronomy 11:12). This isn't ordinary real estate; it's a land with special spiritual properties, uniquely suited for the Jewish people to fulfill their divine purpose.

## Is Living in Israel a Commandment?

Among Torah scholars, there's an important discussion about whether living in Israel is a biblical commandment (*mitzvah d'oraita*) or a rabbinic ideal. The medieval sage Nachmanides (Ramban) argues forcefully that dwelling in the Land of Israel is indeed a positive commandment from the Torah, incumbent upon every generation.

In his commentary on Maimonides' *Book of Commandments*, Nachmanides writes: "We were commanded to take possession of the land which the Almighty gave to our forefathers, to Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob; and not to abandon it to other nations, or to leave it desolate."

Maimonides (Rambam), while not listing it among the 613 commandments, writes extensively about the special laws and spiritual advantages of living in Israel. He states: "It is forbidden to leave the Land of Israel for the Diaspora permanently, except to study Torah, to marry, or to save [oneself] from non-Jews" (*Mishneh Torah*, Laws of Kings 5:9).

Even if one debates the technical classification, the practical importance is clear: living in Israel has been a central aspiration of Jewish life for millennia, and the Talmud states that "One who lives in the Land of Israel is considered as one who has a God, and one who lives outside the Land is considered as one who has no God" (Ketubot 110b)—strong words emphasizing the spiritual significance of the land.

## Historical Context: From Ancient Times to Modern Return

The Jewish connection to Israel isn't theoretical—it's been lived out across thirty-three centuries. King David established Jerusalem as the capital around 1000 BCE. His son Solomon built the First Temple, which stood for over 400 years until the Babylonian conquest in 586 BCE.

Even in exile in Babylon, Jews never forgot Jerusalem. The prophet Jeremiah wrote letters urging the exiles to "Seek the welfare of the city to which I have exiled you" (Jeremiah 29:7), yet also promised: "For I know the plans I have for you...plans to give you hope and a future...and I will bring you back to the place from which I exiled you" (Jeremiah 29:11-14).

True to this prophecy, many Jews returned after seventy years, rebuilding the Temple under Ezra and Nehemiah. The Second Temple period lasted five centuries, producing the Talmudic sages and witnessing the ministries of countless prophets and teachers. Even after the Roman destruction in 70 CE and the subsequent exile, a continuous Jewish presence remained in the land.

Throughout the medieval period, individual Jews and small groups made the difficult journey to Israel. The Spanish expulsion of 1492 brought waves of refugees to Safed, which became a center of Kabbalah. The 1700s saw Chassidic rebbes and their followers immigrating. In the 1800s, religious Zionist movements began, and in 1882, the First Aliyah launched modern Jewish agricultural settlement.

The establishment of the State of Israel in 1948 marked an unprecedented moment: for the first time in nearly two thousand years, Jews could freely return to their homeland. Since then, over three million Jews have made aliyah from over 120 countries.

## The Spiritual Dimensions of Aliyah

Beyond geography and politics, aliyah represents spiritual ascent. The Talmud teaches that "the air of the Land of Israel makes one wise" (Bava Batra 158b). This isn't about oxygen content—it's about the unique spiritual environment that facilitates Torah study and Jewish living.

Many commandments can only be fulfilled in Israel: agricultural laws like *terumah* (priestly gifts), *ma'aser* (tithes), and *shmitah* (sabbatical year); the offering of sacrifices (when the Temple stands); and the obligation to make pilgrimage three times yearly to Jerusalem.

The Kabbalistic tradition teaches that Israel is uniquely aligned with higher spiritual worlds. The holy Zohar describes it as the land where divine energy flows most directly into the physical world. For the spiritually attuned, living in Israel provides opportunities for growth unavailable elsewhere.

Moreover, there's the concept of *eretz kodesh*—holy land. Just as Shabbat is holy time, carved out from ordinary time, Israel is holy space, carved out from ordinary geography. Living there means dwelling in sanctity, where everyday activities take on added spiritual significance.

## Practical Realities: The Challenges of Aliyah

Making aliyah isn't a fairy tale, and Jewish tradition has never pretended otherwise. The Talmud frankly discusses the hardships: "It is difficult to live in the Land of Israel, for a livelihood there is as difficult as splitting the Red Sea" (Sanhedrin 94a).

Modern immigrants face real challenges: learning Hebrew, finding employment, navigating Israeli bureaucracy, adjusting to a different culture, being far from family, and sometimes dealing with security concerns. For Chinese Jews or converts from China, there's the additional dimension of being visibly different in a society that's still learning to embrace its diversity.

Financial realities matter. Israeli salaries are often lower than Western countries, while cost of living—especially housing in major cities—can be high. Career paths may not transfer smoothly. Credentials and degrees sometimes require validation or supplementary study.

Cultural adjustment goes beyond language. Israeli society is famously direct—what feels like rudeness to someone from a culture that values indirectness is simply normal Israeli communication. The bureaucracy operates differently. Social norms around personal space, time, and hierarchy vary significantly.

Yet the Talmud's statement about difficulty isn't meant to discourage—it's meant to prepare. The Red Sea ultimately split, and the Jewish people crossed to freedom. Similarly, the challenges of aliyah, while real, lead to profound reward.

## Why People Make Aliyah Today

Despite challenges, hundreds of thousands have chosen aliyah in recent decades. Their reasons vary but often include:

**Religious fulfillment**: The desire to live where Jewish history unfolded, where the prophets walked, where the Temple stood, and where the commandments find their fullest expression.

**Jewish continuity**: Raising children in a country where being Jewish is the norm, not the exception; where Jewish holidays are national holidays; where Hebrew is the spoken language.

**Community**: Being part of a society that shares fundamental values and worldview, where neighbors understand your religious practice, where kosher food is everywhere, and where Shabbat transforms the entire country.

**Safety and identity**: After centuries of exile and persecution, having a homeland where Jews determine their own destiny provides profound security—not just physical, but psychological and spiritual.

**Historical participation**: Being part of the greatest ingathering of Jewish exiles in history, witnessing prophecies fulfilled in real time, contributing to the rebuilding of the Jewish state.

**Personal growth**: The challenge itself becomes a catalyst for development. Learning a new language, building a career from scratch, integrating into a new culture—these difficulties forge character and deepen faith.

## A Chinese Perspective: The Concept of Homeland

Chinese culture has its own deep connection to ancestral land. The concept of *jiaxiang* (家乡), one's hometown or ancestral village, carries profound emotional weight. Chinese people traditionally return home for Spring Festival regardless of distance or difficulty, maintaining connection to their roots.

The Jewish attachment to Israel parallels this in some ways but goes deeper. *Jiaxiang* is personal and familial; Israel is national and eternal. It's as if all Chinese people shared one ancestral village, promised to them by heaven itself, where their ancestors lived for a thousand years before being scattered—yet never stopped considering it home.

The Chinese also understand long historical memory. Just as China traces continuous civilization back millennia, maintaining cultural identity through dynasties and upheavals, Jews maintained their identity through exile, holding fast to the promise of return.

There's also a parallel to the educated classes who left rural areas for cities or went abroad, yet maintain spiritual connection to their origins. Many successful Chinese professionals experience tension between modern life and ancestral roots. Jews experienced this for two thousand years—succeeding in diaspora societies while never fully belonging, always remembering Jerusalem.

The act of making aliyah resembles the traditional Chinese concept of *luoye guigen* (落叶归根)—fallen leaves return to their roots. It's the natural conclusion of a long journey, the fulfillment of an ancient promise to both oneself and one's ancestors.

## First Steps: How to Begin

If you're feeling drawn to make aliyah, here are practical first steps:

**Visit first**: Come as a tourist, explore different cities and regions, visit on holidays to experience Israeli Jewish life, and if possible, spend extended time (a few months) to get beyond tourist impressions.

**Learn Hebrew**: Begin studying now. Even basic Hebrew dramatically eases transition. Many resources exist online, and ulpanim (intensive Hebrew schools) await new immigrants in Israel.

**Research communities**: Israel offers incredible diversity—from Jerusalem's religious neighborhoods to Tel Aviv's secular cosmopolitanism, from agricultural kibbutzim to development towns in the Negev. Find the environment that suits you.

**Understand the process**: The Jewish Agency for Israel (JAFI) and Nefesh B'Nefesh (for North American and UK immigrants) provide comprehensive support—from initial counseling through flights, absorption, employment assistance, and community integration.

**Consider pilot programs**: Many organizations offer trial programs—spending a year in yeshiva or seminary, participating in work programs, or joining young adult communities that ease transition.

**Consult with those who've done it**: Connect with olim (immigrants) from your home country. They'll offer honest insights about challenges and joys, practical advice about jobs and neighborhoods, and emotional support for the journey.

**Prepare financially**: Save money for the initial transition period. While Israel offers absorption benefits, having a financial cushion reduces stress and allows time to find appropriate employment.

**Involve family**: If you have family in the diaspora, discuss your plans. While not everyone will understand, maintaining connection helps. If you're making aliyah with a spouse and children, ensure you're aligned in expectations and commitments.

## The Spiritual Journey

Beyond practicalities, making aliyah is a spiritual journey. The Talmud teaches that "the mere walking in the Land of Israel atones for sin" (Ketubot 111a). Living there transforms ordinary life into continuous connection with Jewish history and destiny.

When you walk streets where prophets walked, when you touch stones that witnessed David and Solomon, when you pray at the Western Wall—the last remnant of the Temple—you're not just visiting tourist sites. You're participating in an unbroken chain reaching back to Abraham.

Every Shabbat in Israel connects to the first Shabbat observed in the land by Joshua and the Israelites. Every Passover echoes the Passovers of the Temple period. Every harvest season reflects the ancient agricultural rhythms that structure the Jewish calendar.

Moreover, there's the collective dimension. You're not just living your individual life—you're contributing to the Jewish people's return to their homeland, rebuilding what was destroyed, fulfilling what was promised, and participating in potential redemption.

## Conclusion: Your Personal Exodus

The Exodus from Egypt is our foundational story—from slavery to freedom, from degradation to dignity, from exile to homeland. Every Passover, we retell it, remembering that "we were slaves to Pharaoh in Egypt, and God took us out from there."

Making aliyah is a personal exodus. You leave behind what's familiar, journey through uncertainty, and arrive at the land of promise. Like the Israelites who complained in the desert, you'll face difficulties. Like them, you'll discover that the challenges forge you into something greater.

The medieval sage Yehuda Halevi, one of Spain's greatest poets, spent his life yearning for Jerusalem. Late in life, he finally made the journey. Legend says that as he approached Jerusalem's gates, overwhelmed with emotion, he was trampled by a horse and died—but died fulfilled, having reached his destination.

Most who make aliyah aren't that dramatic. They land at Ben Gurion Airport, collect their luggage, and begin the prosaic work of building a new life: finding apartments, enrolling children in schools, learning Hebrew, making friends. Yet within that ordinariness lies something extraordinary.

You're going home. You're climbing the ladder Jacob dreamed of, connecting earth and heaven. You're inscribing yourself into the story that began with Abraham and continues until the end of days.

The word *aliyah*—ascending—captures it perfectly. You're not just moving countries. You're rising to meet your destiny, your heritage, your purpose. You're going up.
