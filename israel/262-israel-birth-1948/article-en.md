## Introduction

On May 14, 1948, something unprecedented happened: the Jewish people, after nearly two thousand years of exile, declared the establishment of a sovereign state in their ancient homeland. This moment — which occurred during what corresponds to the early years of the People's Republic of China (中华人民共和国成立前夕) — marked one of the most remarkable national restorations in human history. For centuries, Jews around the world had ended their Passover seders with the words "Next year in Jerusalem," never truly knowing if that prayer would be answered. In 1948, it finally was. But the birth of Israel was neither simple nor peaceful. It was the culmination of decades of Zionist activism, international diplomacy, Holocaust trauma, and Middle Eastern conflict. Understanding how Israel came to be requires understanding not just the events of 1948, but the long historical journey that led to that momentous day.

## The Historical Background: Exile and Return

The Jewish connection to the Land of Israel stretches back nearly four thousand years — to the time of China's Shang Dynasty (商朝, ~1600-1046 BCE) — when Abraham first arrived in Canaan. The Jewish kingdoms of Israel and Judah flourished in this land during periods corresponding to China's Zhou and Spring & Autumn periods (周朝和春秋时代), before the Romans destroyed the Second Temple in 70 CE and expelled most Jews from their homeland.

For the next eighteen centuries, Jews lived scattered across the globe — in Europe, North Africa, the Middle East, and beyond. Yet they never forgot Jerusalem. Three times daily, Jews prayed facing toward the Temple Mount. At every wedding, a glass was broken to remember the Temple's destruction. The land was never abandoned in Jewish consciousness, even when physical return seemed impossible.

### The Birth of Modern Zionism

The late 19th century — during China's late Qing Dynasty (晚清时期, 1840s-1912) — saw a dramatic shift. Theodor Herzl, an Austrian Jewish journalist, witnessed the Dreyfus Affair in France, where a Jewish officer was falsely accused of treason amid violent antisemitism. Herzl concluded that Jews would never be safe as a minority in other nations. In 1896, he published "Der Judenstaat" (The Jewish State), arguing for the establishment of a Jewish homeland.

This was not merely a political movement; it was deeply rooted in Jewish religious longing. While Herzl and other early Zionist leaders were secular, they tapped into a spiritual yearning that had sustained Jewish identity for millennia. Religious Jews, particularly followers of Rabbi Avraham Yitzchak Kook, saw Zionism as the beginning of messianic redemption.

Small groups of Jewish pioneers began immigrating to Ottoman-controlled Palestine in waves (called "aliyot" — עליות, meaning "ascents"). They drained swamps, planted forests, built kibbutzim (collective farms), and revived Hebrew as a spoken language. By the early 20th century, a small but determined Jewish community was re-establishing roots in the ancient homeland.

## The British Mandate and Rising Tensions

During World War I (1914-1918) — while China experienced the early Republican period (民国初期) — Britain sought Jewish support for the war effort. In 1917, British Foreign Secretary Arthur Balfour issued the famous Balfour Declaration, stating that Britain viewed "with favour the establishment in Palestine of a national home for the Jewish people."

After the Ottoman Empire's defeat, Britain received a League of Nations mandate to govern Palestine. The British were now caught between their promise to the Jews and their relationships with the Arab population, who also claimed the land. Arab riots erupted in 1920, 1921, and 1929, attacking Jewish communities. Tensions escalated throughout the 1930s.

### The Holocaust and Its Impact

The 1930s and 1940s brought unimaginable catastrophe. Nazi Germany's systematic murder of six million Jews during the Holocaust — occurring during China's War of Resistance Against Japan and the subsequent Civil War (抗日战争和内战时期, 1937-1949) — transformed the question of Jewish statehood from ideological to existential.

Jewish refugees desperately sought entry to Palestine, but Britain, fearing Arab unrest, severely restricted immigration through the 1939 White Paper. Ships carrying Holocaust survivors were turned away. The Jewish community in Palestine, known as the Yishuv (ישוב), organized underground operations to bring refugees in despite British blockades. The SS Exodus incident in 1947, where a ship carrying 4,500 Holocaust survivors was forcibly returned to Europe, shocked world opinion and highlighted the Jewish people's desperate need for a homeland.

## The United Nations Partition Plan

By 1947, Britain, exhausted from World War II and unable to manage the escalating violence in Palestine, decided to hand the problem to the newly formed United Nations. The UN Special Committee on Palestine (UNSCOP) investigated and recommended partitioning the land into separate Jewish and Arab states, with Jerusalem under international administration.

On November 29, 1947, the UN General Assembly voted 33 to 13 (with 10 abstentions) to adopt Resolution 181, approving the partition plan. The Jewish community accepted the plan, despite it offering much less territory than they hoped for, and despite the proposed state having indefensible borders. For them, a small state was infinitely better than no state at all.

The Arab leadership rejected the partition entirely, refusing to accept any Jewish state in what they considered Arab land. Violence erupted immediately. Arab irregular forces attacked Jewish communities, roads, and convoys. The period from November 1947 to May 1948 saw brutal fighting, with both sides committing atrocities. Jewish forces, initially on the defensive, gradually gained the upper hand as they received weapons shipments from Czechoslovakia and organized under the Haganah (הגנה), the main Jewish paramilitary organization.

## May 14, 1948: Declaration of Independence

As the British Mandate was set to expire at midnight on May 14-15, 1948, David Ben-Gurion, head of the Jewish Agency, gathered the leadership in Tel Aviv's small art museum. At 4:00 PM on Friday afternoon — just hours before the Sabbath would begin — Ben-Gurion stood beneath a portrait of Theodor Herzl and read the Declaration of Independence:

> "The Land of Israel was the birthplace of the Jewish people. Here their spiritual, religious and political identity was shaped... After being forcibly exiled from their land, the people kept faith with it throughout their Dispersion and never ceased to pray and hope for their return to it and for the restoration in it of their political freedom... Accordingly we, members of the People's Council... hereby declare the establishment of a Jewish state in Eretz-Israel, to be known as the State of Israel."

The document was signed by 37 leaders representing different political movements and communities. Conspicuously absent were representatives of Religious Zionism and Agudat Yisrael (ultra-Orthodox groups), who had their own complex relationships with the nascent state, though Rabbi Yehuda Leib Maimon of the Religious Zionist movement did sign.

The room erupted in spontaneous singing of "Hatikvah" (התקווה, "The Hope"), which would become Israel's national anthem. Outside, crowds danced in the streets of Tel Aviv. In synagogues, special prayers of thanksgiving were recited. For the first time in nearly two thousand years, there was a Jewish sovereign state.

## Immediate War and Survival

The celebration was brief. At midnight, as the British withdrew, five Arab armies — Egypt, Transjordan, Syria, Lebanon, and Iraq — invaded the newly declared state. They were joined by the Arab Liberation Army and local Palestinian Arab militias. The Arab leaders openly declared their intention to destroy Israel and drive the Jews into the sea.

Israel's situation was desperate. The country had no formal army, limited weapons, and a population of only 650,000 (compared to surrounding Arab populations of over 40 million). Holocaust survivors, many arriving straight from displaced persons camps, were immediately pressed into military service. Women and men fought side by side. Weapons were smuggled in through creative means — hidden in crates labeled "agricultural equipment," smuggled through sympathetic European ports, even manufactured in secret underground factories within Israel.

The War of Independence (מלחמת העצמאות) lasted until 1949, with several ceasefires and armistice agreements. Approximately 6,000 Israelis died — about 1% of the entire Jewish population. But Israel survived. By war's end, Israel controlled more territory than the UN partition plan had allotted, though Jerusalem remained divided, with Jordan controlling the Old City and its holy sites.

## The Cost and the Refugees

The war created a massive refugee crisis on both sides. Approximately 700,000 Palestinian Arabs fled or were expelled from their homes during the conflict, creating a refugee population whose descendants still live in camps today. This catastrophe, known to Palestinians as the Nakba (النكبة, "the catastrophe"), remains the core grievance of the Israeli-Palestinian conflict.

Simultaneously, between 1948 and 1952, approximately 850,000 Jews were forced to flee or expelled from Arab countries where they had lived for centuries — including Iraq, Yemen, Egypt, Syria, and Morocco. Most of these Jewish refugees were absorbed into Israel, doubling the country's population within a few years. Unlike the Palestinian refugees, they were not kept in camps but integrated (though not without difficulty) into Israeli society.

## Cultural Connections: National Restoration in History

The establishment of Israel bears interesting parallels to Chinese historical experiences. Just as China experienced periods of national humiliation and division (如晚清的屈辱和民国时期的分裂) followed by reunification and the restoration of national sovereignty, so too did the Jewish people endure centuries of powerlessness before restoring national independence.

The concept of a people maintaining cultural and national identity despite exile and dispersion resonates with overseas Chinese communities (海外华人) who preserved Chinese culture across generations while living far from the homeland. The Jewish emphasis on education, family, and cultural transmission during exile mirrors how Chinese diaspora communities maintained their identity.

Both Israel and modern China faced the challenge of nation-building in the mid-20th century — creating modern states from ancient civilizations, balancing traditional culture with modernization, and navigating complex international relationships while establishing legitimacy and security.

## Religious Significance and Secular-Religious Tensions

From a Jewish religious perspective, the establishment of Israel was profoundly significant. Religious Zionists saw it as "the beginning of the sprouting of our redemption" (ראשית צמיחת גאולתנו), a phrase added to prayers in religious Zionist communities. The ability to visit the Western Wall, pray at ancient sites, and live by the Jewish calendar in a Jewish state fulfilled biblical prophecies about the ingathering of exiles.

However, tensions emerged between secular and religious visions for the state. Ben-Gurion and many founding leaders were secular socialists who saw Judaism primarily as a national identity rather than a religious obligation. Orthodox rabbis disagreed fundamentally on whether political action could "force God's hand" in bringing the Messiah, or whether such efforts were heretical presumption.

These tensions persist today in debates over Shabbat observance, military service for ultra-Orthodox students, marriage laws, and Israel's religious character. The "status quo" agreement from 1947 created a delicate balance: Israel would have no official religion, but would maintain public Shabbat observance, rabbinical control over personal status issues, and kosher food in public institutions.

## Israel's Founding Documents and Democratic Character

The Declaration of Independence established Israel as a Jewish and democratic state, promising "complete equality of social and political rights to all its inhabitants irrespective of religion, race or sex." This dual character — both Jewish and democratic — has been a source of strength and tension throughout Israel's history.

The declaration called on Arab inhabitants "to preserve peace and participate in the upbuilding of the State on the basis of full and equal citizenship." It appealed to the United Nations for admission and declared Israel's commitment to the UN Charter. These founding principles, despite the immediate reality of war, reflected values that would shape Israeli society.

Israel adopted many democratic institutions from British parliamentary tradition, including a multi-party system, independent judiciary, and free press. Yet it struggled (and continues to struggle) with balancing security needs with civil liberties, particularly during times of conflict.

## Practical Application

Understanding Israel's founding teaches several enduring lessons:

**The power of hope sustained over generations**: For two thousand years, Jews never abandoned the dream of return. This persistence, seemingly irrational from a pragmatic standpoint, ultimately led to national restoration. What dreams or values do we maintain even when they seem impossible?

**The complexity of historical justice**: The establishment of Israel was both the fulfillment of Jewish self-determination and the cause of Palestinian displacement. Historical events rarely have clear moral simplicity. Understanding requires holding multiple truths simultaneously — celebrating one people's liberation while acknowledging another's loss.

**The relationship between trauma and action**: The Holocaust did not cause Zionism (which predated it by decades), but it transformed it from a movement to an imperative. How do communities respond to catastrophic suffering? Sometimes through creative rebuilding, sometimes through cycles of conflict. The challenge is channeling trauma into constructive rather than destructive responses.

**The fragility and resilience of new beginnings**: Israel in 1948 was impossibly vulnerable — a tiny state surrounded by enemies, with no natural resources, little military capacity, and a population of traumatized refugees. Yet it survived. New ventures often look impossible until they succeed. Resilience matters more than initial conditions.

## Key Takeaways

- **Israel's establishment in 1948** fulfilled a two-thousand-year Jewish dream but was born amid war and displacement that created ongoing conflict.

- **The UN Partition Plan** offered a compromise that the Jewish leadership accepted and the Arab leadership rejected, leading to immediate violence.

- **David Ben-Gurion's Declaration of Independence** on May 14, 1948 proclaimed a Jewish and democratic state just hours before five Arab armies invaded.

- **Two refugee populations** emerged from the 1948 war — Palestinian Arabs displaced from Israel and Jewish refugees expelled from Arab countries.

- **Religious and secular Jews** viewed Israel's meaning differently — as messianic redemption or national liberation — creating tensions that persist today.

- **Israel's founding combined** ancient heritage with modern democratic institutions, Jewish identity with universal human rights, creating a unique and sometimes contradictory national character.
