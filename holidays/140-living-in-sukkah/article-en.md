# Living in the Sukkah: A Week of Radical Trust

*The experience of eating, sleeping, and living in a temporary hut — practical building tips and the spiritual lesson of impermanence*

## The Fragile Shelter

There's something profoundly unsettling about moving your life into a hut. For seven days during Sukkot, Jewish law invites us to eat our meals, and ideally sleep, in a sukkah — a temporary structure with walls that might wobble and a roof that must let in rain and starlight.

The sukkah forces an uncomfortable question: What does it mean to dwell somewhere that offers no real protection?

## Building Your Temporary Palace

**The Basics That Matter:**
- **Walls:** Three minimum, any material. Fabric, wood, even books stacked high enough.
- **Roof (S'chach):** Natural materials only — bamboo, palm fronds, pine boughs. Nothing processed.
- **Height:** Between 7 feet and 35 feet (any higher and it's not a sukkah).
- **Sky Rule:** You must see stars through the covering, but it should provide more shade than sun.

**Pro Tips for Comfort:**
- Hang battery-powered lights for evening meals (no permanent electrical)
- Waterproof cushions save soggy meals during unexpected showers
- A small side table keeps essentials dry
- Wind chimes add gentle sounds but check your s'chach doesn't blow away

The strangest part? You're building something designed to be imperfect. A shelter that deliberately can't fully shelter.

## The Deeper Architecture

The sukkah embodies *bitachon* — trust. Not the superficial kind that assumes good weather, but the radical trust that accepts vulnerability as a spiritual practice.

When the first cool rain drops through your palm frond roof onto your dinner plate, you're experiencing what our ancestors knew in the wilderness: security isn't found in our constructions. It emerges from something beyond our control.

**The Talmud asks:** Why do we leave our permanent homes for temporary ones?

**The answer:** To remember that all dwellings are temporary.

Your house with its solid roof and locked doors creates the illusion of permanence. The sukkah strips away that illusion. Here, under stars visible through deliberate gaps, you taste the truth — we are all wanderers building temporary shelters in time.

## The Seven-Day Journey

Day 1: Excitement. The novelty of eating outside, the fun of a new space.

Day 3: Reality. It's actually inconvenient. The cushions are damp. You miss your kitchen counter.

Day 5: Surrender. Something shifts. The inconvenience becomes... simplicity.

Day 7: Sadness. You don't want to leave. This flimsy hut has become home.

This progression mirrors the Jewish journey from Egypt to the Promised Land — from the security of slavery through the vulnerability of wilderness to the discovery that home isn't a place but a way of being.

## Ancient Wisdom in Modern Chaos

We live in an age of digital permanence — cloud storage, social media archives, the illusion that our creations will last forever. The sukkah whispers otherwise.

Your photos will be corrupted. Your tweets will be forgotten. Your carefully curated life will be dismantled by time.

But here's what the sukkah teaches: This isn't cause for despair. It's an invitation to live differently.

When you know your shelter is temporary, every meal becomes precious. When your roof shows you stars, every conversation deepens. When your walls could blow down, every moment of peace feels like grace.

## The Return Home

After seven days, you'll walk back into your house, and it will feel strange. Solid but somehow less real than the swaying structure you just left.

That strangeness is the gift. You've tasted a different way of dwelling — one that doesn't depend on control, accumulation, or the illusion of permanence.

The sukkah doesn't teach us to abandon our homes. It teaches us to hold them lightly.

Your kitchen table isn't more sacred than the wobbly table in your sukkah. Your bedroom ceiling isn't more protective than palm fronds under stars. The difference isn't in the structures — it's in how consciously you inhabit them.

## The Invitation

This year, if you build a sukkah, don't build it perfectly. Let the walls be a little crooked. Let the roof show plenty of sky.

Then sit in your beautiful, imperfect shelter and ask: What would it mean to live every day with this kind of trust? What would change if you remembered that all your securities are temporary and all your homes are just stopping places on a longer journey?

The sukkah doesn't answer these questions. It just holds space for them.

And sometimes, holding space is enough.

*Next time the rain falls through your roof onto your dinner, don't rush inside. Stay a moment longer. Let the water bless your meal. This too is dwelling.*
