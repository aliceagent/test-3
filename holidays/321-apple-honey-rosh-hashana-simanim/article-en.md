## Introduction

On the evening of Rosh Hashana, the Jewish New Year, families around the world gather around tables laden with symbolic foods. Each item carries a prayer, a hope, a blessing for the year ahead. The most famous of these is the apple dipped in honey—a wish for a sweet new year. But this is only the beginning. The tradition of *simanim* (סימנים, "signs" or "symbols") includes a carefully chosen menu of fruits, vegetables, and other foods, each representing a specific blessing we ask God to grant us in the coming year.

This custom has roots going back nearly two thousand years — during what corresponds to China's Han Dynasty (汉朝, 206 BCE–220 CE) — when the Talmudic sages taught that "a person should make it a habit to see certain things at the beginning of the year" (Horayot 12a). Just as Chinese New Year customs involve symbolic foods like fish (鱼, *yú*, representing surplus/abundance, 余, *yú*), dumplings (wealth), and long noodles (longevity), Jewish tradition uses the language of food to speak our deepest hopes to Heaven.

What makes the *simanim* so powerful is not magic, but intention. We don't believe these foods automatically bring blessing—rather, eating them with *kavanah* (intention) while saying the accompanying prayers opens our hearts to what we truly desire and need. Let's explore this beautiful tradition course by course.

## The Core Simanim: A Guide to Each Symbolic Food

### 1. **Apple and Honey (תפוח בדבש)**

**The Food:** A slice of apple (preferably sweet, like Fuji or Gala) dipped in honey.

**The Prayer:**  
*"Yehi ratzon milfanekha, Hashem Elokeinu vEilokei avoteinu, shetechadesh aleinu shanah tovah u'metukah."*  
("May it be Your will, Lord our God and God of our ancestors, that You renew for us a good and sweet year.")

**The Meaning:**  
The apple represents the sweetness and beauty of life; honey is nature's pure sweetness. Together they express our hope that the year ahead will be filled with goodness, joy, and pleasant experiences. This is universally the most recognized Rosh Hashana symbol.

**Cultural Parallel:**  
Like the Chinese tradition of eating sweet rice cakes (*niangao*, 年糕) on New Year to wish for progress and growth (*nian nian gao sheng*, 年年高升), the apple and honey speak a language of sweetness transcending bitterness.

---

### 2. **Dates (תמרים)**

**The Food:** Fresh or dried dates.

**The Prayer:**  
*"Yehi ratzon... shetitamu sonenu."*  
("May it be Your will... that our enemies be consumed/finished.")

**The Meaning:**  
In Hebrew, the word for date (*tamar*) sounds like *yitamu* ("be consumed/finished"). The prayer is that those who wish us harm—whether external enemies or internal negative forces—should cease to trouble us. This isn't a curse; it's a prayer for peace and the end of enmity.

**Note:**  
Some families reinterpret this as "May hatred and enmity be consumed from the world," shifting focus from personal enemies to universal peace.

---

### 3. **Pomegranate (רימון)**

**The Food:** Fresh pomegranate seeds (arils).

**The Prayer:**  
*"Yehi ratzon... shenarbeh zechuyot k'rimon."*  
("May it be Your will... that our merits increase like [the seeds of] a pomegranate.")

**The Meaning:**  
A pomegranate traditionally contains 613 seeds—corresponding to the 613 commandments (*mitzvot*) in the Torah. The prayer is that our good deeds and merits should multiply in the coming year. It's a commitment to spiritual growth.

**Cultural Parallel:**  
In Chinese culture, the pomegranate (石榴, *shíliu*) symbolizes fertility and abundance—many seeds, many blessings. Both traditions see this fruit as a vessel of plenitude.

---

### 4. **Leek, Scallion, or Cabbage (כרתי)**

**The Food:** Cooked leek (*karti* in Aramaic).

**The Prayer:**  
*"Yehi ratzon... sheyikartu oyvenu."*  
("May it be Your will... that our enemies be cut off.")

**The Meaning:**  
The Aramaic word *karti* (leek) sounds like *yikartu* ("be cut off"). Similar to the dates, this is a prayer for the removal of those who oppose us or wish us harm. Leeks, scallions (*kara'ot*), or cabbage (*kruv*) can all be used based on availability.

**Modern Adaptation:**  
Some families say: "May all harsh decrees against us be cut off," focusing on obstacles rather than people.

---

### 5. **Beets or Chard (סלק)**

**The Food:** Cooked beets or Swiss chard.

**The Prayer:**  
*"Yehi ratzon... sheyistalku oyvenu."*  
("May it be Your will... that our enemies be removed.")

**The Meaning:**  
The Hebrew word *selek* (beet or chard) sounds like *yistalku* ("be removed"). Again, a prayer for the departure of those who oppose us or cause us harm.

**Culinary Note:**  
In many Sephardi and Mizrahi communities, beets are pickled or served as a salad. Ashkenazi families often use chard instead.

---

### 6. **Gourd, Squash, or Pumpkin (קרא)**

**The Food:** Cooked pumpkin, zucchini, or any gourd.

**The Prayer:**  
*"Yehi ratzon... shetikarei gzar dinenu, v'yikaru lefanekha zechuyoteinu."*  
("May it be Your will... that the harsh judgment against us be torn up, and that our merits be proclaimed before You.")

**The Meaning:**  
The word *kara* (gourd) sounds like *yikarei* ("be torn up") and *yikaru* ("be proclaimed/read out"). This is a double prayer: may negative decrees be annulled, and may our good deeds be recognized.

**Cultural Connection:**  
Pumpkin and squash are symbols of harvest abundance in both Jewish and Chinese autumn festivals.

---

### 7. **Fish Head (ראש דג)**

**The Food:** The head of a fish (or the whole fish, depending on custom).

**The Prayer:**  
*"Yehi ratzon... shenihyeh l'rosh v'lo l'zanav."*  
("May it be Your will... that we be like the head and not like the tail.")

**The Meaning:**  
This is a prayer for leadership, excellence, and being proactive rather than passive. To be "a head" means to lead, to take initiative, to set the pace. To be "a tail" means to follow others without direction.

**Why Fish?**  
Fish are also symbols of blessing and fertility (they multiply abundantly) and are associated with the evil eye protection (fish live underwater, hidden from view, thus protected from harmful gazes).

**Cultural Parallel:**  
In Chinese cuisine, serving a whole fish (鱼, *yú*) at New Year symbolizes surplus and completeness—head and tail intact represents wholeness and a good beginning leading to a good ending.

**Practical Adaptation:**  
If eating a fish head is culturally uncomfortable, some families use a sheep's head or simply the head portion of a whole fish fillet.

---

### 8. **Carrots (גזר)**

**The Food:** Sliced or cooked carrots (often sliced into circles resembling coins).

**The Prayer:**  
*"Yehi ratzon... shetigzor alenu gezeirot tovot."*  
("May it be Your will... that You decree upon us good decrees.")

**The Meaning:**  
The word *gezer* (carrot) sounds like *tigzor* ("You should decree") and also *gezeirot* ("decrees"). Carrots are also sweet, adding to the theme of sweetness.

**Visual Symbolism:**  
When sliced into rounds, carrots resemble gold coins—a folk symbol for prosperity (similar to Chinese gold ingot imagery).

---

### 9. **Black-Eyed Peas or Fenugreek (רוביא / רוביה)**

**The Food:** Black-eyed peas (common in Sephardi tradition) or fenugreek (*rubia* in Hebrew).

**The Prayer:**  
*"Yehi ratzon... sheyirbu zechuyoteinu."*  
("May it be Your will... that our merits multiply.")

**The Meaning:**  
*Rubia* sounds like *yirbu* ("may they multiply"). This is another prayer for increasing merit and good deeds.

**Regional Note:**  
This is especially popular in Sephardi and Mizrahi communities. Ashkenazi families may substitute green beans.

---

### 10. **Pomegranate's Alternative: Head of a Ram (Ashkenazi Custom)**

**The Food:** In some Ashkenazi communities, a sheep or ram head is used.

**The Prayer:**  
Same as fish head: "May we be like the head and not the tail."

**Additional Layer:**  
The ram also recalls the *Akeidat Yitzchak* (Binding of Isaac, Genesis 22), when God provided a ram as a substitute for Isaac. It symbolizes God's mercy and the merit of our ancestors.

**Note:**  
This is less common today and most families use fish instead.

## How to Conduct the Simanim at the Rosh Hashana Seder

### Timing
The *simanim* are eaten at the festive meal on the first night of Rosh Hashana, after *kiddush* (the blessing over wine) and *hamotzi* (the blessing over bread). Some families repeat the custom on the second night as well.

### Order
1. **Apple and honey** is always first (universal custom).
2. The other *simanim* can follow in any order, though many families follow the order listed above or the order found in their prayer book (*machzor*).

### Procedure
- One person (usually the head of the household) holds up each food item.
- Everyone at the table says the corresponding prayer together (or listens while one person recites it).
- Each person takes a small taste of the food.
- The symbolic food is then served as part of the meal or set aside.

### Participation
Encourage children to say the prayers (in Hebrew or translation). Explain each symbol so they understand the hope being expressed. Make it interactive—ask, "What do you hope for in the coming year?"

## The Deeper Meaning: Why Symbolic Foods?

### 1. **Language Beyond Words**
The Talmud teaches that *"ma'aseh avot siman l'banim"* — "the actions of the ancestors are a sign for the descendants" (Sotah 34a). Symbolic actions create spiritual imprints. Eating these foods with intention plants seeds of blessing.

### 2. **Engaging the Senses**
Judaism involves the whole person: mind, heart, and body. Prayers engage the mind; symbolic foods engage the senses—taste, smell, sight. The body participates in the prayer.

### 3. **Hope Made Tangible**
Saying "I hope for a sweet year" is abstract. Dipping an apple in honey and tasting the sweetness makes the hope real, physical, memorable.

### 4. **Community and Continuity**
These customs connect us to Jews across time and space. When you recite the prayer over the pomegranate, you join a chain stretching back to the Talmudic sages nearly two millennia ago and forward to future generations. 

### 5. **Puns as Prayer**
Hebrew is a language of wordplay and meaning. The puns in the *simanim* aren't superficial—they reflect the belief that words shape reality. The sages taught, "Death and life are in the power of the tongue" (Proverbs 18:21). By speaking blessings, we call forth blessing.

## Cultural Connection: Symbolic Foods in Chinese and Jewish Traditions

Both Chinese and Jewish cultures use the language of food to express hope, blessing, and connection:

| **Jewish Rosh Hashana** | **Chinese New Year** |
|-------------------------|---------------------|
| Apple & Honey (sweetness) | Rice cakes 年糕 (progress, rising higher) |
| Pomegranate (abundance) | Fish 鱼 (surplus, abundance 余) |
| Fish Head (leadership) | Whole fish (completeness, good beginning & end) |
| Dates (end of enemies) | Dumplings 饺子 (wealth, like ancient gold ingots) |
| Round challah (continuity of year) | Tangyuan 汤圆 (family reunion, wholeness) |

In both traditions, food is never "just food"—it is a vessel of meaning, a medium of prayer, a bearer of culture.

## Modern Variations and Creative Additions

While the traditional *simanim* have been practiced for centuries, some families add modern interpretations:

- **New fruit**: Eating a fruit you haven't tasted this season (to say the *Shehecheyanu* blessing—"who has kept us alive to reach this moment").
- **Regional foods**: Sephardi families may add sesame seeds, fennel, or other locally meaningful items.
- **Positive reinterpretations**: Instead of praying for the removal of enemies, some say: "May we have the strength to turn enemies into friends" or "May all harsh decrees be sweetened."
- **Personal symbols**: Some families choose a food that represents a personal hope for the year (e.g., a student might eat a book-shaped cookie and pray for success in studies).

## Practical Tips for Your Rosh Hashana Table

### Sourcing Ingredients
- **In China**: Most ingredients (apples, honey, pomegranates, dates, beets, carrots, pumpkin, fish) are readily available in supermarkets or wet markets.
- **Substitutions**: If you can't find a specific item (e.g., black-eyed peas), it's better to use a substitute than to skip the category entirely. The intention matters more than the exact ingredient.

### Presentation
Arrange the *simanim* on a beautiful platter or individual small dishes. In some communities, special decorative plates are used for this purpose.

### Printable Cards
Create small cards with the Hebrew, transliteration, and translation of each prayer. Place them on the table so everyone can participate even if they don't know the prayers by heart.

### Children's Engagement
- Let children help prepare the *simanim* (washing the apple, scooping pomegranate seeds).
- Use pictures or drawings to explain each symbol.
- Ask each child to share one thing they hope for in the new year after each *siman*.

## Theological Reflection: Does God Need Our Puns?

A question naturally arises: Does God really respond to Hebrew wordplay? Do these linguistic connections truly invoke divine blessing?

The deeper answer is that the *simanim* are not about tricking God or mechanically producing outcomes. They are about *directing our intention*. The Kabbalists teach that the Hebrew language is the "language of creation"—the medium through which God brought the world into being ("Let there be light," Genesis 1:3). When we speak in Hebrew, especially in prayer, we align ourselves with the creative power of divine speech.

The puns help us *remember* and *focus*. When you see a pomegranate, you think about multiplying good deeds. When you taste honey, you focus on the desire for sweetness. The foods become *triggers* for *kavanah* (intention), and intention is the gateway to connection with God.

Rabbi Yehuda HaLevi (11th century Spain, during China's Song Dynasty 宋朝) taught: "The heart is the seat of intention, and the tongue is its instrument." The *simanim* are the instruments through which the heart's hopes are articulated.

## Key Takeaways

- **The *simanim* are symbolic foods eaten on Rosh Hashana night, each accompanied by a prayer for the new year.**
- **The most famous is apple dipped in honey, symbolizing a sweet year.**
- **Other *simanim* include pomegranate (merits), dates (end of enmity), beets, leeks, squash, fish head (leadership), and more.**
- **The custom is based on Talmudic tradition nearly 2,000 years old.**
- **The Hebrew puns create associations that direct our *kavanah* (intention).**
- **Similar to Chinese New Year symbolic foods, Jewish *simanim* use taste and sight to express hope and blessing.**
- **Participation is key—say the prayers, taste the foods, share your hopes for the year ahead.**
