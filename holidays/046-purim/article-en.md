## Introduction

Imagine a holiday where you're commanded to dress up in costumes, give gifts of food to friends, donate generously to the poor, and feast until you're merry — all while celebrating one of the most dramatic reversals of fortune in Jewish history. This is Purim, one of Judaism's most joyous festivals, commemorating how the Jewish people were saved from annihilation in ancient Persia nearly 2,500 years ago — during what corresponds to China's Spring and Autumn Period (春秋时代, approximately 770-476 BCE).

What makes Purim unique among Jewish holidays is that God's name never appears in the Megillat Esther (the Book of Esther), the scroll that tells the Purim story. Yet the entire narrative reveals divine providence working behind the scenes through seemingly natural events and human choices. This teaches a profound lesson: sometimes God's greatest miracles are the ones disguised as coincidences.

## The Story Behind Purim

The events of Purim took place in the Persian Empire under King Achashverosh (Ahasuerus), who ruled from India to Ethiopia. The story begins when the king's advisor, Haman, became enraged at Mordechai the Jew for refusing to bow to him. In his fury, Haman plotted not just to kill Mordechai, but to annihilate all Jews throughout the empire on a single day — the 13th of Adar.

What Haman didn't know was that the queen herself, Esther, was Jewish — though she had hidden her identity at the instruction of her cousin and guardian, Mordechai. Through a series of seemingly coincidental events — a sleepless night that led the king to review old records, the discovery that Mordechai had once saved the king's life, perfectly timed palace banquets — the plot unfolded in reverse.

Esther risked her life by approaching the king uninvited to plead for her people. Haman's scheme was exposed, and he was executed on the very gallows he had prepared for Mordechai. The Jews were granted the right to defend themselves, and on the 13th of Adar, they fought and defeated their enemies. The following day, the 14th of Adar (15th in walled cities like Shushan), became a day of celebration and thanksgiving.

This reversal — from destruction to deliverance, from mourning to joy — is the essence of Purim.

## The Four Mitzvot of Purim

Unlike many Jewish holidays that focus on ritual observances and restrictions, Purim is primarily about action and joy through four specific commandments:

### 1. **Megillah Reading (קריאת המגילה)**

The primary mitzvah of Purim is hearing the entire Book of Esther read aloud, both in the evening and again in the morning. The Megillah is read from a special parchment scroll, just like the Torah, but unlike regular Torah reading, the Megillah is unrolled and folded like a letter — because the story of Esther was originally sent as letters throughout the Persian Empire.

When Haman's name is mentioned (which happens 54 times), it's customary to make noise with special noisemakers called "groggers" (or "ra'ashanim" in Hebrew) to blot out his name, symbolically erasing the memory of this enemy of the Jewish people. This tradition teaches children and adults alike that evil must be actively opposed, not passively tolerated.

**Cultural Note:** Just as traditional Chinese opera uses specific sounds and gestures to represent characters, the Purim service uses noise to mark the villain. Both traditions engage the community in participatory storytelling.

### 2. **Mishloach Manot (משלוח מנות) — Sending Food Gifts**

On Purim day, every Jew is required to send at least two different ready-to-eat food items to at least one friend. These gift packages, called "mishloach manot," are meant to increase friendship and unity among the Jewish people.

The custom typically includes baked goods, fruits, candies, or wine — foods that can be enjoyed immediately. Many people prepare elaborate themed baskets and send them to multiple friends, and children especially love delivering these gifts door-to-door throughout the day.

Why this mitzvah? The Talmud explains that Haman's plot succeeded initially because the Jewish people were divided. The decree to annihilate them was enabled by their disunity. Therefore, on Purim we actively strengthen the bonds between us through this tangible expression of friendship.

**Practical Tip:** In China or other places without a large Jewish community, you can fulfill this mitzvah by sending packages to any Jewish friends you know, even if they live in another city — express delivery counts! The key is that the gift must arrive on Purim day itself.

### 3. **Matanot La'evyonim (מתנות לאביונים) — Gifts to the Poor**

Every person is obligated to give monetary gifts to at least two poor people on Purim day. This mitzvah emphasizes that our own celebration cannot be complete while others lack the means to celebrate. Unlike regular tzedakah (charity), which is given thoughtfully and often anonymously, on Purim we give immediately to anyone who asks, without investigation into their need.

The Shulchan Aruch (Code of Jewish Law) states: "It is better to increase gifts to the poor than to increase one's festive meal or sending portions to friends. For there is no greater and more glorious joy than to gladden the hearts of the poor, orphans, widows, and strangers."

This teaching resonates deeply with the Confucian virtue of benevolence (仁, rén) — the idea that true human excellence includes caring for those less fortunate. On Purim, this isn't just a virtue; it's a commandment that ensures every Jew can participate in the joy of the day.

### 4. **The Purim Feast (סעודת פורים)**

Purim day includes a festive meal, traditionally held in the afternoon, where family and friends gather to eat, drink, sing, and celebrate the miracle of deliverance. It's customary to drink wine during this meal — in fact, the Talmud states that one should drink "until they cannot distinguish between 'blessed is Mordechai' and 'cursed is Haman,'" though many rabbis interpret this as drinking slightly more than usual rather than to actual intoxication, and emphasize that one should never drink in a way that leads to improper behavior.

Traditional Purim foods include triangular pastries called "hamantaschen" (or "oznei Haman" — Haman's ears — in Hebrew), filled with poppy seeds, dates, or chocolate. Some also eat kreplach (dumplings) because the concealed filling represents the hidden miracles of Purim.

**Parallel to Chinese Culture:** Just as Chinese New Year reunions emphasize family togetherness over a shared feast, the Purim seudah (meal) brings the Jewish community together in shared joy and gratitude.

## The Hidden Hand of God

What makes Purim theologically unique is the *concealment* of divine intervention. Unlike Passover, where God's hand is revealed through open miracles (the ten plagues, the splitting of the sea), Purim's miracles are hidden within what appears to be natural political events.

Consider the "coincidences":
- The king's sudden insomnia at exactly the right moment
- His choice to read the specific record of Mordechai's loyalty
- Haman's arrival at the palace at that precise time
- Esther's perfectly timed banquets
- The king's sudden fury at Haman's behavior

None of these events required supernatural intervention, yet their precise timing and sequence could only have been orchestrated by divine providence. This is why God's name doesn't appear in the Megillah — the entire book is teaching us to recognize God's presence in the "natural" unfolding of history.

The Talmud says that even when the Jews were exiled and God's presence seemed hidden, "I will surely hide My face" (Deuteronomy 31:18) — a double hiding, a concealment within concealment. Yet even in that darkest moment, God was there, protecting and guiding. This is the deepest message of Purim: God is present even when He seems absent.

**Philosophical Connection:** This concept mirrors the Daoist teaching of *wuwei* (无为) — action through non-action, achieving results without obvious intervention. On Purim, God acts through the natural flow of events, never revealing His hand yet steering everything to its proper conclusion.

## Customs and Traditions

Beyond the four main mitzvot, Purim has developed many joyous customs:

**Costumes and Masks:** It's customary, especially for children, to dress in costumes on Purim. This tradition connects to the theme of hidden identity (Esther concealing that she was Jewish) and the hidden nature of the miracles. Masks represent that things are not always as they appear.

**Purim Spiels:** Many communities perform humorous plays retelling the Purim story, often with jokes, parodies, and commentary on current events. This is one day when respectful humor is encouraged even in the synagogue.

**Special Foods:** Beyond hamantaschen, different communities have their own Purim delicacies. Sephardic Jews make folares (cheese pastries), while Persian Jews prepare special rice dishes.

**The Fast of Esther:** The day before Purim, the 13th of Adar, is a fast day commemorating Esther's three-day fast before approaching the king. This reminds us that the joy of Purim came only after peril, prayer, and fasting.

## Purim in the Absence of a Temple

Interestingly, Purim is one of the few Jewish holidays established *after* the giving of the Torah at Sinai. While most holidays are commanded in the Five Books of Moses, Purim was instituted by Mordechai and Esther themselves, and later ratified by the rabbis. The Talmud states that even when the Messianic era arrives and other holidays may be nullified, Purim and its observances will remain forever.

Why? Because Purim teaches an eternal lesson relevant in every generation: that even when God seems hidden, even when we face existential threats, even when redemption seems impossible — salvation can come in an instant through the convergence of human courage and divine providence.

## Practical Application: Purim in China

If you're observing Purim in China or another place with a small Jewish community:

1. **Connect with Chabad:** Many Chabad Houses in major Chinese cities (Shanghai, Beijing, Hong Kong) hold public Megillah readings and Purim celebrations. Check with your local Chabad in advance.

2. **Read the Megillah Online:** If no community reading is available, you can fulfill the mitzvah by listening to a live-streamed Megillah reading, provided you hear every word clearly.

3. **Prepare Mishloach Manot:** Even if you only know one or two Jewish people in your city, prepare gift packages for them. Include traditional items like hamantaschen if you can find or make them, or any two types of ready-to-eat foods.

4. **Give Tzedakah:** Donate to organizations that help poor Jews around the world. Many organizations have special Purim campaigns that distribute your donations immediately to those in need.

5. **Hold a Festive Meal:** Invite any Jewish friends or fellow learners to join you for a Purim feast. Share the story, sing songs, and celebrate together — even a small gathering fulfills the spirit of the day.

## The Message of Purim for Today

In an age of cynicism and despair, Purim delivers a message of hope: that the darkest circumstances can reverse in an instant, that ordinary people can become heroes, and that God's providence is working even when we can't perceive it.

The Megillah teaches that Esther became queen "for such a time as this" — her entire life trajectory positioned her for the single moment when her people needed her. Similarly, each of us has unique circumstances, relationships, and opportunities that may seem random but are actually positioning us for moments when we can make a difference.

Mordechai's refusal to bow to Haman, Esther's courage to risk her life, the Jewish people's unity in the face of destruction — these weren't passive acceptances of fate, but active choices to stand for truth and justice. Purim reminds us that while we trust in God's providence, we must also act with courage when the moment calls for it.

## Key Takeaways

- **Purim commemorates the salvation of the Jewish people from destruction in ancient Persia, around 2,500 years ago during China's Spring and Autumn Period**
- **The four mitzvot of Purim are: hearing the Megillah, sending food gifts to friends, giving charity to the poor, and eating a festive meal**
- **God's name never appears in the Book of Esther, teaching us to recognize divine providence in seemingly natural events**
- **Purim emphasizes joy, unity, and generosity — celebrating through giving to others**
- **The holiday teaches that salvation can come suddenly through the combination of human courage and hidden divine guidance**
