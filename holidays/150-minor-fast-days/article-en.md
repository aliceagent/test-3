## Introduction: Remembering Through Restraint

The Jewish calendar includes four "minor" fast days that commemorate tragic events in Jewish history, particularly related to the destruction of the First and Second Temples in Jerusalem. These observances differ from major fast days like Yom Kippur and Tisha B'Av in their duration, restrictions, and spiritual emphasis. While the major fasts focus on personal atonement or national catastrophe, the minor fasts mark specific historical moments that led to or followed from the destructions that ended Jewish sovereignty and displaced the people from their land.

The term "minor" refers not to their significance but to their practical observance—they last from dawn to nightfall rather than a full 25 hours, and their restrictions are less comprehensive. Yet these fasts carry profound meaning, serving as historical markers that keep alive the memory of events that shaped Jewish destiny and as opportunities for reflection on the patterns that led to catastrophe.

Understanding these fast days requires recognizing that Jewish historical consciousness doesn't simply record the past but mines it for ongoing lessons. Each fast asks not just "What happened?" but "What can we learn?" and "How should this shape our present behavior?" In this way, ancient events continue to instruct contemporary life.

## The Four Minor Fast Days

### 1. Fast of Gedaliah (Tzom Gedaliah) - 3rd Tishrei

The Fast of Gedaliah, observed the day after Rosh Hashanah (or after Shabbat if the 3rd falls on Shabbat), commemorates the assassination of Gedaliah ben Ahikam, the Jewish governor appointed by Nebuchadnezzar after the destruction of the First Temple in 586 BCE (corresponding roughly to the late Spring and Autumn period in Chinese history, 春秋晚期).

**Historical Context:** After Jerusalem's destruction and the exile of most Jews to Babylonia, a remnant remained in the land of Judah. Nebuchadnezzar appointed Gedaliah, a righteous and capable leader from a distinguished family (his grandfather Shaphan had helped King Josiah's religious reforms), to govern this surviving community from Mizpah, north of ruined Jerusalem. Gedaliah's leadership offered hope that some form of Jewish life could continue in the ancestral homeland despite the Temple's destruction.

However, Ishmael ben Nethaniah, a member of the royal house of David, motivated by political rivalry, jealousy of Gedaliah's appointment, and possibly incited by Baalis, the Ammonite king, assassinated Gedaliah along with the Jews and Babylonians who were with him. This occurred on Rosh Hashanah, but the fast was postponed to avoid fasting on the holiday itself.

Gedaliah had been warned of the plot but refused to believe evil of a fellow Jew, demonstrating perhaps excessive naivety. His murder led the remaining Jewish population to flee to Egypt in fear of Babylonian reprisal, effectively ending any Jewish autonomous presence in the land for generations. The book of Jeremiah (chapters 40-41) provides the detailed account of this tragedy.

**The Lesson:** The Talmud teaches that the death of righteous individuals is equivalent to the burning of the Temple (Rosh Hashanah 18b). The fast thus mourns not just one man but the final extinguishing of hope for Jewish continuity in the land after the First Temple's destruction. More profoundly, it teaches about the destructive power of *sinat chinam* (baseless hatred) among Jews and the dangers of political zealotry that values ideological purity over communal survival.

The timing of this fast—immediately after Rosh Hashanah, when Jews have just begun the Ten Days of Repentance—is significant. It suggests that national catastrophes stem from moral and spiritual failings that require introspection and correction, not merely mourning.

### 2. Tenth of Tevet (Asarah B'Tevet) - 10th Tevet

This fast, observed in mid-winter, marks the beginning of the siege of Jerusalem by Nebuchadnezzar's Babylonian army in 588 BCE, which ultimately led to the destruction of the First Temple thirty months later.

**Historical Significance:** The prophet Ezekiel, himself among the exiles in Babylonia, received a divine message on this exact date: "Son of man, write down the name of this day, this very day. The king of Babylon has laid siege to Jerusalem this very day" (Ezekiel 24:2). The siege lasted two and a half years, during which the city endured terrible famine, suffering, and ultimately, destruction. Mothers ate their own children due to starvation, as recorded in Lamentations.

The Tenth of Tevet is considered particularly significant—so much so that it is observed even when it falls on Friday (requiring fasting until immediately before Shabbat), unlike other minor fasts that would be postponed. Some authorities suggest this is because the siege's beginning was the point of no return, where the chain of events leading to destruction became inevitable.

**Modern Observance:** In the State of Israel, Yom HaShoah (Holocaust Remembrance Day) was initially proposed for the Tenth of Tevet, and this date was ultimately designated as a general *Kaddish* day (*Yom HaKaddish HaKlali*) for Holocaust victims whose date of death is unknown or whose families were entirely destroyed, leaving no one to recite Kaddish for them. This modern addition links ancient and contemporary catastrophes, suggesting continuity in Jewish suffering and resilience.

The connection resonates with the Chinese historical experience of remembering national trauma—such as the Nanjing Massacre memorial day (December 13)—where collective memory serves both to honor victims and to derive lessons about resilience and the importance of preventing future atrocities.

### 3. Seventeenth of Tammuz (Shivah Asar B'Tammuz) - 17th Tammuz

This fast, observed in early summer, commemorates five tragic events that the Mishnah (Ta'anit 4:6) records as having occurred on this date:

1. **The breaking of the first set of tablets** by Moses upon descending Mount Sinai and seeing the Israelites worshipping the Golden Calf (approximately 1250 BCE, late Shang Dynasty, 商朝晚期)
2. **The cessation of the daily sacrifice** (*Tamid*) in the First Temple due to the siege, when no more sheep could be found to offer
3. **The breaching of Jerusalem's walls** before the destruction of the Second Temple in 70 CE (during the Han Dynasty period, 汉朝)
4. **The burning of a Torah scroll** by Apostomos, a Greek or Roman official, representing deliberate desecration of the sacred
5. **The placement of an idol** in the Temple sanctuary by either the Greek king Antiochus or a Roman official

**The Three Weeks:** Shivah Asar B'Tammuz begins the period known as "The Three Weeks" (*Bein HaMetzarim*, literally "between the straits"), culminating in Tisha B'Av on the 9th of Av. During this period, observant Jews refrain from weddings, haircuts, listening to live music, and other joyous activities, creating a graduated intensification of mourning.

The clustering of multiple disasters on this single date (according to tradition) suggests a pattern: certain times become vessels for tragedy, and historical catastrophes echo and resonate with each other. The Talmud teaches that "When Av enters, joy decreases," recognizing that the calendar itself can carry emotional and spiritual weight.

### 4. Fast of Esther (Ta'anit Esther) - 13th Adar

Unlike the other three fasts, which mourn destruction, Ta'anit Esther commemorates Queen Esther's call for the Jewish people to fast before she approached King Ahasuerus to save them from Haman's genocidal decree during the Persian period (approximately 5th century BCE, corresponding to the Warring States period in Chinese history, 战国时代).

**Historical Context:** According to the Megillah (Book of Esther), when Esther learned of Haman's plot to annihilate the Jews throughout the Persian Empire, she sent word to Mordechai: "Go, gather all the Jews who are in Shushan and fast for me; do not eat or drink for three days, night and day. I and my maidens will also fast in the same way" (Esther 4:16). Only after this communal fast did Esther risk her life by appearing unbidden before the king.

The original fast lasted three days and nights and occurred in the month of Nissan. The current observance—a single day immediately before Purim—doesn't precisely reenact that historical fast but serves as a spiritual preparation for the holiday, reminding us that Jewish salvation came through prayer, fasting, repentance, and ultimately divine intervention, not merely through political maneuvering.

**Unique Aspects:** This fast is fundamentally different in character from the others. While they focus on mourning and catastrophe, Ta'anit Esther emphasizes preparation, spiritual readiness, and the recognition that even celebrations like Purim emerged from situations of mortal danger. The Jewish people's very survival was at stake, making the subsequent joy of Purim all the sweeter and more meaningful.

Some communities observe the fast with particular intensity, seeing it as an opportunity to nullify harsh decrees and to pray for miracles, much as Esther did. The proximity to Purim creates a powerful narrative arc: fasting and prayer give way to feasting and celebration, mourning transforms into joy—precisely what the prophet Zechariah promises will eventually happen with all the fast days (Zechariah 8:19).

## Laws and Customs: The Practical Framework

**Duration:** All minor fasts last from *alot hashachar* (dawn, when the first light appears on the eastern horizon) until *tzet hakochavim* (nightfall, when three medium-sized stars become visible). This typically means approximately 12-13 hours depending on the season and latitude, unlike Yom Kippur and Tisha B'Av, which last from sunset to sunset (approximately 25 hours).

**Prohibitions:** The primary prohibition is eating and drinking. Some authorities include additional restrictions similar to Tisha B'Av—washing for pleasure, wearing leather shoes, and marital relations—though common practice follows the lenient view that only eating and drinking are forbidden. The focus is fasting, not comprehensive self-denial.

**Who Must Fast:**
- Healthy adults (post-bar/bat mitzvah age) are obligated
- Pregnant and nursing women are generally exempt, following the principle of *pikuach nefesh* (preservation of life and health takes precedence)
- Those who are ill, even with minor illness, are exempt and should consult a rabbi if uncertain
- The elderly or those for whom fasting causes serious difficulty may be exempt

**Postponement:** If a minor fast falls on Shabbat (when fasting is inappropriate except on Yom Kippur), it is postponed to Sunday. The Tenth of Tevet is considered so significant that some authorities suggest it would be observed even on Shabbat if that were ever possible (though the calendar is structured so this doesn't occur).

**Liturgical Additions:** Special Torah readings (*Exodus* 32:11-14 and 34:1-10, about Moses's intercession after the Golden Calf) and haftarot (*Isaiah* 55:6-56:8, calling for repentance and return to God) are read at both morning and afternoon services. Selichot (penitential prayers) and Avinu Malkeinu are recited. In the afternoon service, individuals reciting the Amidah add *Aneinu* (Answer us), a special prayer for fast days.

## Spiritual Significance: Beyond Historical Memory

The Rambam (Maimonides) teaches that these fast days are not merely commemorations of past tragedies but active spiritual opportunities. In his *Mishneh Torah* (Laws of Fasts 5:1), he writes: "There are days when all of Israel fasts because of the calamities that occurred to them on those days, to arouse the hearts and initiate repentance. This will serve as a reminder of our wicked conduct and that of our ancestors, which resembles our present conduct and therefore brought those calamities upon them and upon us. By remembering these matters, we will repent and improve."

The fasts thus serve multiple purposes:

**Historical Memory:** Preserving awareness of pivotal events that shaped Jewish destiny  
**Collective Responsibility:** Recognizing that national tragedies stem from communal moral failures, particularly *sinat chinam* (baseless hatred among Jews)  
**Spiritual Introspection:** Using physical deprivation to focus on spiritual correction  
**Eschatological Hope:** Maintaining faith that these days of mourning will eventually transform into festivals

During these fasts, customs include:
- Increased Torah study, particularly texts related to repentance and the Temple
- Additional charity (*tzedakah*), as the prophet teaches: "Is not this the fast I choose: to loose the bonds of injustice, to share your bread with the hungry?" (Isaiah 58:6-7)
- Soul-searching and examination of one's behavior  
- Reading special Torah portions emphasizing divine mercy and human repentance

The physical experience of hunger serves as a catalyst for spiritual hunger—longing for connection with God, for restoration of what was lost, for healing of communal wounds. The discomfort is purposeful, redirecting attention from physical to spiritual needs.

## The Path to Redemption: From Mourning to Joy

The prophet Zechariah envisions a time when "the fast of the fourth month, the fast of the fifth, the fast of the seventh, and the fast of the tenth shall become occasions for joy and gladness, happy festivals for the house of Judah" (Zechariah 8:19). The four fasts mentioned correspond to the Seventeenth of Tammuz, Ninth of Av, Fast of Gedaliah, and Tenth of Tevet.

This prophecy teaches that the fasts are not eternal fixtures but contingent on the Jewish people's spiritual state. When genuine repentance and unity are achieved, when the causes that led to the original catastrophes are corrected, these days of mourning will be transformed into celebrations. The Temple will be rebuilt, Jewish sovereignty restored, and the long exile ended.

Until that time, these observances serve as reminders of an incomplete redemption, spurring Jews toward the spiritual and ethical work necessary for final restoration. They function as both memory and motivation—looking backward to understand what went wrong, looking forward to what could be.

## Conclusion: Fasting as Historical Consciousness

The minor fast days represent Judaism's insistence that history matters, that memory has moral weight, and that past events should shape present behavior. Unlike purely commemorative dates that simply mark anniversaries, these fasts demand active participation—physical discomfort that focuses attention, liturgical additions that articulate meaning, and personal introspection that connects ancient events to contemporary life.

They teach that Jewish survival depends not only on external circumstances—political power, military strength, economic prosperity—but fundamentally on internal spiritual condition: unity, righteousness, devotion to Torah, and commitment to the sacred mission of being "a light to the nations." When these internal qualities fail, even military might cannot prevent catastrophe; when they flourish, even apparent weakness can lead to salvation.

As we observe these fasts, we join generations of Jews who have maintained this practice through centuries of exile and dispersion, keeping alive the memory of what was lost and the hope of what will be restored. In this continuity of observance, we demonstrate that the Jewish people remain *am olam*—an eternal people whose relationship with history, memory, and destiny cannot be severed by time or circumstance.

*May we merit to transform these fasts into feasts, these days of mourning into days of joy, speedily and in our days.*
