# The Fast of Gedaliah and the Minor Fast Days Explained

The Jewish calendar includes four "minor" fast days that commemorate tragic events in Jewish history, particularly related to the destruction of the First and Second Temples. These observances differ from major fast days like Yom Kippur in their duration and restrictions.

## The Four Minor Fast Days

### 1. Fast of Gedaliah (Tzom Gedaliah) - 3rd Tishrei

The Fast of Gedaliah commemorates the assassination of Gedaliah ben Ahikam, the Jewish governor appointed by Nebuchadnezzar after the destruction of the First Temple in 586 BCE. Gedaliah's murder, perpetrated by Jewish zealots, marked the final blow to Jewish autonomy in the land of Israel and led to the exile of the remaining Jewish population.

**Historical Context:** After Jerusalem's destruction, Gedaliah was appointed to govern the surviving Jewish remnant in Mizpah. His leadership offered hope for Jewish continuity in the land. However, Ishmael ben Nethaniah, motivated by political rivalry and possibly incited by the Ammonite king, assassinated Gedaliah along with his Jewish and Babylonian supporters. This act of fratricide effectively ended Jewish self-governance for centuries.

**The Lesson:** The fast teaches us about the destructive power of baseless hatred (sinat chinam) among Jews, which our sages identify as the root cause of the Temple's destruction.

### 2. Tenth of Tevet (Asarah B'Tevet) - 10th Tevet

This fast marks the beginning of the siege of Jerusalem by Nebuchadnezzar's Babylonian army in 588 BCE, which ultimately led to the destruction of the First Temple.

**Historical Significance:** The siege lasted two and a half years, during which the city endured terrible famine and suffering. The Tenth of Tevet is considered so significant that it is observed even when it falls on Friday, unlike other minor fasts that would be postponed.

**Modern Observance:** In Israel, this day has also been designated as a general Kaddish day for Holocaust victims whose date of death is unknown.

### 3. Seventeenth of Tammuz (Shivah Asar B'Tammuz) - 17th Tammuz

This fast commemorates five tragic events that occurred on this date:
- The breaking of the first set of tablets by Moses upon seeing the Golden Calf
- The cessation of the daily sacrifice (Tamid) in the First Temple
- The breaching of Jerusalem's walls before the destruction of the Second Temple
- The burning of a Torah scroll by Apostomos (a Greek official)
- The placement of an idol in the Temple sanctuary

**The Three Weeks:** This fast begins the period of mourning known as "The Three Weeks," culminating in Tisha B'Av.

### 4. Fast of Esther (Ta'anit Esther) - 13th Adar

Unlike the other three fasts, Ta'anit Esther is not primarily about mourning destruction but commemorates Queen Esther's call for the Jewish people to fast before she approached King Ahasuerus to save them from Haman's decree.

**Historical Context:** According to the Megillah, Esther requested that all Jews fast for three days before she risked her life to petition the king. The fast day before Purim reminds us that salvation came through prayer, fasting, and divine intervention.

**Unique Aspects:** This fast is more about preparation and spiritual readiness than mourning, serving as a prelude to the joyous celebration of Purim.

## Laws and Customs

**Duration:** All minor fasts last from dawn (alot hashachar) until nightfall (tzet hakochavim), unlike Yom Kippur and Tisha B'Av, which last from sunset to sunset.

**Prohibitions:**
- Eating and drinking
- Some authorities include washing for pleasure, wearing leather shoes, and marital relations (though practices vary)

**Who Must Fast:**
- Healthy adults (bar/bat mitzvah age and older)
- Pregnant and nursing women are generally exempt
- Those who are ill are exempt and should consult a rabbi

**Postponement:** If a minor fast falls on Shabbat, it is postponed to Sunday (except the Tenth of Tevet, which is never postponed).

## Spiritual Significance

The Rambam teaches that these fast days are not merely commemorations of past tragedies but opportunities for introspection and repentance. They remind us to examine our actions and return to God with complete hearts.

During these fasts, it is customary to:
- Increase Torah study and prayer
- Give additional charity
- Engage in soul-searching and repentance
- Read special Torah portions and haftarot

## The Path to Redemption

Our sages teach that if the Jewish people properly observe these fast days with genuine repentance, these days of mourning will be transformed into days of joy and celebration when the Messiah comes. Until then, these observances serve as reminders of our spiritual mission and the importance of unity among the Jewish people.

The minor fast days ultimately teach us that Jewish survival depends not only on external circumstances but on our internal spiritual condition and our commitment to our sacred mission in the world.
