## Introduction

Imagine inviting Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, Moses, Aaron, Joseph, and King David to dinner. Not metaphorically — but actually setting places for them at your table, welcoming them into your home, and speaking to them as honored guests. This is the ancient Jewish custom of *Ushpizin* (Aramaic for "guests"), a mystical tradition practiced during the seven nights of Sukkot, the Feast of Tabernacles.

Every night of Sukkot, observant Jews symbolically invite one of these seven great spiritual leaders — *ushpizin* — into their sukkah, the temporary dwelling we build to commemorate the shelters our ancestors lived in during their forty years in the wilderness. This isn't mere symbolism. According to kabbalistic teaching, the souls of these righteous ancestors actually *do* visit the sukkah, bringing unique blessings and spiritual energy. The practice transforms the sukkah from a simple hut into a portal where past and present, heaven and earth, momentarily merge.

For someone unfamiliar with Jewish mysticism, this might sound fantastical. But in a culture where ancestor veneration is deeply valued and the boundary between the living and the departed is understood to be permeable, the *Ushpizin* tradition may resonate in unexpected ways.

## The Seven Guests and Their Qualities

Each night of Sukkot, a different patriarch or leader is honored as the primary guest, while the other six accompany him. The order follows their historical appearance, and each represents a specific spiritual quality or *sefirah* (divine attribute) in kabbalistic thought:

### Night 1: Abraham (אברהם — Avraham)
**Quality:** *Chesed* (Loving-kindness, 慈爱)

Abraham, the first Jew, who lived approximately four thousand years ago during China's Shang Dynasty period (商朝, ~1600-1046 BCE), embodies boundless kindness and hospitality. He is famous for sitting at the entrance of his tent even while recovering from circumcision, eagerly waiting to welcome strangers. His tent famously had four entrances — open to travelers from every direction.

On the first night, we invoke Abraham's spirit of generosity. Many families use this as an opportunity to invite guests — especially the poor, the stranger, or someone spending the holiday alone — echoing Abraham's legendary hospitality.

### Night 2: Isaac (יצחק — Yitzchak)
**Quality:** *Gevurah* (Strength, discipline, 严谨)

Isaac represents inner strength and self-restraint. Where Abraham reached outward with kindness, Isaac turned inward with discipline. He is known for re-digging his father's wells (Genesis 26), an act of quiet persistence and honoring tradition. He allowed himself to be bound on the altar in the ultimate test of faith.

Isaac teaches us that spiritual life requires boundaries, focus, and sometimes saying "no" to the world to protect what is sacred.

### Night 3: Jacob (יעקב — Yaakov)
**Quality:** *Tiferet* (Beauty, harmony, balance, 和谐)

Jacob, who later became Israel and gave his name to the Jewish people, synthesizes the qualities of his grandfather Abraham (kindness) and his father Isaac (strength). He was both a scholar who "sat in tents" studying and a practical man who worked as a shepherd for twenty years. His life was defined by struggle — wrestling with angels, managing a complicated family, surviving exile and return.

Jacob represents the beauty of balance: integrating opposites, finding harmony between the spiritual and material, between justice and mercy.

### Night 4: Moses (משה — Moshe)
**Quality:** *Netzach* (Endurance, eternity, victory, 永恒)

Moses, the greatest prophet in Jewish tradition who lived around 1300 BCE (late Shang Dynasty), led the Jewish people out of Egyptian slavery and received the Torah at Mount Sinai. He embodies *netzach* — endurance, the ability to persist despite overwhelming obstacles. For forty years he led a frequently rebellious nation through the desert, never giving up on them despite constant complaints and challenges.

Moses teaches us that leadership means endurance, that truth is eternal, and that even when we ourselves don't reach the promised land, our efforts shape the future.

### Night 5: Aaron (אהרן — Aharon)
**Quality:** *Hod* (Splendor, humility, gratitude, 谦逊)

Aaron, Moses's older brother and the first High Priest, represents humility and the ability to make peace. The Talmud says, "Be a student of Aaron: love peace and pursue peace, love people and bring them closer to Torah" (Pirkei Avot 1:12). Unlike his brother Moses who spoke truth bluntly, Aaron was known for his gentle diplomacy — walking between feuding parties, telling each that the other regretted the conflict, until they reconciled.

Aaron teaches that splendor lies not in dominance but in humility, in serving others, in bringing harmony to the world.

### Night 6: Joseph (יוסף — Yosef)
**Quality:** *Yesod* (Foundation, connection, righteousness, 根基)

Joseph, Jacob's beloved son, was sold into slavery by his brothers, rose to become viceroy of Egypt (during what corresponds to Egypt's Middle Kingdom period), and ultimately saved his family from famine through forgiveness. He is called *Yosef HaTzaddik* — "Joseph the Righteous" — particularly for his moral courage in resisting the advances of Potiphar's wife despite being a young man far from home.

Joseph represents the *yesod*, the foundation of moral integrity that holds up the entire structure of a life, and the power of connection — reconnecting estranged brothers, connecting Egypt's resources with Canaan's need, connecting dreams with reality.

### Night 7: David (דוד — David)
**Quality:** *Malchut* (Kingship, sovereignty, 王权)

King David, who lived around 1000 BCE (early Western Zhou Dynasty, 西周), was the shepherd boy who became Israel's greatest king. He unified the twelve tribes, established Jerusalem as the capital, and composed many of the Psalms. Despite his flaws — and the Bible does not hide them — David represents authentic kingship: leadership rooted in humility, vulnerability, and constant turning toward God.

David teaches that true sovereignty means recognizing that all power ultimately comes from above, that a leader must be a servant, and that even a king must constantly do *teshuvah* (repentance).

## The Kabbalistic Origins

The *Ushpizin* tradition originates in the *Zohar*, the foundational text of Jewish mysticism written (or according to tradition, revealed) in 13th-century Spain. The Zohar explains that during Sukkot, the "shade of faith" (*tzila d'mehemnuta* in Aramaic) descends upon the sukkah, and these seven righteous shepherds of Israel come to dwell there.

According to the *Arizal* (Rabbi Isaac Luria, the great 16th-century kabbalist), each of the seven days of Sukkot corresponds to one of the seven lower *sefirot* — divine emanations through which God's infinite light flows into the created world. Each patriarch embodies one of these qualities in perfection:

- Abraham: *Chesed* (loving-kindness)
- Isaac: *Gevurah* (strength)
- Jacob: *Tiferet* (beauty)
- Moses: *Netzach* (endurance)
- Aaron: *Hod* (splendor)
- Joseph: *Yesod* (foundation)
- David: *Malchut* (kingship)

When we welcome the *Ushpizin*, we're not just honoring historical figures — we're inviting these divine attributes into our lives. We're asking: How can *I* embody Abraham's kindness today? How can *I* access Isaac's inner strength? The sukkah becomes a classroom where the greatest teachers in Jewish history offer us their spiritual gifts.

## How the Ushpizin Are Welcomed

### The Traditional Formula

Each night, before or after the evening meal in the sukkah, a special *Ushpizin* invitation is recited. Though customs vary, a common version in Aramaic goes like this:

**"Enter, exalted holy guests, enter!  
Enter, fathers of the world, enter!  
Sit, exalted holy guests, sit!  
Sit, fathers of faith, sit in the shade of the Holy One, blessed be He!"**

Then the specific guest of the night is addressed:

**"I invite to my meal the exalted guest, Abraham (or Isaac, Jacob, etc.), and with him Isaac, Jacob, Moses, Aaron, Joseph, and David."**

Many families have a custom to symbolically set an empty chair or place at the table for the *Ushpizin*. Some even prepare a decorative *Ushpizin* poster showing all seven figures, which they display prominently in the sukkah.

### Modern Additions: The Ushpizata (Female Guests)

In recent decades, many Jewish communities — particularly in egalitarian and feminist circles — have added a parallel tradition: *Ushpizata* (Aramaic feminine form of guests). Just as we invite the seven patriarchs, we also invite seven great women of Jewish history:

1. **Sarah** — Abraham's wife, the first matriarch
2. **Rebecca (Rivka)** — Isaac's wife, known for her kindness
3. **Rachel and Leah** — Jacob's wives, mothers of the tribes
4. **Miriam** — Moses's sister, prophet and leader
5. **Deborah (Devorah)** — Judge and military leader
6. **Ruth** — Convert, great-grandmother of King David
7. **Esther** — Queen who saved the Jewish people

This beautiful expansion recognizes that the spiritual legacy of the Jewish people comes through both men and women, and that qualities like wisdom, courage, faith, and leadership are not gendered.

## The Connection to Hospitality

The *Ushpizin* tradition places a special emphasis on inviting *actual living guests* into your sukkah, particularly those who are poor, alone, or strangers. This is rooted in a teaching from the Zohar: **when the seven holy guests come to the sukkah, they look around to see if other guests are present. If they find that the owner of the sukkah has invited the poor and needy, the** ***Ushpizin*** **rejoice and bestow blessings. But if no human guests are present, the spiritual guests depart, saying, "We do not sit at a table where no charity is given."**

This teaching is profound: the presence of the righteous ancestors is conditional on our treatment of living people in need. Mysticism here doesn't float above practical ethics — it *demands* it. You want to experience the spiritual energy of Abraham? Then practice his hospitality. You want King David's blessing? Then care for the vulnerable as he instructed in his Psalms.

In this way, the *Ushpizin* tradition bridges heaven and earth, mysticism and action, ancient souls and present need.

## Parallels to Chinese Ancestor Veneration

For readers from Chinese cultural backgrounds, the *Ushpizin* tradition may recall aspects of ancestor veneration practices. Just as families honor their ancestors during festivals like Qingming (清明节) or Zhongyuan (中元节), Jews symbolically welcome the souls of the righteous into the sukkah during Sukkot.

Both traditions rest on similar intuitions:
- **The boundary between the living and the dead is permeable**. The departed are not gone but present in another form.
- **Rituals create connections across time**. Through specific words, foods, and practices, we bridge generations.
- **Honoring the past shapes the present**. Remembering and welcoming our ancestors isn't nostalgia — it's a way of accessing their wisdom and virtue.

However, there's a key difference: Jewish tradition generally discourages worship or petitioning of the dead (that's reserved for God alone). The *Ushpizin* aren't prayed *to* but welcomed *with* — they are esteemed guests, not deities. We don't ask them for favors; we ask to learn from their example, to embody their qualities, to be inspired by their lives.

## Practical Application: Bringing the Ushpizin to Life

Even if you don't build a sukkah (perhaps you live in an apartment with no balcony, or your climate makes outdoor structures impractical), the *Ushpizin* concept offers powerful spiritual practices:

### 1. The Seven-Day Self-Reflection
Each day of Sukkot, focus on one quality:
- **Day 1 (Abraham):** Practice radical generosity. Give without expecting return.
- **Day 2 (Isaac):** Exercise self-discipline. Say "no" to something that tempts you away from your values.
- **Day 3 (Jacob):** Seek balance. Find harmony between competing priorities.
- **Day 4 (Moses):** Persist through difficulty. Don't give up on a hard task.
- **Day 5 (Aaron):** Make peace. Reach out to someone you've had conflict with.
- **Day 6 (Joseph):** Act with integrity. Do the right thing even when it costs you.
- **Day 7 (David):** Lead with humility. Influence others by serving them.

### 2. Study Their Stories
Read the biblical narratives of each figure. For Chinese speakers, consider reading a Chinese-Hebrew bilingual Bible or using Sefaria.org's Chinese translations. Ask: What was this person's greatest test? What was their character flaw? How did God work through them despite their imperfections?

### 3. Invite Real Guests
The core of *Ushpizin* is hospitality. During Sukkot (or any time), make a point of inviting someone into your home — especially someone who might be lonely, far from family, or in need. Set an extra place at your table. Make it beautiful. Welcome them with warmth. In doing so, you embody Abraham's tent with four open doors.

### 4. Create Your Own "Ushpizin"
Who are the spiritual ancestors *you* want to learn from? Perhaps they're:
- Great teachers who shaped your thinking
- Family members who exemplified certain virtues
- Historical figures whose lives inspire you

Write their names. Each week, focus on one. Ask: What quality did this person embody? How can I cultivate that quality in my life this week?

## Why This Matters

The *Ushpizin* tradition reminds us that we are not isolated individuals. We are links in a long chain of people who struggled, loved, failed, repented, and tried again. The challenges we face — moral dilemmas, relational conflicts, the tension between ideals and reality — are not new. The great men and women who came before us navigated the same territory. Their wisdom is available to us.

More radically, the tradition suggests that *holiness is accessible*. The sukkah — a temporary hut made of branches — becomes a space where the greatest souls in history dwell. You don't need a temple or a palace. You need only a fragile shelter, a meal shared with others, and an open heart.

In a world that often feels spiritually empty, where the sacred seems distant or irrelevant, the *Ushpizin* offer an alternative vision: **the holy is not far off. It can sit right next to you at dinner. It arrives when you open your home, your heart, and your life to both the living and the legacy of those who walked before.**

## Key Takeaways

- **The** ***Ushpizin*** **are seven biblical figures symbolically invited into the sukkah each night of Sukkot:** Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, Moses, Aaron, Joseph, and David.

- **Each represents a specific spiritual quality** (*sefirah*) in Jewish mystical thought, offering a different lesson for how to live.

- **The tradition originates in the** ***Zohar*** and emphasizes that spiritual guests only remain if living guests — especially the poor — are also welcomed.

- **Modern practice has expanded to include** ***Ushpizata*** — seven great women of Jewish history — recognizing the full heritage of Jewish spiritual leadership.

- **The teaching is both mystical and ethical:** welcoming the spiritual ancestors is inseparable from practicing hospitality toward real people in need.

- **For learners from Chinese backgrounds,** the practice may resonate with ancestor veneration traditions, while maintaining the Jewish distinction of honoring (not worshiping) the righteous dead.
