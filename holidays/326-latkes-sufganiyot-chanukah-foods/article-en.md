## Introduction

Walk into any Jewish home during the eight nights of Chanukah — which typically falls in late November or December — and your nose will tell you immediately: something is frying. The sizzle of oil in a pan is as much a part of the holiday as the lighting of the menorah. But *what* exactly is frying varies dramatically depending on where in the world you're standing.

In Israel and much of the Jewish world today, the answer is *sufganiyot* (סופגניות) — round jelly doughnuts dusted with powdered sugar. In Eastern European and American Jewish homes, it's *latkes* (לאַטקעס) — crispy potato pancakes served with applesauce or sour cream. And increasingly, in Jewish homes across Asia, there's a delicious third option that bridges cultures: scallion pancakes, those crispy Chinese street-food favorites that happen to fit perfectly into the Chanukah oil-frying tradition.

This article explores the halachic (Jewish legal) reason behind eating fried foods on Chanukah, provides authentic recipes for both latkes and sufganiyot, and shows how the beautiful Chinese tradition of *cong you bing* (葱油饼, scallion pancakes) can become part of your family's Chanukah celebration.

## The Halachic Reason: Why Oil?

### The Miracle of the Oil

To understand why we eat fried foods on Chanukah, we need to understand what the holiday commemorates. The story takes place approximately 2,200 years ago — during what corresponds to China's Western Han Dynasty (西汉, 206 BCE - 9 CE) — when the Jewish people, led by the Maccabees, successfully revolted against the Greek-Syrian Empire that had occupied the Land of Israel and attempted to suppress Jewish religious practice.

After recapturing the Holy Temple in Jerusalem, the Maccabees wanted to relight the *menorah* (the seven-branched candelabrum that stood in the Temple). According to tradition, they found only one small flask of pure olive oil, enough to burn for just one day. Miraculously, that oil burned for eight days — exactly the time needed to prepare new pure oil.

### Publicizing the Miracle

The rabbis established Chanukah as an eight-day festival to commemorate this miracle. One of the core customs is *pirsumei nisa* (פרסומי ניסא) — "publicizing the miracle." We light the menorah in a window or doorway where passersby can see it, declaring that God performed wonders for our ancestors.

Eating foods fried in oil serves this same purpose. Every bite of a crispy latke or a sugar-dusted sufganiyah is a reminder of the miracle. It's a way of incorporating the miracle into our bodies, making it tangible and delicious.

### Not Just Any Food — Specifically Oil

The Talmud and later halachic authorities emphasize that the custom is specifically to eat foods *fried in oil*, not merely foods containing oil. This is why we don't just drizzle olive oil on salad and call it a Chanukah custom. The act of frying — immersing food in hot oil until it becomes golden and crispy — mirrors the way the oil in the Temple lamp surrounded the wick and produced light.

Different Jewish communities developed different specialties based on what ingredients were locally available, but the common thread is always oil.

## Latkes: The Eastern European Classic

### What Are Latkes?

*Latkes* is the Yiddish word for potato pancakes. The Hebrew term is *levivot* (לביבות). These are simple, humble foods: grated potatoes mixed with egg and a bit of flour or matzo meal, formed into patties, and fried until golden and crispy on both sides.

Latkes became the Chanukah food of choice among Ashkenazi Jews (those from Eastern and Central Europe) because potatoes were cheap, plentiful, and kept well through the winter. The custom of eating them on Chanukah became widespread in the 19th and early 20th centuries.

### Cultural Parallel: The Peasant Food Connection

There's a beautiful parallel here to Chinese culinary history. Just as latkes were originally peasant food — using the most basic, affordable ingredients to create something delicious — many beloved Chinese dishes like *jiaozi* (饺子, dumplings) or *youtiao* (油条, fried dough sticks) originated as simple foods for common people that became treasured traditions.

Jewish tradition teaches *histapkut* (הסתפקות) — the value of being satisfied with little, of transforming simple ingredients into something meaningful. The potato latke embodies this perfectly.

### Classic Latke Recipe

**Ingredients:**
- 4 large potatoes (about 2 pounds / 900g)
- 1 medium onion
- 2 eggs, beaten
- 3-4 tablespoons all-purpose flour or matzo meal
- 1 teaspoon salt
- ½ teaspoon black pepper
- Vegetable oil for frying (enough to fill your pan about ½ inch / 1 cm deep)

**Instructions:**

1. **Grate**: Peel the potatoes and onion. Grate them using the large holes of a box grater or the grating blade of a food processor.

2. **Drain**: This is crucial! Place the grated potato and onion mixture in a clean kitchen towel or several layers of cheesecloth. Twist and squeeze firmly over the sink to remove as much liquid as possible. The drier your mixture, the crispier your latkes.

3. **Mix**: In a large bowl, combine the squeezed potato-onion mixture with the beaten eggs, flour, salt, and pepper. Mix until just combined.

4. **Heat oil**: Pour oil into a large, heavy skillet (cast iron works beautifully) to a depth of about ½ inch (1 cm). Heat over medium-high until the oil shimmers — test by dropping a tiny bit of the mixture in; it should sizzle immediately.

5. **Fry**: Using a large spoon or your hands, form patties about 3 inches (8 cm) wide and ½ inch (1 cm) thick. Carefully slide them into the hot oil. Don't crowd the pan. Fry for 3-4 minutes per side until deep golden brown.

6. **Drain**: Transfer to a plate lined with paper towels to drain excess oil.

7. **Serve**: Traditionally served hot with applesauce and/or sour cream on the side.

**Yield:** About 12-15 latkes

**Pro tip:** Latkes are best eaten immediately, but if you need to prepare ahead, you can keep them warm in a 200°F (95°C) oven on a wire rack.

## Sufganiyot: The Israeli Favorite

### What Are Sufganiyot?

*Sufganiyot* (singular: *sufganiyah*) are round, puffy doughnuts, deep-fried until golden, filled with jam (traditionally strawberry or raspberry), and dusted generously with powdered sugar. They are the quintessential Chanukah food in modern Israel.

While the origins of sufganiyot trace back to European Jewish communities (similar to the *krapfen* of German-speaking countries or the *paczki* of Poland), they became wildly popular in Israel starting in the 1920s-1930s, when the Histadrut (Israel's labor federation) promoted them as the official Chanukah food.

Today, Israeli bakeries compete to create the most creative sufganiyot flavors: chocolate, halva, dulce de leche, pistachio cream, and even savory versions. But the classic jelly-filled version remains beloved.

### Why Doughnuts Became Popular

Unlike latkes, which are typically made at home, sufganiyot are usually purchased from bakeries. In modern Israel, where many people live in apartments without extensive cooking facilities, buying sufganiyot became more practical than frying latkes at home. The tradition caught on and became a cultural phenomenon.

There's also something joyful and festive about a doughnut — it's a treat, something special, not everyday food. This fits the spirit of Chanukah as a time of celebration and light.

### Classic Sufganiyot Recipe

**Ingredients:**

*For the dough:*
- 3½ cups (440g) all-purpose flour
- ¼ cup (50g) sugar
- 2¼ teaspoons (1 packet) active dry yeast
- ¾ cup (180ml) warm milk (about 110°F/43°C)
- 2 eggs, room temperature
- ¼ cup (60g) butter, softened
- ½ teaspoon salt
- Zest of 1 lemon (optional)

*For frying and filling:*
- Vegetable oil for deep frying
- 1 cup strawberry or raspberry jam
- Powdered sugar for dusting

**Instructions:**

1. **Activate yeast**: In a small bowl, combine warm milk with 1 teaspoon of the sugar and the yeast. Let stand 5-10 minutes until foamy.

2. **Make dough**: In a large bowl, combine flour, remaining sugar, salt, and lemon zest. Make a well in the center and add the yeast mixture, eggs, and softened butter. Mix until a soft dough forms.

3. **Knead**: Turn out onto a floured surface and knead for 8-10 minutes until smooth and elastic. (You can also use a stand mixer with a dough hook.)

4. **First rise**: Place in a greased bowl, cover with a damp towel, and let rise in a warm place for 1-2 hours until doubled.

5. **Shape**: Roll dough out to about ½ inch (1 cm) thickness. Cut circles using a 2½-3 inch (6-8 cm) round cutter. Place on parchment paper.

6. **Second rise**: Cover and let rise another 30-45 minutes until puffy.

7. **Heat oil**: Pour oil into a deep pot or wok to a depth of at least 3 inches (8 cm). Heat to 350°F (175°C) — use a thermometer for best results.

8. **Fry**: Carefully slide 2-3 doughnuts at a time into the hot oil. Fry for 2-3 minutes per side until golden brown. Don't overcrowd.

9. **Drain**: Remove with a slotted spoon and drain on paper towels.

10. **Fill**: Once cooled, use a piping bag with a long tip to inject jam into the side of each doughnut.

11. **Dust**: Generously dust with powdered sugar just before serving.

**Yield:** About 15-18 sufganiyot

**Pro tip:** The oil temperature is critical. Too hot and the outside burns before the inside cooks; too cool and they absorb too much oil and become greasy.

## The Chinese Twist: Scallion Pancakes for Chanukah

### Cong You Bing: A Natural Fit

Here's where things get deliciously cross-cultural. *Cong you bing* (葱油饼), or scallion pancakes, are a beloved Chinese street food — crispy, layered flatbreads flavored with scallions and fried in oil until golden.

For Jews living in China or those with an appreciation for Chinese cuisine, scallion pancakes are a perfect Chanukah food. They meet the halachic requirement (fried in oil), they're delicious, and they create a beautiful bridge between Jewish and Chinese culinary traditions.

### Historical Parallel

It's worth noting that Chinese-Jewish culinary fusion has deep roots. The Jewish community of Kaifeng (开封) in central China, which dates back to the Song Dynasty (宋朝, 960-1279 CE), developed their own unique customs that blended Jewish law with Chinese culture. While we don't have specific records of their Chanukah observances, the principle of adapting local foods to Jewish requirements has always been part of Jewish life in diaspora.

### Scallion Pancake Recipe (Chanukah Style)

**Ingredients:**

*For the dough:*
- 2 cups (250g) all-purpose flour
- ¾ cup (180ml) boiling water
- ¼ teaspoon salt

*For the filling:*
- 4-5 scallions (green onions), finely chopped
- 3 tablespoons sesame oil
- 1 teaspoon salt
- ½ teaspoon white pepper (optional)

*For frying:*
- Vegetable oil (about ¼ cup per pancake)

**Instructions:**

1. **Make dough**: In a large bowl, combine flour and salt. Pour in boiling water and stir with chopsticks or a fork until it starts to come together. Once cool enough to handle, knead into a smooth dough (about 5 minutes). Cover and rest 30 minutes.

2. **Prepare filling**: Mix chopped scallions with sesame oil, salt, and pepper.

3. **Roll and fill**: Divide dough into 4 equal pieces. Roll each piece into a thin circle about 8-10 inches (20-25 cm) across. Brush the surface with sesame oil and sprinkle with scallion mixture.

4. **Form spiral**: Starting from one edge, roll the dough into a tight cylinder, like a scroll. Then coil the cylinder into a spiral, tucking the end underneath. Flatten slightly with your palm and roll out gently to about ½ inch (1 cm) thick.

5. **Fry**: Heat ¼ cup oil in a flat pan over medium heat. Carefully place one pancake in the pan. Fry 3-4 minutes per side until golden and crispy. Add more oil as needed.

6. **Serve**: Cut into wedges and serve hot. Optionally serve with soy sauce for dipping (though traditionally latkes are served with applesauce or sour cream, you might enjoy the scallion pancakes with a light soy-vinegar dipping sauce).

**Yield:** 4 large pancakes

### Making It a Family Tradition

Consider starting a new tradition in your home: on one night of Chanukah, make latkes; on another, try sufganiyot; and on a third, celebrate with scallion pancakes. Explain to your children or guests that all three foods honor the same miracle — the oil that burned for eight days — while also celebrating the diversity of the Jewish people scattered across the world.

You might even create a "Chanukah around the world" night, serving all three alongside foods from other Jewish communities: *bimuelos* (Sephardi fried dough balls), *zalabiya* (Middle Eastern fried fritters), or *gulab jamun* (eaten by some Indian Jews).

## The Deeper Meaning: Food as Memory

### Taste and Torah

Jewish tradition places profound importance on food. We don't just eat to nourish our bodies; we eat to remember, to celebrate, to connect.

The Talmud teaches that taste is one of the most powerful triggers for memory. When we eat latkes, we're not just enjoying potatoes; we're tasting the perseverance of our ancestors who kept Jewish tradition alive even in poverty. When we bite into a sufganiyah, we're tasting the sweetness of victory and the light that refused to go out. When we make scallion pancakes for Chanukah, we're affirming that Jewish tradition is alive and creative, able to embrace the best of every culture while remaining distinctly itself.

### The Question of "Authentic" vs. "Appropriate"

Some people might ask: "Is it really appropriate to eat Chinese scallion pancakes on Chanukah? Isn't that not authentic?"

Here's the thing: latkes only became widespread about 150 years ago. Sufganiyot became popular in Israel less than 100 years ago. Jewish tradition has always adapted local foods to Jewish practice. What matters is the *principle* — fried in oil to remember the miracle — not the specific form.

As long as you're eating something fried in oil and you're remembering the miracle while doing so, you're fulfilling the custom. The rest is cultural preference.

## Practical Tips for Chanukah Frying

### Safety First

Frying involves hot oil, which can be dangerous. Always:
- Use a deep, heavy pot with high sides
- Never fill the pot more than halfway with oil
- Keep a lid nearby to smother any flames (never throw water on a grease fire!)
- Keep children at a safe distance
- Never leave hot oil unattended

### Oil Selection

Any neutral-flavored oil with a high smoke point works well:
- **Vegetable oil** (most common, affordable)
- **Canola oil** (lighter flavor)
- **Peanut oil** (great for high-heat frying, but check for allergies)
- **Sunflower oil** (popular in Israel)

For scallion pancakes, some cooks use a mix of vegetable oil and sesame oil for extra flavor.

### Reusing Oil

If you're making multiple batches (which you probably are on Chanukah!), you can strain and reuse frying oil 2-3 times. After frying, let the oil cool completely, strain through a fine-mesh sieve or cheesecloth to remove any particles, and store in a sealed container. Discard if it smells rancid or looks dark.

### Keeping Things Kosher

If you keep a kosher kitchen:
- Latkes and scallion pancakes can be served with either dairy (sour cream) or meat meals
- Sufganiyot are *pareve* (neutral) unless you use milk in the dough; use plant-based milk or water instead if you want to serve them after a meat meal
- Use separate utensils and pans for dairy and meat preparations

## Conclusion: The Unity of Light and Oil

At its heart, Chanukah is about one thing: light. The Maccabees fought for the right to live as Jews, to light the menorah, to keep the light of Torah alive. The oil miracle reminds us that sometimes, when things look darkest, when it seems we only have resources for one day, God provides what we need to keep going.

Every food we fry in oil — whether it's a potato latke from Poland, a jelly doughnut from Israel, or a scallion pancake from China — is a small act of defiance against darkness. It's a declaration that the light still burns, that Jewish tradition is alive and vital, that miracles still happen.

So this Chanukah, gather your family around the kitchen. Heat up the oil. Fry something delicious. Tell the story of the Maccabees. Light the menorah. Eat, celebrate, and remember: we are the people who refused to let the light go out.

*Chag Chanukah sameach* — Happy Chanukah!

## Key Takeaways

- **Fried foods on Chanukah** are not just tradition but a halachic custom rooted in *pirsumei nisa* — publicizing the miracle of the oil.
- **Latkes** (potato pancakes) represent the Ashkenazi tradition, emphasizing simplicity and resourcefulness.
- **Sufganiyot** (jelly doughnuts) became the Israeli favorite, bringing sweetness and celebration to the holiday.
- **Scallion pancakes** offer a beautiful example of adapting local cuisine to Jewish practice, perfect for Jewish families in Asia.
- **The miracle** of Chanukah occurred during China's Western Han Dynasty, about 2,200 years ago.
- **What matters most** is not the specific food but the oil, the memory, and the light.
