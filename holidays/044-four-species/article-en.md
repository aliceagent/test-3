## Introduction

Imagine holding in your hands four plants that together represent the entire Jewish people — from the most learned scholar to someone just beginning their journey. This is the mitzvah of the Four Species (Arba Minim in Hebrew, ארבעת המינים), one of the most beautiful and symbolic commandments of Sukkot, the Festival of Booths.

For someone new to Judaism, especially coming from a Chinese cultural background where symbolism in nature is deeply appreciated — think of the "Four Gentlemen" (plum, orchid, bamboo, and chrysanthemum 梅蘭竹菊) in traditional Chinese painting — the Four Species will feel both foreign and strangely familiar. Like those plants representing noble character traits in Chinese art, these four plants carry profound meaning about community, unity, and our relationship with the Divine.

This mitzvah is commanded directly in the Torah, in Leviticus 23:40, where God instructs: "On the first day you shall take the product of hadar trees, branches of palm trees, boughs of leafy trees, and willows of the brook, and you shall rejoice before the Eternal your God seven days." These four species are taken and waved together each day of Sukkot (except Shabbat) in a ceremony that is both physically tangible and spiritually transcendent.

## Main Content

### What Are the Four Species?

The Four Species consist of:

1. **Etrog (אתרוג)** — The citron, a fragrant lemon-like fruit with a thick, bumpy rind
2. **Lulav (לולב)** — The palm branch, long and straight with tightly bound leaves
3. **Hadassim (הדסים)** — Myrtle branches, characterized by leaves growing in clusters of three
4. **Aravot (ערבות)** — Willow branches, with oblong leaves and smooth edges

These are bundled together (except the etrog, which is held separately) and waved in six directions during prayers, creating a stunning ritual that engages all the senses: the visual beauty of the plants, the texture of the palm fronds, and the sweet citrus fragrance of the etrog.

### The Deep Symbolism: Four Types of Jews

The Talmud (Vayikra Rabbah 30:12) teaches that each of the four species represents a different type of Jew, based on two qualities: taste (representing Torah study) and fragrance (representing good deeds/mitzvot):

- **Etrog** — Has both taste and fragrance. Represents Jews who possess both Torah knowledge and good deeds.
- **Lulav (palm)** — Has taste (dates are sweet) but no fragrance. Represents those who study Torah but lack good deeds.
- **Hadas (myrtle)** — Has fragrance but no taste. Represents those with good deeds but little Torah study.
- **Arava (willow)** — Has neither taste nor fragrance. Represents those who lack both Torah and good deeds.

The revolutionary teaching? **All four must be held together**. The mitzvah cannot be fulfilled with just three, or just the "best" one. Every type of Jew — regardless of their level of observance or knowledge — is essential to the community. When we bind them together and hold them before God, we demonstrate that Jewish unity transcends differences.

This is particularly relevant for someone considering conversion or just beginning to explore Judaism. You might feel like the "willow" — lacking knowledge and unsure of how to do mitzvot. But the Torah itself declares: you are part of the bundle. Without you, the mitzvah is incomplete.

### Historical Context: From Ancient Israel to Today

The mitzvah of the Four Species dates back over 3,300 years to the time of Moses — during what corresponds to China's late Shang Dynasty (商朝晚期, ~1300-1046 BCE). When the Israelites wandered in the desert after the Exodus from Egypt, they celebrated Sukkot and took these four plants as God commanded.

In the Holy Temple in Jerusalem, which stood during the First and Second Temple periods (roughly corresponding to China's Spring and Autumn period through the Han Dynasty 春秋時期至漢朝, ~957 BCE - 70 CE), the Four Species were central to Sukkot celebrations. Every morning, worshippers would circle the altar while holding their lulav and etrog, singing Hoshanot (prayers for salvation). On the seventh day of Sukkot (Hoshana Rabbah), they would circle seven times — a practice that continues in synagogues today.

After the Temple's destruction in 70 CE, the rabbis extended the mitzvah from just the first day in the Temple to all seven days of Sukkot in the Diaspora, ensuring this beautiful tradition would never be forgotten.

### How to Choose Your Four Species

**Selecting an Etrog:**
The etrog is the "star" of the show — it must be beautiful (hadar in Hebrew means beauty) and unblemished. Look for:
- A bumpy, textured peel (not smooth like a lemon)
- Bright yellow color (though greenish is acceptable)
- The pitom (small stem on top) should ideally be intact, though some etrogs naturally grow without it
- No brown spots, cuts, or significant blemishes
- Shape can be round, oblong, or slightly irregular — there are different traditions

**Selecting a Lulav:**
- The spine should be straight, not bent
- The leaves should be closed and tightly bound along the spine
- The center leaf (tiyomet) must not be split more than halfway
- Fresher is better — the leaves should be green, not dry or brown
- Minimum length: about 4 tefachim (approximately 12-16 inches or 30-40 cm)

**Selecting Hadassim (3 branches minimum):**
- Leaves must grow in clusters of three from the same point on the stem (meshulash pattern)
- Leaves should cover most of the stem length
- Fresh, green leaves (not dry or brown)
- Minimum length: 3 tefachim (about 9-12 inches or 23-30 cm)

**Selecting Aravot (2 branches minimum):**
- Oblong leaves with smooth edges (not serrated)
- Leaves should be fresh and green
- The stem should be reddish (not green, which indicates a different willow species)
- Minimum length: 3 tefachim (about 9-12 inches or 23-30 cm)

**Pro tip for those in Asia:** If you live in China or another country without a Jewish community nearby, you can order Four Species sets from Israel or Jewish suppliers who ship internationally. Contact your nearest Chabad house — they often organize bulk orders for expats. The sets usually arrive a week or two before Sukkot. While prices can be high due to import costs, remember: this is a once-a-year mitzvah, and beautifying it (hiddur mitzvah) is considered very meritorious.

### How to Assemble and Use Them

**Assembly:**
Your set will likely come pre-assembled or with instructions. Typically:
1. The lulav (palm) is in the center
2. Two willow branches go on the left side of the lulav
3. Three myrtle branches go on the right side
4. They are bound together with strips made from palm leaves
5. The etrog is kept separate in its own box

**The Blessing and Ritual:**
On the first day of Sukkot (or your first time performing the mitzvah), you recite two blessings. On subsequent days, only the first blessing:

**First blessing (every day):**
*Baruch atah Adonai, Eloheinu melech ha-olam, asher kid'shanu b'mitzvotav v'tzivanu al netilat lulav.*
"Blessed are You, Lord our God, King of the Universe, who has sanctified us with His commandments and commanded us concerning the taking of the lulav."

**Second blessing (first day only):**
*Baruch atah Adonai, Eloheinu melech ha-olam, shehecheyanu v'kiy'manu v'higianu lazman hazeh.*
"Blessed are You, Lord our God, King of the Universe, who has granted us life, sustained us, and enabled us to reach this occasion."

**How to hold them:**
1. Before the blessing: Hold the lulav bundle (with myrtle and willow) in your right hand, with the spine facing you. Hold the etrog in your left hand with the pitom (stem) pointing DOWN.
2. Say the blessing(s).
3. After the blessing: Turn the etrog upright so the pitom faces UP. Bring both hands together so the etrog touches the lulav bundle.
4. Wave them together in six directions: east, south, west, north, up, and down. (Some customs do the directions in a different order.)
5. The waving motion: Shake gently three times in each direction, bringing them back to your chest between each direction.

**When to perform it:**
- Every morning of Sukkot except Shabbat
- In the synagogue, this is done during Hallel (songs of praise) and during the Hoshanot circuit around the bimah (reading platform)
- At home, you can do it after morning prayers in your sukkah

### Cultural Connection: Unity in Diversity — A Universal Value

The Four Species echo a value deeply embedded in both Jewish and Chinese wisdom: unity requires diversity.

In Chinese philosophy, the concept of harmony (和 hé) doesn't mean uniformity — it means different elements coming together to create something greater. The ancient text *Guoyu* (國語) states: "和實生物,同則不繼" — "Harmony brings forth new things; sameness cannot continue." Just as a delicious dish requires different flavors working together, a strong community needs people of different strengths.

The Four Species embody this truth. The Torah could have commanded us to take four etrogs — four "perfect" Jews. Instead, it insists we take four different plants, including ones without fragrance or taste. The message: authentic Jewish community isn't about everyone being the same. It's about bringing our different gifts together.

This is especially meaningful for those converting to Judaism or new to observance. In traditional Chinese culture, fitting in and not standing out is often emphasized. But in Jewish community, your unique background — Chinese heritage, life experience, questions, struggles — is not something to hide. It's your contribution to the bundle. Without it, something would be missing.

### Practical Application: Bringing It Home

**If This Is Your First Sukkot:**
- Don't worry about getting the "perfect" set. Any kosher set fulfills the mitzvah.
- If cost is an issue, consider sharing a set with another family (you can each use it at different times of the day, though there are rules about borrowing on the first two days).
- Focus on the experience: holding these ancient symbols, connecting to millions of Jews worldwide doing the same thing, and feeling the joy of Sukkot.

**Living Far from a Jewish Community:**
- The Four Species can be your tangible connection to Jewish practice when you're isolated.
- Performing this mitzvah in your sukkah (or even just in your home if building a sukkah isn't possible) creates a powerful Jewish moment in an otherwise non-Jewish environment.
- Take photos, journal about it — mark this as significant in your spiritual journey.

**After Sukkot:**
- Some people save their etrog to make "etrog jam" or use it as besamim (spices) for Havdalah by studding it with cloves.
- The lulav and other branches are traditionally saved until Pesach, when they are burned with the chametz (leavened bread).
- Treat these items with respect even after the holiday — they were used for a mitzvah and retain a degree of holiness.

## Key Takeaways

- **The Four Species are four plants commanded in Leviticus 23:40:** etrog (citron), lulav (palm), hadas (myrtle), and arava (willow).
- **They symbolize Jewish unity:** Each represents a different type of Jew, and all must be held together to fulfill the mitzvah.
- **Choose carefully:** Look for an unblemished etrog, a straight lulav, properly structured myrtles (leaves in threes), and fresh willows.
- **The ritual:** After reciting blessings, wave all four species together in six directions, acknowledging God's presence everywhere.
- **Your uniqueness is essential:** Like the four species, Jewish community needs every type of person — scholars and beginners, the knowledgeable and the searching.
- **This mitzvah connects you to 3,300 years of Jewish history**, from the desert wanderings through today.
