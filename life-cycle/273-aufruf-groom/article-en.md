## Introduction

There's something magical about the week before a wedding. In Jewish tradition, this time isn't just about finalizing seating charts and confirming vendors—it's about publicly celebrating the groom's upcoming transition into married life. The **aufruf** (Yiddish: אויפרוף, literally "calling up") is a beautiful synagogue ceremony where the groom receives an aliyah—a special honor to be called up to the Torah—on the Shabbat before his wedding. What follows is unlike any other synagogue service: friends and family shower him with candy, sing special songs, and mark this sacred milestone with communal joy.

If you've ever attended a traditional Chinese wedding banquet where the entire community gathers to celebrate and honor the couple, the aufruf carries a similar spirit of collective participation in a life-changing moment. It transforms a regular Shabbat morning service into a profound celebration of love, commitment, and community blessing. Let's explore this ancient custom, its meaning, and how it's practiced today.

## What Exactly Is an Aufruf?

The aufruf is a pre-wedding synagogue honor where the groom (and sometimes the bride, depending on the community) is called up to the Torah for an aliyah during Shabbat morning services. The word "aufruf" comes from Yiddish and means "to call up," referring to the moment when someone's name is called to recite blessings over the Torah reading.

### The Basic Flow

Here's what typically happens during an aufruf:

1. **Regular Service**: The Shabbat morning service proceeds as usual until the Torah reading portion
2. **The Call-Up**: The groom's name is announced: "Ya'amod [Hebrew name] ben [father's Hebrew name], chatan!" (Let [name] son of [father's name], the groom, rise!)
3. **Torah Blessings**: The groom recites the blessings before and after a portion of the Torah is read
4. **Candy Throwing**: Immediately after the blessings, the congregation throws wrapped candies and sometimes nuts at the groom
5. **Special Mi Shebeirach**: The rabbi recites a blessing for the couple's future happiness
6. **Kiddush Celebration**: After services, the groom's family typically sponsors a kiddush (festive reception) with enhanced food and drinks

The entire ceremony takes only about 10-15 minutes, but its impact resonates throughout the community.

## The History and Origins

### Ancient Roots

The custom of honoring a groom before his wedding has roots in Talmudic times (approximately 200-500 CE, during China's Northern and Southern Dynasties period 南北朝, 420-589 CE). The Talmud discusses various ways communities would celebrate and support a groom, though the specific aufruf ceremony as we know it developed later in European Jewish communities.

The tradition became particularly established among Ashkenazi Jews (those of Central and Eastern European descent) during the medieval period (roughly 1000-1500 CE, corresponding to China's Song through Ming Dynasties 宋-明朝). Sephardic Jews (those of Spanish, North African, and Middle Eastern descent) developed similar but distinct customs.

### The Candy-Throwing Tradition

Why candy? The sweet treats symbolize several blessings:

- **Sweet Life**: May the couple's marriage be as sweet as these candies
- **Fertility**: The many small candies represent the hope for many children
- **Community Joy**: Everyone participates actively in blessing the couple
- **Playfulness**: Marriage should include joy, playfulness, and celebration

Some communities throw soft candy, others throw wrapped candies or even nuts (which also symbolize fertility and abundance). The playful "attack" creates a memorable, joyous moment that breaks the usual solemnity of Torah services.

## When Does the Aufruf Take Place?

### Standard Timing

The most common timing for an aufruf is:

**The Shabbat immediately before the wedding**

For example:
- Wedding on Tuesday → Aufruf on the previous Shabbat (3-4 days before)
- Wedding on Sunday → Aufruf on the previous Shabbat (1 day before)

### Alternative Timings

Some communities and families choose different timing based on circumstances:

1. **Two Shabbats Before**: If the groom's family lives far away or if many wedding guests will attend the aufruf, having it two weeks before gives everyone time to travel

2. **Shabbat After the Wedding**: Some communities, particularly certain Sephardic traditions, celebrate a similar ceremony on the Shabbat after the wedding, sometimes called "Shabbat Chatan" (Groom's Sabbath)

3. **Monday or Thursday**: In communities with frequent Torah readings, some hold the aufruf on a weekday when the Torah is read (Monday or Thursday morning), though this is less common

### Special Considerations

- If the wedding falls during a period when there are no Torah readings (like certain holidays), the aufruf might be moved to an earlier or later date
- Destination weddings often require flexibility in timing
- The couple's rabbi helps determine the most appropriate date

## Modern Variations: Including the Bride

### Traditional Practice

Traditionally, only the groom received an aufruf. This reflected historical gender roles where women did not receive aliyot (plural of aliyah) in Orthodox synagogues. The bride would often attend and sit in the women's section, enjoying the celebration but not participating directly in the Torah honors.

### Contemporary Adaptations

Today, many communities have evolved their practices:

1. **Orthodox Communities**: Generally maintain the traditional groom-only aufruf, but the bride is honored with special prayers, may address the congregation during the kiddush, or receives her own separate celebration

2. **Conservative and Reform Communities**: Frequently call both the bride and groom together for a joint aliyah, celebrating them as equal partners

3. **Separate Honors**: Some synagogues call the groom for one aliyah and the bride for another, giving each their own special moment

4. **Egalitarian Orthodox Communities**: In Modern Orthodox congregations that allow women's aliyot during special women's prayer services, some hold a parallel celebration for the bride

The specific practice depends entirely on the community's customs and the couple's personal preferences.

## What the Groom Does During the Aufruf

If you're the groom (or bride in egalitarian communities), here's what to expect:

### Preparation

- **Know Your Hebrew Name**: You'll be called up using your Hebrew name and your father's Hebrew name (e.g., "Moshe ben Avraham")
- **Review the Blessings**: Even if you've had a bar mitzvah, review the Torah blessings beforehand. The synagogue can provide a transliteration if needed
- **Dress Appropriately**: Wear nice Shabbat clothing; many grooms wear a new tallit (prayer shawl) for the first time at their aufruf
- **Don't Bring Your Phone**: Remember, it's Shabbat—leave electronics at home

### During the Ceremony

1. **Wait for Your Name**: Listen for the gabbai (synagogue sexton) to call your name
2. **Walk to the Bimah**: Walk up to the raised platform where the Torah is read
3. **Stand at the Right Side**: Position yourself to the right of the Torah reader
4. **Hold the Torah Handles**: When invited, touch the Torah with your tallit corner, then kiss the tallit
5. **Recite the First Blessing**: "Barchu et Adonai hamevorach..." (Bless the Lord who is blessed)
6. **Listen to the Reading**: A portion of that week's Torah reading is chanted
7. **Recite the Second Blessing**: Thank God for giving us the Torah
8. **Duck (Slightly)**: Prepare for the candy bombardment!
9. **Smile**: This is a celebration—enjoy it!

### Emotional Impact

Many grooms describe the aufruf as deeply moving. Standing before the community, wrapped in a tallit, with your parents and future in-laws watching, hearing your name connected to your father's in the ancient chain of tradition—it's a powerful moment of transition. One groom described it: "I realized I wasn't just getting married; I was joining the generations of Jewish men who stood at this same spot, at this same threshold."

## What Guests Should Know

### If You're Invited to an Aufruf

1. **Dress Code**: Synagogue-appropriate modest clothing (shoulders and knees covered for women, kippahs for men)

2. **Bring Candy**: Some synagogues provide candy, but it's thoughtful to bring your own small bag of wrapped kosher candies

3. **Throwing Etiquette**:
   - Wait until AFTER the final Torah blessing
   - Throw gently—celebration, not assault!
   - Aim high so candy showers down
   - Soft candies are preferable to hard ones

4. **Stay for Kiddush**: The family usually sponsors an enhanced kiddush (refreshments after services)—it's considered respectful to stay and celebrate

5. **Offer Mazal Tov**: Congratulate the groom and both families with a hearty "Mazal tov!" (Congratulations!)

### Synagogue Etiquette

- **Arrive on Time**: Shabbat morning services typically start between 9:00-10:00 AM
- **Turn Off Your Phone**: (Better yet, leave it at home—it's Shabbat)
- **Follow Along**: Use a siddur (prayer book); ask a neighbor for help if needed
- **Stand When Others Stand**: Follow the congregation's lead
- **Silence During Torah Reading**: The Torah reading requires complete attention and silence

## The Deeper Meaning

### Public Commitment

The aufruf isn't just a party—it's a profound public declaration. By standing before the Torah and community, the groom (and sometimes bride) is essentially saying:

> "I am about to create a new Jewish home. I stand before you, before God's Torah, and before our tradition, ready to take on this sacred responsibility. I ask for your blessings, your support, and your witness to this commitment."

### Community Responsibility

Just as the community blesses the groom, they also accept responsibility. By attending and celebrating, community members implicitly commit to supporting the new couple. In Jewish thought, **kol Yisrael areivim zeh lazeh** (כל ישראל ערבים זה לזה)—"All of Israel is responsible for one another." The aufruf makes this abstract principle tangible.

### Transition Moment

Jewish tradition marks life transitions with public ceremony. Just as a bar or bat mitzvah marks the transition to religious adulthood, the aufruf marks the transition to married life. Standing at the Torah—the center of Jewish spiritual life—at this threshold moment connects personal change to eternal values.

### Connection to Ancestors

There's something profound about standing where generations before you stood at similar life moments. The aufruf connects today's groom to his father, grandfather, and great-grandfather, all the way back through Jewish history. One participant described it: "When they called my name 'ben [son of] my father,' I felt the weight and beauty of the chain of generations."

## Regional Variations

### Ashkenazi Customs

- Aufruf typically on the Shabbat before the wedding
- Heavy candy throwing
- Often the groom's first time wearing a tallit
- Generally male-only, though modern variations exist
- May include special niggunim (melodies) or songs

### Sephardic Customs

- Often called "Shabbat Chatan" (Groom's Sabbath)
- May take place after the wedding instead of before
- Less emphasis on candy throwing
- Special piyutim (liturgical poems) may be recited
- Sometimes includes reading of the ketubah (marriage contract)

### Yemenite Customs

- Called "Henna celebration" when combined with pre-wedding festivities
- Often includes special songs and readings
- May be a multi-day celebration
- Strong emphasis on community participation

### Modern Israeli Customs

- Increasingly common to include the bride
- Often held at the couple's regular synagogue
- May incorporate contemporary music
- Sometimes combined with a bigger party or tisch (gathering)

## Practical Planning Tips

### For the Groom and Family

1. **Coordinate with the Synagogue**: Book the date well in advance and confirm details with the rabbi and gabbai

2. **Guest List Considerations**: 
   - Notify out-of-town guests about Shabbat restrictions (no driving, phones, etc.)
   - Consider providing nearby accommodations
   - Send invitations at least 3-4 weeks in advance

3. **Kiddush Planning**:
   - Work with the synagogue's caterer or kiddush committee
   - Typical kiddush includes: wine, challah, salads, fish, kugels, desserts
   - Budget varies widely; ask the synagogue for guidance

4. **Candy Logistics**:
   - Buy wrapped kosher candies (about 10-15 pounds for a medium-sized congregation)
   - Soft candies are preferable
   - Arrange for cleanup volunteers afterward

5. **Photography**:
   - Remember: no photography on Shabbat
   - Some families arrange for professional photos before or after services (outside Shabbat times)
   - Alternatively, designate someone to take mental "snapshots" and write down memories afterward

### For the Synagogue

- Announce the aufruf in advance so the community can attend
- Prepare the special Mi Shebeirach blessing
- Ensure cleanup supplies are available
- Consider providing candy if the family hasn't arranged it

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can you have an aufruf if you're getting married on Shabbat?**
A: Traditional Jewish weddings cannot take place on Shabbat (it's prohibited to write the ketubah or complete financial transactions). If someone is planning a Shabbat ceremony, they should consult with their rabbi about appropriate traditions.

**Q: What if the groom can't read Hebrew?**
A: No problem! The Torah blessings can be transliterated, or some communities will say the blessings quietly alongside the groom. The honor is in standing before the Torah and being called up, not in flawless pronunciation.

**Q: Do you tip the person who does the Torah reading?**
A: In most synagogues, no monetary transactions occur on Shabbat. However, it's appropriate to make a donation to the synagogue afterward or send a thank-you note.

**Q: What if the bride's family belongs to a different synagogue?**
A: Sometimes couples have two aufrufs—one in each family's synagogue. Alternatively, they choose one location and invite both communities.

**Q: Can you decline an aufruf?**
A: While it's traditional, it's not mandatory. Some couples prefer more private celebrations. Discuss with your family and rabbi.

**Q: What happens to all the candy afterward?**
A: Children typically scramble to collect it, or volunteers gather it to share. Some families donate the candy to local schools or organizations.

## The Aufruf as Blessing

Ultimately, the aufruf is about community blessing. In a tradition that values family, continuity, and communal responsibility, this ceremony embodies all three. The groom stands at the source of Jewish wisdom (the Torah), surrounded by the community that has shaped him, about to embark on the sacred task of building a Jewish home.

The candy raining down is more than sugar—it's physical prayers. Each piece represents a hope: for sweetness, for children, for joy, for a strong marriage. The community isn't just watching; they're actively participating in blessing this new union.

In an age where weddings can become elaborate productions focused on aesthetics and entertainment, the aufruf brings us back to essentials: Torah, community, and blessing. It reminds us that marriage isn't just a contract between two people; it's a sacred covenant witnessed and supported by an entire community of faith.

## Practical Application

Whether you're a groom preparing for your aufruf, a family member planning one, or a guest invited to attend:

1. **See It as More Than a Formality**: This is a profound spiritual moment disguised as a joyful celebration

2. **Involve the Community**: Don't be shy about inviting people—Jewish celebrations gain meaning through participation

3. **Connect to Tradition**: Research your family's specific customs and try to incorporate them

4. **Be Present**: In our distracted age, the aufruf is a moment to be fully present—no phones, no photos on Shabbat, just pure experience

5. **Carry It Forward**: If you attend an aufruf, let it inspire you to support the couple throughout their marriage

## Key Takeaways

- The **aufruf** is a synagogue honor where the groom receives an aliyah on the Shabbat before his wedding
- The tradition includes calling the groom to the Torah, candy throwing, and special blessings
- It has ancient roots but developed into its current form in medieval European Jewish communities
- Modern practices vary, with some communities including the bride in egalitarian ceremonies
- The aufruf symbolizes public commitment, community blessing, and the transition to married life
- Regional variations exist among Ashkenazi, Sephardic, Yemenite, and other Jewish communities
- Guests should dress modestly, bring candy, and stay for the kiddush celebration
- The deeper meaning centers on communal responsibility, generational connection, and sacred transition
