# Brit Milah (Circumcision): The Covenant in the Flesh

---

When a Jewish baby boy is eight days old, his family gathers for one of Judaism's most ancient and meaningful ceremonies: brit milah, the covenant of circumcision. This mitzvah (commandment) has been performed continuously for nearly four thousand years, linking Jewish fathers to their sons in an unbroken chain stretching back to Abraham himself.

## The Biblical Foundation

The commandment of brit milah appears in Genesis 17, when God establishes His covenant with Abraham:

> "This is My covenant which you shall keep, between Me and you and your descendants after you: Every male among you shall be circumcised. You shall be circumcised in the flesh of your foreskin, and it shall be the sign of the covenant between Me and you. And at the age of eight days, every male among you shall be circumcised throughout your generations" (Genesis 17:10-12).

This wasn't merely a symbolic gesture. The Torah describes how Abraham, at age ninety-nine, immediately circumcised himself, his son Ishmael, and every male in his household. Later, his son Isaac was circumcised on the eighth day, establishing the pattern that Jews follow to this very day.

The timing is precise and medically significant. Modern medical research has discovered that an infant's blood-clotting ability reaches optimal levels around the eighth day of life, when vitamin K production peaks. What doctors discovered only in recent centuries, the Torah commanded millennia ago.

## The Physical Sign of a Spiritual Bond

Why does God require this particular physical sign? The great commentators offer profound insights.

Maimonides (Rambam) explains that circumcision serves multiple purposes. It marks the Jewish people as distinct, creating a permanent physical reminder of our covenant with God. It also represents the perfection of the human being through divine law—nature provides the foundation, but we complete ourselves through following God's will.

The Zohar, the primary text of Jewish mysticism, teaches that the circumcision represents the refinement of physical desire. The area of the body that produces future generations is sanctified, ensuring that the Jewish people's continuation is rooted in holiness.

Nachmanides (Ramban) notes that the brit milah affects the very organ of procreation, meaning that every future Jewish child will be conceived through a body part that bears God's seal. In this way, the covenant literally continues through the generations.

## The Ceremony: A Community Celebration

A brit milah is far more than a medical procedure. It's a joyous celebration that involves specific rituals and honored roles.

**The Day Before**: Many communities hold a celebration called a "Shalom Zachar" (welcoming the male child) on the first Friday night after the birth, where guests visit the baby's home to celebrate.

**The Morning of the Brit**: The baby is brought into the room where family and guests have gathered. The mohel (ritual circumciser) is a specially trained expert in both the religious laws and medical procedures of circumcision.

**The Sandek**: One of the greatest honors at a brit is being chosen as the sandek—the person who holds the baby during the circumcision. This role is often given to a grandfather or respected rabbi. The sandek sits in a special chair, sometimes called "Elijah's chair," because according to tradition, the prophet Elijah attends every brit milah as a spiritual guardian.

**The Blessings**: Before the circumcision, the father recites a blessing thanking God for commanding us to bring his son into the covenant of Abraham. After the procedure, additional blessings are recited, and the baby is given his Hebrew name.

**The Celebration**: Following the ceremony, everyone shares a festive meal. It's considered a seudat mitzvah—a meal celebrating the performance of a commandment—similar in spiritual significance to a wedding feast.

## The Mohel: Expert and Guardian

Becoming a mohel requires extensive training in both Jewish law and medical practice. A mohel must:

- Master the intricate halachic (Jewish legal) requirements
- Study the precise surgical techniques
- Learn proper sterilization and safety protocols
- Understand infant anatomy and physiology
- Know how to identify and respond to medical complications

In Orthodox communities, mohelim (plural of mohel) are often ordained rabbis who have received additional specialized training. They use surgical instruments that meet both religious and modern medical standards. The procedure itself typically takes only a few minutes, and the baby usually calms quickly afterward.

Modern mohelim work closely with pediatricians and use local anesthetics when appropriate. They maintain the highest medical standards while preserving the religious integrity of the ceremony.

## Medical Considerations and Health Benefits

Contemporary medical research has documented several health benefits of circumcision:

- Reduced risk of urinary tract infections in infancy
- Decreased risk of certain sexually transmitted infections in adulthood
- Lower rates of penile cancer (though this cancer is already extremely rare)
- Easier genital hygiene

The American Academy of Pediatrics has stated that the health benefits of newborn male circumcision outweigh the risks, though they stop short of recommending universal circumcision.

However, for Jews, these medical benefits are secondary. We perform brit milah because God commanded it, regardless of medical opinion. That the procedure also offers health benefits merely demonstrates the wisdom inherent in divine law.

## When Brit Milah Is Delayed

Jewish law is extraordinarily protective of infant health. If a baby is premature, jaundiced, ill, or otherwise not completely healthy, the brit is postponed until the child is strong enough. The rule is absolute: "We wait for the baby; the baby doesn't wait for us."

If a baby is born ill or premature, the brit is delayed until the child is completely healthy, plus an additional seven days. Only when the pediatrician gives full clearance does the brit proceed.

In rare cases where a family has lost two sons due to bleeding complications, subsequent sons may not be circumcised at all, as pikuach nefesh (saving a life) overrides this commandment.

## Converting to Judaism

For adult male converts to Judaism, circumcision is also required (if not already circumcised). If someone was circumcised before conversion, a symbolic ritual called hatafat dam brit is performed—a single drop of blood is drawn from the area of circumcision to fulfill the covenant requirement.

The conversion circumcision is performed by a mohel, followed immediately by immersion in a mikvah (ritual bath), with a rabbinic court (beit din) overseeing the process.

## Historical Persecution and Heroism

Throughout Jewish history, brit milah has often been outlawed by hostile regimes. The ancient Greeks prohibited it. The Romans banned it under penalty of death. During the Inquisition, Spanish and Portuguese authorities used circumcision as evidence to identify and execute secret Jews.

Soviet authorities tried to stamp out the practice, forcing families to perform circumcisions in secret. Even in modern times, several European countries have debated banning or restricting the practice.

Despite all persecution, Jewish communities have risked everything to maintain this commandment. Parents circumcised their sons in hiding, mohelim traveled in disguise, and communities developed coded language to arrange ceremonies without alerting authorities.

The continued practice of brit milah represents not just obedience to God, but the unbreakable continuity of the Jewish people.

## A Permanent Symbol of Identity

Unlike many Jewish practices that can be hidden or performed in private, brit milah creates a permanent, physical mark. A circumcised Jewish male carries the sign of the covenant literally written on his body.

This permanence has profound implications. Throughout history, during times of persecution, Jews who wanted to hide their identity found themselves unable to do so. The brit milah revealed them. Yet this same permanence also strengthened Jewish identity—it's a constant reminder, inscribed in flesh, of who we are and to Whom we belong.

The Talmud tells us that in the World to Come, Abraham sits at the entrance to Gehinnom (purgatory) and doesn't allow any circumcised Jewish soul to enter. The covenant marked in our flesh extends beyond this world.

## Cultural Parallels: Rites of Passage in Chinese Tradition

While brit milah is unique to Judaism, the concept of a ritual marking a child's entry into the community has parallels in Chinese culture. The Mǎnyuè (满月) ceremony celebrates a baby's first full month of life, marking survival through the most vulnerable period. Similarly, the Zhuāzhōu (抓周) ceremony at one year old celebrates development and predicts the child's future.

Both Jewish and Chinese traditions recognize that bringing a child into the community requires more than birth—it demands ritual, celebration, and the blessing of family and community. The brit milah simply takes this principle and elevates it to a covenant with the Divine.

## Modern Questions and Timeless Answers

In contemporary Western society, some question why brit milah remains necessary. Isn't it outdated? Shouldn't the child decide for himself when older?

The Jewish response is clear: brit milah is not about personal choice—it's about covenant. Just as a child doesn't choose to be born Jewish, neither does he choose whether to bear the covenant's sign. His parents, acting as guardians of the tradition, make this decision on his behalf, just as they decide his name, his education, and his values.

Furthermore, the commandment specifically requires circumcision at eight days old, not at adulthood. Delaying would not be "letting the child choose"—it would be disobeying the commandment.

As Rabbi Jonathan Sacks wrote: "The covenant is not a contract we negotiate; it is a destiny we inherit."

## The Message of the Eighth Day

The eighth day holds symbolic significance beyond medical timing. In Jewish thought, the number seven represents the natural world—seven days of creation, seven days of the week. The number eight represents that which transcends nature: the supernatural, the miraculous, the divine.

By performing brit milah on the eighth day, we acknowledge that our covenant with God goes beyond the natural order. We are not just another nation among nations; we are a people bound to the Creator of the universe by an eternal, supernatural covenant.

## Conclusion: A Four-Thousand-Year Chain

When a Jewish father holds his eight-day-old son during the brit milah ceremony, he joins an unbroken chain stretching back forty centuries to Abraham. The same blessing he recites was recited by his father, his grandfather, and countless generations before them. The same covenant that marked Abraham marks his son.

In a world of constant change, brit milah remains unchanged. Empires have risen and fallen, technologies have transformed human life, and ideologies have come and gone. Yet Jewish fathers still bring their sons into the covenant of Abraham on the eighth day, just as the Torah commands.

This is the miracle of brit milah: it is both intensely personal—a father and son in a sacred moment—and cosmically significant—a link in the eternal chain of Jewish destiny.

The covenant is in the flesh, but its meaning reaches far beyond the physical. It is a declaration that this child belongs not only to his parents, but to the Jewish people and to God. It is a statement of faith, continuity, and eternal connection.

When we perform brit milah, we do more than follow an ancient ritual. We affirm that the covenant is alive, that our connection to God is real, and that the Jewish people will continue, generation after generation, marked in flesh and spirit as God's treasured nation.
