## Introduction

Walking into a house of mourning is one of the most difficult social situations you'll ever face. What do you say to someone whose parent just died? How do you comfort a friend who lost their spouse? Should you bring food? Should you stay long or leave quickly? And what if you accidentally say the wrong thing?

In Jewish tradition, visiting a mourner during shiva—the seven-day mourning period after a funeral—is considered one of the highest forms of kindness (chesed). Yet many people avoid making shiva calls because they feel awkward or unsure of the protocol. This guide will help you understand exactly what to do, what to say, and—perhaps most importantly—what *not* to say when making a shiva call.

## What Is Shiva?

Shiva (from the Hebrew word for "seven") is the intensive seven-day mourning period that begins immediately after a Jewish funeral. During this week, mourners stay home and receive visitors who come to offer comfort and support.

The mourners (called *avelim* in Hebrew) sit on low chairs or stools, don't wear leather shoes, cover mirrors, and refrain from work or entertainment. Their focus is entirely on processing their grief and honoring the memory of the deceased.

The practice of sitting shiva dates back thousands of years—Job's friends came and sat with him in mourning (Job 2:13), and during what corresponds to China's Spring and Autumn Period (春秋时代, 770-476 BCE), these mourning customs were already well established in Jewish communities.

## The Purpose of a Shiva Call

The Hebrew term for a condolence visit is *nichum avelim*—comforting the mourners. Notice it doesn't say "cheering up the mourners" or "distracting the mourners." The goal is *comfort*, not entertainment.

According to Jewish tradition, when you make a shiva call, you're performing several important functions:

1. **Sharing the burden of grief** — Your presence says, "You're not alone in this."
2. **Honoring the deceased** — By coming, you acknowledge that this person's life mattered.
3. **Providing practical support** — Sometimes mourners need help with logistics, meals, or just having someone to talk to.
4. **Bearing witness** — Grief needs an audience. Your willingness to sit with someone in pain validates their experience.

There's a beautiful saying in the Talmud: "More than the calf wants to suckle, the cow wants to nurse." Meaning: the mourner needs your visit even more than you might think they do.

## When to Visit

### Timing of the Shiva Period

Shiva begins immediately after the funeral and lasts for seven days. However:
- **Shabbat** (Friday evening through Saturday evening) is counted as one of the seven days, but formal shiva observance is suspended during Shabbat. Most communities don't receive visitors then.
- **Major holidays** interrupt and conclude shiva early.

If you're not sure when shiva is being observed, ask the family or check the funeral home announcement.

### Best Times to Visit

**Morning visits** (after morning prayers, around 9-11 AM) tend to be quieter and are good if you want more intimate conversation with the mourners.

**Evening visits** (after evening prayers, around 7-9 PM) are usually busier, which can be comforting for mourners who want company but also easier for shy visitors who prefer to blend into a group.

**Avoid mealtimes** unless you're bringing food or helping serve. Don't arrive right at dinner time expecting to be fed.

### How Long Should You Stay?

**15-30 minutes** is typical. Long enough to show genuine care, short enough not to exhaust the mourners.

If the house is packed with visitors, you can stay briefly just to show your face. If you're one of only a few people there, you might stay longer—but take cues from the mourners. If they seem tired, don't overstay.

## What to Do When You Arrive

### The Traditional Protocol

1. **Enter quietly.** Don't ring the doorbell—the door is usually unlocked during shiva hours. If it's not, knock softly.

2. **Wash your hands** (if a basin is provided at the entrance). This ritual washing symbolizes leaving the cemetery/funeral behind and entering a space of life and comfort.

3. **Find the mourners** and sit near them—but don't immediately start talking. Traditional practice is to wait for the mourner to speak first. This shows that you're following their lead.

4. **Let silence be okay.** If the mourner doesn't feel like talking, your presence alone is valuable. Sit quietly. Be there.

5. **When the mourner does speak**, listen. Really listen. Don't interrupt with your own stories unless invited.

6. **When you're ready to leave**, offer the traditional phrase of comfort (see below) and exit quietly.

### Modern Variations

Not all shiva homes follow every tradition strictly. You might find:
- Chairs at normal height (some mourners, especially elderly ones, find low chairs physically difficult)
- More casual visiting hours
- Food served buffet-style
- Photo albums or video tributes set up for viewing

Adapt to whatever the family has arranged. The core principles—respect, quietness, focus on the mourners—remain the same.

## What to Say

### The Traditional Phrase

When you leave, the traditional Jewish condolence phrase is:

**"HaMakom yenachem etchem betoch she'ar avelei Tzion v'Yerushalayim."**

Translation: "May the Omnipresent comfort you among the mourners of Zion and Jerusalem."

If you're not comfortable with Hebrew, the English translation is perfectly acceptable. The phrase connects this individual loss to the larger story of Jewish mourning and hope.

### Other Appropriate Things to Say

**Share a specific memory**: "I'll always remember when your father helped me fix my car. He was so patient and kind."

**Acknowledge the pain**: "I can't imagine how hard this must be for you."

**Offer specific help**: "I'm going to the grocery store tomorrow—can I pick anything up for you?" (Better than vague "Let me know if you need anything.")

**Ask about the deceased**: "What was your mother like as a young woman?" (Mourners often want to talk about their loved one.)

**Express admiration**: "Your father raised an amazing family. His legacy is clear."

### What NOT to Say

❌ **"I know how you feel."** (You don't. Even if you've experienced loss, each grief is unique.)

❌ **"At least he lived a long life."** (This minimizes the loss. Long or short, death still hurts.)

❌ **"She's in a better place now."** (This may be true, but it doesn't comfort someone who wants their loved one here.)

❌ **"God needed another angel."** (This can sound like God cares more about populating heaven than about this person's suffering.)

❌ **"Everything happens for a reason."** (Even if this is philosophically true, saying it during acute grief feels dismissive.)

❌ **"You're so strong."** (This can pressure mourners to hide their pain. It's okay for them to be *not* strong right now.)

❌ **"Time heals all wounds."** (Trite and unhelpful in the immediate aftermath.)

❌ **Comparing losses**: "When my aunt died, I..." (This shifts focus from the mourner to you.)

### The Power of Silence

Sometimes the best thing you can say is *nothing*. 

The Talmud teaches that Job's friends sat with him in silence for seven days, and only when they started talking did they cause him pain (Job 2:13). Sometimes your quiet presence—just being there—is the most profound comfort you can offer.

If you're not sure what to say, try: "I don't have the right words, but I wanted you to know I'm here for you."

## What to Bring

### Food

Bringing food is traditional and helpful, but coordinate with others to avoid having 15 people all bring lasagna.

**Good options:**
- Meals that can be easily reheated
- Paper plates and disposable utensils (mourners shouldn't have to worry about dishes)
- Foods that can be frozen for later
- Breakfast items (often overlooked)
- Coffee and tea supplies

**Label everything** with your name and reheating instructions.

### What Not to Bring

❌ **Flowers** — Not traditional in Jewish mourning. Donations to charity in the deceased's memory are preferred.

❌ **Elaborate gifts** — This isn't a celebration. Keep it simple and practical.

❌ **Alcohol** — Some families appreciate wine for kiddush, but don't assume. Ask first.

### Non-Food Ways to Help

**Offer specific services:**
- "Can I walk your dog tomorrow afternoon?"
- "I can drive your kids to school this week."
- "I'm free Thursday morning if you need help with errands."

**Money** — If the family is struggling financially (funeral costs, lost work time), a discreet monetary gift or covering a specific bill can be incredibly helpful.

## Special Situations

### If You Didn't Know the Deceased Well

It's still appropriate to visit if you know the mourner. You might say: "I didn't have the privilege of knowing your mother well, but I know how much she meant to you, and I wanted to be here for you."

### If You're Not Jewish

You're still welcome to make a shiva call. The principles of comfort transcend religious boundaries. Follow the same guidelines—respect, quietness, focusing on the mourners. You don't need to say the Hebrew condolence phrase; a sincere "I'm so sorry for your loss" is perfect.

### If You're Uncomfortable or Anxious

It's normal to feel awkward. Mourners understand—they probably felt the same way before they were sitting shiva themselves. Show up anyway. Your discomfort is small compared to their pain, and your willingness to push through it to be there for them is exactly what the mitzvah of nichum avelim is about.

### Virtual Shiva Calls

Since the COVID-19 pandemic, virtual shiva calls have become more common. The same principles apply:
- Keep it brief (10-15 minutes)
- Let the mourner lead the conversation
- Offer specific help within your capacity
- Your presence—even on a screen—still matters

## The Deeper Meaning

In Jewish thought, visiting the bereaved is considered an imitation of God Himself. How? After Abraham's wife Sarah died, God "appeared to him" (Genesis 18:1)—the Talmud interprets this as God coming to comfort Abraham in his mourning. When you make a shiva call, you're emulating the Divine attribute of compassion.

There's also a profound lesson here about human connection. In Chinese philosophy, particularly in Confucian thought, the concept of *ren* (仁)—benevolence or humaneness—emphasizes caring for others in their times of need. The practice of visiting mourners embodies this universal human value: we show our humanity most clearly when we're willing to sit with someone in their darkest hour.

## Practical Application

### Your First Shiva Call: A Checklist

- [ ] Confirm shiva times and location
- [ ] Decide if you're bringing food (coordinate if possible)
- [ ] Dress modestly and respectfully (no bright colors, no party attire)
- [ ] Arrive during appropriate hours (not too early, not too late)
- [ ] Enter quietly
- [ ] Wash hands if basin is provided
- [ ] Sit near mourners but wait for them to speak first
- [ ] Listen more than you talk
- [ ] Share a memory or offer specific help if appropriate
- [ ] Say the traditional condolence phrase (or English equivalent) when leaving
- [ ] Follow up after shiva ends—grief doesn't end when shiva does

### After Shiva

Many people think their obligation ends when shiva ends. In fact, the weeks and months after shiva—when everyone else has moved on—are often when mourners need support most.

**Continue to:**
- Reach out with calls or messages
- Invite them to social events (they might not come, but they should be asked)
- Remember significant dates (birthday of deceased, yahrzeit anniversary)
- Mention the deceased by name—mourners want to talk about their loved ones

## Key Takeaways

- **Your presence matters more than your words.** Show up even if you're not sure what to say.
- **Follow the mourner's lead.** If they want to talk, listen. If they want silence, sit quietly.
- **Avoid clichés that minimize their pain.** "I'm so sorry" is better than "Everything happens for a reason."
- **Offer specific help rather than vague offers.** "Can I bring dinner Thursday?" beats "Let me know if you need anything."
- **The traditional phrase "HaMakom yenachem..." connects individual grief to the larger Jewish experience of loss and hope.**
- **Grief doesn't end when shiva ends.** Continue to support mourners in the weeks and months afterward.
