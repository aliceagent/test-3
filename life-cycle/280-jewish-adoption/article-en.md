## Introduction

Adoption is one of the most profound acts of love a person can perform—choosing to make a child your own, not through biology, but through commitment, care, and covenant. In Jewish tradition, adoption carries deep spiritual significance that goes far beyond legal paperwork. It's viewed not just as taking in a child, but as partnering with the Divine in giving a soul its place in the world.

But adoption in Jewish law (halacha) raises important questions: What happens when a Jewish family adopts a child who wasn't born Jewish? How does conversion work for children? What are the long-term halachic implications? And perhaps most importantly, how can adoptive parents navigate these questions while creating a loving, secure home for their child?

This article explores the halachic framework for Jewish adoption, the conversion process for adopted children, and the beautiful traditions that have developed around welcoming children into Jewish families.

## The Jewish View of Adoption: "Whoever Raises a Child"

Jewish tradition places immense value on the act of raising children, even more than the biological act of bringing them into the world. The Talmud teaches: **"Whoever raises an orphan in their home is considered as if they gave birth to that child"** (Megillah 13a, Sanhedrin 19b).

This statement isn't just poetic—it's halachic. In Jewish law, the parent-child relationship is primarily defined by who raises and educates the child, not solely by biology. This is why adoptive parents are considered the child's true parents in every meaningful sense.

### Historical Context: Adoption in Jewish History

Adoption is deeply rooted in Jewish history, stretching back thousands of years—during what corresponds to China's Shang and Zhou dynasties (商朝-周朝, ~1600-256 BCE).

The most famous adoption in the Torah is that of **Moses (Moshe)**, who was adopted by Pharaoh's daughter after she found him floating in the Nile River. Despite being born to Israelite parents, Moses was raised in the Egyptian palace, and Jewish tradition honors Pharaoh's daughter (called Batya in the Midrash) for her compassion. The Talmud says she is called "the daughter of God" for raising Moses as her own.

Similarly, **Queen Esther** was orphaned as a child and adopted by her cousin Mordechai, who raised her as his own daughter. The Megillah (Book of Esther) describes Mordechai as taking her "as his daughter" (Esther 2:7), and Jewish tradition celebrates this adoptive relationship as exemplary.

These biblical precedents establish that adoption isn't just permitted in Judaism—it's honored and elevated as a profound mitzvah (commandment).

## When a Non-Jewish Child Is Adopted: The Need for Conversion

If a Jewish family adopts a child who was not born to a Jewish mother, halacha requires that the child undergo conversion (giyur katan, "conversion of a minor") to become Jewish. This isn't about rejecting the child's birth background—it's about formally bringing them into the Jewish covenant so they can fully participate in Jewish life.

### Understanding Giyur Katan (Conversion for Minors)

Conversion for adopted children follows special halachic rules because children cannot make informed decisions about religion. The principle is called **zekhut**, meaning "merit" or "benefit." Jewish law allows parents (including adoptive parents) to bring a child into the Jewish covenant because being Jewish is considered a spiritual benefit.

The conversion process for an adopted child involves three main steps:

### 1. Brit Milah (Circumcision) or Hatafat Dam Brit

For adopted boys, circumcision is required if they were not already circumcised, or if they were circumcised for medical reasons, a symbolic drop of blood (hatafat dam brit) is drawn by a mohel (ritual circumciser). This represents their entrance into the covenant of Abraham.

For adopted girls, this step is not applicable.

### 2. Tevilah (Ritual Immersion)

Both boys and girls must immerse in a mikvah (ritual bath). This immersion symbolizes rebirth and transformation—the child emerges from the water as a full member of the Jewish people.

Traditionally, the parents bring the child to the mikvah accompanied by a beit din (rabbinic court) of three rabbis who supervise the immersion. For infants and young children, a parent typically holds the child during immersion. For older children, they immerse on their own with guidance.

### 3. Kabbalat Ol Mitzvot (Acceptance of Commandments)

While a young child cannot formally accept the commandments, the parents accept on their behalf, committing to raise the child with a Jewish education and observance. The beit din records this acceptance as part of the conversion process.

### Cultural Parallel: Confucian Family Adoption

The Jewish emphasis on raising a child as paramount to biological birth finds a parallel in Confucian thought. In traditional Chinese culture, particularly during the Han Dynasty (汉朝, 206 BCE - 220 CE)—which coincides with the Second Temple and early Talmudic period—family relationships were defined by ritual and education rather than biology alone. A child who was raised with Confucian values and filial piety (孝, xiao) could be considered a true heir, just as Jewish tradition views an adopted child raised with Torah values as a full child of the family.

## The Child's Right to Choose: Conversion Upon Reaching Maturity

One unique aspect of giyur katan is that the conversion is not fully binding until the child reaches adulthood. According to halacha, when a child converted as a minor reaches bar or bat mitzvah age (12 for girls, 13 for boys), they have the **right to renounce the conversion** if they choose not to be Jewish.

This might sound concerning to adoptive parents, but in practice, it rarely happens. A child raised in a loving Jewish home, educated in Torah and mitzvot, and fully integrated into Jewish community life almost always affirms their Jewish identity when they come of age.

Many families mark this milestone with a special ceremony where the child (now a young adult) formally declares their commitment to Judaism before a beit din. Some immerse in the mikvah again as a symbolic reaffirmation. This transforms the childhood conversion into an adult choice—a powerful moment of personal commitment.

### Why This Right Exists

The rabbis instituted this rule to respect personal autonomy. They recognized that while parents can make decisions for young children, a person's ultimate religious identity must be a matter of free will. This principle reflects Judaism's deep respect for individual choice in matters of faith—something that emerged clearly during the Talmudic period (200-500 CE), which corresponds to China's Three Kingdoms and Jin Dynasty period (三国-晋朝).

## Practical Halachic Considerations for Adoptive Parents

### Naming and Hebrew Identity

Adopted children are given Hebrew names like any Jewish child. The naming convention depends on the family's tradition:

- **Ashkenazi custom**: Often the child is named "_____ ben/bat Avraham v'Sarah" (son/daughter of Abraham and Sarah), connecting them to the first converts and spiritual ancestors of all Jews.
- **Some families**: Choose to use the adoptive father's Hebrew name, e.g., "_____ ben Yaakov" if the father is Jacob. This emphasizes that the adoptive parent is the child's true parent in every sense.

The Hebrew name is used for all ritual purposes: Torah aliyot (being called to the Torah), ketubah (marriage contract), and get (divorce document).

### Informing the Child About Their Adoption

Judaism strongly encourages honesty. The Talmud teaches that truth is one of the pillars upon which the world stands (Pirkei Avot 1:18). Adoptive parents should tell their children about their adoption in an age-appropriate way.

Many families share the adoption story as a beautiful narrative: "You were chosen with extra love. God brought you to our family in a special way." The conversion is presented not as something negative, but as the formal entry into the Jewish people—a moment of celebration, not shame.

### Bar/Bat Mitzvah and Other Milestones

Children who underwent giyur katan celebrate their bar or bat mitzvah like any Jewish child. There is no difference in how they participate in Jewish life.

However, some families use the bar/bat mitzvah as an opportunity for the child to reaffirm their conversion (as mentioned above), turning it into an even more meaningful celebration of identity and choice.

### Lineage and Tribal Affiliation

Adopted children are not considered kohanim (priests) or levites (Levites) through their adoptive families, even if the adoptive parents are from these lineages. This is one of the few areas where biological descent still matters in halacha.

However, in all other respects—inheritance, family relationships, mitzvot—adopted children are fully equal to biological children.

### Disclosure for Marriage Purposes

Halachically, there is no requirement for an adopted child to disclose their adoption to a future spouse. However, many poskim (halachic authorities) recommend transparency as part of building trust in a marriage.

If the child was adopted from a non-Jewish background and converted, some authorities suggest mentioning this to ensure there are no concerns about lineage (yichus) for families who are particular about such matters.

## The Emotional and Spiritual Journey

Beyond halacha, adoptive parents must navigate the emotional complexity of raising a child who may look different, have a different ethnic background, or wrestle with identity questions.

### Creating a Jewish Home That Embraces All Backgrounds

Judaism is not a race or ethnicity—it's a covenant. Jewish communities include people from every ethnic background: Ashkenazi Jews from Europe, Sephardi Jews from the Middle East and North Africa, Mizrahi Jews from the Arab world, Ethiopian Jews, Indian Jews, Chinese Jews, and countless converts from every nation.

Adoptive parents can model this diversity by teaching their children about the global Jewish people. Reading stories about Ethiopian Jewish immigration to Israel, or about the historic Jewish community of Kaifeng, China (开封犹太社区), helps children see that being Jewish encompasses many cultures.

### Addressing Identity Questions with Love

As adopted children grow, they may ask questions about their birth family, their ethnic heritage, or why they "needed" to convert to Judaism. These questions are normal and healthy.

Answers should emphasize:
1. **Love and choice**: "We chose you because we wanted you to be part of our family."
2. **Jewish values**: "Being Jewish is about what you do and believe, not just what you're born as."
3. **Historical precedent**: "Some of the greatest Jews in history were converts or adopted, like Ruth and Moses."

## Special Cases and Practical Scenarios

### Adopting from Abroad

Many Jewish families adopt internationally—from China, Eastern Europe, Latin America, or elsewhere. The conversion process is the same regardless of the child's country of origin.

Some practical considerations:
- **Timing**: Ideally, the conversion should happen soon after the adoption is finalized, so the child grows up always knowing they are Jewish.
- **Local rabbinic authorities**: Work with a respected Orthodox beit din to ensure the conversion is universally recognized within the Jewish community.

### Single-Parent Adoption

Halacha permits single individuals to adopt, and the conversion process for the child is the same. Single Jewish mothers and fathers can raise Jewish children and provide loving, Torah-observant homes.

### Same-Sex Couples and Adoption

While traditional halacha does not recognize same-sex marriages, the question of adoption by same-sex couples is complex and evolving. Some progressive Orthodox and Conservative communities work with such families to facilitate conversions for adopted children, recognizing that a loving Jewish home is beneficial for any child.

Families in this situation should consult with a compassionate and knowledgeable rabbi who can guide them through the process.

### Adopting a Child with Special Needs

Jewish law views caring for vulnerable individuals as one of the highest mitzvot. Families who adopt children with physical, developmental, or emotional special needs are performing an extraordinary act of chesed (loving-kindness).

The conversion process may need to be adapted based on the child's abilities. For example, if a child cannot physically immerse independently, a parent or caregiver assists. Rabbinic authorities are generally accommodating in these situations, prioritizing the child's well-being and inclusion.

## The Blessing of Building a Jewish Family

Jewish tradition teaches that there are many ways to build a family, and adoption is one of the most beautiful. The Midrash says that when a person brings someone into the Jewish people through conversion (or by raising a child as Jewish), it's as if they created a new world (Sanhedrin 37a).

Adoptive parents are not just fulfilling their personal desire to be parents—they are performing a cosmic mitzvah: giving a Jewish soul its place in the covenant, educating a new generation in Torah, and ensuring Jewish continuity.

### A Blessing for Adoptive Parents

Though there is no formal liturgical blessing for adoption, many families create their own. Here's one example:

*"Baruch Atah Adonai, Eloheinu Melech ha'olam, shehecheyanu v'kiyemanu v'higianu lazman hazeh."*  
*Blessed are You, Lord our God, Ruler of the universe, who has kept us alive, sustained us, and brought us to this moment.*

*"Thank you, Hashem, for bringing [child's name] into our family. May we raise them with love, with Torah, and with a strong Jewish identity. May they grow to be a blessing to the Jewish people and to the world."*

## Conclusion: Love, Law, and Legacy

Adoption in Judaism is a blend of love and law, emotion and halacha. It requires navigating complex questions with both the head and the heart—ensuring halachic integrity while creating a warm, secure home.

But at its core, Jewish adoption is about one simple, profound truth: **family is not just about biology; it's about commitment, love, and covenant.** The child you raise is your child in every sense that matters. The love you give, the values you teach, the traditions you pass down—these are what make a family Jewish.

And in a world that often emphasizes blood ties, Judaism offers a radical, beautiful alternative: that the most important thing isn't where a child comes from, but where they're going—and that with love, commitment, and Torah, any child can become part of the eternal Jewish story.

## Key Takeaways

- **Jewish tradition honors adoption**: The Talmud teaches that whoever raises a child is considered as if they gave birth to that child.
- **Conversion for minors (giyur katan)** is required when a non-Jewish child is adopted by Jewish parents, involving circumcision (for boys) and mikvah immersion.
- **The child has a right to choose** upon reaching bar/bat mitzvah age, though children raised in loving Jewish homes almost always affirm their Jewish identity.
- **Transparency and love** are key: Families should be honest about adoption and conversion while emphasizing the beauty of being chosen and loved.
- **Adopted children are equal** to biological children in nearly all halachic respects, with full participation in Jewish life.
