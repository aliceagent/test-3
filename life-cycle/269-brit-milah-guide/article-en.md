## Introduction

On the eighth day of a Jewish boy's life, family and friends gather for one of the oldest continuously practiced rituals in human history: brit milah, the covenant of circumcision. This ceremony — unchanged for nearly four thousand years since the time of Abraham (corresponding to China's Shang Dynasty, 商朝, ~1600-1046 BCE) — marks the formal entry of a Jewish male into the covenant between God and the Jewish people.

For Chinese readers unfamiliar with this practice, brit milah may seem unusual or even extreme. Yet this ancient ritual carries profound spiritual meaning that transcends its physical component. It is simultaneously a medical procedure, a religious obligation, a family celebration, and a powerful statement of identity and continuity.

Understanding brit milah means understanding how Jews have maintained an unbroken connection to their ancestors across millennia, surviving empires, exiles, and persecutions while holding fast to this covenant commanded to Abraham.

## The Biblical Origin: Abraham's Covenant

The commandment of circumcision appears in the Torah (Hebrew Bible) in Genesis Chapter 17, when God makes an eternal covenant with Abraham:

*"This is My covenant, which you shall keep, between Me and you and your offspring after you: Every male among you shall be circumcised. You shall circumcise the flesh of your foreskin, and that shall be the sign of the covenant between Me and you. And throughout the generations, every male among you shall be circumcised at the age of eight days."* (Genesis 17:10-12)

This covenant (brit in Hebrew) established circumcision as a physical mark — literally inscribed in the flesh — that would identify Abraham's descendants forever. Unlike other signs or symbols that can be removed or hidden, brit milah is permanent and intimate, a reminder that the relationship between the Jewish people and God touches every aspect of life, even the most private.

### The Eighth Day Significance

Why specifically the eighth day? The Torah does not explicitly explain, but Jewish tradition offers several interpretations:

1. **Completion and Transcendence**: Seven represents the natural world (seven days of creation), while eight represents that which transcends nature — the supernatural covenant with God.

2. **Medical Wisdom**: Modern medicine has discovered that vitamin K levels (crucial for blood clotting) reach their peak on the eighth day of life, and an enzyme that aids coagulation is present in higher concentrations then than at any other time in a newborn's life. This suggests that the eighth day is medically optimal for the procedure.

3. **Shabbat Experience**: By waiting eight days, the newborn will have experienced at least one Shabbat (Sabbath) before entering the covenant, symbolically experiencing the sanctity of Jewish time.

## The Ceremony: What Happens at a Brit Milah

A brit milah typically takes place in the morning of the eighth day after birth. It can be held at home, in a synagogue, or even in a hospital. The ceremony includes several key participants and ritual elements:

### Key Participants

**The Mohel (מוהל)**: A specially trained ritual circumciser, usually a rabbi or doctor with extensive training in both the religious laws and surgical techniques of brit milah. A mohel must be a religiously observant Jew who has performed hundreds of circumcisions under expert supervision.

**The Sandek (סנדק)**: The "godfather" who holds the baby during the circumcision. This is considered a great honor, often given to a grandfather, distinguished rabbi, or close family friend. According to Jewish mysticism (Kabbalah), the sandek's merit protects him from harsh spiritual judgments, making this role highly sought after.

**The Kvatter and Kvatterin**: The "godparents" who carry the baby into the room and hand him to the sandek. These roles are often given to a married couple who do not yet have children, as tradition holds this honor brings them a blessing for their own children.

### The Ritual Sequence

1. **The Welcoming**: As the baby is brought into the room, those assembled call out "Baruch haba!" ("Blessed is the one who comes!")

2. **Chair of Elijah**: The baby is placed momentarily on a special chair designated for Elijah the Prophet, who according to tradition spiritually attends every brit milah as the guardian of the covenant.

3. **Blessings Before**: The mohel recites blessings praising God for commanding this mitzvah (commandment). The father recites a blessing accepting the responsibility to bring his son into the covenant.

4. **The Circumcision**: The mohel performs the procedure, which takes only seconds when done by an expert. The foreskin is removed, and the mohel draws a drop of blood to complete the covenant.

5. **Naming**: Immediately after, the baby receives his Hebrew name in a special blessing recited over a cup of wine. A few drops of wine are placed on the baby's lips, symbolizing the sweetness of Torah and tradition.

6. **Celebration**: The ceremony concludes with a festive meal (se'udat mitzvah), celebrating the entry of a new soul into the Jewish people.

### Pain Management

Modern mohelim use various methods to minimize the baby's discomfort:
- Local anesthetic creams or injections
- Sweet wine or sugar solution (which has been shown to reduce infant pain perception)
- Quick, expert technique minimizing procedure time

The baby typically cries briefly during the procedure but calms within moments and often sleeps peacefully shortly afterward.

## The Deeper Meaning: Why Circumcision?

For those outside the Jewish tradition, the question naturally arises: Why would God command a permanent physical alteration? Jewish thought offers several profound explanations:

### 1. **Partnership with God in Perfecting the World**

Judaism teaches that God deliberately created the world incomplete, requiring human effort to perfect it. Circumcision symbolizes this principle: just as humans improve the earth by planting gardens, building homes, and creating civilization, so too do we "complete" the human body through brit milah.

This challenges the notion that nature is always perfect as-is. Instead, it suggests that human beings are God's partners in the ongoing work of creation — refining, elevating, and sanctifying the physical world.

### 2. **Control Over Physical Desire**

In Jewish thought, circumcision represents the sanctification of the most powerful physical drive: sexuality. By marking the organ of reproduction with a covenant sign, brit milah declares that physical intimacy is not merely instinctual but sacred — subject to moral boundaries, spiritual purpose, and divine commandment.

This parallels Confucian teachings about the regulation of desire (制欲) and the importance of ritual propriety (禮) in governing human behavior. Both traditions recognize that civilization requires channeling natural impulses toward higher purposes.

### 3. **Identity That Cannot Be Erased**

Throughout Jewish history — especially during periods of persecution — there were times when external signs of Jewishness could be hidden for survival. A Star of David could be removed, kosher dietary laws could be practiced in secret, and religious texts could be concealed.

But brit milah cannot be hidden or undone. It is a permanent mark of identity that declares: "I am a Jew." During the Greek occupation (circa 167-160 BCE, during China's Western Han Dynasty, 西汉), when Hellenistic rulers outlawed circumcision on pain of death, many Jewish families risked execution to perform brit milah. It became the ultimate act of resistance and faith.

### 4. **Generational Continuity**

When a father brings his son into the covenant through brit milah, he continues an unbroken chain stretching back to Abraham — approximately 3,800 years. Each generation promises to transmit this covenant to the next, creating a living link across time.

This resonates deeply with Chinese values of filial piety (孝, xiào) and ancestral continuity. Just as Chinese culture emphasizes honoring one's ancestors and maintaining family traditions, brit milah ensures each generation acknowledges its debt to the past and responsibility to the future.

## Practical Questions and Answers

### What if the baby is sick or premature?

Jewish law absolutely prioritizes the baby's health. If the infant is ill, premature, underweight, or has any medical condition that could be aggravated, the brit milah is postponed until doctors confirm the baby is fully healthy. There is no time limit — the ceremony can be delayed weeks or even months. As the Talmud states: "Shabbat can be pushed off for brit milah, but brit milah cannot push off danger to life."

### What about converts to Judaism?

Adult males converting to Judaism also undergo brit milah (or if already circumcised for medical reasons, a symbolic ritual called hatafat dam brit — drawing a single drop of blood). This demonstrates that conversion is not merely intellectual acceptance but a complete physical and spiritual transformation.

### Can a medical doctor perform the brit milah instead of a mohel?

While some liberal Jewish movements permit hospital circumcisions performed by doctors to fulfill the obligation, Orthodox Judaism requires a mohel to perform the ritual. This is because brit milah is not simply a medical procedure but a religious ceremony requiring specific prayers, blessings, and intentions. A doctor can remove the foreskin, but only a mohel can perform the covenant.

### What about medical circumcision performed before the eighth day?

If a baby is circumcised in a hospital for medical reasons before the eighth day, Orthodox Judaism does not consider this a valid brit milah. A symbolic ceremony (hatafat dam brit) must be performed on the eighth day or later to fulfill the religious obligation.

### Is there any controversy about this practice?

In recent decades, some secular movements have questioned the ethics of performing circumcision on infants who cannot consent. Jewish responses generally emphasize:

1. **Parental Rights**: Parents make countless medical and ethical decisions for their children — from vaccinations to education to religious upbringing. Brit milah falls within this parental responsibility.

2. **Medical Benefits**: Numerous studies have confirmed medical benefits of circumcision, including reduced risk of urinary tract infections, certain sexually transmitted infections, and penile cancer.

3. **Religious Freedom**: Attempts to ban brit milah (as proposed in some European jurisdictions) are seen as attacks on religious freedom and Jewish continuity, reminiscent of historical persecutions.

4. **Generations of Evidence**: Millions of Jewish men across thousands of years have undergone brit milah with no psychological trauma or resentment. The overwhelming majority are glad to carry this mark of their heritage.

## Cultural Parallels: Chinese and Jewish Coming-of-Age Rituals

While circumcision itself has no direct Chinese parallel, the concept of a ritual marking entry into community identity resonates across cultures:

**The Full Month Celebration (满月酒, mǎn yuè jiǔ)**: Chinese families celebrate a baby's first month of life with a banquet introducing the child to extended family and community. While the rituals differ, both traditions mark the baby's transition from newborn fragility to recognized community member.

**Hair-Cutting Rituals**: In some Chinese traditions, a baby's first haircut (usually around one month or one year) is a ceremonial event. Similarly, many Jewish families hold an upsherin — a ritual first haircut for boys at age three — though this is separate from brit milah.

**Name-Giving**: Both Jewish and Chinese cultures place great significance on naming ceremonies. Just as Chinese families carefully choose names based on auspicious meanings and family heritage, the Hebrew name given at the brit milah carries spiritual significance and often honors deceased relatives.

## Modern Practice: Brit Milah Today

In contemporary Jewish communities worldwide, brit milah remains nearly universal:

- In Israel, over 98% of Jewish boys undergo brit milah.
- In Orthodox communities globally, the rate approaches 100%.
- Even among secular or less observant Jews, the majority continue this practice, seeing it as a fundamental marker of Jewish identity.

Modern mohelim often have medical training and use sterile surgical instruments, anesthetics, and contemporary medical knowledge while maintaining the traditional ritual structure. The result is a ceremony that honors ancient practice while incorporating modern safety and comfort standards.

## The Emotional Dimension: A Parent's Perspective

For many Jewish parents, particularly fathers, the brit milah evokes complex emotions:

**Pride**: Bringing one's son into the covenant that has sustained the Jewish people through exile, persecution, and miraculous survival is profoundly meaningful.

**Anxiety**: No parent wants to see their child experience pain, even briefly. Yet most find the moment passes quickly, and the spiritual significance outweighs the temporary discomfort.

**Connection**: Standing where one's father, grandfather, and great-grandfather stood — continuing a practice Abraham performed four thousand years ago — creates a powerful sense of continuity and belonging.

**Responsibility**: The brit milah ceremony reminds parents that they are not merely raising a child but transmitting a heritage, welcoming a new link in an ancient chain.

## Conclusion: The Covenant That Endures

Brit milah represents one of Judaism's most distinctive and enduring practices. In an age when many traditions fade and religious observance declines, this ancient ritual continues to mark nearly every Jewish boy at eight days old — just as it has for nearly four millennia.

For Chinese readers encountering this practice, it offers a window into Jewish values: the belief that the physical and spiritual realms intertwine; that identity is not merely belief but embodied commitment; that God's covenant with humanity is intimate and permanent; and that each generation bears responsibility to transmit sacred traditions to the next.

Whether one sees brit milah as a beautiful expression of faith or a perplexing custom, its persistence testifies to the power of ritual to bind communities across time and space. The same ceremony that Abraham performed for Isaac (corresponding to China's Shang Dynasty) continues today in hospitals, homes, and synagogues worldwide — a living link between past, present, and future.

In the words of the blessing recited at every brit milah: "Just as he has entered the covenant, so may he enter into Torah, marriage, and good deeds." The physical mark becomes a spiritual promise — the beginning of a Jewish life dedicated to learning, family, community, and the ethical transformation of the world.

## Key Takeaways

- **Brit milah** is the covenant of circumcision performed on Jewish boys on the eighth day of life, commanded to Abraham nearly 4,000 years ago.
- The ceremony includes specific roles (mohel, sandek, kvatter) and rituals (blessings, naming, Elijah's chair) that have remained unchanged for millennia.
- **The eighth day** has both spiritual significance (transcending nature) and medical advantages (peak vitamin K levels).
- Circumcision symbolizes **partnership with God** in perfecting creation, **sanctification of physical desire**, permanent **Jewish identity**, and **generational continuity**.
- Jewish law prioritizes the baby's health above all — if the infant is ill, the ceremony is postponed indefinitely.
- The practice remains nearly universal in Jewish communities worldwide, combining ancient ritual with modern medical standards.
- Brit milah parallels Chinese values of ancestral continuity, ritual propriety, and the importance of welcoming new generations into community identity.
