# Antisemitism in the Modern World: From the Far Right to the Far Left

**Section: Antisemitism**

Many people assume antisemitism is primarily a phenomenon of the far right — neo-Nazis, white supremacists, and ultranationalists. While these groups certainly pose a major threat, contemporary antisemitism is more complex and widespread. It appears across the political spectrum, from extreme right to extreme left, in religious fundamentalism and secular ideologies, in democratic societies and authoritarian regimes.

Understanding modern antisemitism requires recognizing its many forms and how different political movements adapt age-old prejudices to contemporary contexts.

## Far-Right Antisemitism: The Traditional Threat

### White Supremacy and Neo-Nazism

The most recognizable form of contemporary antisemitism comes from white supremacist and neo-Nazi movements. These groups explicitly embrace Nazi ideology or similar racist frameworks:

**Core beliefs**: Jews are a separate, inferior race engaged in a conspiracy to destroy white European civilization through immigration, multiculturalism, and racial mixing.

**"Replacement theory"**: A major contemporary far-right conspiracy claims that Jews are orchestrating mass immigration to "replace" white populations in Western countries. The slogan "Jews will not replace us," chanted at the 2017 Charlottesville rally, made this explicit.

**Online radicalization**: Platforms like 4chan, 8kun, Gab, and Telegram host vibrant far-right antisemitic communities where conspiracy theories, memes, and calls for violence spread.

**Terror attacks**: Far-right antisemitism has motivated numerous deadly attacks:
- Pittsburgh Tree of Life Synagogue (2018): 11 murdered
- Poway Synagogue, California (2019): 1 murdered
- Halle Synagogue, Germany (2019): Attack failed but 2 others killed
- Multiple foiled plots targeting synagogues worldwide

**Rhetoric patterns**: Far-right antisemitism uses coded language — "globalists," "international bankers," "Hollywood elites," "cosmopolitans" — that allows antisemites to signal their prejudice while maintaining plausible deniability.

### Nationalist and Populist Movements

Not all right-wing antisemitism is explicitly Nazi. Some nationalist and populist movements incorporate antisemitic themes:

**Hungary under Viktor Orbán**: While maintaining relations with Israel, Orbán's government has run campaigns against George Soros (a Hungarian-born Jewish philanthropist) using antisemitic tropes about foreign manipulation and conspiracies.

**Poland's nationalism**: Some Polish nationalist rhetoric denies Polish complicity in Holocaust-era crimes and portrays discussions of Polish antisemitism as anti-Polish attacks.

**European far-right parties**: Parties like France's National Rally (formerly National Front), Austria's Freedom Party, and Germany's AfD contain antisemitic elements, though they sometimes try to appear pro-Israel to legitimize themselves.

**Conspiracy theories**: Theories about "Great Replacement," "Cultural Marxism," and "New World Order" often contain antisemitic elements, explicitly or implicitly blaming Jews for social changes nationalists oppose.

## Far-Left Antisemitism: The Blind Spot

While less commonly recognized, antisemitism exists on the far left, often disguised as anti-Zionism or anti-imperialism:

### Anti-Zionism Crossing Into Antisemitism

Not all criticism of Israel is antisemitic — Israel, like any country, can be legitimately criticized. But much far-left anti-Israel activism crosses into antisemitism when it:

**Denies Jewish self-determination**: Arguing that Jews alone among peoples have no right to a state.

**Applies double standards**: Holding Israel to standards applied to no other nation.

**Demonizes Israel**: Using Nazi comparisons, claiming Israel is "genocidal," or portraying it as uniquely evil.

**Holds diaspora Jews responsible**: Demanding that Jews worldwide denounce Israel or treating Jewish institutions as Israeli government proxies.

**Uses antisemitic tropes**: Accusing Israel or Zionists of "controlling" Western governments, media, or finance — echoing *Protocols of the Elders of Zion* conspiracy theories.

**Denies Jewish history**: Claiming Jews have no historical connection to Israel/Palestine or that they're "white colonizers" with no indigenous ties to the land.

### Examples of Far-Left Antisemitism

**UK Labour Party under Jeremy Corbyn**: Multiple investigations found institutional antisemitism within Labour, including tolerance for conspiracy theories about Jewish power, Holocaust minimization, and harassment of Jewish members. This led to an unprecedented intervention by the UK Equality and Human Rights Commission.

**BDS movement**: While supporters claim "Boycott, Divestment, Sanctions" merely opposes Israeli policies, critics note the movement:
- Often targets Jewish cultural events and institutions unrelated to Israeli politics
- Calls for Israel's elimination as a Jewish state
- Frequently employs antisemitic rhetoric
- Singles out the world's only Jewish state while ignoring severe human rights abusers

**Intersectionality weaponized**: Some far-left activists exclude Jews from progressive coalitions, claiming Jews are "too white" or "too privileged" to face real oppression, or that Zionism makes Jews complicit in oppression.

**Soviet-style anti-Zionism**: The Soviet Union pioneered "anti-Zionism" as a cover for state antisemitism, portraying Zionists as racist imperialists while persecuting Soviet Jews. This rhetoric persists in some far-left circles.

### Why Left-Wing Antisemitism Is Dangerous

Left-wing antisemitism is particularly insidious because:

**It's disguised**: Framed as human rights advocacy or anti-racism, making it harder to identify and challenge.

**It attracts well-intentioned people**: Many progressives oppose antisemitism in principle but inadvertently participate in it through certain anti-Israel activism.

**It isolates Jewish progressives**: Jews committed to social justice find themselves excluded from progressive spaces unless they denounce Israel or minimize antisemitism concerns.

**It undermines left-wing movements**: Tolerating antisemitism contradicts progressive values and alienates Jewish allies.

## Islamist Antisemitism

Islamic fundamentalism produces its own forms of antisemitism, distinct from both far-right and far-left varieties:

**Hamas Charter**: The founding document of Hamas (since revised but still influential) cited the *Protocols of the Elders of Zion* and described Jews as enemies of Islam.

**Hezbollah rhetoric**: Lebanese Hezbollah leader Hassan Nasrallah has stated, "If Jews all gather in Israel, it will save us the trouble of going after them worldwide."

**Iranian regime**: Iran's government regularly hosts Holocaust denial conferences, funds antisemitic propaganda, and calls for Israel's destruction while claiming to distinguish between Jews and Zionists.

**MEMRI documentation**: The Middle East Media Research Institute documents extensive antisemitic content in media, sermons, and textbooks across parts of the Muslim world, including:
- Blood libel accusations
- Holocaust denial
- Protocols of Zion citations
- Conspiracy theories about Jewish control

**Important distinction**: This is Islamist (fundamentalist) antisemitism, not Islamic or Muslim antisemitism generally. Many Muslims oppose antisemitism, and historical Muslim-Jewish relations were often better than Christian-Jewish relations. But contemporary Islamist movements have embraced antisemitic ideologies.

## Institutional and Social Media Antisemitism

### International Organizations

**United Nations**: The UN has passed more resolutions condemning Israel than all other countries combined, leading to accusations of institutional bias. The UN's 1975 "Zionism is racism" resolution (revoked 1991) exemplified how anti-Israel sentiment can adopt antisemitic framing.

**Human rights organizations**: Some groups apply different standards to Israel than to other countries, focusing disproportionate attention on Israeli actions while minimizing or ignoring comparable or worse violations elsewhere.

### Social Media Platforms

**Twitter/X**: Conspiracy theories about Jewish power, Holocaust denial, and harassment of Jewish users flourish, especially following Elon Musk's takeover and reduction of content moderation.

**TikTok**: Young users encounter antisemitic conspiracy theories, Holocaust denial disguised as "just asking questions," and anti-Israel content that crosses into antisemitism.

**Facebook/Instagram**: Despite policies against hate speech, antisemitic content spreads through groups, pages, and posts that evade detection.

**YouTube**: While major channels generally avoid overt antisemitism, the recommendation algorithm can lead users from mainstream content to antisemitic conspiracy theories.

**Telegram**: Unmoderated channels host extreme antisemitic content, including terrorist propaganda and neo-Nazi organizing.

## Conspiracy Theories and Antisemitism

Contemporary conspiracy theories frequently incorporate antisemitic elements:

**QAnon**: This wide-ranging conspiracy theory about a secret cabal of pedophiles controlling governments borrows heavily from antisemitic tropes, often targeting Jewish individuals.

**COVID-19 conspiracies**: Jews and Israel were blamed for creating or spreading COVID-19, echoing medieval accusations of well-poisoning.

**"Rothschild" theories**: The Rothschild banking family remains a favorite target for financial conspiracy theories despite being no more powerful than countless non-Jewish banking dynasties.

**"Soros" conspiracies**: George Soros is accused of funding everything from Black Lives Matter protests to immigration advocacy, portraying a single Jewish billionaire as controlling global events.

**Vaccine conspiracies**: Some anti-vaccine activists have blamed Jews or Israel for vaccination campaigns, connecting modern medical anxieties to traditional antisemitism.

## The "Both Sides" Problem

One disturbing aspect of contemporary antisemitism is that Jews face threats from multiple directions simultaneously:

- Far-right extremists see Jews as non-white racial enemies
- Far-left activists see Jews as white oppressors
- Islamist extremists see Jews as religious enemies
- Conspiracy theorists see Jews as secret puppet masters

This creates a unique vulnerability: unlike other minorities who primarily face threats from one political direction, Jews must defend against attacks from multiple, often opposing, ideological camps.

## Measuring Antisemitism

How widespread is contemporary antisemitism? Data provides some answers:

**Antisemitic incidents**: Organizations like the Anti-Defamation League (ADL) and Community Security Trust (UK) track incidents:
- Assaults, vandalism, and harassment have increased in many countries
- Spikes often follow Israeli-Palestinian conflicts
- Online harassment has dramatically increased

**Attitude surveys**: Polls in various countries show:
- Significant minorities hold antisemitic beliefs about Jewish power, loyalty, or money
- Younger generations sometimes hold antisemitic views while claiming to oppose all bigotry
- Antisemitic attitudes often correlate with conspiracy theory belief

**Campus antisemitism**: Jewish students report hostile environments at some universities, particularly regarding Israel-Palestine activism.

## Why Antisemitism Persists Across the Spectrum

Several factors explain antisemitism's political versatility:

**Adaptability**: Antisemitic tropes adapt to any ideology — Jews can be portrayed as capitalists or communists, nationalist or cosmopolitan, religious or secular, depending on what the antisemite opposes.

**Scapegoating utility**: Every political movement benefits from having enemies to blame. Jews' historical role as minorities in many societies makes them convenient scapegoats.

**Ignorance**: Many people absorb antisemitic stereotypes unconsciously through culture and don't recognize them as problematic.

**Israel's existence**: The Israeli-Palestinian conflict provides endless opportunities for antisemitism to disguise itself as political criticism.

**Conspiracy theory appeal**: Antisemitic conspiracy theories provide simple explanations for complex problems, appealing across the political spectrum.

## Fighting Modern Antisemitism

Combating contemporary antisemitism requires multifaceted approaches:

**Education**: Teaching about the Holocaust and antisemitism's history helps people recognize contemporary forms.

**Clear definitions**: The IHRA definition of antisemitism provides a framework for identifying when rhetoric crosses into bigotry.

**Platform accountability**: Social media companies must enforce policies against hate speech consistently.

**Political leadership**: Leaders across the spectrum must reject antisemitism within their movements.

**Interfaith/intergroup solidarity**: Building coalitions between Jews and other communities strengthens resistance to all forms of bigotry.

**Documentation**: Organizations tracking antisemitic incidents create evidence that demands response.

**Legal measures**: Hate crime laws and civil rights protections provide recourse for victims.

**Supporting victims**: Jews facing harassment need concrete support, not just sympathy.

## Conclusion

Modern antisemitism is not a relic of the past or the exclusive domain of one political faction. It appears in white supremacist terror attacks and far-left progressive spaces, in Islamist propaganda and mainstream conspiracy theories, on university campuses and United Nations committees.

This political versatility reflects antisemitism's deep historical roots and adaptability. For over two thousand years, antisemitism has morphed to fit prevailing ideologies and social anxieties, always finding new expressions while maintaining core themes of Jewish difference, power, and danger.

Fighting contemporary antisemitism requires recognizing it across the political spectrum — not to equate left and right antisemitism (they differ significantly in history, expression, and violence), but to refuse to tolerate it anywhere. When movements claim to fight oppression while harboring antisemitism, they betray their stated values.

The test of any progressive movement is whether it includes or excludes Jews. The test of any conservative movement is whether it scapegoats or protects minorities. Antisemitism is a litmus test for a society's moral health.

For those committed to justice, the message is clear: antisemitism must be confronted wherever it appears, whatever form it takes, regardless of which political tribe it comes from. Only then can we build societies truly committed to human dignity and equality.
