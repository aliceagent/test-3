# Shanghai Ghetto: How China Saved 20,000 Jewish Refugees

**Section: Antisemitism**

While six million Jews perished in the Holocaust, one city offered refuge when nearly all others closed their doors: Shanghai, China. Between 1938 and 1941, approximately 20,000 Jewish refugees fled Nazi persecution to this bustling port city, making it one of the few places in the world requiring no visa for entry.

The story of Shanghai's Jewish refugees represents both a bright spot in the darkest period of modern Jewish history and an important chapter in Chinese history. It's a story that deserves to be better known, especially in China, where it demonstrates Chinese humanity and hospitality during a time when the rest of the world turned its back.

## Why Shanghai?

By the late 1930s, escaping Nazi Germany was increasingly difficult. The 1938 Évian Conference, convened to address the refugee crisis, failed spectacularly — 32 countries attended, and almost all declined to increase immigration quotas for Jewish refugees. The United States, Britain, Canada, Australia, and most of Latin America maintained restrictive policies.

President Franklin D. Roosevelt summarized the prevailing attitude: "The trouble is, Jimmy, that if you let them in, they'll all want to come."

Meanwhile, Jews were desperate to leave. After *Kristallnacht* (November 1938), when Nazis destroyed thousands of Jewish businesses and synagogues, arrested 30,000 Jewish men, and sent them to concentration camps, the situation became urgent.

**Shanghai offered a unique advantage**: As an international settlement controlled by multiple foreign powers, it required no visa or official permission for entry. Anyone who could buy a boat ticket could go to Shanghai.

This wasn't a deliberate humanitarian policy — it was a bureaucratic quirk. But it became a lifeline.

## Early Jewish Communities in Shanghai

Jewish refugees didn't arrive in a vacuum. Shanghai already had two established Jewish communities:

**Sephardic Jews**: Beginning in the mid-1800s, Sephardic Jews (originally from Iraq, Syria, and India) settled in Shanghai. Families like the **Sassoons**, **Kadoories**, and **Hardoons** became immensely wealthy through trade, real estate, and industry. They built beautiful synagogues:

- **Ohel Rachel Synagogue** (1920): Built by Jacob Sassoon in memory of his wife, it still stands today
- **Beth Aharon Synagogue** (1927): Built by the Kadoorie family

These Sephardic Jews became pillars of Shanghai society, philanthropists, and business leaders.

**Russian Jews**: After the Russian Revolution (1917) and Russian Civil War, about 5,000 Russian Jews fled to Shanghai. They established a distinct community with their own synagogue, schools, and organizations.

When Central European Jewish refugees began arriving in 1938, these established communities provided crucial assistance — though the sheer numbers eventually overwhelmed their resources.

## The Great Escape (1938-1941)

**The journey**: Refugees traveled by whatever means possible:
- Trans-Siberian Railway through the Soviet Union
- Ships from European ports (Genoa, Trieste, Marseille)
- Some traveled by land through the Middle East, then by ship from ports like Haifa

The journey took weeks or months and was expensive, using families' last resources. Many refugees sold everything they owned to afford passage.

**Who came**: The refugees were primarily middle-class German and Austrian Jews — doctors, lawyers, musicians, engineers, merchants, teachers. They spoke German, dressed in European fashion, and had lived assimilated lives in Europe. Many were elderly or families with young children.

**Arrival**: Shanghai in 1938-1941 was already crowded, chaotic, and dealing with its own crises. The Japanese had invaded China in 1937, occupying Shanghai (except for the International Settlement and French Concession until December 1941). The city teemed with Chinese refugees fleeing the war, Western expatriates, and now Jewish refugees.

Despite these challenges, Shanghai absorbed 20,000 Jewish refugees — more than Canada, Australia, New Zealand, South Africa, and India combined accepted during this period.

## Life in Shanghai (1938-1941)

The first years in Shanghai were challenging but relatively free:

**Housing**: Initially, refugees stayed in camps set up by Jewish aid organizations like the International Committee for Granting Relief to European Refugees (IC), funded by wealthy Sephardic Jews and international Jewish organizations. Later, they rented rooms in the Hongkou district.

**Work**: Finding employment was difficult. Most refugees didn't speak Chinese or English, professional credentials weren't recognized, and the economy was strained. But refugees adapted:

- Doctors opened clinics
- Musicians formed orchestras and taught lessons
- Bakers opened cafes and bakeries
- Tailors, shoemakers, and craftsmen set up shops
- Teachers established schools

The refugees created a "Little Vienna" in Hongkou, with German-language newspapers, cafes serving schnitzel and strudel, and cultural institutions.

**Community organizations**: Refugees established synagogues, schools, hospitals, theaters, and mutual aid societies. The community published German newspapers, performed European theater, and maintained cultural life.

**Chinese relations**: Refugees lived side-by-side with Chinese residents in Hongkou, one of Shanghai's poorest districts. While language and cultural barriers existed, many refugees remembered Chinese neighbors as kind and helpful. Some refugees learned Chinese, employed Chinese staff, and formed genuine friendships.

Chinese vendors sold vegetables at refugee homes, Chinese craftsmen worked alongside European artisans, and Chinese and Jewish children played together in the streets.

## The Shanghai Ghetto (1943-1945)

After Japan attacked Pearl Harbor (December 1941), Japanese forces occupied all of Shanghai, including the International Settlement. The situation for Jewish refugees changed dramatically.

**The Proclamation**: In February 1943, under pressure from Nazi Germany (Japan's Axis ally), Japanese authorities issued a proclamation restricting "stateless refugees" (a code for Jews) to a "Designated Area" in Hongkou — about 1.5 square kilometers (0.6 square miles).

All Jewish refugees who'd arrived after 1937 had to move into this area or get special passes to leave for work. The area became known as the **Shanghai Ghetto**, though it differed significantly from Nazi ghettos in Europe.

**Differences from European ghettos**:
- Not sealed: Chinese residents lived alongside Jews; people could enter and exit (with passes)
- No mass starvation or deliberate murder
- Refugees could work outside the ghetto with passes
- Cultural and religious life continued
- Japanese authorities, while restricting refugees, didn't implement Nazi-style genocide

**Challenges**: Life in the ghetto was extremely difficult:
- Overcrowding: 20,000 people in tiny apartments, often multiple families per room
- Poverty: Most refugees had lost everything; they depended on aid or meager wages
- Disease: Dysentery, typhus, and other illnesses spread in unsanitary conditions
- Malnutrition: Food was scarce and expensive
- Anxiety: Constant fear of what might come next

**Passes and restrictions**: Refugees needed passes from Japanese authorities (issued by **Ghoya Kanoh**, called "the King of the Jews," a unpredictable official who arbitrarily granted or denied passes) to leave for work. This system was humiliating and anxiety-inducing but less deadly than Nazi restrictions.

**Why didn't Japan murder the Jews?**: Despite Nazi pressure, several factors protected Shanghai's Jews:

1. **No antisemitic tradition**: Japan had no history of antisemitism; Jews were exotic foreigners, not historical enemies.

2. **Different priorities**: Japan focused on military objectives, not ideology about Jews.

3. **Some Japanese officials opposed it**: Certain Japanese diplomats and officials actively resisted Nazi pressure.

4. **The Fugu Plan**: Some Japanese entertained a plan to use Jews' supposed influence (based on antisemitic stereotypes!) to gain American financial support — ironic, wrong, but it inadvertently protected Jews.

5. **Chinese resistance**: Chinese residents of Hongkou showed humanity toward Jewish neighbors, making mass murder logistically and politically difficult.

## Chinese Heroism: He Fengshan

One Chinese official deserves special recognition: **Dr. He Fengshan** (何凤山), Chinese Consul-General in Vienna (1938-1940).

When Austrian Jews desperately sought visas to escape after the Nazi *Anschluss* (annexation of Austria, March 1938), Dr. He issued thousands of Chinese visas to Shanghai, defying his own government's orders to stop. He acted on conscience and humanitarian principles, risking his career.

He was eventually recalled and reassigned, but his actions saved thousands of lives. After the war, he never spoke publicly about his heroism.

Only in 2000, after his death, did his children discover documents revealing his actions. In 2001, Yad Vashem (Israel's Holocaust memorial) honored Dr. He as **Righteous Among the Nations** — the highest honor for non-Jews who risked their lives to save Jews during the Holocaust.

Dr. He stands alongside Oskar Schindler and Raoul Wallenberg as a rescuer who embodies the best of humanity during the worst of times.

## Daily Life in the Ghetto

Despite hardship, refugees maintained human dignity and cultural life:

**Education**: Children attended schools teaching German, Hebrew, and Chinese. The Shanghai Jewish Youth Association (SJYA) provided activities for teenagers.

**Religion**: Multiple synagogues served different communities. Refugees observed Shabbat, holidays, and lifecycle events.

**Culture**: The community produced German-language newspapers, theater performances, and concerts. The Hongkou area had cafes, bakeries, and small businesses recreating European life.

**Sports**: Refugees organized soccer teams and sports clubs.

**Romance**: Young people dated, fell in love, and married. Children were born even in the ghetto.

**Mutual aid**: Refugees helped each other through charity organizations, lending circles, and informal networks.

These activities weren't frivolous — they were acts of resistance against dehumanization and despair.

## Liberation (1945)

In August 1945, after atomic bombs fell on Hiroshima and Nagasaki, Japan surrendered. The war ended, and refugees were freed from restrictions.

But liberation brought new challenges:

**Learning the truth**: As news of the Holocaust reached Shanghai, refugees learned that families left behind had been murdered. The joy of survival mixed with devastating grief.

**Statelessness**: As "stateless refugees," they had no country to return to. Germany and Austria were destroyed and had been homes of their murderers anyway.

**Civil War**: China descended into civil war between Nationalists and Communists, making Shanghai increasingly unstable.

## After Shanghai: Where Did They Go?

Between 1945-1950, most refugees left Shanghai:

**United States**: About 10,000 immigrated to the U.S.

**Israel**: Several thousand went to the newly established State of Israel (1948).

**Australia, Canada, South America**: Others found new homes worldwide.

**Some stayed**: A small number remained in Shanghai, especially those who'd married or established businesses. But after the Communist victory (1949), most eventually left.

**Return to Europe**: Very few returned to Germany or Austria. There was nothing to return to.

## The Physical Legacy

In Shanghai today, several sites commemorate this history:

**Ohel Moishe Synagogue** (now Shanghai Jewish Refugees Museum): Located in the former ghetto area, this museum documents the refugee experience with photographs, artifacts, and survivor testimony.

**Ohel Rachel Synagogue**: Still standing, beautifully maintained, occasionally hosting Jewish services.

**Former addresses**: Buildings where refugees lived, many still standing, bear plaques.

**Monuments**: Memorials honor the refugees and Chinese rescuers.

The Shanghai government has invested in preserving this history, recognizing it as a source of pride — a time when Shanghai offered refuge when others didn't.

## The Human Legacy

Many Shanghai refugees went on to achieve great things:

- **Michael Blumenthal**: U.S. Treasury Secretary under President Carter
- **Shaul Eisenberg**: Israeli businessman and philanthropist
- **Peter Max**: Famous pop artist
- **Hans Jacobsohn**: Photographer who documented refugee life in Shanghai

But more importantly, they survived, raised families, and rebuilt lives. Today, thousands of descendants of Shanghai refugees live worldwide — children, grandchildren, and great-grandchildren who exist because Shanghai opened its doors.

## Why This Matters for Chinese Readers

This history should be a source of pride for Chinese people:

1. **China saved 20,000 lives** when almost no one else would.

2. **Ordinary Chinese people showed humanity** to desperate strangers who didn't speak their language or share their culture.

3. **Dr. He Fengshan** represents Chinese courage and moral leadership.

4. **Shanghai's openness** stood in stark contrast to Western nations' closed doors and racial quotas.

At a time when China itself faced brutal Japanese occupation and immense suffering, Chinese people still found it in their hearts to share space, food, and kindness with Jewish refugees.

This wasn't charity from a position of strength — it was solidarity among the suffering.

## Contemporary Echoes

Today, as refugee crises unfold worldwide, the Shanghai story offers lessons:

- **Geography matters**: Sometimes helping isn't about resources but simply opening doors
- **Ordinary people make a difference**: Chinese neighbors who sold vegetables, landlords who rented rooms, children who played together — all contributed
- **Bureaucratic courage saves lives**: Officials like Dr. He who bend rules for humanitarian reasons are heroes
- **Cultural exchange enriches both sides**: The Jewish refugees brought European culture, cuisine, and skills to Shanghai; they learned from Chinese culture in turn

## Conclusion

The Shanghai Ghetto was not Paradise — it was crowded, poor, and difficult. But it was survival. While six million Jews perished in Europe, 20,000 survived in Shanghai because China provided refuge.

This history connects Chinese and Jewish peoples in a profound way. Jews remember Shanghai with deep gratitude. The phrase "Shanghai saved my family" echoes through generations.

For Jewish people, Shanghai represents hope in humanity's darkest hour. For Chinese people, it should represent a proud chapter in a difficult time — a moment when, despite facing invasion and occupation, Chinese society showed its better nature.

As we confront contemporary challenges — refugees, xenophobia, rising nationalism — the Shanghai story reminds us: opening doors saves lives, ordinary people can be heroes, and humanity transcends nationality, language, and culture.

The refugees who found safety in Shanghai never forgot. Their descendants haven't forgotten. And China shouldn't forget either — this is a history worth celebrating, preserving, and learning from.

**谢谢上海** — Thank you, Shanghai. From the Jewish people, then and now.
