# Judaism and Islam: Shared Roots and Important Differences

When Muslims and Jews meet today, they often discover surprising connections. Both communities pray facing a specific direction, avoid pork, circumcise their sons, and follow detailed dietary laws. These aren't coincidences—they reflect a shared heritage that stretches back thousands of years.

## The Common Foundation

Judaism and Islam are sister religions in the truest sense. Both trace their origins to Abraham (Ibrahim in Arabic), who left his homeland to follow the call of one God. Jews descend from Abraham through his son Isaac, while Arabs—and by extension the early Muslim community—trace lineage through Abraham's son Ishmael.

This shared ancestry creates a theological kinship. Both religions are strictly monotheistic, rejecting any compromise on the oneness of God. The Islamic declaration "There is no god but God" (La ilaha illallah) echoes the Jewish Shema: "Hear O Israel, the Lord is our God, the Lord is One" (Deuteronomy 6:4). Both communities fought against paganism and idolatry in their early histories.

## Law and Life: Remarkable Similarities

### Dietary Laws

Jewish kashrut and Islamic halal share fundamental principles. Both prohibit pork, require proper slaughter methods, and forbid consuming blood. In fact, many observant Jews living in areas without kosher facilities will eat halal meat, recognizing the similarities in slaughter requirements. Both traditions require pronouncing God's name before slaughter and ensure the animal's swift, humane death.

The Quran explicitly permits Muslims to eat food prepared by "People of the Book"—a category that includes Jews (Quran 5:5). This reflects Islam's recognition of Judaism's legitimate divine origin.

### Prayer Practice

Both communities pray multiple times daily according to fixed times. Muslims pray five times (salat); traditional Jews pray three times (shacharit, mincha, ma'ariv). Both religions require ritual washing before prayer. Muslims perform wudu; Jews wash hands (netilat yadayim).

The physical act of prayer shows similarities too. Muslims prostrate during salat; Jews traditionally bowed deeply and prostrated on Yom Kippur and other occasions (a practice preserved by some communities today). Both face toward a holy site: Muslims toward Mecca, Jews toward Jerusalem.

### Ritual Purity and Modesty

Islamic concepts of tahara (purity) closely parallel Jewish ideas. Both traditions address menstrual purity, require ritual immersion in certain circumstances, and emphasize modest dress. The Islamic hijab and Jewish practice of married women covering their hair (practiced in Orthodox communities) stem from similar values around modesty and privacy.

### Life Cycle

Circumcision on the eighth day is commanded in Genesis 17:12 for Jews; Muslims typically circumcise boys during childhood. Both traditions prohibit cremation and require burial soon after death. Both have detailed mourning practices and emphasize visiting the sick (bikur cholim in Hebrew, iyadat al-marid in Arabic).

## Key Theological Differences

Despite these similarities, fundamental differences distinguish the two faiths.

### Revelation and Scripture

Jews believe the Torah was given to Moses at Mount Sinai around 1300 BCE. This revelation is unique and final for the Jewish people. Muslims believe the Quran was revealed to Muhammad beginning in 610 CE, representing God's final and perfect revelation to all humanity.

Judaism holds that the Written Torah (Five Books of Moses) and Oral Torah (later codified in the Talmud) together form divine teaching. Islam considers the Quran alone as the pure word of God, though the Hadith (sayings of Muhammad) provide crucial guidance.

### The Role of Peoplehood

Judaism combines religion and peoplehood. One is born Jewish or converts into the Jewish people. Being Jewish is both a religious and ethnic identity. There's a strong connection to a specific language (Hebrew), land (Israel), and historical narrative.

Islam, by contrast, is purely a religious identity that transcends ethnicity and nationality. Anyone from any background who declares the Shahada becomes Muslim. There's no Islamic "ethnicity" comparable to Jewish peoplehood. The Quran explicitly states, "O mankind, indeed We have created you from male and female and made you peoples and tribes that you may know one another" (49:13), emphasizing universal brotherhood under God.

### Chosen People vs. Universal Mission

The Torah describes Israel as "a kingdom of priests and a holy nation" (Exodus 19:6). Jews see themselves as having a unique covenant with God, though this doesn't imply superiority—rather, special obligations. Judaism doesn't seek converts aggressively; the Noahide Laws provide a righteous path for non-Jews.

Islam teaches that Muslims have been given the final revelation meant for all humanity. The Quran states, "You are the best nation produced for mankind" (3:110), referring to the Muslim community's role in spreading God's message globally. Islamic tradition includes active dawah (invitation to Islam).

### Law and Authority

Jewish law (halacha) evolved through rabbinical interpretation across centuries, recorded in the Talmud and later codes like the Shulchan Aruch. Different rabbinical authorities can disagree, and multiple valid opinions often coexist. Judaism has no single global authority; it's deliberately pluralistic within bounds.

Islamic law (sharia) derives from the Quran and Hadith, interpreted by schools of jurisprudence (madhabs). While different schools exist (Hanafi, Maliki, Shafi'i, Hanbali in Sunni Islam), there's generally less official tolerance for disagreement on fundamental matters. Some Muslim-majority countries implement sharia as state law—something impossible in Judaism, which separated religious and civil authority after the Temple's destruction in 70 CE.

### Messianic Vision

Jews await a Messiah (Mashiach) who will be a human leader, descendant of King David, who will rebuild the Temple, gather the exiles, and bring world peace. This hasn't happened yet.

Muslims believe Jesus (Isa) was the Messiah but not divine—a prophet whose message was later distorted. They await Jesus's return alongside the Mahdi to establish justice before the Day of Judgment. Muslims also revere Muhammad as the "Seal of the Prophets"—the final messenger.

## Historical Relations: Complex and Varied

Jewish-Muslim relations have been complex. During Islam's golden age (roughly 700-1200 CE), Jews in Muslim lands often fared better than Jews in Christian Europe. Under Islamic rule in Spain, Jews served as doctors, poets, philosophers, and government officials. Maimonides, Judaism's greatest medieval scholar, wrote in Arabic and served as physician to the Sultan.

However, Jews in Muslim lands were dhimmis—protected but second-class citizens who paid special taxes and faced restrictions. Treatment varied by time and place, from relatively tolerant to oppressive.

The Israeli-Palestinian conflict has deeply strained Muslim-Jewish relations in modern times, often conflating political disputes with religious identity. Yet many Muslims and Jews work together for peace, recognizing that political conflict shouldn't erase centuries of coexistence and shared values.

## Conclusion: Respect Through Understanding

For Chinese readers encountering both traditions, understanding their relationship is crucial. Judaism and Islam are more similar to each other than either is to Chinese traditional religions or Christianity. Both emphasize orthopraxy (correct practice) over orthodoxy (correct belief), law over abstract theology, and community responsibility over individual salvation.

Recognizing both the profound similarities and real differences allows for respectful interfaith dialogue. As Maimonides wrote in his legal code, Muslims are pure monotheists with whom Jews can pray and study. This recognition—that two communities can worship the same God in different ways while maintaining distinct identities—offers a model for religious coexistence in our interconnected world.
