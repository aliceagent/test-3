# Mizrachi Jews: The Forgotten Third Stream

When people speak of Jewish diversity, they often mention Ashkenazi and Sephardi traditions. But this binary oversimplifies Jewish history. There exists a third major stream, often overlooked: Mizrachi Jews — the ancient communities of the Middle East and Central Asia who never left the region where Judaism was born.

The term "Mizrachi" comes from the Hebrew word "mizrach" (מִזְרָח), meaning "east." These are Jews from Iraq, Iran, Kurdistan, Yemen, Afghanistan, Uzbekistan, Georgia, and other regions east of the Land of Israel. Their traditions are distinct from both Ashkenazi (European) and Sephardi (Spanish) practice, preserving customs that, in some cases, date back to the Babylonian Talmud itself.

For Chinese readers, understanding Mizrachi Jews opens a window into a Judaism that never passed through Europe, never encountered the Enlightenment in its Western form, and maintained continuous presence in lands where Jews had lived since the Babylonian exile 2,500 years ago.

## Who Are Mizrachi Jews?

The category "Mizrachi" is itself complex, encompassing diverse communities:

**Iraqi Jews**: Perhaps the oldest continuous Jewish diaspora community, Iraqi Jews trace their presence to the Babylonian exile in 586 BCE. Baghdad was home to the great Talmudic academies that produced the Babylonian Talmud. Iraqi Jewish traditions are considered among the most authentic preservations of ancient Babylonian practice.

**Persian (Iranian) Jews**: Jews have lived in Persia since the Achaemenid Empire (6th century BCE) — the story of Esther takes place in Persia. Persian Jews developed unique customs influenced by Persian culture while maintaining distinct identity. The city of Isfahan once had thriving Jewish quarters.

**Yemenite Jews**: Perhaps the most isolated Jewish community, Yemenite Jews (Teimanim) preserved extraordinarily ancient traditions. Their Hebrew pronunciation is considered closest to the original ancient Hebrew. Their Torah scrolls are written differently, and their liturgy includes texts found nowhere else. They lived in Yemen for over 2,000 years, possibly since the First Temple period.

**Kurdish Jews**: Jews from Kurdistan (regions of modern Iraq, Iran, Turkey, and Syria) had unique Aramaic dialects and customs. Many lived as villagers and shepherds, unlike the urban Jews of Baghdad or Damascus.

**Bukharian Jews**: From Uzbekistan, Tajikistan, and surrounding Central Asian regions, Bukharian Jews spoke Judeo-Tajik (a Persian dialect) and developed distinctive colorful traditional clothing and architectural styles.

**Georgian Jews**: Jews in Georgia (in the Caucasus) claim presence since the Babylonian exile. They maintained unique traditions influenced by Georgian culture, including distinctive alphabets for writing Judeo-Georgian.

**Afghan Jews**: A small community with Persian-influenced traditions, now largely emigrated.

## Why "Mizrachi" Isn't "Sephardi"

Many mistakenly lump Mizrachi Jews with Sephardim. The confusion arose because:

1. **Sephardi Chief Rabbinate in Israel**: When Israel was established, it created two chief rabbinates: Ashkenazi and Sephardi. All non-Ashkenazi Jews were administratively placed under the Sephardi rabbinate, even though many had no connection to Spain.

2. **Liturgical Influence**: After the expulsion from Spain (1492), some Sephardi refugees settled in Middle Eastern cities, bringing Sephardi liturgy and customs that influenced local communities. Over time, some Mizrachi communities adopted Sephardi prayer books.

3. **Common Ground**: Both Mizrachi and Sephardi communities follow Babylonian Talmudic tradition (unlike Ashkenazim who followed Palestinian tradition initially), creating some overlap.

However, the differences are significant:

**Distinct Languages**: Sephardim spoke Ladino (Judeo-Spanish). Mizrachi Jews spoke Judeo-Arabic, Judeo-Persian, Judeo-Aramaic, Bukharian (Judeo-Tajik), and other languages with no Spanish component.

**No Spanish Connection**: Mizrachi communities never lived in Spain and don't share the Sephardi historical experience of the Spanish golden age, the Inquisition, or the expulsion.

**Ancient vs. Medieval Traditions**: Many Mizrachi customs predate the development of Sephardi tradition. Yemenite practices, for example, are more ancient than medieval Spanish customs.

**Cultural Influences**: Sephardi culture was influenced by Islamic Spain and later by Ottoman, North African, and Mediterranean cultures. Mizrachi culture was shaped by Persian, Kurdish, Central Asian, and Arabian Peninsula influences.

## Distinctive Mizrachi Customs

### Prayer and Liturgy

**Yemenite Nusach**: Yemenite prayer tradition (called Baladi) is considered extremely ancient, possibly preserving versions of prayers from before the final editing of standard siddurim. Yemenites read Torah with a unique cantillation system different from both Ashkenazi and Sephardi traditions.

**Iraqi Tradition**: Iraqi Jews maintained what many scholars consider the most authentic Babylonian tradition, since they lived in Babylon itself. Their prayer melodies and customs are distinctive, using Middle Eastern musical modes.

**Persian Customs**: Iranian Jews had unique piyyutim (liturgical poems) in Judeo-Persian and distinctive melodies influenced by Persian classical music.

### Pronunciation of Hebrew

**Yemenite Hebrew**: Considered by linguists to be closest to ancient Hebrew pronunciation, Yemenite Hebrew distinguishes between sounds that Ashkenazi and Sephardi pronunciations merged. For example, they pronounce the letter ג (gimel) with a soft sound after certain vowels, preserving an ancient distinction.

**Iraqi Pronunciation**: Iraqi Jews also preserved ancient distinctions, with pronunciation differing from modern Israeli (mostly Sephardi-based) Hebrew.

### Torah Scrolls

**Yemenite Sefer Torah**: Yemenite Torah scrolls look different from Ashkenazi and Sephardi scrolls. The script (called "Temaani") has distinctive letter forms. The parchment preparation and scribal traditions follow unique ancient standards.

### Food Traditions

Mizrachi cuisine reflects Middle Eastern and Central Asian influences:

**Iraqi Jewish Food**: 
- T'bit (Shabbat overnight rice and chicken dish)
- Kubba (semolina dumplings with meat filling)
- Hamin (Iraqi cholent with chicken, chickpeas, and rice)
- Maqluba (upside-down rice and vegetable dish)

**Yemenite Food**:
- Jachnun (rolled dough baked overnight for Shabbat)
- Malawach (flaky flatbread)
- Hilbeh (fenugreek relish)
- Zhug (spicy herb paste)

**Persian Jewish Food**:
- Gondi (chickpea-flour and chicken dumplings)
- Tahdig (crispy rice)
- Ghormeh sabzi (herb and kidney bean stew)

**Bukharian Food**:
- Plov (Central Asian rice pilaf)
- Samsa (filled pastries)
- Non (traditional bread)

These dishes are worlds apart from Ashkenazi gefilte fish or Sephardi Mediterranean mezze, reflecting different culinary traditions.

### Life Cycle Customs

**Henna Ceremonies**: Many Mizrachi communities, particularly Iraqi, Yemenite, and Persian Jews, celebrated weddings with elaborate henna ceremonies where the bride's hands and feet were decorated with henna. This custom, shared with surrounding Muslim populations, was distinctly Middle Eastern.

**Mimouna**: While Mimouna is often described as a Moroccan (Sephardi) custom, similar post-Passover celebrations existed in other Mizrachi communities, suggesting ancient shared roots.

**Bar Mitzvah Traditions**: Some communities had unique coming-of-age customs. Yemenite bar mitzvahs might include the boy chanting from the Torah in the distinctive Yemenite cantillation.

## Historical Experience

Mizrachi Jewish history differs dramatically from Ashkenazi and Sephardi narratives:

**Ancient Presence**: Many Mizrachi communities date to the Babylonian exile (586 BCE) or earlier. Unlike European or Spanish Jewish communities that were established later, Mizrachi Jews never left the Middle East.

**Dhimmi Status**: Under Islamic rule, Jews (and Christians) were "dhimmis" — protected minorities with second-class status but legal protection. This system, while discriminatory by modern standards, allowed Jewish communities to survive for over a millennium. Relations with Muslim neighbors were complex: sometimes cooperative, sometimes marked by persecution, but generally allowing Jewish communal life.

**Relative Isolation**: Communities like Yemenite Jews lived in relative isolation for centuries, preserving ancient customs precisely because they had less contact with Jews elsewhere.

**Modern Upheaval**: The 20th century brought dramatic change. With the rise of Arab nationalism, the establishment of Israel, and regional conflicts, most Mizrachi Jewish communities were displaced:

- Iraqi Jews were forced to flee in the early 1950s, leaving behind ancient communities.
- Yemenite Jews were airlifted to Israel in "Operation Magic Carpet" (1949-1950).
- Iranian Jews left in waves after the 1979 Islamic Revolution.
- Kurdish, Georgian, and Bukharian Jews emigrated mostly to Israel.

Today, these ancient Middle Eastern communities have largely ceased to exist in their original locations. Most Mizrachi Jews now live in Israel, with smaller communities in Europe and North America.

## Mizrachi Jews in Modern Israel

Mizrachi Jews constitute a significant portion of Israeli society — by some estimates, over 40% of Israeli Jews have Mizrachi ancestry. Their integration into Israeli society was complex:

**Initial Discrimination**: Early Israeli society, dominated by European Jewish leadership, often discriminated against Mizrachi immigrants. They were settled in development towns, faced economic challenges, and sometimes experienced cultural condescension from Ashkenazi elites.

**Cultural Preservation and Loss**: Some Mizrachi customs were preserved; others faded. The Israeli education system, Ashkenazi-dominated initially, didn't always value Mizrachi traditions. Judeo-Arabic languages largely gave way to Hebrew.

**Cultural Renaissance**: In recent decades, Mizrachi culture has experienced revival. Mizrachi music, food, and religious customs are now celebrated and recognized as authentic Jewish heritage. Politicians, musicians, and religious leaders of Mizrachi background have gained prominence.

**Religious Authority**: Mizrachi religious authorities, once sidelined, now have significant influence. Yemenite, Iraqi, and other Mizrachi rabbis contribute to contemporary halachic discussions, bringing ancient traditions into modern discourse.

## What Mizrachi Jews Teach Us

**Judaism's Geographic Breadth**: Judaism isn't just a European or Mediterranean religion. It's Middle Eastern at its core, and Mizrachi communities remind us of this.

**Diversity Within Unity**: The existence of dramatically different Jewish traditions — all claiming legitimacy from ancient sources — shows how diverse authentic Judaism can be.

**Ancient Authenticity**: Mizrachi communities, especially Yemenites and Iraqis, preserve practices that may be closer to ancient Judaism than Ashkenazi or Sephardi customs, offering windows into how Jews prayed and lived millennia ago.

**Cultural Adaptation**: Like Chinese Jewish communities who adapted Judaism to Chinese cultural contexts, Mizrachi Jews show how Judaism can maintain core identity while absorbing Persian, Kurdish, or Central Asian cultural elements.

For Chinese readers exploring Judaism, Mizrachi Jews represent a fascinating parallel: ancient communities maintaining religious identity within Middle Eastern and Central Asian cultural contexts, much as Jews in Kaifeng once maintained Jewish identity within Chinese culture.

The story of Mizrachi Jews is not one of a lost footnote, but of a vital stream of Jewish tradition deserving recognition alongside Ashkenazi and Sephardi heritage. They are the keepers of ancient flames, bridges connecting modern Jews to the Babylonian academies and the biblical heartland.
