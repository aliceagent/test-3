# Rebuilding the Holy Temple: What Would It Look Like?

**Section: Messiah**

The rebuilding of the Holy Temple in Jerusalem is central to Jewish messianic belief. It appears in daily prayers, messianic criteria, and prophetic visions. But what exactly would this Third Temple be? Where would it stand? What would happen there? And why does it matter to Jews today, especially those who've never seen a Temple?

## The First Two Temples: A Brief History

### The First Temple (Solomon's Temple)

Built by King Solomon around 950 BCE, the First Temple stood for approximately 410 years. It was the center of Jewish worship, housing the Ark of the Covenant in its inner sanctum, the Holy of Holies. The Kohanim (priests) offered daily sacrifices, and Jews from across Israel made pilgrimages three times yearly.

The Temple was magnificent—built with cedar from Lebanon, overlaid with gold, featuring intricate carvings and craftsmanship. It represented God's presence dwelling among the people.

In 586 BCE, the Babylonians under Nebuchadnezzar destroyed the Temple, burned Jerusalem, and exiled the Jewish people. The Ark of the Covenant disappeared, never to be recovered.

### The Second Temple

Seventy years later, as prophesied by Jeremiah, Jews returned from Babylonian exile and rebuilt the Temple under Zerubbabel's leadership (completed around 516 BCE). This Second Temple was less grand than Solomon's and lacked the Ark, but it functioned similarly for nearly 500 years.

Around 20 BCE, King Herod massively renovated and expanded the Temple, creating the magnificent structure that stood during the time of the Mishnah's composition. This is the Temple Jesus knew, with its massive plaza (the Temple Mount), towering colonnades, and impressive gates.

In 70 CE, the Romans destroyed the Second Temple during the Great Jewish Revolt. Only the Western Wall (Kotel) of the retaining wall remains—the site where Jews pray today.

## The Third Temple: What Do We Know?

### Biblical Sources

**Ezekiel's Vision** (Chapters 40-48)

The most detailed Temple description comes from the Prophet Ezekiel, who received an elaborate vision around 573 BCE, while in Babylonian exile after the First Temple's destruction. 

Ezekiel describes:
- Precise measurements for gates, courtyards, and chambers
- The Temple building itself, with its Holy Place and Holy of Holies
- The altar for sacrifices
- Chambers for priests
- The flow of water from beneath the Temple that becomes a healing river
- The division of the land among the tribes around the Temple

Some measurements differ from Solomon's Temple, leading to debate: Does Ezekiel describe the Second Temple (which was much simpler) or a future Third Temple?

Traditional interpretation: Ezekiel's vision was partially fulfilled in the Second Temple but will be completely fulfilled in the Third Temple built by the Messiah.

**Other Prophetic References**

- **Isaiah 2:2-3**: "In the days to come, the Mount of the LORD's house shall stand firm above the mountains... and all the nations shall gaze on it with joy. And many peoples shall go and say, 'Come, let us go up to the Mount of the LORD, to the House of the God of Jacob.'"

- **Zechariah 6:12-13**: "Thus said the LORD of Hosts: Behold, a man called the Branch shall branch out from the place where he is, and he shall build the Temple of the LORD... He shall assume majesty and shall sit on his throne and rule."

- **Haggai 2:9**: "The glory of this latter House shall be greater than that of the former."

These passages establish that:
1. The Temple will be rebuilt
2. It will be a center for all nations, not just Israel
3. The Messiah (often called "Branch" or "Shoot" from David's line) will build it
4. It will surpass previous Temples in glory

### Talmudic and Rabbinic Discussions

The Talmud contains extensive discussions about Temple architecture, ritual, and function. Tractate Middot specifically describes the Second Temple's structure. While these describe the past, they preserve knowledge for the future.

Maimonides dedicates much of his Mishneh Torah to Temple laws—how sacrifices are offered, priestly duties, festival observances at the Temple, etc. Why? Because when the Temple is rebuilt, this knowledge will be needed.

He writes (Laws of the Temple 1:4): "It is a positive commandment to build a House for God, prepared for sacrifices to be offered within... as it states: 'And you shall make Me a sanctuary.'"

This commandment remains obligated, awaiting fulfillment.

## What Would It Look Like?

### Location

The Temple Mount in Jerusalem's Old City. Specifically, tradition holds that both previous Temples stood on the same rock—the Foundation Stone (Even HaShetiyah), now located inside the Dome of the Rock.

This creates an obvious practical problem: The Temple Mount currently houses the Dome of the Rock and Al-Aqsa Mosque, Islam's third holiest site. How this situation resolves is unknown—but if the Messiah brings world peace and universal recognition of God, presumably peaceful resolution becomes possible.

### Architecture

Based on Ezekiel, rabbinic sources, and tradition:

**The Temple Complex** would include:
- Multiple courtyards, separated by ascending levels
- Gates on the east, north, and south (not west, as the Holy of Holies is on the west)
- Chambers for various Temple functions (storing utensils, vestments, wood, wine, oil)
- The Court of Israel (for Jewish men)
- The Court of Women
- The Court of Priests (where the altar stands)

**The Temple Building** itself:
- The Ulam (entrance hall)
- The Heichal (Holy Place), containing the Menorah, Table of Showbread, and Incense Altar
- The Holy of Holies (Kodesh HaKodashim), a perfect cube where the High Priest enters once yearly on Yom Kippur

**The Altar**: A large structure in the Priests' Court where sacrifices are offered, with a ramp (not steps) leading up to it.

### Size

Ezekiel's Temple is larger than Solomon's. The entire complex would cover a substantial portion of the Temple Mount, though exact dimensions are debated due to interpretation of Ezekiel's measurements.

### Materials

Tradition suggests the finest materials:
- Stone, precisely cut and fitted without metal tools (based on the rule for Solomon's Temple)
- Gold and silver ornamentation
- Cedar and cypress wood
- Fine fabrics for curtains and priestly garments
- Precious stones

### Divine Presence

Unlike the Second Temple, which lacked the manifest divine presence that filled Solomon's Temple, the Third Temple will—according to prophecy—have God's glory return visibly.

Ezekiel 43:4-5 describes: "The glory of the LORD entered the House... and I saw that the glory of the LORD filled the House."

This restoration of manifest divine presence is what makes the Third Temple incomparable to anything that came before.

## What Would Happen There?

### Daily Service

The Temple would function as previous Temples did:

**Daily Offerings**: The Tamid sacrifice—a lamb offered every morning and evening, accompanied by meal offerings, wine libations, and incense.

**Shabbat and Festival Sacrifices**: Additional offerings on Sabbaths, new moons, and pilgrimage festivals.

**Individual Offerings**: People bringing sacrifices for various purposes—thanksgiving, sin offerings, voluntary offerings, etc.

**Priestly Blessing**: Kohanim blessing the people daily.

**Levitical Music**: Levites singing psalms and playing instruments during services.

### Pilgrimage Festivals

Three times yearly—Passover, Shavuot, and Sukkot—Jews would ideally travel to Jerusalem to celebrate at the Temple, as commanded in the Torah. This would resume in the messianic era.

Imagine millions of Jews from around the world (having been ingathered) coming to Jerusalem for festivals, creating an atmosphere of unity, joy, and worship.

### Universal Worship

Isaiah 56:7 states: "My House shall be called a house of prayer for all peoples."

The Third Temple will somehow accommodate universal worship. Gentiles who acknowledge the God of Israel will come to learn and pray. Zechariah 8:23 prophesies: "Ten men from nations of every tongue will take hold of every Jew by a corner of his cloak and say, 'Let us go with you, for we have heard that God is with you.'"

How exactly this works—whether non-Jews can enter certain courts, what their participation looks like—is subject to various opinions.

### The Priesthood

Kohanim (descendants of Aaron) would resume their service. Maimonides states that in the messianic era, Elijah will resolve questions about disputed lineages, clarifying who has authentic priestly descent.

The High Priest (Kohen Gadol) would be appointed, and priestly families would serve in rotation, as they did in Temple times.

### Modern Questions About Sacrifices

Many modern Jews, even observant ones, feel uncomfortable with animal sacrifice. How do we understand this in context of the Third Temple?

**Traditional view**: Sacrifices are God's commandment, not subject to our feelings. When the Temple stands and the Messiah leads, we'll understand their spiritual significance more fully.

**Some philosophical approaches**: 
- Sacrifices represent surrendering our animal nature to God
- They create a tangible experience of holiness
- The ritual provides structure for collective worship
- They remind us that approaching God requires intention and offering

Maimonides (Guide for the Perplexed 3:32) suggests God allowed sacrifices because ancient Israel was accustomed to this worship mode, channeling their instinct toward proper worship rather than idolatry. This raises questions about whether sacrifice is eternal or transitional.

Most Orthodox authorities maintain that sacrifices will resume as prescribed. Some Conservative and Reform thinkers suggest they might be symbolic or transformed in some way.

## Why It Matters Today

### Spiritual Center

The Temple represents God's presence dwelling among humanity. Its absence marks incompleteness in the world's spiritual structure. Its rebuilding means restoration of the divine-human connection in its fullest form.

### National Redemption

The Temple is inseparable from Jewish political sovereignty, security, and flourishing. Its rebuilding marks the full restoration of Jewish national life.

### Universal Significance

According to prophecy, the Temple will be a center for all nations to learn about and worship God. Its rebuilding marks humanity's spiritual coming-of-age.

### Continuity of Tradition

The Temple connects us to our ancestors—Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob; Moses and Aaron; David and Solomon; the prophets and sages. Rebuilding it continues the story that began at Sinai.

### Catalyst for Perfection

Maimonides suggests the Temple's rebuilding is connected to the messianic era's broader transformation—peace, knowledge of God, and human flourishing. The Temple is both symbol and catalyst for this change.

## Practical Preparations

Some groups in Israel today prepare for the Third Temple:

**The Temple Institute**: Creates authentic Temple vessels, priestly garments, and musical instruments according to biblical specifications. They maintain that when the Temple is ready to be built, everything must be prepared.

**Priestly Training**: Some Kohanim study Temple service laws and practice rituals to be ready when needed.

**Architectural Planning**: Various architects have created detailed plans based on Ezekiel's vision and rabbinic sources.

**Breeding Red Heifers**: Numbers 19 requires the ashes of a red heifer for purification. Some have tried to breed perfect red heifers in anticipation of Temple needs.

Are these efforts valid? Maimonides indicates the Messiah himself will build the Temple—human preparations may or may not be necessary. But the impulse to prepare shows the living nature of this hope.

## When Will It Happen?

Jewish tradition is clear: When the Messiah comes and is confirmed by accomplishing his tasks (gathering exiles, bringing peace), he will rebuild the Temple.

Some sources suggest miraculous elements—the Temple descending from heaven fully formed. Others emphasize human construction under messianic leadership. Many understand "divine construction" to mean "divinely ordained and guided construction by human hands."

Regardless, it will happen. Until then, our prayers repeatedly request: "May it be Your will, O LORD our God and God of our ancestors, that the Holy Temple be rebuilt speedily in our days."

## Conclusion

The Third Temple is not just a building—it's the physical manifestation of redemption, the center from which Torah and wisdom will spread to all nations, the place where divine presence dwells palpably among humanity.

We've never seen it, yet we pray for it daily. We don't fully understand how sacrifices work spiritually, yet we study their laws. We can't imagine how peaceful resolution of the Temple Mount will occur, yet we trust it will.

This is messianic faith in action: believing in a future we can barely envision, working toward a goal we may not achieve in our lifetimes, maintaining hope for the restoration of something we've only known through texts and tradition.

The Third Temple matters because it represents the world as it should be—a world where God's presence is recognized, where peace reigns, where Jerusalem is truly what its name means: the city of peace (Yerushalayim, from "yeru" - foundation, and "shalom" - peace).

May we merit to see it, speedily in our days.
