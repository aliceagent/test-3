# 412. Jewish Bookshelf Essentials: What Every Home Should Have

Walk into a traditional Jewish home, and you'll likely find a bookshelf—or several—filled with sacred texts, some worn from use, some pristine and waiting, some inherited from grandparents who brought them across oceans. This library isn't decorative; it's a working collection, consulted for daily prayers, holiday preparations, life cycle events, and study.

But if you're just starting to build a Jewish home, what books do you actually need? Which are essential, which are helpful, and how can you build this library affordably, especially in places like China where Jewish books may be hard to find?

## The Core Foundation: The Five Essentials

### 1. Chumash (חומש) - The Five Books of Moses

**What it is:** The Torah—Genesis, Exodus, Leviticus, Numbers, and Deuteronomy—with commentary. Called "Chumash" from the Hebrew word for "five."

**Why you need it:** This is the foundation of everything Jewish. It contains the Creation story, the lives of the Patriarchs and Matriarchs, the Exodus from Egypt, the giving of the Torah at Sinai, and the laws that govern Jewish life.

**What to look for:** A Chumash with English translation and commentary is essential. The most popular commentator is Rashi (Rabbi Shlomo Yitzchaki, 1040-1105), whose explanations are foundational to Jewish learning. The Stone Edition Chumash (ArtScroll) is widely used and includes Rashi's commentary in English alongside other traditional commentaries.

**Practical tip:** Get one with the Hebrew and English on facing pages. Even if you don't read Hebrew yet, seeing the original text connects you to the source. The weekly Torah portion (parashah) is read in synagogue each Shabbat, so having this at home lets you follow along and prepare.

**Affordable option:** Used copies are common. Digital versions like Sefaria (sefaria.org) are completely free and searchable.

### 2. Siddur (סידור) - Prayer Book

**What it is:** The daily and Shabbat prayer book, containing morning, afternoon, and evening prayers, plus blessings for various occasions.

**Why you need it:** Prayer is central to Jewish life. The Siddur contains the Shema (Judaism's central declaration of faith), the Amidah (the standing prayer said three times daily), and blessings for everything from bread to rainbows.

**What to look for:** Choose a Siddur that matches your community's tradition (Ashkenazi, Sephardi, or specific variations like Nusach Sefard used by Chassidic communities). Look for one with English translations and instructions.

Popular options:
- **ArtScroll Siddur** (Ashkenazi): Comprehensive, with detailed English translation and instructions
- **Koren Siddur** (available in multiple nusachot): Beautiful typography, includes transliteration
- **Sephardic Siddur**: If you follow Sephardic tradition

**Practical tip:** You might eventually want separate Siddurim for weekday, Shabbat, and holidays, but one comprehensive Siddur can start you off. Many families have multiple copies so everyone can pray together.

**Affordable option:** Basic Siddurim can be found inexpensively. Synagogues sometimes give them away. The website Chabad.org has prayers online for free.

### 3. Tehillim (תהלים) - Book of Psalms

**What it is:** The 150 Psalms written primarily by King David—poetry, prayers, songs expressing the full range of human emotion before God.

**Why you need it:** Psalms are recited during times of joy and distress, as part of daily prayers, before Shabbat, and in times of illness or need. Many Jews have the custom to complete the entire book monthly or weekly. The words of Tehillim have comforted Jews for three thousand years.

**What to look for:** A pocket-sized edition is traditional—something you can carry to the hospital, pull out during a difficult moment, or read from at any time. Editions with English translation help you understand these powerful words.

**Practical tip:** Psalm 23 ("The Lord is my shepherd"), Psalm 121 ("I lift my eyes to the mountains"), and Psalm 150 (the final psalm of praise) are good places to start. Many people find immense comfort in simply reading Psalms, even without formal prayer.

**Affordable option:** Tehillim editions are widely available and inexpensive. Digital versions are free.

### 4. Machzor (מחזור) - Holiday Prayer Book

**What it is:** Special prayer books for the High Holy Days (Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur) and sometimes for the three pilgrimage festivals (Passover, Shavuot, Sukkot).

**Why you need it:** The holiday liturgy is different from daily prayers—longer, with special piyyutim (liturgical poems) and unique prayers. The Yom Kippur Machzor, for instance, contains the moving Kol Nidrei prayer and the confessional prayers (Vidui).

**What to look for:** At minimum, get a Machzor for Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur. These are the holiest days of the year, and the prayers are profound. ArtScroll and Koren both publish excellent editions with extensive commentary explaining the deeper meanings.

**Practical tip:** These books are used only a few days a year but are emotionally and spiritually intense. Invest in a good edition with commentary that helps you connect to these prayers.

**Affordable option:** Machzorim hold up well—buy used. Synagogues often provide these during services, but having your own lets you prepare beforehand.

### 5. Haggadah (הגדה) - Passover Guide

**What it is:** The text read at the Seder (Passover ritual meal), telling the story of the Exodus from Egypt and guiding the evening's rituals.

**Why you need it:** Passover is one of Judaism's most observed holidays, and the Seder is a home-based ritual. You need a Haggadah—ideally, enough copies for everyone at your table.

**What to look for:** There are thousands of Haggadot (plural), from traditional to modern, illustrated to minimalist, child-friendly to scholarly. For a first Haggadah, choose one with:
- Clear instructions for the Seder order
- The full Hebrew text with English translation
- Some explanatory notes
- Ideally, transliteration if guests don't read Hebrew

**Practical tip:** Many families collect Haggadot over time. It's also customary to give them as gifts. Children's versions with colorful illustrations help engage young participants.

**Affordable option:** Basic Haggadot are very inexpensive. Free PDFs abound online. Some families print custom ones.

## The Second Tier: Building Your Library

Once you have the five essentials, consider adding:

### 6. Tanakh (תנ״ך) - The Complete Hebrew Bible

The full Hebrew Bible: Torah (Five Books of Moses), Nevi'im (Prophets), and Ketuvim (Writings)—hence the acronym TaNaKh. This includes books like Joshua, Judges, Samuel, Kings, Isaiah, Jeremiah, Psalms, Proverbs, Job, and more.

**Recommendation:** The Stone Edition Tanakh or Koren Tanakh.

### 7. Mishnah (משנה)

The foundational text of the Oral Law, compiled by Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi around 200 CE. It's organized into six orders covering agricultural laws, festivals, family law, damages, holy things, and purity.

**Why it matters:** The Mishnah is where you begin to understand how biblical law was interpreted and applied. It's the bridge between the written Torah and the Talmud.

**Recommendation:** The Kehati Mishnah (Hebrew with English translation and commentary) or the ArtScroll Mishnah.

### 8. Ethics and Philosophy

- **Pirkei Avot (Ethics of the Fathers)**: A tractate of the Mishnah containing ethical maxims. Short, profound, accessible—perfect for weekly study.
- **Kitzur Shulchan Aruch**: An abridged code of Jewish law covering daily life. Practical and readable.
- **Mesillat Yesharim (Path of the Just)** by Rabbi Moshe Chaim Luzzatto: A systematic guide to spiritual development and ethical perfection.

### 9. Talmud

The massive central text of Rabbinic Judaism—the Mishnah plus centuries of debate and commentary (Gemara). It comes in two versions: Babylonian Talmud (Bavli) and Jerusalem Talmud (Yerushalmi). The Babylonian Talmud is the standard, comprising 37 tractates across thousands of pages.

**Reality check:** The Talmud is not a beginner text. It's complex, written in Aramaic and Hebrew, and assumes extensive background knowledge. But having it in your home—even if you're not ready to learn it yet—is itself valuable. The complete Talmud is the crown jewel of a Jewish library.

**Recommendation:** The Koren Talmud Bavli (Hebrew with English translation and notes) or the Schottenstein Edition (ArtScroll) are excellent but expensive. The William Davidson Talmud is free on Sefaria.org.

### 10. Shabbat and Holiday Guides

Books explaining the laws and customs of Shabbat and festivals in accessible language. The ArtScroll "Shabbos" book and "The Jewish Home" by Rabbi Daniel Kohn are helpful.

## Building Your Library Affordably

**1. Start digital.** Sefaria.org is an incredible free resource with Torah, Talmud, Mishnah, commentaries, and more—all searchable, with translations.

**2. Buy used.** Jewish books hold up remarkably well. Check used bookstores, synagogue sales, or online marketplaces.

**3. Ask your community.** Synagogues often have libraries and sometimes give away duplicate copies.

**4. Prioritize wisely.** Don't buy everything at once. Start with the essentials, add books as you need them for study or observance.

**5. Quality over quantity.** Better to have five well-chosen books that you actually use than fifty sitting unread.

**6. For readers in China:** Consider e-books or print-on-demand if shipping costs are prohibitive. Some organizations ship Jewish books internationally at subsidized rates—Chabad emissaries may be able to help.

## The Living Library

In Chinese culture, books are treasured—a personal library reflects education, cultivation, and connection to tradition. The Jewish bookshelf serves the same function but goes further: these books aren't just read, they're lived with. They're consulted for practical questions ("How do I kasher my kitchen for Passover?"), studied for spiritual growth, and used for daily connection to God through prayer.

The Talmud (Shabbat 30b) teaches that one's home should be filled with books of wisdom. The Yiddish term for a well-stocked Jewish library is *shtender*—a reading stand piled with open books, always ready for study.

Your Jewish bookshelf will grow with you—acquiring new volumes for new stages of life, adding children's books when kids arrive, collecting commentaries as you dive deeper. Each book represents not just knowledge but connection to centuries of Jewish learning and to Jews around the world studying the same texts.

Start with the essentials, add gradually, and most importantly: actually open them. A Jewish library isn't a monument—it's a toolbox and a treasure map combined.
