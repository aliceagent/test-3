# The Holocaust: A Concise History for Chinese Readers

**Section: Antisemitism**

The Holocaust — called the *Shoah* (שואה, "catastrophe") in Hebrew — was the systematic, state-sponsored murder of six million Jews by Nazi Germany and its collaborators between 1933 and 1945. It represents one of history's worst atrocities: an industrialized genocide that sought to eliminate an entire people from existence.

For readers less familiar with European history, understanding the Holocaust is essential not just for knowing Jewish history, but for understanding how civilized societies can descend into barbarism, how propaganda and hatred operate, and why "never again" remains an urgent imperative.

## Background: Jews in Pre-War Europe

Before 1933, European Jews numbered approximately 9.5 million. They lived across the continent, with largest populations in Poland (3.3 million), the Soviet Union (3 million), and Romania (850,000).

Jewish communities had lived in Europe for over 2,000 years. By the early 20th century, many Jews were highly integrated into European society: doctors, lawyers, professors, artists, merchants, craftsmen. Some were wealthy, but most were working-class or poor. They spoke local languages, contributed to national cultures, and served in national militaries.

Yet antisemitism persisted. Jews faced legal discrimination, social prejudice, economic restrictions, and periodic violence (pogroms). This "background" antisemitism created the conditions for Nazi ideology to take root.

## Nazi Rise to Power (1933)

In January 1933, Adolf Hitler became Chancellor of Germany. The Nazi Party (National Socialist German Workers' Party) gained power through a combination of economic crisis (following World War I and the Great Depression), political instability, propaganda, and resentment over Germany's WWI defeat.

**Nazi ideology** centered on several core beliefs:

1. **Racial hierarchy**: "Aryan" (Northern European) peoples were superior; Jews were an inferior "race" that corrupted and weakened societies.

2. **Conspiracy theory**: Jews were blamed for Germany's problems — defeat in WWI, economic crisis, communism, capitalism, cultural "degeneracy," and plotting world domination.

3. **Social Darwinism**: Life was a struggle between races, and the "stronger" race must dominate or eliminate the "weaker."

4. **Lebensraum** ("living space"): Germany needed to expand eastward, conquering territory and enslaving or eliminating "inferior" populations.

These ideas weren't invented by Hitler — they drew on centuries of European antisemitism, 19th-century racial pseudoscience, and nationalist movements. But the Nazis systematized and weaponized them.

## Persecution Begins (1933-1939)

Nazi persecution of Jews unfolded gradually, testing how far they could go:

**1933**: Jewish businesses boycotted; Jews expelled from civil service, universities, and professions.

**1935**: The Nuremberg Laws stripped Jews of citizenship, forbade marriage between Jews and "Aryans," and legally defined who counted as Jewish (based on ancestry, not religion).

**1938**: *Kristallnacht* (Night of Broken Glass, November 9-10) — a nationwide pogrom where Nazi paramilitary and civilians destroyed thousands of Jewish businesses and synagogues, murdered about 100 Jews, and arrested 30,000 Jewish men, sending them to concentration camps.

**Throughout this period**: Jews faced increasing restrictions — banned from parks, theaters, schools; required to carry identity cards marked with "J"; forced to add "Israel" or "Sara" to their names; stripped of property and assets.

Many Jews fled Germany — about half of Germany's 500,000 Jews emigrated by 1939. But finding refuge was difficult. Most countries, including the United States, Britain, and others, imposed restrictive immigration quotas. The 1938 Évian Conference, meant to address refugee crises, failed when 32 countries declined to increase Jewish immigration.

**China's exception**: Shanghai was one of the few places Jews could reach without visas, leading to approximately 20,000 Jewish refugees settling there (covered in the next article).

## World War II Begins (1939-1941)

In September 1939, Germany invaded Poland, beginning World War II. Poland held Europe's largest Jewish population. With German conquest, 3.3 million Polish Jews came under Nazi control.

**Ghettos**: Nazis forced Jews into sealed-off urban districts called ghettos. The largest was the Warsaw Ghetto, where 400,000 Jews were confined in horrific conditions — overcrowding, starvation, disease. Ghettos served as holding areas before deportation to death camps.

**Einsatzgruppen** (mobile killing units): As Germany invaded the Soviet Union (June 1941), special SS units followed the army, massacring Jews by shooting. These units murdered an estimated 1.5 million Jews. At Babi Yar (near Kyiv, Ukraine), 33,771 Jews were shot over two days (September 1941).

This phase represented "the Holocaust by bullets" — mass murder through shooting rather than gas chambers.

## The "Final Solution" (1941-1945)

In January 1942, Nazi leaders met at the Wannsee Conference in Berlin to coordinate the "Final Solution to the Jewish Question" — bureaucratic language for systematic genocide of all European Jews.

**Death camps**: The Nazis built six main extermination camps in occupied Poland, designed specifically for mass murder:

1. **Auschwitz-Birkenau**: The largest, where approximately 1.1 million Jews were murdered
2. **Treblinka**: About 900,000 murdered
3. **Bełżec**: About 600,000 murdered
4. **Sobibor**: About 250,000 murdered
5. **Chełmno**: About 320,000 murdered
6. **Majdanek**: About 80,000 Jews murdered (also killed many Poles and Soviet POWs)

**The killing process**:

1. **Deportation**: Jews were rounded up from ghettos and occupied territories, told they were being "resettled" for work, and loaded onto overcrowded cattle cars for multi-day journeys without food, water, or sanitation. Many died in transit.

2. **Selection**: Upon arrival at camps like Auschwitz, SS doctors made instant decisions — able-bodied people sent to forced labor; elderly, children, sick, and mothers with young children sent immediately to gas chambers. About 75-80% were murdered immediately.

3. **Gas chambers**: Victims were told they'd shower and be deloused. They were herded into rooms, the doors sealed, and poison gas (Zyklon B at Auschwitz, carbon monoxide at other camps) released. Death took 15-20 minutes.

4. **Disposal**: *Sonderkommandos* (Jewish prisoners forced to do this work) removed corpses, extracted gold teeth, cut hair (used for industrial purposes), and burned bodies in crematorium ovens or open pits.

5. **Property theft**: All possessions were stolen — clothing, shoes, suitcases, even glasses and artificial limbs. This theft financed the Nazi war machine.

**Industrial efficiency**: The death camps represented industrialized murder. Auschwitz could kill 6,000 people per day. The entire process — from arrival to cremation — took hours. This wasn't spontaneous violence but carefully planned, bureaucratic genocide.

## Collaboration and Resistance

**Collaborators**: Nazi Germany didn't act alone. Local populations, police forces, and governments in occupied territories often collaborated:
- France's Vichy government deported tens of thousands of Jews
- Romanian forces massacred hundreds of thousands of Jews
- Ukrainian, Lithuanian, and Latvian collaborators participated in mass shootings
- Hungarian authorities deported 440,000 Jews in just eight weeks (1944)

**Resistance**: Despite overwhelming odds, Jews resisted:
- **Warsaw Ghetto Uprising (1943)**: Fighters held out for a month against German forces
- **Treblinka and Sobibor uprisings**: Prisoners revolted and some escaped
- **Partisan fighters**: Jews joined resistance movements in forests across Eastern Europe
- **Spiritual resistance**: Continuing religious practice, education, and documentation despite prohibitions

**Righteous Among the Nations**: Some non-Jews risked their lives to save Jews:
- **Oskar Schindler** (Germany): Saved 1,200 Jews by employing them in his factories
- **Raoul Wallenberg** (Sweden): Saved tens of thousands of Hungarian Jews through diplomatic protection
- **Irena Sendler** (Poland): Smuggled 2,500 Jewish children out of Warsaw Ghetto
- Thousands of others, including entire villages, hid Jewish neighbors

## Other Victims

While Jews were the primary target, Nazis also murdered:
- **Roma (Gypsies)**: 220,000-500,000 murdered
- **People with disabilities**: 250,000+ murdered in "euthanasia" programs
- **Polish civilians**: 1.8-2 million killed
- **Soviet POWs**: 3+ million killed
- **Political prisoners, homosexuals, Jehovah's Witnesses**: Tens of thousands killed

The Holocaust specifically refers to the Jewish genocide, but these groups also suffered Nazi persecution and murder.

## Liberation (1944-1945)

As Allied forces advanced, they liberated concentration camps:

- **Majdanek** (July 1944): First major camp liberated by Soviets
- **Auschwitz** (January 1945): Soviet forces found 7,000 survivors
- **Bergen-Belsen** (April 1945): British forces found 60,000 prisoners, many near death
- **Dachau** (April 1945): American forces liberated survivors

Liberators found piles of corpses, starving survivors, and evidence of systematic murder. Photos and films from liberation shocked the world and provided undeniable evidence of Nazi crimes.

## Death Marches

As camps were evacuated ahead of Allied advances, Nazis forced prisoners on "death marches" — walking hundreds of kilometers in winter without food or shelter. Those who couldn't keep up were shot. Tens of thousands died.

## The Aftermath

**Death toll**: Approximately 6 million Jews murdered — two-thirds of European Jewry, one-third of all Jews worldwide. This included 1.5 million children.

**Destruction of communities**: Entire Jewish civilizations were erased — Polish shtetls, Greek Sephardic communities, Hungarian Jewish life. Languages (Yiddish), cultures, and centuries of learning were destroyed.

**Survivors**: Jews who survived faced returning to homes where their families were murdered, their property stolen, and sometimes continuing antisemitism. Some survivors faced pogroms even after liberation (Kielce, Poland, 1946: 42 Jewish Holocaust survivors murdered).

**Displaced persons**: Hundreds of thousands of Jewish survivors lived in DP (displaced persons) camps for years, unable to return home and barred from immigrating to many countries.

**Justice**: The Nuremberg Trials (1945-1946) prosecuted major Nazi leaders. Many received death sentences or long prison terms, though many perpetrators escaped justice.

**Israel's founding**: The Holocaust contributed to international support for establishing a Jewish state. In 1948, Israel was created, offering survivors a homeland.

## Why It Happened

How did a civilized, educated society commit such atrocities? Multiple factors:

1. **Historical antisemitism**: Centuries of Christian anti-Jewish prejudice created the foundation
2. **Propaganda**: Constant dehumanization of Jews in media, education, and speeches
3. **Incrementalism**: Persecution escalated gradually, allowing normalization
4. **Bureaucratic murder**: Responsibility was diffused across many people, making it psychologically easier
5. **War**: Military conflict enabled extreme violence and dehumanization
6. **Obedience**: People followed orders from authority figures
7. **Bystander effect**: International inaction enabled the genocide to continue

## Why We Must Remember

**"Never again"** is more than a slogan. The Holocaust teaches:

- **Democracies can fail**: Germany was a developed, educated society
- **Prejudice kills**: Antisemitism wasn't just beliefs — it justified genocide
- **Silence enables violence**: Bystanders who did nothing allowed murder to continue
- **Propaganda works**: Constant dehumanization made ordinary people complicit
- **Vigilance is necessary**: Warning signs exist, and societies must respond

## Holocaust Denial

Disturbingly, some claim the Holocaust never happened or was exaggerated. This is false and offensive. The evidence is overwhelming:

- Nazi documents, orders, and records
- Survivor testimony from thousands of witnesses
- Perpetrator confessions and trial testimony
- Physical evidence of camps, mass graves, and murder weapons
- Film and photographic documentation
- Population statistics showing 6 million missing Jews

Holocaust denial is illegal in many countries and is recognized as antisemitic propaganda.

## China and the Holocaust

During World War II, as Europe's Jews were being murdered, Shanghai provided refuge for 20,000 Jewish refugees (covered in the next article). This represents one of the few bright spots in a dark chapter.

Chinese people sometimes ask: "Why didn't Jews fight back?" They did — but resistance against industrialized genocide by a modern military state is nearly impossible. The question itself can blame victims for their victimization.

The better question: "Why did so many people participate, collaborate, or remain silent?" That question challenges us all.

## Conclusion

The Holocaust was not inevitable. It resulted from specific choices made by individuals, governments, and societies. People chose to hate, to dehumanize, to kill, or to look away.

But others chose differently — to resist, to rescue, to document, to maintain human dignity against impossible odds.

The Holocaust stands as a warning: civilization is fragile, prejudice is dangerous, and "it can't happen here" is always a lie. Vigilance, moral courage, and commitment to human dignity are necessary in every generation.

For the six million, we remember. For the future, we say "never again" — and mean it.

**Kaddish** (Jewish mourner's prayer) is recited: "May their memory be a blessing." 

Let their memory also be a warning and a call to action against hatred in all its forms.
