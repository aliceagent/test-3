# What Is Antisemitism? A Clear Definition and Historical Overview

*Section: Antisemitism*

Antisemitism is hatred of Jews as Jews. This simple definition conceals extraordinary complexity, because antisemitism has shape-shifted across centuries and civilizations, adapting to different cultures and ideologies while maintaining a consistent core: the idea that Jews are fundamentally different, dangerous, and deserving of hostility.

For readers encountering this topic for the first time, especially those from cultures without a long history of Jewish presence, understanding antisemitism is essential not only for understanding Jewish history but also for recognizing patterns of prejudice that can affect any minority group.

## Defining Antisemitism

The International Holocaust Remembrance Alliance (IHRA) provides a working definition adopted by dozens of countries:

> "Antisemitism is a certain perception of Jews, which may be expressed as hatred toward Jews. Rhetorical and physical manifestations of antisemitism are directed toward Jewish or non-Jewish individuals and/or their property, toward Jewish community institutions and religious facilities."

This definition highlights several key points:

First, antisemitism is not about what Jews actually do or believe, but about what antisemites *perceive* them to be. It is a projection, a fantasy, a conspiracy theory that says more about the antisemite than about Jews.

Second, antisemitism targets not only individual Jews but also Jewish institutions, property, and anyone associated with Jews.

Third, antisemitism can be rhetorical (words, ideas, accusations) or physical (violence, destruction, murder).

## Why "Antisemitism" and Not "Anti-Semitism"?

The term was coined in 1879 by German journalist Wilhelm Marr, who wanted a scientific-sounding word to replace *Judenhass* (Jew-hatred). "Semitic" refers to a language family including Hebrew, Arabic, Aramaic, and others—but the term "antisemitism" has never meant hatred of all Semitic peoples. It has always meant hatred of Jews specifically.

Many scholars prefer to write "antisemitism" without the hyphen or capital S to avoid implying that there is such a thing as "Semitism" that one could be opposed to. There is only antisemitism.

## Ancient Antisemitism: The Classical World

Jew-hatred did not begin with Christianity or Islam. It has earlier roots.

### Ancient Egypt

The earliest recorded antisemitic incident may be the expulsion described in the Book of Exodus, though historical verification is complex. Later, the Greco-Egyptian historian Manetho (3rd century BCE) wrote that Jews were descended from Egyptian lepers who were expelled for impurity—an early example of portraying Jews as diseased and contaminating.

### Hellenistic Antisemitism

As Jews spread throughout the Mediterranean world, they encountered hostility from Greek and Roman writers who saw Jewish practices as antisocial and superstitious. Jewish refusal to worship the emperors, eat certain foods, or work on the Sabbath was seen as evidence of hatred for humanity.

The philosopher Apion accused Jews of ritual murder, claiming they kidnapped Greeks and fattened them for sacrifice. This charge—completely false—would echo through history in the blood libels of medieval Europe.

In 38 CE, anti-Jewish riots in Alexandria, Egypt, resulted in the burning of synagogues and the massacre of Jews. Thousands died. The Roman prefect did nothing to stop it.

### What Made Jews Different?

In the ancient world, most peoples defined themselves by ethnicity, geography, and gods. Jews were different: they were scattered across many lands, yet maintained a distinct identity. They rejected polytheism and refused to participate in civic religious rituals. This made them suspect—were they loyal to their host countries? Could they be trusted?

These questions would be asked again and again across the centuries.

## Christian Antisemitism: The Long Dark Ages

The emergence of Christianity transformed antisemitism, giving it a theological foundation that would persist for nearly two thousand years.

### Decide Accusation

Early Christian doctrine held that Jews were collectively responsible for the death of Jesus (deicide, or God-killing). The Gospel of Matthew has a crowd saying, "His blood be on us and on our children" (Matthew 27:25)—a passage that would be used to justify violence against Jews for millennia.

Never mind that Jesus himself was Jewish, as were all his early followers. Never mind that crucifixion was a Roman punishment and the historical circumstances are far more complex. The narrative of Jewish guilt became central to Christian teaching.

### Theological Anti-Judaism

Medieval Christianity taught that God's covenant with the Jews had been transferred to Christians (replacement theology or supersessionism). Jews were portrayed as spiritually blind, rejected by God, cursed to wander the earth until they accepted Christ.

Yet Jewish survival was necessary—as "witness people" to biblical truth and as a reminder of the consequences of rejecting Jesus. This created a paradox: Jews must survive but must also suffer as proof of Christian truth.

### Restrictions and Humiliations

Throughout medieval Europe, Jews faced:

- **Forced ghettoization**: Confined to specific neighborhoods, locked in at night
- **Occupational restrictions**: Barred from most professions, often pushed into moneylending (which Christians considered sinful), then hated for being moneylenders
- **Distinctive clothing**: Required to wear special badges or hats marking them as Jews
- **Forced conversions**: Especially during the Crusades and Spanish Inquisition
- **Expulsions**: England (1290), France (repeatedly), Spain (1492), Portugal (1497), and dozens of German and Italian cities

### Violence and Massacres

When the First Crusade was called in 1095, mobs massacred Jewish communities in the Rhineland, reasoning: "Why travel to kill infidels in Jerusalem when there are infidels here?" Thousands of Jews were murdered. Many took their own lives rather than be forcibly baptized.

Similar massacres occurred during later Crusades, the Black Death (when Jews were accused of poisoning wells), and countless local pogroms.

## Islamic Antisemitism: A Complex History

The Jewish experience under Islam was markedly different from that under Christianity, though not without discrimination and violence.

### Dhimmi Status

Under Islamic law, Jews (along with Christians) were *dhimmis*—protected minorities. They could practice their religion but faced restrictions:

- Special taxes (*jizya*)
- Prohibited from building synagogues taller than mosques
- Required to show deference to Muslims
- Restricted in dress and movement

In exchange, they received protection and legal rights. This was genuinely better than the Christian treatment of Jews for much of history—but it was still second-class citizenship.

### The Golden Age

Medieval Spain under Muslim rule (roughly 900-1200 CE) saw remarkable Jewish flourishing in philosophy, poetry, science, and religious scholarship. This "Golden Age" produced figures like Maimonides, Judah Halevi, and Ibn Gabirol.

Yet even this period included massacres, such as the 1066 Granada massacre where a Jewish vizier and thousands of Jews were killed, and the Almohad persecutions of the 12th century.

### Modern Antisemitism in the Islamic World

Classical Islamic anti-Judaism was primarily religious and social, not racial or eliminationist. However, the Israeli-Palestinian conflict and the importation of European antisemitic ideas have created a new, virulent strain of antisemitism in parts of the Muslim world.

European texts like the Protocols of the Elders of Zion have been translated and widely distributed. Holocaust denial is common in some regions. State media in some countries promotes conspiracy theories about Jewish control.

This represents a tragic fusion of imported European antisemitism with local political grievances, creating something more dangerous than traditional Islamic anti-Judaism.

## Modern Antisemitism: Racial Pseudoscience

The 19th century saw the emergence of a new form of antisemitism based not on religion but on race.

### The Enlightenment Paradox

The European Enlightenment promised equality and reason. In the late 18th and 19th centuries, many European countries granted Jews civil rights. Ghetto walls came down. Jews entered universities, professions, arts, and sciences.

But this created a new anxiety: if Jews were now equal citizens, what made them different? And if they succeeded, was it through unfair advantage or conspiracy?

### Racial Antisemitism

The new antisemitism claimed Jews were a race, not just a religion. This meant conversion couldn't save Jews—their alleged defects were biological, permanent, and dangerous.

Pseudoscientific theories portrayed Jews as racially inferior, parasitic, incapable of creative work, and corrosive to "host" societies. These ideas were dressed up in the language of science and progress.

This racial antisemitism was secular—it didn't need religion. An atheist could hate Jews on "scientific" grounds. This made it particularly dangerous and particularly adaptable.

## The Eternal Accusations

Across different times and places, certain accusations appear repeatedly:

**Jews are too separate**: They keep to themselves, maintain strange customs, refuse to assimilate.

**Jews assimilate too much**: They infiltrate society, hide their identity, manipulate from within.

**Jews are too poor**: They're dirty, diseased, criminal, a burden on society.

**Jews are too rich**: They control banks, media, governments, the world economy.

**Jews are too weak**: They're cowardly, passive, scholarly rather than martial.

**Jews are too powerful**: They pull strings behind the scenes, start wars, topple governments.

**Jews are too religious**: They're superstitious, backward, unable to modernize.

**Jews are too secular**: They're atheistic, morally corrupt, destroying traditional values.

Notice the pattern: whatever Jews are accused of, the opposite is also true. This is because antisemitism is not responsive to what Jews actually do. It's a framework for explaining whatever goes wrong in society by blaming Jews.

## How Jews Have Responded

Jewish responses to antisemitism have varied:

**Accommodation**: Trying to make themselves less threatening through cultural assimilation, demonstrating loyalty, and minimizing distinctiveness.

**Resistance**: Armed self-defense, from the Warsaw Ghetto Uprising to modern Israeli military preparedness.

**Migration**: Moving to safer locations, from medieval expulsions to modern immigration to Israel and other countries.

**Dialogue and education**: Teaching about Judaism to reduce misunderstanding and fear.

**Legal and political advocacy**: Fighting for civil rights, anti-discrimination laws, and hate crime legislation.

**Community building**: Creating strong internal networks of support and mutual aid.

None of these has eliminated antisemitism, but all have helped Jews survive and sometimes thrive despite it.

## Why Understanding Antisemitism Matters

For non-Jews, understanding antisemitism is important for several reasons:

First, it reveals how prejudice works. The mechanisms of antisemitism—scapegoating, conspiracy thinking, dehumanization—apply to other forms of hatred as well.

Second, antisemitism often serves as an early warning sign of broader social dysfunction. Societies that turn on their Jews often turn on other minorities as well.

Third, in an interconnected world, hatred anywhere threatens peace everywhere. Antisemitism in Europe led to the Holocaust, which reshaped the entire world.

Finally, many people today encounter antisemitic ideas without recognizing them as such. Understanding the patterns helps identify and counter them.

## Antisemitism Today

Antisemitism did not end with the Holocaust. According to the Anti-Defamation League, antisemitic incidents remain common worldwide. Jews in Europe report increasing fear and harassment. Online spaces are rife with antisemitic conspiracy theories. Violent attacks on synagogues and Jewish institutions continue.

Yet there is also unprecedented awareness and opposition. Many countries have adopted the IHRA definition. Interfaith dialogue has increased. Holocaust education is more widespread than ever.

The fight against antisemitism continues, requiring vigilance, education, and the willingness to speak out against hatred in all its forms.

As the great theologian Abraham Joshua Heschel said, "In a free society, some are guilty, but all are responsible." Understanding antisemitism is the first step toward taking responsibility for creating a world where such hatred cannot flourish.
