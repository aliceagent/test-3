# The Rebbe's Ten Campaigns: Practical Mitzvot for Every Jew

## From Shabbat Candles to Tefillin to Mezuzah—Making Core Mitzvot Accessible

In 1967, on the eve of the Six-Day War, the Lubavitcher Rebbe made an unusual request. He asked Jewish men around the world—whether religiously observant or not—to put on tefillin (phylacteries), the black leather boxes containing Torah verses that traditional Jews wear during morning prayers. The Rebbe believed this simple act would create spiritual protection for Israeli soldiers entering battle. Thousands responded. Chabad emissaries fanned out across airports, streets, and public squares asking Jewish men: "Are you Jewish? Would you like to put on tefillin?"

This tefillin campaign became the first of what would eventually be known as the Rebbe's "Ten Campaigns"—focused initiatives to encourage all Jews, regardless of background or observance level, to perform specific mitzvot (commandments). These campaigns were revolutionary in their simplicity, accessibility, and universal reach. Rather than demanding comprehensive religious transformation, the Rebbe said: Start with one action. One mitzvah. One connection. That's enough to begin.

For Chinese readers encountering these campaigns, they offer fascinating insight into how an ancient religious tradition adapts to modern realities—making 3,000-year-old practices accessible to people who may never have heard of them, all while maintaining the practices' traditional form and spiritual significance.

## The Philosophy Behind the Campaigns

Several key principles informed the Rebbe's approach:

**Every Mitzvah Has Infinite Value**: Jewish mysticism teaches that each mitzvah connects a person to the Infinite. Therefore, even one mitzvah performed once has infinite worth. You don't need to do everything; doing *something* is itself complete.

**No Prerequisites**: Traditional Jewish education often assumed you needed extensive knowledge before practicing. The Rebbe reversed this: practice first, understanding follows. Light Shabbat candles even if you don't understand the mystical significance. The action itself creates transformation.

**Meet People Where They Are**: Rather than expecting Jews to come to synagogues or institutions, bring the mitzvot to where Jews are—streets, schools, malls, airports, beaches. Make it easy, accessible, welcoming.

**Action Over Belief**: Judaism has always emphasized practice over theology. You can have questions, doubts, even disbelief—but still do the mitzvah. The action itself has power regardless of your intellectual position.

**Building From Foundation**: The ten campaigns focus on accessible, fundamental practices that don't require extensive preparation, expense, or lifestyle change. Anyone can do them.

**Unity in Diversity**: These campaigns brought together Jews across all divides—Ashkenazi and Sephardi, Israeli and American, wealthy and poor, observant and secular. One simple action unites diverse communities.

## The Ten Campaigns

### 1. Ahavat Yisrael—Love of Fellow Jews

**The Practice**: Actively cultivate love for all Jews, expressed through concrete acts of kindness, respect, and connection.

**Why It's First**: Love is the foundation. Without genuine care for others, all other religious practices become self-serving. The Rebbe emphasized that Ahavat Yisrael must be unconditional—not dependent on the other person's observance, beliefs, or behavior.

**Practical Actions**:
- Greet other Jews warmly
- Help someone in need
- Avoid gossip or negative speech about others
- Seek opportunities to do kindness
- Overcome personal prejudices

**The Challenge**: This isn't just a feeling; it's active work. You're asked to love even Jews you disagree with, who irritate you, whose choices you don't understand. This mirrors the Torah commandment: "Love your neighbor as yourself" (Leviticus 19:18).

### 2. Torah Study

**The Practice**: Study Torah—even just a few minutes daily. This can be reading a passage from the Five Books of Moses, studying Talmud, reading Jewish philosophy, or even studying Jewish ethics and law.

**Why Essential**: Judaism is a learning tradition. Torah study is itself a form of worship and a direct connection to divine wisdom. The content matters less than the consistency.

**Accessible Entry Points**:
- Daily portion of Chumash (Five Books)
- One verse with commentary
- A paragraph of Tanya (Chabad philosophy)
- A Jewish law or ethical teaching
- Online Torah classes or apps

**The Revolution**: Traditionally, serious Torah study was reserved for scholars. The Rebbe democratized it—anyone can study, whatever your level, whatever you can manage. Five minutes counts.

**Modern Tools**: The Rebbe encouraged using technology. Audio recordings for commutes, video classes, apps, email subscriptions—whatever makes study accessible and consistent.

### 3. Tefillin—Phylacteries

**The Practice**: Jewish men (from bar mitzvah age onward) wrap leather straps with black boxes containing Torah verses on their arm and head during weekday morning prayers.

**Biblical Source**: Deuteronomy 6:8—"Bind them as a sign on your hand, and let them be an emblem between your eyes."

**Symbolic Meaning**: 
- The arm tefillin (opposite the heart) represents dedicating emotions to God
- The head tefillin represents dedicating intellect to God
- Together: complete dedication of the whole person

**The Campaign**: Chabad emissaries ask Jewish men worldwide to put on tefillin, even once, even if just for a moment. "Tefillin stands" appear at airports, universities, business districts, wherever Jews gather. The brief ceremony takes only a minute or two.

**The Impact**: Millions of Jewish men who never considered themselves religious have put on tefillin through this campaign. For many, it was their first direct engagement with Jewish practice. Some continued daily; others do it occasionally; all experienced a moment of connection.

**For Chinese Readers**: This might seem strange—black leather boxes? But consider it a physical ritual that creates mindful intention at the start of each day, connecting mind and heart to something beyond daily concerns. The physicality makes the spiritual concrete.

### 4. Mezuzah

**The Practice**: Affix a mezuzah (a small case containing a parchment scroll with Torah verses) on doorposts of Jewish homes.

**Biblical Source**: Deuteronomy 6:9—"Write them on the doorposts of your house and on your gates."

**Symbolic Meaning**: The mezuzah marks a Jewish home, transforming a physical space into sacred space. Every time you enter or exit, you touch or kiss it, conscious of God's presence.

**The Campaign**: The Rebbe emphasized that every Jewish home should have kosher (properly written) mezuzot on all required doorposts. Chabad emissaries offer mezuzah checking services and help people install them properly.

**Universal Appeal**: Even very secular Jews often have mezuzot. It's visible, beautiful, carries cultural identity as well as religious significance. It says: "A Jewish family lives here."

**Practical Note**: Different rooms require mezuzot; specific laws govern placement. But the basic act is simple—affix a small case to your doorframe.

### 5. Tzedakah—Charity

**The Practice**: Give charity regularly, ideally daily. The amount matters less than the consistency and the spiritual intention—recognizing that what you have is a gift, and you have responsibility to share.

**Why Daily**: Making charity a daily habit—even a few coins—trains generosity into your character. It becomes automatic, not something you think about.

**The Rebbe's Innovation**: Charity boxes (*pushke*) in every Jewish home, particularly in locations where children can easily access them. Children learn to give before every Shabbat, before leaving home, on special occasions.

**Beyond Money**: Tzedakah also includes giving time, knowledge, kindness—anything that helps another person. But monetary charity has unique value in Jewish thought; it requires real sacrifice.

**The Philosophy**: The Hebrew root of tzedakah is "tzedek" (justice, righteousness). It's not optional goodwill; it's an obligation. What you have isn't fully yours; you're a steward. Sharing is justice, not charity.

**Practical Approach**: Keep charity box visible. Establish a percentage of income for charity (traditionally at least 10%). Give to local causes, Jewish causes, any genuine need.

### 6. Jewish Books in the Home

**The Practice**: Have Jewish books in your home—Torah, prayer books, Shabbat guides, Jewish philosophy, whatever connects you to Jewish wisdom.

**The Reasoning**: Books aren't just information storage; they're spiritual objects. A Jewish home with Jewish books has different energy than one without. The books' presence reminds you of your heritage and provides resources for growth.

**The Campaign**: The Rebbe encouraged every Jewish home to have at least a Chumash (Five Books of Moses), a Siddur (prayer book), and a Tanya (Chabad philosophy). These three provide foundation—scripture, prayer, and spiritual guidance.

**Modern Extensions**: This now includes Jewish apps, e-books, audio recordings—the format matters less than having accessible Jewish content.

**Subtle Impact**: When Jewish books sit on your shelf, children see them. Guests notice them. You're reminded of resources available when questions arise or you want to study.

### 7. Shabbat and Festival Candles

**The Practice**: Jewish women and girls (from age three upward) light candles before Shabbat (Friday evening) and before festivals, reciting blessings.

**Timing**: Candles must be lit 18 minutes before sunset on Friday evening (or festival eve), marking the transition from weekday to Shabbat.

**Symbolic Meaning**: 
- Light represents divine wisdom and soul
- Women lighting candles bring peace and holiness into the home
- The act separates sacred time from ordinary time

**The Impact**: This campaign has been remarkably successful. Countless Jewish women who observe little else light Shabbat candles. It's simple, beautiful, brief, and deeply meaningful.

**The Rebbe's Emphasis**: Even if you can't observe full Shabbat restrictions, light the candles. That one act sanctifies your home and connects you to Jewish women worldwide doing the same act at the same moment.

**Community Dimension**: Before major Jewish holidays, Chabad emissaries distribute candlesticks and candles, ensuring every Jewish woman has the means to perform this mitzvah.

### 8. Kashrut—Kosher Food

**The Practice**: Eat only kosher food—meat from permitted animals slaughtered according to Jewish law, separation of meat and dairy, avoidance of prohibited foods.

**The Challenge**: Of all the campaigns, this one requires the most lifestyle adjustment. Keeping kosher affects where you eat, what you buy, how you prepare food, and your social interactions.

**The Rebbe's Approach**: Start somewhere. Maybe keep kosher only at home. Maybe avoid obviously non-kosher foods (pork, shellfish). Maybe buy kosher-certified products even if you don't separate dishes. Something is better than nothing.

**Why It Matters**: 
- Physical health (some argue kosher laws have hygiene benefits)
- Spiritual refinement (Jewish mysticism sees kosher food as containing "holy sparks")
- Discipline and mindfulness (you can't eat thoughtlessly; every meal requires conscious choice)
- Community connection (kosher observance connects you to global Jewish community)

**Modern Resources**: Kosher food is increasingly available—certified products in regular supermarkets, kosher restaurants in major cities, online resources for finding kosher options anywhere.

### 9. Taharat HaMishpacha—Family Purity

**The Practice**: Jewish married couples observe laws governing intimate relations, including separation during and after menstruation, and immersion in a mikvah (ritual bath) before resuming intimacy.

**The Sensitive Nature**: This is the most private of the campaigns, dealing with intimate aspects of marriage. The Rebbe emphasized it should be approached with sensitivity, privacy, and respect for individuals' readiness.

**The Philosophy**: These laws aren't about considering women impure; rather, they create cycles of separation and reunion that many couples find strengthen their relationship. The monthly rhythm creates space for non-physical connection, followed by renewal.

**The Campaign**: Chabad built mikvaot (ritual baths) worldwide, ensuring access even in communities without existing facilities. They offer private education for couples interested in learning more.

**Modern Discourse**: Some see these laws as restrictive; others find them deeply meaningful. The Rebbe's approach: make information and facilities available, respect each couple's choices, emphasize the positive aspects.

### 10. Hachnassat Orchim—Hospitality

**The Practice**: Welcome guests, particularly for Shabbat and festival meals. Open your home to travelers, students, newcomers, anyone who might otherwise be alone.

**Biblical Model**: Abraham, the first Jew, is the paradigm of hospitality—his tent was open on all four sides so he could welcome guests from any direction.

**The Meaning**: Hospitality isn't just kindness; it's a mitzvah with spiritual dimensions. You're giving someone not just food but dignity, connection, belonging. You're creating community.

**Chabad Houses as Expression**: The entire Chabad House movement embodies this campaign. Every Chabad center offers Shabbat meals to anyone who comes—students, travelers, locals, Jews of any background.

**Personal Practice**: Invite someone to your Shabbat or holiday table. Host a Jewish newcomer for a meal. Create space at your table for those who might otherwise be alone.

**The Transformation**: Both guests and hosts are transformed. Guests find welcome and connection; hosts develop generosity and overcome insularity. Hospitality breaks down barriers.

## Why These Ten?

You might wonder: Jewish tradition has 613 commandments—why these specific ten?

The Rebbe chose mitzvot that are:
- **Fundamental**: Core practices that touch essential aspects of Jewish life
- **Accessible**: Anyone can do them without extensive training or resources
- **Regular**: Most can be done daily or weekly, creating rhythm and habit
- **Universal**: Applicable to all Jews regardless of background
- **Visible**: Many have public dimensions, strengthening Jewish identity and community

Together, they cover different life areas—home (mezuzah, books, candles), body (tefillin, kashrut), relationships (hospitality, family purity, love of others), mind (Torah study), and material resources (charity).

## The Larger Strategy

These campaigns weren't random initiatives; they reflected sophisticated strategy:

1. **Critical Mass**: If enough people do even one mitzvah, collective spiritual impact grows exponentially.

2. **Gateway Effect**: One mitzvah leads to another. Someone who starts with candles might explore Shabbat observance. Someone who puts on tefillin once might become curious about daily prayer.

3. **Strengthening Identity**: Visible Jewish practices strengthen Jewish identity, particularly important in assimilated communities where Jewish identity was weakening.

4. **Preventing Assimilation**: By making Jewish practice accessible and positive, the campaigns countered assimilation trends. Young Jews who might otherwise drift away found entry points to their heritage.

5. **Preparing for Redemption**: The Rebbe taught that widespread mitzvah observance would hasten the coming of Mashiach (Messiah) and the redemption. Each mitzvah brings the world closer to its ultimate purpose.

## The Results

Decades later, the impact is clear:

- Millions have put on tefillin through the campaigns
- Hundreds of thousands of Jewish homes now light Shabbat candles who didn't before
- Mezuzah awareness and usage has increased dramatically
- Kosher food availability has expanded exponentially, partly due to increased demand
- Daily Torah study has spread far beyond scholarly circles
- Charity-giving has become normalized as regular practice
- New mikvaot have been built in communities worldwide

Beyond numbers, attitudes shifted. Jewish practice, which had seemed old-fashioned or inaccessible, became relevant again. Young Jews discovered their heritage through simple practices. Secular Jews found ways to connect without comprehensive lifestyle change.

## Criticisms and Responses

Critics have raised concerns:

**"This is piecemeal—Judaism is a complete system"**: Chabad responds that starting with one mitzvah is better than overwhelm that leads to doing nothing. The goal isn't piecemeal Judaism but accessible entry points.

**"It's manipulative—tricking people into religion"**: Chabad responds that they're completely transparent. The emissary says: "This is a mitzvah, a connection to God, a Jewish practice. Would you like to try?" There's no deception.

**"It waters down Judaism"**: Chabad responds that authentic practice is maintained—tefillin are kosher, candles are lit at proper times, mezuzot are properly written. The accessibility doesn't compromise authenticity.

**"It's proselytizing"**: This is interesting because Chabad only reaches out to Jews, not gentiles. It's not conversion; it's helping Jews access their own tradition. Still, some Jews prefer not to be approached. Chabad tries to balance enthusiasm with sensitivity.

## For Chinese Readers: Practical Wisdom

Even if you're not Jewish, these campaigns offer insights applicable to any tradition or goal:

- **Start Small**: Grand transformations begin with small, consistent actions
- **Consistency Over Intensity**: Daily brief practice beats occasional intense practice
- **Make It Accessible**: Lower barriers to entry; remove excuses
- **Action First, Understanding Later**: Sometimes doing leads to understanding better than the reverse
- **Use What You Have**: Modern technology, contemporary contexts—adapt while maintaining core essence
- **Build Community**: Individual practice is good; collective practice is transformative
- **Focus on Specific, Measurable Actions**: Vague spiritual aspirations rarely work; specific practices do

## Continuing Evolution

The campaigns continue today, adapted to new contexts:

- **Online Campaigns**: Virtual candle-lighting, tefillin via video call, digital Torah study groups
- **Targeted Outreach**: Campaigns for specific populations—college students, hospital patients, military personnel
- **Cultural Adaptation**: The essential practices remain, but explanations and approaches adapt to different cultural contexts

In China, where small Jewish communities exist, Chabad centers run these campaigns adapted to local realities—explaining practices to Chinese spouses, navigating different cultural contexts, finding ways to make ancient practices relevant in contemporary Chinese settings.

## The Enduring Message

The Rebbe's Ten Campaigns embody a powerful message: transformation is possible, one step at a time. You don't need to be perfect. You don't need to change everything. You don't need to become someone else. Start where you are. Do one thing. That one thing has infinite value.

This approach—radical in its simplicity, profound in its accessibility—has brought millions of Jews back to their heritage. More importantly, it's brought joy, meaning, and connection to countless lives. The campaigns continue, carried forward by emissaries worldwide, each day asking: "Would you like to light Shabbat candles?" "Would you like to put on tefillin?" "Would you like to give some charity?"

One mitzvah. One person. One moment of connection. Building, slowly but surely, toward a world where the divine is visible in everyday life, where ancient wisdom lives in contemporary practice, where no Jew is too far from their heritage to find a way home.
