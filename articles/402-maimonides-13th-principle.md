# Maimonides' 13th Principle: "I Believe in the Coming of the Messiah"

**Section: Messiah**

In the 12th century, Rabbi Moses ben Maimon—known as Maimonides or the Rambam (1138-1204)—crystallized Jewish belief into thirteen fundamental principles. The 13th and final principle addresses belief in the Messiah: "I believe with perfect faith in the coming of the Messiah, and even though he may tarry, nevertheless I await his coming every day."

This brief statement contains profound theological and practical implications. Let's explore what Maimonides meant, why this belief matters, and how it shapes Jewish life today.

## The Historical Context

Maimonides lived during a tumultuous period. Born in Córdoba, Spain, his family fled persecution, eventually settling in Egypt. He witnessed Jewish communities suffering under both Christian and Muslim rule, with messianic hopes running high. False messianic movements had caused great damage, most notably Bar Kokhba's revolt (132-136 CE) that resulted in catastrophic Jewish losses.

Against this backdrop, Maimonides sought to define authentic Jewish messianic belief—distinguishing it from false hopes and excessive mysticism while maintaining its essential place in Jewish faith.

## What Does the Principle Require?

Maimonides elaborated on this principle in his legal code, the *Mishneh Torah* (Laws of Kings 11-12), and in his commentary on the Mishnah. The belief encompasses several elements:

### 1. Certainty of Coming

The Messiah will definitely come. This isn't wishful thinking or allegory—it's a core conviction. As Maimonides writes in the Mishneh Torah: "Anyone who does not believe in him or does not await his coming denies not only the other prophets but also the Torah and Moses our teacher."

This strong language shows that messianic belief isn't peripheral to Judaism—it's foundational. It represents faith that history has meaning and direction, that good will ultimately triumph, and that God's promises are reliable.

### 2. Human Agency

Maimonides emphasized that the Messiah will be a human being—not an angel, not a semi-divine figure, but a descendant of King David who will be wise, righteous, and thoroughly observant of Torah law. He wrote: "Do not think that the Messianic king must perform signs and wonders... nor that he will resurrect the dead... The matter is not so."

This grounds messianic belief in reality rather than fantasy. The Messiah's qualification comes from his character, wisdom, leadership, and successful accomplishment of specific tasks—not from supernatural displays.

### 3. Verifiable Criteria

Maimonides provided clear, practical criteria for identifying the Messiah:

- **Stage 1 - Presumed Messiah**: A king from the house of David who studies Torah, observes commandments, influences Israel to follow the Torah, fights God's battles, and begins to rebuild the Temple and gather exiles.

- **Stage 2 - Confirmed Messiah**: If he successfully completes these tasks—rebuilds the Temple on its site, gathers the dispersed of Israel, and the world achieves universal peace—then he is certainly the Messiah.

Note the practical nature: we'll know by results, not by claims or prophecies alone.

### 4. "Even Though He May Tarry"

This phrase, added to the principle as it appears in the *Yigdal* hymn and *Ani Maamin* prayer, comes from Habakkuk 2:3: "Though it tarry, wait for it, because it will surely come; it will not delay."

What does this mean practically? Maimonides insisted we must believe the Messiah could come today, even though he hasn't come yet. We shouldn't calculate dates or set deadlines. We shouldn't despair because of delay or become cynical about the possibility.

## How This Belief Shapes Daily Life

### Morning Prayers

Traditional Jewish liturgy includes multiple references to messianic redemption. The *Amidah* (standing prayer) recited three times daily includes blessings for:
- Ingathering of exiles
- Restoration of justice
- Rebuilding of Jerusalem
- Coming of the Davidic king

Each day, observant Jews pray for these realities multiple times. This keeps messianic consciousness alive as a daily practice, not just abstract theology.

### Ethical Behavior

Maimonides concludes his entire law code with these words: "One should not occupy himself with the aggadot [homiletical interpretations]... nor should one make them of primary importance... The Sages and Prophets did not long for the messianic era in order to dominate the world... but rather to be free to devote themselves to Torah and its wisdom... In that era, there will be no hunger, no war, no jealousy, no competition."

The message: We await the Messiah not for personal gain but for a world where goodness, wisdom, and knowledge of God can flourish. This should shape our ethics now—working toward peace, justice, and learning.

### Hope and Action

Messianic belief creates a tension between patience and urgency. We can't force the Messiah's coming, yet we're commanded to act as if he might come today. This produces a unique outlook:

- **Optimism without naiveté** - The world can and will improve, but we recognize current imperfections
- **Responsibility without despair** - We work to better the world while trusting ultimate redemption comes from God
- **Long-term thinking** - We plant trees we may never sit under, in the famous Talmudic phrase

### Living with Uncertainty

The "I await his coming every day" phrase means maintaining readiness and expectation. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 97b-98a) records debate about whether the Messiah's coming depends on Israel's repentance or will come at a predetermined time. Maimonides acknowledged both possibilities.

This uncertainty is actually productive: we can't calculate or predict, so we must always be prepared, always acting righteously, always working toward a better world.

## Common Misunderstandings

**"Messianic belief means passivity"** - False. Maimonides emphasized human responsibility. We prepare the world for redemption through just actions.

**"It's about the afterlife"** - No. The messianic era is about transforming this world, not escaping it.

**"Only the ultra-Orthodox care about this"** - While observance levels vary, messianic hope has inspired Jewish survival, Zionism, social justice movements, and cultural renaissance throughout history.

## The Principle Today

In our time, after the Holocaust, the establishment of Israel, and dramatic Jewish demographic shifts, how does this principle function?

For many, the return to Zion and establishment of a Jewish state represents a step toward redemption, though clearly not its completion (no Temple, no universal peace). Maimonides' practical criteria remind us that partial progress isn't final redemption—but it can be meaningful progress.

The 13th principle challenges modern Jews to maintain hope without falling into messianic fanaticism, to work for improvement without demanding instant perfection, and to believe in ultimate redemption while acting responsibly in the present.

## Conclusion

"I believe with perfect faith in the coming of the Messiah" is not a statement about magic or miracles. It's a declaration that history has purpose, that justice will prevail, that the long arc bends toward goodness. It demands we live with hope, act with responsibility, and never abandon the vision of a perfected world.

This belief has sustained the Jewish people through centuries of exile and persecution. It continues to inspire work toward peace, justice, and human flourishing today.
