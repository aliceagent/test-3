# Lashon Hara: The Jewish Laws of Speech

**Section: Jewish Values**

## Introduction: The Power of Words

In Jewish tradition, speech is considered one of the most powerful forces in the world. The Talmud teaches that "life and death are in the power of the tongue" (Proverbs 18:21). While this might sound dramatic, Jewish law takes speech so seriously that an entire body of halacha (Jewish law) has developed around it. At the center of these laws is the concept of *lashon hara* — literally "evil tongue" — which is often called one of the hardest commandments to keep.

## What is Lashon Hara?

Lashon hara refers to true, but negative or harmful, speech about another person. This is a crucial distinction: we're not talking about lies or slander (which is called *motzi shem ra*, "spreading a bad name"). Lashon hara is when you say something negative that is completely true.

For example, if you tell someone, "David is always late to meetings," that might be factually accurate. But if saying this serves no constructive purpose and could harm David's reputation, it's lashon hara.

The Chofetz Chaim (Rabbi Yisrael Meir Kagan, 1839-1933), who wrote the definitive work on the laws of speech, explained that lashon hara includes:

- Speaking negatively about someone's character, actions, or possessions
- Revealing information that could cause someone harm, embarrassment, or financial loss
- Even listening to lashon hara (which encourages the speaker)
- Believing lashon hara you hear

## Biblical Sources

The prohibition against lashon hara comes from several Torah verses:

**"You shall not go about as a talebearer among your people"** (Leviticus 19:16)

**"You shall not bear a false report"** (Exodus 23:1) — which the Rabbis extended to include even true negative reports

**"Guard your tongue from evil, and your lips from speaking deceit"** (Psalms 34:13)

The seriousness of this prohibition is highlighted in the Torah's account of Miriam, Moses's sister. After she spoke critically about Moses's marriage (Numbers 12), she was struck with *tzara'at* (often translated as leprosy). The Talmud points to this as evidence that gossip is compared to the three cardinal sins: idolatry, sexual immorality, and murder (Arachin 15b).

## Why Is It So Serious?

The Talmud states: "Lashon hara kills three people: the one who speaks it, the one who hears it, and the one about whom it is spoken" (Arachin 15b). But why such harsh language?

Jewish tradition understands that words create reality. When you speak negatively about someone:

1. **You damage their reputation** — People who hear your words will think less of the person, affecting their relationships, livelihood, and standing in the community.

2. **You damage yourself** — Speaking negatively cultivates a mindset of judgment and negativity, shaping your character.

3. **You damage the listener** — You've now burdened them with negative information and potentially turned them against someone.

4. **You damage community trust** — When gossip is normalized, community bonds weaken and suspicion grows.

The 16th-century work *Sefer HaChinuch* explains that the prohibition protects social harmony. Society functions on trust and good faith; lashon hara erodes this foundation.

## When Is Negative Speech Permitted?

Jewish law isn't absolutist. There are situations where sharing negative information is permitted or even required:

**For a constructive purpose (*to'elet*)**: If someone asks you about hiring a person and you know they're unreliable, you may (and should) share relevant information. The key requirements are:

- You must have firsthand knowledge (not rumors)
- Your intent must be purely constructive
- You should share only necessary details
- You must be sure of the facts
- There should be no other way to achieve the goal

**To protect someone from harm**: If you know someone is being deceived or endangered, you may speak up.

**In a business or legal context**: Providing accurate references, testifying in court, or warning about financial impropriety are permitted when done responsibly.

The Chofetz Chaim provides detailed guidelines for these exceptions, emphasizing that the speaker must examine their motivations carefully.

## Common Scenarios

Let's look at everyday situations:

**"Did you hear what happened to Sarah?"** If this is sharing unfortunate news (she lost her job, had an accident), it's often lashon hara unless Sarah would want people to know or there's a practical reason to share.

**Complaining about coworkers**: "Michael never pulls his weight" — even if true, if it's just venting rather than addressing a problem constructively, it's lashon hara.

**"Innocent" sharing**: "Their house is so messy!" Even observations that seem harmless can be hurtful and serve no constructive purpose.

**Social media**: Posts that mock, criticize, or expose others are often lashon hara — and they're permanent and public, multiplying the harm.

## The Hardest Mitzvah

The Chofetz Chaim called guarding one's tongue one of the most difficult commandments. Why?

- It's constant — you can speak lashon hara multiple times a day
- It feels harmless — "I'm just talking"
- Social pressure — conversations often revolve around discussing others
- It's satisfying — sharing gossip creates bonding and makes us feel informed
- Self-righteousness — criticizing others can make us feel superior

The Talmud acknowledges this difficulty, suggesting that almost everyone transgresses this prohibition occasionally (Bava Batra 164b).

## Practical Steps

Jewish tradition offers practical guidance for avoiding lashon hara:

1. **The pause**: Before speaking, ask: "Is it true? Is it kind? Is it necessary?"

2. **Change the subject**: When gossip starts, redirect the conversation.

3. **Don't listen**: Politely excuse yourself or say, "I'd rather not hear this."

4. **Give the benefit of the doubt**: Often, negative behavior has a reasonable explanation.

5. **Speak to the source**: If you have an issue with someone, address them directly rather than talking to others.

6. **Study the laws**: Many people study a daily portion of the Chofetz Chaim's writings to stay mindful.

7. **Self-reflection**: At the end of each day, review your speech. Did you speak negatively? What triggered it?

## The Positive Alternative

Judaism doesn't just prohibit lashon hara; it encourages positive speech. The concept of *lashon tov* (good tongue) means:

- Speaking well of others
- Giving compliments
- Sharing positive news
- Defending someone's reputation
- Words of encouragement and comfort

The Talmud teaches: "One who judges others favorably will be judged favorably by Heaven" (Shabbat 127b).

## Conclusion: Words Build Worlds

In Genesis, God creates the world through speech: "And God said, 'Let there be light.'" Humans, created in God's image, also create through speech. We can build people up or tear them down. We can create trust or sow suspicion. We can foster community or destroy it.

The laws of lashon hara aren't about suppressing truth or avoiding difficult conversations. They're about using one of our greatest powers — speech — with wisdom, compassion, and intention. In a world where careless words spread instantly through technology, the ancient wisdom of guarding our tongues is more relevant than ever.

As the Chofetz Chaim wrote: "The Holy One says, 'I and the speaker of lashon hara cannot dwell in the same world.'" Strong words — but they remind us that how we speak reflects our values, shapes our character, and creates the world we live in.

*For readers wanting to learn more, the Chofetz Chaim's "Sefer Chofetz Chaim" (1873) remains the authoritative guide, available in English translation. A daily study program called "Shmirat HaLashon" (Guarding the Tongue) helps people integrate these laws into daily life.*
