# What Is Antisemitism? A Clear Definition and Historical Overview

**Section: Antisemitism**

Antisemitism has been called "the longest hatred" — a form of prejudice that has persisted for over two millennia, adapted to every culture and political system, and survived in forms ranging from religious hatred to racial pseudoscience to political conspiracy theories. Understanding what antisemitism is, where it comes from, and how it operates is essential not just for Jews, but for anyone who cares about justice and human dignity.

## Defining Antisemitism

At its core, antisemitism is hostility toward or prejudice against Jews as a people, religion, or ethnic group. But this simple definition masks tremendous complexity.

The International Holocaust Remembrance Alliance (IHRA) offers a working definition adopted by dozens of countries: "Antisemitism is a certain perception of Jews, which may be expressed as hatred toward Jews. Rhetorical and physical manifestations of antisemitism are directed toward Jewish or non-Jewish individuals and/or their property, toward Jewish community institutions and religious facilities."

Key aspects of this definition:

**It's about perception**: Antisemitism often has little to do with actual Jewish behavior. It's about what antisemites imagine Jews to be — powerful, conspiratorial, alien, threatening.

**It includes multiple forms**: Religious contempt, racial hatred, political demonization, and conspiracy theories can all be antisemitic.

**It targets more than individuals**: Antisemitism attacks Jewish institutions, religious buildings, and even ideas associated with Jews.

**It affects non-Jews too**: People perceived as Jewish, friends of Jews, or defenders of Jewish rights can become targets.

The term "antisemitism" itself was coined in 1879 by German journalist Wilhelm Marr, who wanted a scientific-sounding word for Jew-hatred. Ironically, "Semitic" is a linguistic term referring to many peoples (including Arabs), not a racial category. But the term stuck and now specifically means prejudice against Jews.

## Ancient Roots: Classical Antisemitism

The earliest recorded instances of organized antisemitism appear in the ancient world:

**Greco-Roman antisemitism** centered on Jewish "otherness." Jews refused to worship the emperors or participate in polytheistic rituals. They maintained distinct dietary laws, circumcised their sons, and observed a different calendar. To Greeks and Romans who valued cultural conformity, this separateness seemed antisocial and suspicious.

The Egyptian priest Manetho (3rd century BCE) spread false claims that Jews were expelled from Egypt due to leprosy and impurity — an inversion of the Exodus story that portrayed Jews as diseased outcasts. Similar accusations appeared throughout the ancient world.

In the biblical book of Esther, Haman articulates classic antisemitic logic: "There is a certain people scattered and dispersed among the peoples in all the provinces of your kingdom; their laws are different from those of all other people and they do not observe the king's laws, so it is not befitting the king to tolerate them" (Esther 3:8). The accusation: Jews are disloyal, different, and dangerous.

Yet ancient antisemitism was limited. Conversion to Judaism was possible, and hatred focused on cultural and religious difference rather than claims about inherent Jewish evil or power.

## Medieval Transformation: Christian Antisemitism

With Christianity's rise, antisemitism took on new and deadlier forms. Christian theology developed a narrative that Jews were responsible for killing Jesus and rejecting the true faith. Several theological ideas fueled hatred:

**Deicide charge**: The accusation that Jews killed God. Never mind that Jesus was Jewish and Roman authorities executed him — Christian teaching blamed "the Jews" collectively and eternally.

**Supersessionism**: The belief that Christianity replaced Judaism and that Jews' continued existence was evidence of their spiritual blindness and divine rejection.

**Devil association**: Medieval Christians increasingly portrayed Jews as allies of Satan, engaging in dark magic and devil worship.

These religious ideas enabled horrific accusations:

**Blood libel** (covered in detail in a dedicated article): The false claim that Jews murdered Christian children to use their blood in religious rituals. This lie sparked countless pogroms and massacres throughout medieval Europe.

**Host desecration**: Accusations that Jews stole consecrated communion wafers to torture Jesus again. This fantasy led to torture and execution of entire communities.

**Well poisoning**: During the Black Death (1348-1350), Jews were accused of poisoning wells to kill Christians. The fact that Jews died of plague at similar rates didn't matter — mobs massacred Jewish communities across Europe.

**Medieval ghettos**: Beginning in the 13th century, European cities forced Jews into segregated neighborhoods with gates locked at night. This physical separation reinforced the idea of Jews as alien and dangerous.

**Restrictions and expulsions**: Jews were banned from most professions, forced into money-lending (forbidden to Christians), then hated for being money-lenders. They were expelled from England (1290), France (multiple times), Spain (1492), Portugal (1497), and numerous German territories.

The Crusades (beginning 1096) brought waves of massacre as crusader armies killed Jews en route to Jerusalem. The Spanish Inquisition (established 1478) tortured and executed Jews and Jewish converts to Christianity.

## Early Modern Period: Reformation and Enlightenment

The Protestant Reformation didn't end Christian antisemitism — it often intensified it. Martin Luther initially hoped Jews would convert to his reformed Christianity. When they didn't, he wrote viciously antisemitic tracts calling for synagogue burnings and Jewish expulsion.

The Enlightenment brought mixed results. Some Enlightenment thinkers advocated for Jewish rights and equality. But others, like Voltaire, spread secular forms of antisemitism, attacking Jews as backwards, clannish, and opposed to progress.

**The Haskalah** (Jewish Enlightenment) encouraged Jews to integrate into European society through education and cultural assimilation. Many Jews enthusiastically embraced European culture, serving in militaries, contributing to science and arts, and seeking citizenship.

But integration sparked backlash. The late 18th and 19th centuries saw new forms of antisemitism emerge.

## Modern Antisemitism: Racial Theories and Nationalism

In the 19th century, antisemitism transformed again. As European Jews gained civil rights and succeeded in business, academia, and politics, a new form of hatred emerged based on pseudoscientific racial theory:

**Racial antisemitism** claimed Jews were inherently inferior or dangerous due to biology, not religion. Unlike earlier religious antisemitism, racial antisemitism couldn't be solved by conversion — being Jewish was now defined as an immutable racial characteristic.

This shift had enormous implications. A Jew who converted to Christianity was no longer safe from persecution. Jewish identity became racialized and pathologized.

**The Protocols of the Elders of Zion** (published 1903): A fabricated document claiming to reveal a Jewish plot for world domination. Though proven a forgery, it became the most influential antisemitic text of the modern era, inspiring Hitler and antisemites worldwide (covered in detail in a dedicated article).

**Political antisemitism**: Jews were blamed for capitalism (as wealthy bankers), communism (as revolutionary leaders), liberalism, conservatism — whatever political threat a society feared, Jews could be blamed for it. This contradictory scapegoating reveals antisemitism's irrationality.

**Nationalism and antisemitism**: As European nations developed strong national identities, Jews — who lived across borders and maintained transnational connections — were seen as rootless cosmopolitans lacking national loyalty.

## The Holocaust: Industrial Genocide

All these threads came together in Nazi Germany (1933-1945). The Nazis combined racial pseudoscience, conspiracy theories, Christian antisemitism, and modern state power to create an industrial genocide. Six million Jews — one-third of all Jews worldwide — were murdered in the Holocaust, the Shoah.

The Holocaust revealed how "ordinary" people could participate in extraordinary evil when antisemitic ideology was normalized, systematized, and backed by state power. It also showed how quickly a civilized society could descend into barbarism (covered in detail in a dedicated article).

## Post-Holocaust Antisemitism

Many hoped the Holocaust would end antisemitism. It didn't. New forms emerged:

**Holocaust denial**: Claiming the Holocaust never happened or was exaggerated, often as part of continued antisemitic propaganda.

**Anti-Zionism as antisemitism**: After Israel's establishment (1948), antisemitism increasingly disguised itself as political opposition to Zionism. While legitimate criticism of Israeli policies exists, much anti-Zionism crosses into antisemitism by denying Jewish self-determination, applying double standards, or using classic antisemitic tropes about Jewish power and conspiracy (covered in detail in a dedicated article).

**Soviet antisemitism**: The USSR combined official "anti-Zionism" with traditional antisemitic themes, persecuting Jews while denying it was antisemitism.

**Contemporary forms**: Today, antisemitism appears across the political spectrum — from far-right white supremacists to far-left anti-Israel activists, from Islamist extremists to conspiracy theorists blaming Jews for everything from 9/11 to COVID-19.

## Why Does Antisemitism Persist?

Several factors explain antisemitism's persistence:

**Scapegoating function**: When societies face crises, complex problems, or anxiety, blaming Jews provides simple explanations and targets.

**Minority status**: Jews are large enough to be visible but small enough (0.2% of world population) to lack power to defend themselves effectively.

**Success despite persecution**: Jewish emphasis on education and mutual aid often led to disproportionate success, which generated resentment.

**Religious and cultural distinctiveness**: Jewish commitment to maintaining identity despite assimilation pressure creates both admiration and resentment.

**Conspiracy theory appeal**: The idea of a small, powerful group secretly controlling events is psychologically appealing to people feeling powerless.

**Transmission across generations**: Antisemitic tropes get passed down through literature, jokes, stereotypes, and family teaching.

## Jewish Responses Throughout History

Jews responded to antisemitism in various ways:

**Legal defense**: Working within systems to gain rights and protection.

**Conversion**: Some Jews converted to Christianity to escape persecution, though racial antisemitism made even this strategy ineffective.

**Migration**: Moving to safer locations, from Spain to Poland to America to Israel.

**Self-defense**: Organizing militias and defense organizations.

**Cultural preservation**: Maintaining Jewish identity despite pressure to assimilate.

**Intellectual response**: Arguing against antisemitic lies through scholarship and debate.

**Zionism**: Building a Jewish state where Jews could defend themselves.

**Solidarity building**: Forming alliances with other marginalized groups and social justice movements.

## Understanding Antisemitism Today

Recognizing antisemitism requires understanding its patterns:

- **Double standards**: Expecting behavior from Jews/Israel not expected from others
- **Conspiracy theories**: Attributing vast power or coordinated evil to Jews
- **Dehumanization**: Portraying Jews as subhuman, demonic, or inherently evil
- **Collective guilt**: Blaming all Jews for actions of some Jews or the Israeli government
- **Holocaust minimization**: Comparing everything to the Holocaust or denying its uniqueness

## Conclusion

Antisemitism is not just another form of prejudice. Its persistence across millennia and cultures, its adaptability to different ideological frameworks, and its genocidal potential make it a unique phenomenon demanding serious study.

For non-Jews, understanding antisemitism is essential for recognizing how hatred operates, how conspiracy theories spread, and how seemingly rational societies can commit atrocities. For Jews, understanding this history is necessary for both defense and for refusing to let persecution define Jewish identity.

The fight against antisemitism isn't just a Jewish issue — it's a test of whether societies can resist scapegoating, conspiracy thinking, and dehumanization. As history shows, societies that persecute Jews rarely stop there. Antisemitism is often the canary in the coal mine for broader societal collapse.

Vigilance, education, and moral courage are the antidotes. Knowing this history is the first step.
