# Building a Minyan: How Ten Jews Change Everything

In Jewish tradition, there's something almost magical about the number ten. When ten Jewish adults gather, something shifts. Prayers that were recited individually suddenly become communal. Words that were private become public. A group of individuals transforms into a community—a minyan.

For Jews living in small communities, especially in places like China where finding even ten Jews can be challenging, understanding the minyan's importance and how to maintain one becomes crucial.

## What Is a Minyan?

Minyan (מנין) comes from the Hebrew root meaning "to count." It refers to the quorum of ten adult Jews required for certain prayers and rituals. In Orthodox Judaism, this means ten adult males (over age 13); in Conservative, Reform, and Reconstructionist Judaism, it includes women equally.

The minyan isn't just a practical gathering—it's a theological statement. Certain prayers can only be recited when the community assembles. These prayers don't work individually because they're inherently communal. They require the presence of a "congregation of Israel."

## Why Ten?

Why specifically ten? The Talmud offers several explanations:

**Abraham's pleading for Sodom**: In Genesis 18:32, Abraham bargains with God to save Sodom if ten righteous people can be found there. The Talmud derives that ten righteous individuals constitute a community worthy of God's presence (Megillah 23b).

**The ten evil spies**: When Moses sent twelve spies to scout the Land of Israel, ten returned with a negative report, causing national panic (Numbers 13-14). God called this group an "evil congregation" (edah). If ten can form an "evil congregation," ten can also form a holy one.

**Divine presence**: The Talmud teaches, "When ten Jews gather, the Divine Presence (Shekhinah) dwells among them" (Sanhedrin 39a). God's presence doesn't require thousands—just ten sincere people creates sacred space.

Ten represents a complete community—enough diversity for different perspectives, enough people that it's clearly public, small enough that everyone matters.

## What Requires a Minyan?

Several key prayers and rituals require a minyan:

### 1. **Kaddish**

The Kaddish prayer—recited by mourners and at various points in services—sanctifies God's name publicly. It's written in Aramaic (the common language of Talmudic times) specifically because it was meant to be understood by all assembled, not just scholars who knew Hebrew.

Mourners reciting Kaddish need a minyan. For someone in the eleven-month mourning period for a parent, finding daily minyan becomes urgent. This drives many small communities to organize specifically so mourners can say Kaddish.

### 2. **Barchu and Kedushah**

These prayers—responsive blessings where the congregation answers the leader—require a minyan. They're dialogues between leader and community, impossible without a community present.

### 3. **Torah Reading**

The public reading of the Torah on Shabbat, Monday, Thursday, and holidays requires a minyan. Without ten, there's no public reading—one of Judaism's oldest and most central rituals.

### 4. **Repetition of the Amidah**

On Shabbat and holidays, the Amidah (standing prayer) is repeated aloud by the prayer leader with congregational responses. This repetition requires a minyan.

### 5. **Priestly Blessing (Birkat Kohanim)**

When kohanim (descendants of the priestly line) bless the congregation, a minyan must be present.

### 6. **Wedding Blessings**

Traditional Jewish weddings include seven blessings (Sheva Brachot) that require a minyan. The joy of marriage is shared with the community.

### 7. **Comforting Mourners**

The formal consolation of mourners during shiva (the seven-day mourning period) involves special prayers requiring a minyan.

### 8. **Certain Holiday Rituals**

Reading the Megillah on Purim, saying certain Selichot (penitential prayers), and other rituals traditionally require ten.

## Why Does This Matter?

The minyan requirement forces Jews to maintain community. You can pray alone—and individual prayer is deeply valued in Judaism—but certain experiences require others.

**Accountability**: When your presence completes a minyan, you matter. You're not replaceable. Nine people need you.

**Community building**: The minyan requirement forces regular gathering. You can't just pray at home; you must connect with others.

**Shared joy and sorrow**: The requirement for minyan at weddings and funerals ensures that life's most significant moments are witnessed and shared by community.

**Prevents isolation**: Judaism's emphasis on community prevents the isolation that can come with purely individual spirituality. You can't be Jewish alone.

**Preserves tradition**: In small communities, the minyan requirement often drives organization. Without it, Jewish practice might slip away.

## The Challenge: When You Don't Have Ten

For Jews in small communities—a reality in many Chinese cities—not having a minyan is a constant challenge. What do you do?

### 1. **Recruit and Organize**

Small Jewish communities often put enormous effort into finding every Jew in the area. This might mean:

- Contacting universities (Jewish students and professors)
- Reaching out to multinational companies (Jewish employees)
- Connecting with Israeli businesses or embassies
- Using social media and Jewish geography ("Do you know anyone in Beijing?")
- Welcoming travelers (Jewish businesspeople passing through)

In Shanghai, for example, the Jewish community maintains lists of all known Jews and reaches out before holidays to ensure a minyan.

### 2. **Virtual Minyan?**

Can a minyan work over Zoom? This became urgent during COVID-19 when in-person gathering was dangerous.

**Orthodox view**: No. A minyan requires physical presence in one location. Virtual presence doesn't count. During COVID, most Orthodox communities simply didn't hold services requiring a minyan.

**Conservative movement**: Initially resistant, but some communities allowed virtual minyan during the pandemic emergency, with the requirement that all ten have video on and are actively participating. This was controversial and seen as temporary.

**Reform movement**: More flexible, allowing virtual participation to count toward a minyan, seeing community as transcending physical space.

As restrictions lifted, most communities returned to in-person minyanim, but the question remains debated.

### 3. **What to Do Without a Minyan**

If you can't gather ten, Jewish law provides alternatives:

**Skip what requires a minyan**: Simply omit Kaddish, Barchu, Kedushah, and Torah reading. Pray the rest individually or in a group of fewer than ten.

**Read Torah without blessings**: Some authorities permit reading the Torah portion without the formal blessings if you don't have ten.

**Say "Amen" to recordings**: Some allow saying "Amen" to Kaddish played from a recording, though you can't say the Kaddish itself without a minyan.

**Make special effort for key moments**: If you need a minyan for a wedding, yahrzeit, or unveiling, plan well in advance. Invite Jewish friends from other cities, coordinate with travelers, or travel to a larger community.

**Study the prayers**: If you can't say certain prayers, study their meaning and history. Understanding is its own form of connection.

### 4. **Be the Tenth**

In small communities, being the "tenth man" (or tenth person) carries special weight. There's even a Yiddish term—"der tsenter" (the tenth one).

Stories abound of people going to extraordinary lengths to complete a minyan:

- Walking miles in snow to allow a mourner to say Kaddish
- Delaying flights to ensure a minyan for someone's yahrzeit
- Strangers stopping on the street when someone calls out, "We need one more for a minyan!"

The Talmud teaches that completing a minyan is so important that even Torah study should be interrupted for it (Berakhot 6a). Making the minyan is considered a profound mitzvah.

## Gender and the Minyan: Different Approaches

The question of whether women count toward a minyan divides Jewish denominations:

**Orthodox**: Only adult males (13+) count. This is based on traditional interpretation that specific obligations (like public Torah reading) fall on men, so only they create the quorum for prayers connected to those obligations. Women can attend and participate but don't count toward the ten.

**Conservative**: Since the 1970s-80s, most Conservative congregations count women equally. The Committee on Jewish Law and Standards ruled that women, equally obligated in communal prayer, equally count toward the quorum.

**Reform and Reconstructionist**: Women count equally. These movements see gender equality as a core value and reinterpret tradition accordingly.

**Partnership minyanim**: Some Orthodox communities have created "partnership" prayer spaces where women count for some purposes but not others, seeking middle ground.

This disagreement creates practical challenges. An Orthodox Jew visiting a Reform service with ten people (six women, four men) wouldn't consider it a valid minyan. A Reform Jew visiting an Orthodox service with nine men and ten women wouldn't understand why they're waiting for another man.

In small communities with few Jews, this can cause tension. Do you maintain traditional standards (potentially never having a minyan) or adapt to ensure community prayer? Different communities make different choices.

## Building a Minyan in China: Practical Strategies

For Jewish communities in Chinese cities, here are practical approaches:

### 1. **Know Your Community**

Maintain a list of every Jew in the city—residents, students, businesspeople, diplomats. Update it regularly as people arrive and leave.

### 2. **Regular Times**

Establish fixed times for Shabbat services, even if you can't always guarantee a minyan. Consistency helps. People plan around it.

### 3. **High Holidays Priority**

Even if you can't maintain weekly minyanim, prioritize Rosh Hashanah, Yom Kippur, and Passover. People will make extra effort for major holidays.

### 4. **Welcome Non-Jewish Spouses**

While they can't count toward the minyan, non-Jewish family members should be welcomed to attend. They support the community even if they don't complete the quorum.

### 5. **Coordinate with Visitors**

Beijing and Shanghai have significant business travel. Coordinate with Jewish visitors—they're often happy to help complete a minyan, and it connects them to community while traveling.

### 6. **Partner with Chabad**

Chabad Houses in major Chinese cities work specifically to ensure minyanim. Even if not your usual affiliation, partnering with Chabad can ensure access to communal prayer.

### 7. **Technology for Organization**

Use WeChat groups, email lists, or apps to coordinate. A message "Need two more for minyan tomorrow morning" can bring people out.

### 8. **Celebrate the Tenth**

When you do achieve a minyan, acknowledge it. "Baruch Hashem, we have a minyan!" This celebrates the achievement and motivates continued effort.

## When You're Far from Any Community

If you're a Jew in a city with no organized community and no possibility of a minyan, remember:

**Individual prayer is valid**: You can pray alone. God hears individual prayer. The minyan enhances but doesn't replace personal connection with God.

**Study and observance continue**: Keep Shabbat, study Torah, observe holidays. These don't require a minyan.

**Connect virtually**: While it may not count as a formal minyan, joining Shabbat services via livestream maintains connection to global Jewish community.

**Visit larger communities**: Plan trips to coincide with holidays when possible. Experiencing community prayer periodically sustains you when isolated.

**Build toward the future**: Your efforts—even alone—plant seeds. Jews in new places often start as one person or family. Over time, community grows.

## The Deeper Meaning

The minyan teaches that Judaism is irreducibly communal. Yes, you can be Jewish alone, but certain experiences—public Torah reading, mourners' Kaddish, wedding blessings—require witnesses, participants, community.

This stands against the modern trend toward individualized, customized spirituality. Judaism says: some things can't be personalized. Some prayers need other voices. Some joys and sorrows must be shared.

When ten Jews gather, they create something greater than the sum of parts. They become a congregation—a dwelling place for God's presence, a link in the chain connecting Sinai to today, a community that supports its members through life's journey.

In a small community, every person knows this viscerally. You're not anonymous. You're needed. When you show up, you complete something sacred.

That's the power of ten—not magic, but covenant. Not numbers, but presence. Not individuals, but community.
