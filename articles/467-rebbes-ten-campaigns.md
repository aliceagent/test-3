# The Rebbe's Ten Campaigns: Practical Mitzvot for Every Jew

**Section: Chabad**

In 1967, just before the Six-Day War, the Lubavitcher Rebbe launched the first of what would become known as his "Ten Campaigns"—focused initiatives to encourage all Jews to perform specific mitzvot (commandments). Unlike abstract spiritual teachings, these campaigns were intensely practical: Light Shabbat candles. Put on tefillin. Affix a mezuzah. Give charity. Each campaign targeted a single, actionable mitzvah that anyone could perform.

For Chinese readers encountering Judaism, these campaigns offer insight into how Jewish practice works. Rather than demanding wholesale religious transformation, the Rebbe's approach was incremental: Start with one act. That single mitzvah has infinite value and might spark further engagement. For a parallel, imagine a campaign encouraging everyone—regardless of religious background—to try one session of meditation, give one charitable donation, or perform one act of filial piety. Small, specific, accessible.

Let's explore the ten campaigns, understanding both their practical details and deeper significance.

## Campaign 1: Ahavat Yisrael (Love of Fellow Jews)

**The Practice**: Actively work to love every Jew, regardless of their level of observance or background.

This might seem more like an attitude than an action, but the Rebbe made it concrete: Greet fellow Jews warmly. Avoid speaking negatively about others. Seek opportunities to help. Judge others favorably.

**Why It Matters**: The Rebbe considered *Ahavat Yisrael* foundational. All other campaigns rest on this principle. If you approach someone with judgment or condescension, they'll reject your message. If you approach with genuine love, hearts open.

**How to Practice**: Look for opportunities to connect with other Jews—a kind word, an invitation to Shabbat dinner, helping someone in need. Focus particularly on those who feel marginalized or disconnected from Jewish community.

## Campaign 2: Torah Study

**The Practice**: Study Torah regularly—ideally daily, even if only for a few minutes.

"Torah" in this context means any Jewish text: Bible, Talmud, Jewish law, ethics, philosophy, Chassidic teachings. The key is regular engagement with Jewish wisdom.

**Why It Matters**: Judaism considers Torah study not merely educational but transformative. Engaging with these texts connects you to millennia of wisdom and to the Divine mind expressing itself through Torah.

**How to Practice**: The Rebbe suggested starting small—perhaps studying one verse of Torah with commentary, or one page of Talmud daily. Many Chabad centers offer accessible classes. Online resources (including Chabad.org) provide daily study portions that take just minutes.

## Campaign 3: Tefillin (Phylacteries)

**The Practice**: Jewish men (age 13 and up) should put on tefillin every weekday morning during prayers.

Tefillin are black leather boxes containing scrolls with Torah passages, worn on the arm and head with leather straps. The practice dates to biblical commandment: "Bind them as a sign upon your arm and let them be an ornament between your eyes" (Deuteronomy 6:8).

**Why It Matters**: Tefillin represents total dedication to God—the box on the head symbolizes dedicating the mind, the box near the heart symbolizes dedicating emotions, the strap wound on the arm and hand symbolizes dedicating actions.

**How to Practice**: The Rebbe launched an intensive tefillin campaign, with Chabad emissaries approaching Jewish men everywhere—in streets, offices, airports—offering to help them put on tefillin. The entire ritual takes about five minutes. Many Chabad centers keep tefillin sets for anyone to use.

**Famous Story**: During the weeks before the Six-Day War in 1967, the Rebbe intensified the tefillin campaign, suggesting it would provide spiritual protection for Israeli soldiers. Thousands of soldiers put on tefillin at the Western Wall immediately after Israel captured Jerusalem—a moment captured in iconic photographs.

## Campaign 4: Mezuzah

**The Practice**: Affix a mezuzah to the doorpost of every qualified room in your home.

A mezuzah is a small case containing a parchment scroll with two Torah passages (Deuteronomy 6:4-9 and 11:13-21). Jewish law requires placing mezuzot on doorframes of living spaces.

**Why It Matters**: The mezuzah serves multiple functions: It declares the home Jewish space. It reminds residents of God's presence and commandments each time they enter or exit. Traditionally, it's considered to provide spiritual protection.

**How to Practice**: Have your mezuzot checked by a scribe to ensure the parchments are kosher (properly written according to Jewish law). Affix them correctly (on the right side entering, at the upper third of the doorpost, slightly angled). Chabad centers often help with mezuzah checking and installation.

## Campaign 5: Tzedakah (Charity)

**The Practice**: Give charity regularly—ideally daily, and before prayer.

The Hebrew word *tzedakah* comes from *tzedek* (justice/righteousness), suggesting that charity isn't optional generosity but obligatory justice. Jewish law requires giving at least 10% of income to those in need.

**Why It Matters**: Giving charity elevates both giver and receiver. It redistributes resources, creates interconnection, and reminds us that what we have comes from God and must be shared.

**How to Practice**: The Rebbe encouraged keeping a charity box (*pushka*) at home and dropping coins in daily, especially before candle-lighting or prayer. Children should be taught to give their own coins. The amount matters less than the regularity.

## Campaign 6: Jewish Books in the Home

**The Practice**: Keep Jewish books in your home—at minimum, a Chumash (Torah), Siddur (prayer book), and Tanya (Chabad's foundational text).

**Why It Matters**: Having Jewish books transforms your home into a place of Torah. Even if you don't read them constantly, their presence makes the home itself a sacred space. When questions arise, you have resources to consult.

**How to Practice**: Start with basics—a Chumash and Siddur (available in various translations). Add books that interest you: Jewish philosophy, Chassidic teachings, practical guides to Jewish law. Many Chabad centers distribute free Jewish books.

## Campaign 7: Neirot Shabbat U'Yom Tov (Shabbat and Holiday Candles)

**The Practice**: Jewish women and girls light candles eighteen minutes before sunset on Friday (for Shabbat) and before Jewish holidays.

Traditionally, married women light two candles (some add one for each child). Girls light one candle from age three (with adult supervision).

**Why It Matters**: The candles usher in Shabbat holiness, transform the home atmosphere, and represent bringing light into the world. The Rebbe particularly emphasized this campaign, noting that even one additional Jewish home lighting candles adds light globally.

**How to Practice**: Set reminders for candle-lighting time (eighteen minutes before local sunset on Fridays). Use candles or oil lamps (LED candles don't fulfill the mitzvah traditionally, though they're better than nothing). After lighting, cover eyes, recite the blessing, and take a moment for personal prayer.

## Campaign 8: Kashrut (Keeping Kosher)

**The Practice**: Observe Jewish dietary laws, eating only kosher food.

Kashrut includes numerous laws: certain animals permitted and others forbidden, requirement for ritual slaughter, separation of meat and dairy, and more.

**Why It Matters**: Keeping kosher elevates eating from mere physical sustenance to spiritual service. It brings mindfulness to daily life—you can't eat unconsciously when you must consider whether food is kosher.

**How to Practice**: Start small. Begin by avoiding biblically forbidden foods (pork, shellfish, mixing meat and milk). Buy products with reliable kosher certification. As you grow comfortable, explore deeper aspects of kashrut. Chabad centers often offer guidance and kashrut classes.

## Campaign 9: Taharas HaMishpacha (Family Purity)

**The Practice**: Married couples observe laws of menstrual separation and ritual immersion.

Jewish law requires physical separation during menstruation and for seven "clean days" afterward. The woman then immerses in a mikvah (ritual bath) before resuming physical intimacy.

**Why It Matters**: These laws sanctify marital intimacy, create cycles of separation and reunion that can strengthen relationships, and discipline desires in service of holiness.

**How to Practice**: This campaign requires study and commitment. Chabad centers offer classes (often taught by women for women) on family purity laws. Communities maintain mikvaot for ritual immersion.

**Cultural Note**: For Chinese readers, this topic may seem private or unusual. In Jewish tradition, these laws are considered sacred and discussed respectfully within appropriate contexts.

## Campaign 10: Jewish Education

**The Practice**: Provide children with comprehensive Jewish education.

This includes formal schooling (day school if possible), Hebrew language, Jewish texts and traditions, and creating a Jewish home environment.

**Why It Matters**: Each generation must transmit Jewish knowledge and identity to the next. Without education, Judaism cannot survive. Moreover, children deserve connection to their heritage.

**How to Practice**: Enroll children in Jewish educational programs—day schools, afternoon Hebrew schools, or Chabad youth programs. Study Jewish topics together as a family. Celebrate Jewish holidays with children, explaining meanings and practices. Model Jewish living.

## The Philosophy Behind the Campaigns

Why these particular ten mitzvot? The Rebbe chose them strategically:

**Universality**: Each campaign is accessible to all Jews—male and female, young and old, educated and uneducated.

**Concreteness**: Each involves specific, actionable practices rather than abstract beliefs.

**Daily Impact**: Most campaigns involve regular (often daily) practice, creating ongoing connection rather than occasional engagement.

**Incremental Approach**: You don't need to adopt all ten at once. Start with one. Add another when ready. Each mitzvah has independent value.

**Ripple Effects**: The Rebbe believed that even one person performing one additional mitzvah affects the spiritual state of the entire world, potentially hastening redemption.

## Practical Implementation: Start Where You Are

If you're new to Jewish practice, how do you approach these campaigns?

**Choose One**: Don't feel overwhelmed by ten campaigns. Pick one that resonates—perhaps lighting Shabbat candles or giving daily charity.

**Start Small**: Perform that mitzvah once. Then again next week. Build consistency before adding another practice.

**Seek Support**: Connect with a Chabad center or Jewish community. Having support and guidance makes practice easier.

**Don't Expect Perfection**: You'll forget sometimes. You'll do it imperfectly. That's normal. The effort matters more than flawless execution.

**Notice Effects**: Pay attention to how these practices affect your life. Do you feel more connected? More mindful? More purposeful?

## Beyond Chabad: Influence on Judaism

The Rebbe's campaigns influenced Jewish life beyond Chabad. The accessible, action-oriented approach inspired other Jewish movements to create similar initiatives. The emphasis on reaching out to unaffiliated Jews—meeting them where they are with specific, doable asks—became more common across denominations.

## Conclusion: Light Small Candles

The Rebbe often quoted a Chassidic teaching: "A little light dispels much darkness." You don't fight darkness by attacking it. You simply light a candle.

Each of the ten campaigns is a candle—a small, specific act that brings light. Light Shabbat candles: that's literal and metaphorical light. Give charity: you've illuminated someone's darkness. Study Torah: you've brought divine light into your mind.

For Chinese readers, this approach might resonate with concepts like *de* (德) in Confucianism—virtue cultivated through concrete practices—or the Buddhist emphasis on right action in daily life. Judaism, like these traditions, insists that spirituality must be embodied in regular, practical deeds.

The genius of the Rebbe's campaigns is their realism. They don't demand that you become a different person overnight. They suggest that you add one candle. Then another. Then another. Eventually, your life fills with light—not through dramatic transformation but through steady, incremental practice.

As the Rebbe himself would say: Do one mitzvah. Then do another. Each has infinite value, and together they change the world.
