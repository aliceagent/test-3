## The Deep Historical Context: Jews in China

Before discussing modern Chabad presence, it's worth understanding that Jews in China is not a new phenomenon.

### The Kaifeng Jews (Song Dynasty - 20th Century)

The most famous historical Jewish community in China was in Kaifeng, capital of Henan Province. Jews likely arrived via the Silk Road during the Song Dynasty (10th-11th centuries), establishing a community that would persist for nearly a thousand years. At its peak, Kaifeng Jews had a synagogue, maintained Jewish practices, and produced distinguished scholars and officials.

What makes the Kaifeng story poignant is both survival and assimilation. Isolated from other Jewish communities, over centuries they gradually assimilated into Chinese society while retaining some Jewish practices and identity. By the 19th century, the synagogue had fallen into ruin, and few members could read Hebrew or maintain traditional observances. Yet even today, some Kaifeng descendants identify as Jewish and seek to reconnect with global Jewish community.

The Kaifeng experience demonstrates both Judaism's adaptability and the challenges of maintaining minority identity without sustained community connection—a tension that modern Chabad addresses directly.

### 20th Century: Refugees and Revolution

The 20th century brought dramatic new chapters:

**Russian Jews (Early 1900s)**: Following pogroms and the Russian Revolution, thousands of Russian Jews fled to China, particularly Harbin (Manchuria) and Shanghai. They established synagogues, schools, newspapers, and vibrant communities. Harbin's Jewish population peaked at around 20,000 in the 1920s.

**Holocaust Refugees (1930s-1940s)**: Shanghai became a critical refuge during World War II. Unlike most countries, Shanghai required no visa for entry (technically it was outside Chinese government control). Between 1938-1941, approximately 20,000 Jewish refugees from Nazi Europe found sanctuary in Shanghai—more than Canada, Australia, and New Zealand combined accepted during the same period.

The Shanghai ghetto (Hongkou district) where Japanese authorities confined Jews was crowded and difficult, but it was survival. Chinese neighbors showed remarkable kindness, often sharing scarce food with refugees. Stories abound of Chinese families hiding Jews, Chinese doctors treating them, Chinese merchants extending credit. This period created deep emotional bonds between Chinese and Jews.

**The Mir Yeshiva**: During this same period, the entire Mir Yeshiva (a renowned Talmudic academy) relocated from Poland to Shanghai via Lithuania and Japan, spending the war years studying Torah in Shanghai. Hundreds of rabbis and students survived the Holocaust specifically because of Shanghai's refuge. This yeshiva later re-established in Jerusalem and Brooklyn, and many of its leaders maintained warm connections to China.

**Communist Era (1949-1980s)**: With the Communist victory in 1949, most Jews left China. Those who remained faced challenges under radical communist policies, particularly during the Cultural Revolution. Synagogues were closed or converted to other uses. By the 1980s, organized Jewish life in mainland China had essentially ceased to exist.

## The Modern Revival: Chabad Arrives

### Early Re-Establishment (1980s-1990s)

As China opened economically in the late 1970s and 1980s, foreigners—including Jews—began returning as businesspeople, diplomats, students, and teachers. Initially, there were no Jewish institutions. Jewish diplomats and business people would gather informally for holidays, flying in rabbis for High Holidays or Passover.

Hong Kong, which had a longer-standing Jewish community dating to the mid-1800s, served as a regional hub. Several synagogues operated in Hong Kong throughout the 20th century, serving a small but stable community.

### Chabad Establishes Permanent Presence (1990s-2000s)

The Lubavitcher Rebbe's vision of reaching every Jew, everywhere, naturally extended to China. Several factors made China attractive for Chabad expansion:

1. **Growing Jewish Presence**: Increasing numbers of Jewish businesspeople, diplomats, students, and teachers in major Chinese cities
2. **Historical Significance**: China's role as Holocaust refuge created emotional connection
3. **Strategic Importance**: China's rising global importance meant significant Jewish population engagement
4. **No Existing Infrastructure**: Unlike cities with established synagogues, China was essentially open territory where Chabad could build from scratch

**Hong Kong** was first, with Chabad of Hong Kong established in the early 1990s, serving both the local community and visitors.

**Beijing**: Rabbi Shimon Freundlich and his wife arrived as Chabad emissaries to Beijing in 2002 (though some Chabad activity predated this). They established Chabad of Beijing, serving the city's diplomatic, business, and expatriate Jewish community.

**Shanghai**: Rabbi Shalom Greenberg and his wife arrived as Chabad emissaries to Shanghai in 1998. They established Chabad of Shanghai, which has since grown into multiple centers serving different parts of the city.

Additional Chabad presences developed in **Guangzhou, Shenzhen, and other cities** as Jewish populations grew, though often smaller-scale or visiting-rabbi arrangements rather than full-time resident emissaries.

## What Chabad Centers in China Provide

Chabad centers in Chinese cities offer comprehensive Jewish services adapted to local contexts:

### Religious Services

**Shabbat Services**: Regular Friday night and Saturday morning services following traditional Ashkenazi/Chabad liturgy. These are typically informal, welcoming Jews regardless of observance level or familiarity with Hebrew.

**Holiday Services**: High Holiday services for Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur (often the highest-attended events of the year), Passover Seders, Sukkot celebrations, Purim parties, Hanukkah menorah lightings, and all other Jewish festivals.

**Daily Services**: In larger centers like Beijing and Shanghai, daily morning services (minyan) for those interested.

**Lifecycle Events**: Weddings, baby namings, bar/bat mitzvah ceremonies, conversions (following Orthodox standards), and funerals when necessary.

### Kosher Food Services

One of the most appreciated services, given the challenge of maintaining kosher diet in China:

**Kosher Restaurants/Cafeterias**: Some centers operate kosher eating facilities serving both meat and dairy meals (separately, following kosher law).

**Shabbat Meals**: Weekly Shabbat dinners and lunches open to community members, often hosted at the Chabad center or rabbi's home. These meals are social highlights, bringing together diverse Jews—diplomats, businesspeople, students, tourists, long-term residents.

**Kosher Catering**: For events, holidays, or private occasions.

**Kosher Products**: Import and distribution of kosher-certified foods—wine, matzah for Passover, meat, specialty items difficult to find in regular Chinese stores.

**Kosher Certification**: Some Chabad centers work with local food producers to establish kosher certification for items that can be produced locally under supervision.

### Educational Programs

**Adult Education**: Torah classes, Jewish philosophy discussions, Hebrew lessons, Jewish history, and contemporary issues—all tailored for various knowledge levels.

**Children's Programs**: Hebrew school, bar/bat mitzvah preparation, Jewish youth groups, summer camps (often in partnership with international programs).

**University Outreach**: Chabad on Campus programs at universities with significant Jewish student populations, offering Shabbat dinners, study sessions, and social events.

**Women's Programs**: Classes, social gatherings, Rosh Chodesh (new moon) celebrations—spaces for Jewish women to connect and learn.

### Social and Support Services

**Community Building**: Creating networks among Jews in China—professional connections, friendships, support systems.

**Newcomer Assistance**: Helping newly arrived Jewish families navigate life in China—finding apartments, schools, medical care, and cultural adjustment.

**Crisis Support**: Assisting Jews in emergencies—illness, accidents, deaths, legal issues, or other crises.

**Hospital Visits**: Providing pastoral care for Jewish patients in Chinese hospitals.

**Connecting Travelers**: Helping Jewish tourists or business travelers find Jewish services, kosher food, and community wherever they are in China.

### Cultural Bridge-Building

**Jewish-Chinese Cultural Programs**: Events exploring connections between Jewish and Chinese civilizations, introducing Judaism to Chinese audiences, and helping Jews understand Chinese culture.

**Holocaust Education**: Working with Chinese institutions to share Holocaust history and lessons, particularly highlighting Shanghai's role as refuge.

**Museum Collaboration**: Partnering with museums documenting Jewish history in Shanghai and Harbin.

**Academic Partnerships**: Collaborating with Chinese universities on Jewish studies programs.

## The Unique Challenges of China

Chabad emissaries in China face distinctive challenges:

### Cultural and Language Barriers

Most emissaries arrive speaking little or no Mandarin. Learning the language while building community is demanding. Cultural differences—in communication styles, social expectations, business practices—require sensitivity and adaptation.

Many hire Chinese staff to help bridge these gaps, creating interesting cross-cultural workplaces where Chinese employees learn about Judaism while helping Jews navigate Chinese society.

### Regulatory Environment

Operating religious institutions in China requires navigating complex regulations. Chabad centers typically register as cultural or community organizations. They maintain good relationships with government authorities while serving their communities' needs.

This requires careful balance—being authentic to Jewish tradition while respecting Chinese law and cultural norms. Emissaries report generally positive relationships with local authorities, who recognize the contributions these institutions make to expatriate communities.

### Transient Population

Unlike communities with multi-generational roots, Jewish communities in Chinese cities are largely transient. Diplomats rotate every few years. Businesspeople's postings are temporary. Students graduate. This creates constant turnover—just as you build relationships, people leave.

This affects sustainability and community cohesion. Chabad must continually welcome newcomers while maintaining core community, creating structures that work despite constant flux.

### Kosher Food Supply Chain

Maintaining kosher standards in a country without established kosher infrastructure is complex. Meat must be imported (typically from Australia or other approved sources). Many products require special importing or kashering. This makes kosher food expensive and logistically challenging.

Some products can be produced locally under supervision—bread, some produce, certain processed foods—but the supply chain requires constant attention and creativity.

### Distance from Major Jewish Centers

China is far from Israel, the U.S., and other Jewish population centers. Emissaries can't easily visit family. Bringing in guest speakers, obtaining Jewish books and ritual objects, and staying connected to broader Jewish world requires extra effort.

The emissary families themselves make significant sacrifices—raising children far from extended family and mainstream Jewish communities, limited access to Jewish education resources beyond what they create themselves.

## Success Stories and Impact

Despite challenges, Chabad in China has achieved remarkable success:

### Growing Communities

Jewish communities in Beijing, Shanghai, and Hong Kong have grown significantly. Hundreds attend High Holiday services. Weekly Shabbat dinners draw diverse crowds. Holiday celebrations are major community events.

### Jewish Identity Preservation

For Jews living in China—often their first extended time living as a minority—Chabad centers help maintain Jewish identity. Children growing up in China still learn Hebrew, celebrate Jewish holidays, and feel connected to Jewish people worldwide.

### Positive Chinese-Jewish Relations

Chabad's presence facilitates positive interactions between Chinese and Jews. Chinese spouses of Jewish members often attend events. Chinese business partners are invited to Sabbath dinners. Chinese students curious about Judaism attend classes. This creates mutual understanding and respect.

### Historical Memory

Chabad's work helps preserve memory of Shanghai's role as Holocaust refuge. The Shanghai Jewish Refugees Museum has received support from Chabad and international Jewish organizations. Annual commemorations mark this history, attended by Chinese officials, Holocaust survivors, and their descendants.

### Bridge for Chinese Jews

Some Kaifeng descendants and other Chinese citizens with Jewish heritage have found connection to Judaism through Chabad centers. While Orthodox conversion standards are strict, Chabad provides education, welcome, and pathways for those seeking to explore or reclaim Jewish identity.

## How to Connect If You're in China

### Finding Chabad Centers

**Chabad of Beijing**
- Website: ChabadBeijing.com
- Services: Shabbat services, kosher meals, classes, lifecycle events
- Location: Generally in or near diplomatic areas (specific addresses available on website)

**Chabad of Shanghai**
- Website: ChabadShanghai.com (multiple centers exist)
- Services: Comprehensive programming across the city
- Locations: Hongqiao area and other neighborhoods

**Chabad of Hong Kong**
- Multiple centers serving different communities
- Websites: Various (search "Chabad Hong Kong")
- Services: Full range of Orthodox Jewish services

**Other Cities**: For Guangzhou, Shenzhen, or other cities, check Chabad.org's center directory or contact nearest major center for referrals.

### What to Expect

**No Membership Required**: Chabad centers don't charge membership fees. All services are free or by voluntary donation.

**All Welcome**: Whether you're Orthodox, Conservative, Reform, secular, or just curious—you're welcome. Chabad follows Orthodox practice but welcomes Jews of all backgrounds.

**Informal Atmosphere**: Particularly for Shabbat meals, expect warm, informal, family-style atmosphere. Dress respectfully but not necessarily formally.

**Language**: Services typically in Hebrew with some English explanation. Social interaction often mixes English, Hebrew, and sometimes other languages. If you don't understand, ask—people are happy to explain.

**Cultural Mix**: You'll meet Jews from around the world—Americans, Israelis, Europeans, Australians, Africans, Latin Americans. The diversity is part of the richness.

### Practical Tips

1. **Contact in Advance**: Especially for Shabbat meals or holiday events, contact the center to register. This helps them plan for food and space.

2. **Shabbat Timing**: Candle-lighting times vary by season and location. Check the center's website or contact them for exact times.

3. **Kosher Considerations**: If you're not familiar with kosher laws, don't worry—staff will guide you. The main thing is being respectful of the dietary standards maintained.

4. **Donations**: While nothing is mandatory, these centers operate on donations. Contributing according to your means helps sustain services.

5. **Volunteer**: If you're in China long-term, consider volunteering—helping with programs, teaching skills, supporting events.

## The Larger Significance

Chabad's presence in China represents something beyond just religious services. It demonstrates:

**Diaspora Resilience**: How Jewish communities maintain identity and practice even in locations far from traditional Jewish centers.

**Cultural Adaptation**: How ancient traditions adapt to new cultural contexts without losing essential character.

**Universal Values**: The encounters between Jewish and Chinese civilizations highlight universal human values—hospitality, learning, family, community, ethical behavior—that transcend specific traditions.

**Historical Continuity**: The Jewish story in China—from medieval Kaifeng through Holocaust refuge to contemporary communities—is part of broader Jewish and Chinese histories, each stretching back millennia.

**Contemporary Globalization**: Jewish professionals and families living in China, just as Chinese professionals live in Israel and other countries, represents modern global mobility. Religious and cultural institutions help people maintain identity while fully engaging with host cultures.

## Looking Forward

The future of Jewish life in China likely includes:

- **Growing Communities**: As China's economy grows and international engagement deepens, Jewish presence will likely increase.

- **Indigenous Leadership**: Eventually, Jews born or raised in China may become leaders of these communities, bringing unique Chinese-Jewish perspectives.

- **Educational Exchange**: Increased academic exchange on Jewish studies between Chinese and international universities.

- **Cultural Understanding**: Deeper mutual understanding between two ancient civilizations with shared values of learning, family, and ethical living.

- **Kaifeng Revival**: Potential reconnection of Kaifeng Jewish descendants with global Jewish community, with Chabad playing facilitating role.

Chabad's permanent presence ensures that no matter how Jewish demographics in China evolve, there will be accessible Jewish institutions ready to serve, welcome, teach, and build community—continuing a story that began over a thousand years ago and shows no signs of ending.
