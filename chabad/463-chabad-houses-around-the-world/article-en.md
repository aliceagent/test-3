# Chabad Houses Around the World: Judaism's Global Outreach Network

## How Chabad Emissaries Established Centers in Over 100 Countries—Including China

In a small apartment in Kathmandu, Nepal, a young couple serves Shabbat dinner to Israeli backpackers who've just finished trekking the Himalayas. In Beijing, a rabbi leads a Passover Seder for businesspeople, diplomats, and students from around the world. In a rural town in Montana, USA, a family opens their home every Friday night to the only other Jewish family within 200 miles. What connects these seemingly disparate scenes? They're all Chabad Houses—a global network of Jewish centers that has revolutionized how Judaism reaches Jews wherever they are.

Today, there are over 5,000 Chabad institutions in more than 100 countries, run by over 5,000 emissary couples (shluchim and shluchos) who have dedicated their lives to serving Jewish communities worldwide. This remarkable network, all traceable to the vision of one leader—the Lubavitcher Rebbe, Rabbi Menachem Mendel Schneerson—represents one of the most successful grassroots religious movements in modern history.

## The Revolutionary Concept

Before Chabad's global outreach, synagogues and Jewish institutions typically waited for Jews to come to them. You joined a synagogue, paid membership dues, attended services, and participated in programs. If you didn't seek out the community, you remained disconnected.

The Rebbe flipped this model entirely. His directive to young couples was radical: Go to wherever Jews are, even if there are only a few, even if they're not observant, even if they've never set foot in a synagogue. Don't wait for them to find you—find them. Don't judge their level of observance—meet them where they are. Don't ask what they can do for Judaism—ask what you can do for them.

This philosophy—expressed in the Hebrew phrase "Ufaratzta" (you shall spread out), from Jacob's dream in Genesis 28:14—became the driving force behind an unprecedented expansion. Starting in the 1950s, but accelerating dramatically in the 1970s and beyond, the Rebbe began sending young emissary couples to destinations around the globe.

## What Is a Chabad House?

A Chabad House is hard to define precisely because each one adapts to its local context. But typically, it includes:

**A Home**: Most Chabad Houses begin literally in the emissaries' own home. The family opens their doors—their living room becomes a synagogue, their dining table hosts community Shabbat dinners, their children grow up surrounded by guests. This creates an atmosphere of warmth and informality that formal institutions often lack.

**A Synagogue or Prayer Space**: Even if it's just a room with chairs and a Torah scroll, there's a place for daily and Shabbat services, following Chabad-Lubavitch traditions but welcoming Jews of all backgrounds.

**Educational Programs**: Torah classes, Jewish holiday workshops, Hebrew school for children, adult education, university campus programs, and one-on-one learning opportunities. The curriculum often includes Chassidic philosophy alongside traditional texts.

**Lifecycle Services**: Rabbis perform weddings, baby namings, bar/bat mitzvah celebrations, conversions, and funerals—often for Jews who have no other connection to Jewish life.

**Social Services**: Counseling, assistance for travelers in distress, hospital visits, prison chaplaincy, kosher food provision, and helping Jews navigate crises.

**Holiday Celebrations**: Public menorah lightings during Hanukkah, communal Passover Seders, Purim celebrations, High Holiday services—often the only such events available in remote locations.

**No Membership, No Dues**: One of the most revolutionary aspects—Chabad Houses don't charge membership fees. Services are free or by voluntary donation. No one is turned away for lack of funds. This removes a major barrier for unaffiliated Jews.

## The Economics: How Do They Sustain Themselves?

This raises an obvious question: How do these centers survive financially?

Chabad emissaries are largely self-funded. Each couple raises their own budget through fundraising—donations from local community members, supporters from around the world, events, grants, and sometimes the emissaries' own family resources. The central Chabad organization provides some initial support and resources, but most Chabad Houses operate independently.

This creates both challenges and benefits. The challenge: emissaries must be not only rabbis and educators but also fundraisers and organizational leaders. The benefit: each center is entrepreneurial, adaptable, and responsive to local needs rather than bureaucratically constrained.

Many emissaries live modestly, sometimes struggling financially, sustained by deep commitment to their mission. Their compensation comes less in salary than in the satisfaction of watching someone reconnect with Jewish heritage, helping a traveler in distress, or seeing their community grow.

## The Training and Selection

Becoming a Chabad emissary is not a simple process. It typically involves:

1. **Growing up in the Chabad community**: Most emissaries are children of Chabad families, raised with these values.

2. **Advanced Torah Education**: Years of intensive study at Chabad yeshivas (religious academies), where young men study Talmud, Jewish law, Chassidic philosophy, and practical rabbinics. Women attend Chabad seminaries focusing on Jewish education, pastoral skills, and practical community work.

3. **Marriage**: Emissaries work as couples. The matching process (shidduch) often considers complementary skills and shared commitment to outreach work.

4. **Assignment**: Couples meet with the Rebbe's representatives (after the Rebbe's passing in 1994, decisions are made collectively by senior rabbis) to discuss where they might serve. Sometimes emissaries choose their own location; sometimes they're sent to unexpected places.

5. **Pioneering Spirit**: Those who succeed tend to be resourceful, resilient, able to operate with minimal infrastructure, and genuinely love people.

## Chabad in Unexpected Places

Part of Chabad's remarkable story is the diversity of locations:

**Remote and Rural**: Chabad centers exist in places like Alaska, rural Australia, small-town America, and remote islands—anywhere there's even a small Jewish presence.

**Tourist Destinations**: Places where Jews travel—Thailand, Peru, the Caribbean, Nepal—often have Chabad Houses that serve tourists, providing kosher food, holiday celebrations, and emergency assistance.

**University Campuses**: Over 300 Chabad on Campus centers serve Jewish students, providing Shabbat meals, classes, counseling, and a home-away-from-home atmosphere.

**Major Cities**: In metropolitan areas, multiple Chabad centers may serve different neighborhoods, each with its own character and focus.

**Post-Communist Countries**: After the fall of the Soviet Union, Chabad emissaries rushed into former Soviet territories, rebuilding Jewish life in places where it had been nearly destroyed.

**Asia**: This includes China, where Chabad has established centers in multiple cities.

## Chabad in China: A Special Story

China presents unique opportunities and challenges for Chabad's work:

**Shanghai**: The Chabad of Shanghai, located in the Hongqiao area, serves the city's Jewish community with Shabbat services, holiday celebrations, kosher meals, and educational programs. Shanghai has historical significance as a refuge for Jews fleeing the Holocaust and earlier pogroms.

**Beijing**: Chabad of Beijing provides services for the capital's diplomatic, business, and expatriate Jewish community, as well as Jewish tourists. It operates a synagogue, offers kosher catering, and organizes holiday events.

**Hong Kong**: Multiple Chabad centers serve Hong Kong's diverse Jewish community, including long-established families, expatriates, and visitors.

**Other Cities**: Smaller Chabad presences exist in cities like Guangzhou and Shenzhen, adapting to local conditions.

Chinese Chabad centers face particular challenges: language barriers, cultural differences, complex regulations, and serving a transient population (many Jews in China are temporary residents). Yet they persist, offering Jewish services in a land far from traditional Jewish centers.

The emissaries often become bridges between Chinese and Jewish cultures, sometimes learning Mandarin, respecting local customs, and helping their Jewish visitors understand and appreciate their Chinese hosts. This cultural mediation is itself a form of service.

## The Chabad Approach: Why It Works

Several factors explain Chabad's success:

**Unconditional Welcome**: No judgment about observance level. A completely secular Jew receives the same warm welcome as an Orthodox rabbi.

**Personal Connection**: The emissaries live among the people they serve. They're accessible, often becoming friends and confidants, not just religious functionaries.

**Joy and Warmth**: Chabad emphasizes joyful Judaism—singing, dancing, celebrating, eating together. This attracts people who might find traditional synagogues formal or cold.

**Flexibility**: Each Chabad House adapts to its context. A university Chabad looks different from a rural one, which differs from a tourist-destination center.

**Long-term Commitment**: Emissaries typically stay for life. They become permanent fixtures in their communities, watching children grow up, celebrating lifecycle events across generations.

**The Rebbe's Inspiration**: Even decades after the Rebbe's passing, his vision continues to motivate emissaries. Many feel personally directed by studying his teachings and letters.

## Criticisms and Controversies

Chabad's success hasn't been without criticism:

- Some accuse Chabad of being more interested in converting non-observant Jews to Chabad's way of life than respecting diverse Jewish expressions.
- The messianic beliefs some Chabad members hold about the Rebbe being the Messiah have been controversial.
- Traditional synagogues sometimes feel Chabad competes for members while not contributing to communal infrastructure costs.
- Some question the sustainability of the model—can thousands of families continue to raise funds independently?

Chabad responds that they're not trying to make everyone Chabad, just to help every Jew access their heritage; that messianic beliefs are a minority position; that they serve people who wouldn't otherwise engage with Judaism; and that over 70 years of growth demonstrates the model's viability.

## The Human Stories

Behind the statistics are remarkable human stories:

- The Chabad couple in Mumbai who sheltered survivors during the 2008 terrorist attacks, with the rabbi and his wife among those killed while protecting others.
- Emissaries in Nepal who've rescued Israeli backpackers from dangerous situations, addiction, and despair.
- Rabbis who've brought kosher food to elderly Jews in remote locations who hadn't eaten kosher in decades.
- Couples raising large families in challenging conditions—extreme climates, cultural isolation, security risks—sustained by their sense of mission.

## Looking Forward

The Chabad House network continues to expand. New centers open regularly, often in places where Jewish presence is minimal. As global mobility increases, Chabad adapts—creating resources for digital outreach, establishing temporary centers in new locations, and training the next generation of emissaries.

For Chinese readers, the Chabad model offers an interesting example of how an ancient tradition adapts to modern, globalized conditions while maintaining core identity. It demonstrates that religious community can be built on relationships rather than just buildings, on service rather than membership dues, and on going to people rather than waiting for them to come.

Whether you encounter a Chabad House as a traveler seeking a Friday night meal, a student exploring Jewish heritage, or simply someone curious about this global phenomenon, you'll likely find the same elements: warmth, welcomeness, singing, food, and people dedicated to ensuring that no Jew, anywhere in the world, is alone.
