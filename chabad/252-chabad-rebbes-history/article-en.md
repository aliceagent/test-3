## Introduction

When we discussed the Alter Rebbe and the Baal Shem Tov in previous articles, we explored the founding of Chassidic thought and the birth of Chabad. But what happened next? How did this movement survive persecution, exile, war, and modernity to become one of the most influential forces in contemporary Judaism?

The answer lies in an unbroken chain of seven Rebbes — each a scholar, mystic, and leader who took the torch from his predecessor and carried it through some of the darkest chapters in Jewish history. While we've already met the first two (the Alter Rebbe and the Mitteler Rebbe), today we'll meet the third through sixth Rebbes: the Tzemach Tzedek, the Rebbe Maharash, the Rebbe Rashab, and the Frierdiker Rebbe.

Each faced unique challenges: government oppression, internal division, pogroms, the Communist Revolution, the Holocaust. Each responded with courage, innovation, and unwavering commitment to Torah and the Jewish people. Their stories span from the mid-19th century through the eve of the modern Chabad movement — roughly corresponding to China's Qing Dynasty decline, the Republic era, and the early Communist period.

Understanding these Rebbes is essential to understanding how Chabad survived — and why it thrives today.

---

## The Third Chabad Rebbe: The Tzemach Tzedek (1789–1866)

### Who He Was

Rabbi Menachem Mendel Schneersohn, known as the **Tzemach Tzedek** (a name taken from his most famous halachic work), was the third leader of Chabad. Born in 1789 — during what corresponds to China's mid-Qing Dynasty (清朝, 1644–1912) — he was the grandson of the Alter Rebbe and was raised in his home after being orphaned as a child.

The Tzemach Tzedek lived through the twilight of the Russian Empire's "enlightened" period and the beginning of harsh government interference in Jewish life. He was not only a Rebbe — a spiritual leader — but also one of the greatest Talmudic scholars of his generation, authoring thousands of responsa (halachic rulings) and mystical teachings.

### His Challenges

The Tzemach Tzedek faced two major crises:

1. **Government Persecution**: The Russian government, under Tsar Nicholas I, sought to "modernize" the Jews by force. They established government-controlled schools to secularize Jewish children, imposed brutal military conscription (often taking boys as young as 12 for 25 years of service), and tried to suppress traditional Jewish education and dress.

2. **Internal Division**: Some Jews, influenced by the Haskalah (Jewish Enlightenment), advocated for assimilation and abandoning traditional practices. Others wanted to compromise with the government. The Tzemach Tzedek had to navigate these pressures while preserving authentic Torah Judaism.

### His Response

The Tzemach Tzedek adopted a strategy of **quiet resistance**. Publicly, he engaged diplomatically with government officials, seeking to minimize harm to the Jewish community. Privately, he strengthened Jewish education, supported underground yeshivas, and ruled firmly on halachic matters — refusing to compromise on essentials like Shabbat, kashrut, and Torah study.

He famously declared: **"Make yourself a Rav and remove yourself from doubt"** — urging every Jew to be strong in their own convictions rather than follow the crowd. He also established Chabad's tradition of sending emissaries (*shluchim*) to distant Jewish communities to teach and inspire.

### His Legacy

The Tzemach Tzedek's 38-year leadership (1827–1866) solidified Chabad as a movement that combined deep mystical thought with uncompromising halachic standards. His thousands of written responsa became foundational texts studied in yeshivas worldwide. He also had seven sons, each a brilliant scholar, leading to the establishment of multiple Chassidic dynasties (though only one line — through his youngest son — continued the official Chabad leadership).

## The Fourth Chabad Rebbe: The Rebbe Maharash (1834–1882)

### Who He Was

Rabbi Shmuel Schneersohn, known as the **Rebbe Maharash** (acronym for *Morenu HaRav Shmuel*), was the youngest son of the Tzemach Tzedek and became the fourth Rebbe in 1866 — during a period corresponding to China's late Qing Dynasty, marked by the Taiping Rebellion and increasing internal instability.

Unlike his older brothers, who were known for their scholarly brilliance, the Rebbe Maharash was known for his bold, visionary leadership and his willingness to take unconventional approaches.

### His Motto: "L'chatchila Ariber"

The Rebbe Maharash's most famous teaching was a single Yiddish phrase: **"L'chatchila ariber"** — literally, "from the outset, go over it."

What does this mean? Most people approach obstacles by trying to go around them, under them, or through them. The Rebbe Maharash taught a different approach: don't even recognize the obstacle. **Start from the assumption that you will succeed**, and leap over the barrier with faith and determination.

This wasn't reckless optimism — it was a spiritual philosophy rooted in the belief that when you align yourself with God's will, apparent obstacles lose their power. It became a defining characteristic of Chabad activism: rather than being paralyzed by "impossibilities," Chabad emissaries are trained to think, *"What needs to be done? Then let's do it."*

### His Challenges and Responses

The Rebbe Maharash faced escalating anti-Semitic violence (pogroms) and government restrictions. He responded by:
- **Establishing new yeshivas** in secret locations
- **Financially supporting Jewish refugees** fleeing persecution
- **Strengthening Chabad's infrastructure** by appointing mashpi'im (spiritual mentors) in communities across Russia
- **Sending emissaries** to distant regions, often at great personal risk

He also moved Chabad's headquarters from Lubavitch to Lubavitch (after a brief relocation), reinforcing the movement's commitment to staying rooted despite external pressures.

### His Legacy

The Rebbe Maharash's 16-year leadership (1866–1882) transformed Chabad into an activist movement. His "l'chatchila ariber" philosophy became the spiritual DNA of modern Chabad outreach. Every Chabad house opening in a remote corner of the world, every public menorah lighting, every bold initiative — traces back to his teaching: don't calculate the odds; do what needs to be done.

## The Fifth Chabad Rebbe: The Rebbe Rashab (1860–1920)

### Who He Was

Rabbi Sholom Dov Ber Schneersohn, known as the **Rebbe Rashab** (acronym for *Rabbeinu Sholom Ber*), was the son of the Rebbe Maharash and became the fifth Rebbe in 1882 — a period corresponding to China's late Qing Dynasty and the lead-up to the 1911 Xinhai Revolution (辛亥革命).

The Rebbe Rashab is considered one of the greatest Chassidic thinkers in history. His collected teachings (*maamarim*) fill dozens of volumes and are studied intensively in Chabad yeshivas today. But beyond his scholarship, he was a visionary educator who revolutionized Chassidic pedagogy.

### His Great Innovation: The Tomchei Temimim Yeshiva

In 1897, the Rebbe Rashab established **Tomchei Temimim** (literally, "Supporters of the Wholesome Ones"), the first yeshiva in Chassidic history that combined Talmudic study, Jewish law, and deep Chassidic philosophy into a unified curriculum.

Why was this revolutionary? Previous yeshivas focused almost exclusively on Talmud and halacha. Chassidic teachings were studied separately, often informally. The Rebbe Rashab argued that a complete Jewish education required **both the intellectual rigor of Talmud and the spiritual depth of Chassidut** — studied together, not in isolation.

He designed a curriculum where students would spend hours each day studying the Alter Rebbe's *Tanya* and Chassidic discourses alongside Talmud and Shulchan Aruch. The goal was to produce not just scholars, but **"Temimim"** — wholesome, integrated individuals who embodied Torah scholarship, Chassidic warmth, and practical leadership.

This model became the template for all future Chabad yeshivas — and influenced the broader yeshiva world as well.

### His Challenges: Revolution and Persecution

The Rebbe Rashab's leadership coincided with some of the most turbulent decades in Russian Jewish history:
- **Pogroms** became increasingly violent (especially after 1905)
- **The Russian Revolution** (1917) brought the Bolsheviks to power, who sought to destroy religion entirely
- **World War I** devastated Jewish communities across Eastern Europe

The Rebbe Rashab responded by:
- **Sending students and emissaries** to establish new communities and yeshivas
- **Organizing underground Jewish education** after the Communists banned religious schools
- **Risking his own safety** to rescue Torah scrolls, Jewish books, and endangered Jews

In 1915, as World War I raged, the Rebbe Rashab was forced to flee Lubavitch. He spent his final years in exile, first in Rostov (Russia), then constantly on the move. Despite his failing health and the chaos around him, he continued teaching, writing, and guiding his followers.

### His Legacy

The Rebbe Rashab passed away in 1920, just as the Soviet persecution of Judaism intensified. His greatest legacy was **Tomchei Temimim** — an army of spiritually trained, intellectually rigorous Chassidim who would soon be called upon to keep Judaism alive in the Soviet underground.

His son, the sixth Rebbe, would inherit not just a movement but a battlefield.

## The Sixth Chabad Rebbe: The Frierdiker Rebbe (1880–1950)

### Who He Was

Rabbi Yosef Yitzchak Schneersohn, known as the **Frierdiker Rebbe** (Yiddish for "the Previous Rebbe"), was the son of the Rebbe Rashab and became the sixth Rebbe in 1920 — a period corresponding to the early years of the Republic of China (1912–1949) and the chaotic warlord era.

The Frierdiker Rebbe is one of the most heroic figures in Jewish history. For 30 years, he fought an open war against the Soviet regime's attempt to eradicate Judaism — and won.

### His Underground Network

When the Communists came to power, they outlawed:
- Private Jewish schools
- Public prayer gatherings
- Teaching Torah to children
- Yeshivas and seminaries
- Production of Jewish books and religious objects

The penalty for violating these laws ranged from imprisonment in Siberian labor camps to execution.

Most rabbis fled or went silent. The Frierdiker Rebbe did the opposite: **he built a massive underground educational network** across the Soviet Union.

He established:
- **Secret yeshivas** in hidden apartments and basements
- **Underground schools** for children, with teachers who risked their lives daily
- **Smuggling routes** for tefillin, mezuzahs, Torah scrolls, and Jewish books
- **Mikvahs** (ritual baths) disguised as ordinary buildings
- **A secret printing press** that produced Jewish texts in defiance of Soviet censorship

He recruited and trained hundreds of young Chassidim — graduates of his father's Tomchei Temimim yeshiva — to become *moser nefesh* (literally, "those who give their souls") agents in this spiritual resistance movement.

### Arrest, Imprisonment, and Miraculous Release

In 1927, the Soviet secret police (GPU, precursor to the KGB) arrested the Frierdiker Rebbe. He was charged with "counter-revolutionary activities" — a charge that usually meant execution.

For weeks, he was held in the notorious Spalerno Prison in Leningrad (St. Petersburg), interrogated and tortured. The interrogators tried to force him to reveal the names of his underground network and to sign a confession denouncing Judaism.

The Frierdiker Rebbe refused. Even under threat of death, he famously told his interrogators: **"Only my body is in your hands. My soul and my faith, you cannot touch."**

Miraculously — and historians still debate exactly how — international pressure and behind-the-scenes negotiations led to his release. His death sentence was commuted to exile, first to Kostroma (a remote town), and eventually to full freedom on condition that he leave the Soviet Union.

On **12 Tammuz, 5687 (July 3, 1927)**, he was released — a date still celebrated in Chabad as a "festival of liberation."

### Escape and Rebuilding

After his release, the Frierdiker Rebbe moved first to Riga (Latvia), then to Warsaw (Poland), continuing to support the underground network in the Soviet Union. When the Nazis invaded Poland in 1939, he narrowly escaped — first to Riga, then via a harrowing journey to the United States in 1940.

From his new base in Brooklyn, New York, he worked tirelessly to:
- **Rescue Jews** from Europe during and after the Holocaust
- **Rebuild Chabad institutions** in America
- **Send emissaries** to Jewish communities around the world
- **Establish new yeshivas** and schools

### His Final Years and Vision

The Frierdiker Rebbe's health had been shattered by his years of imprisonment and hardship. He suffered multiple heart attacks and strokes. Yet he continued working until his final day, driven by a singular mission: **to ensure that Judaism would not only survive but thrive** in the modern world.

He laid the groundwork for what would become the global Chabad-Lubavitch movement, appointing emissaries, establishing institutions, and inspiring a generation of young Jews — including his son-in-law, who would become the seventh and most famous Rebbe.

The Frierdiker Rebbe passed away on **10 Shevat, 5710 (January 28, 1950)**. Over 10,000 people attended his funeral in Brooklyn, a testament to the movement he had built against impossible odds.

## The Chain of Leadership: A Pattern Emerges

Looking at these four Rebbes together, a pattern becomes clear:

### 1. **Scholarship + Activism**
Each Rebbe was both a brilliant Torah scholar and a practical leader. They didn't just write books — they built institutions, mobilized people, and took personal risks.

### 2. **Adaptation Without Compromise**
Each faced unique challenges (government persecution, revolution, war) and adapted their tactics — but never compromised on Torah principles.

### 3. **Investment in Education**
From the Tzemach Tzedek's underground schools to the Rebbe Rashab's Tomchei Temimim to the Frierdiker Rebbe's Soviet network, education was always the priority.

### 4. **Sending Out Emissaries**
Every Rebbe sent *shluchim* (emissaries) to distant communities — a practice that would explode into a global movement under the seventh Rebbe.

### 5. **Unbroken Chain**
Each Rebbe prepared his successor, ensuring continuity. The leadership passed:
- Alter Rebbe → Mitteler Rebbe (son)
- Mitteler Rebbe → Tzemach Tzedek (nephew/son-in-law)
- Tzemach Tzedek → Rebbe Maharash (youngest son)
- Rebbe Maharash → Rebbe Rashab (son)
- Rebbe Rashab → Frierdiker Rebbe (son)
- Frierdiker Rebbe → **the Rebbe** (son-in-law)

This chain represents not just biological descent but **spiritual transmission** — each generation receiving the *mesorah* (tradition) and adapting it for their time.

## Why These Rebbes Matter Today

When you see a Chabad house in Tokyo, Mumbai, Beijing, or Buenos Aires — you're seeing the fruit of seeds planted by these Rebbes.

When you see young Chabad rabbis boldly establishing institutions in places where Judaism barely exists — that's the Rebbe Maharash's "l'chatchila ariber."

When you see the emphasis on deep Jewish education combined with warmth and outreach — that's the Rebbe Rashab's vision from Tomchei Temimim.

When you see the willingness to risk comfort and safety to bring Judaism to others — that's the Frierdiker Rebbe's *mesirus nefesh*.

The seventh Rebbe — whom we'll explore in a future article — took all of these threads and wove them into the modern Chabad movement. But he didn't invent these ideas. He inherited them from six generations of leaders who refused to let darkness extinguish the light.

## Practical Application

**For Your Life:**
1. **"L'chatchila Ariber" Thinking**: Next time you face an obstacle, ask yourself: Am I assuming it's impossible, or am I assuming I can succeed? Try approaching the challenge with the Rebbe Maharash's faith and boldness.

2. **Integrated Learning**: Like the Rebbe Rashab, seek to integrate intellectual knowledge with spiritual depth. Don't just study — internalize. Don't just know facts — live values.

3. **Stand for Something**: The Frierdiker Rebbe could have fled, compromised, or stayed silent. He chose to fight. When you're pressured to compromise on your values, remember his response: "My soul and my faith, you cannot touch."

## Key Takeaways

- The **Tzemach Tzedek** (1789–1866) navigated government persecution with quiet resistance, strengthening Jewish education and halachic integrity during Russia's attempts to forcibly modernize Jews.

- The **Rebbe Maharash** (1834–1882) introduced the "l'chatchila ariber" philosophy — leap over obstacles with faith — transforming Chabad into an activist movement.

- The **Rebbe Rashab** (1860–1920) established Tomchei Temimim yeshiva, revolutionizing Jewish education by integrating Talmud, halacha, and Chassidic philosophy into one curriculum.

- The **Frierdiker Rebbe** (1880–1950) built a massive underground Jewish educational network in the Soviet Union, survived imprisonment and torture, and laid the foundation for the global Chabad movement.

- Together, these four Rebbes embodied **scholarship, courage, innovation, and unwavering commitment to Torah** — ensuring that Chabad not only survived but thrived through the darkest periods of modern Jewish history.
