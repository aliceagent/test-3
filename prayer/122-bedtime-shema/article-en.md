# The Bedtime Shema: Ending Your Day with God

---

*Kriat Shema Al HaMitah* - A Sacred Bridge Between Day and Night

The final moments before sleep offer a profound opportunity for spiritual connection. In Jewish tradition, the bedtime Shema (*Kriat Shema Al HaMitah*) serves as a sacred bridge between the consciousness of day and the vulnerability of night, wrapping us in divine protection as we surrender to rest.

## The Foundation of Bedtime Prayer

The practice finds its roots in the Torah's instruction: "You shall speak of them when you lie down and when you rise up" (Deuteronomy 6:7). While the Shema is traditionally recited during morning and evening prayers, our sages recognized the unique spiritual significance of this liminal moment between wakefulness and sleep.

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi taught that one should recite the Shema before bed, even after having said it during evening prayers (Berakhot 4b). The Talmud explains this through Psalm 4:5: "Tremble and sin no more; ponder it on your bed, and sigh" - suggesting that bedtime offers a special opportunity for reflection and connection with the Divine.

## The Structure of Bedtime Shema

### 1. **The Act of Forgiveness**
Before beginning the prayers, we engage in a profound spiritual practice: forgiveness. We declare our willingness to forgive anyone who may have wronged us during the day. This act of *selichah* (forgiveness) cleanses our hearts, preventing us from carrying resentment into sleep and dreams.

*"I hereby forgive anyone who has angered or vexed me, or sinned against me, whether physically or financially, against my honor or anything else that is mine, whether by accident or intention, whether through speech or action..."*

### 2. **Birkat HaMapil - The Sleep Blessing**
The formal bedtime Shema begins with this beautiful blessing:

*"Praised are You, Lord our God, King of the universe, who brings sleep to my eyes, slumber to my eyelids..."*

This blessing acknowledges God as the source of rest and restoration, transforming the act of sleeping from a mere biological necessity into a spiritual practice.

### 3. **The Core Shema**
The central declaration follows:
*"Shema Yisrael, Adonai Eloheinu, Adonai Echad"*
*"Hear, O Israel: The Lord our God, the Lord is One."*

This foundational statement of Jewish faith becomes our final conscious thought, imprinting divine unity upon our minds as we enter sleep.

### 4. **The First Paragraph (V'ahavta)**
The full first paragraph of the Shema follows, emphasizing our love for God "with all your heart, with all your soul, and with all your might."

### 5. **Protection Prayers**
Additional prayers request divine protection through the night:
*"Help us, our Father, to lie down in peace; and awaken us to life again, our King. Spread over us Your shelter of peace..."*

### 6. **Biblical Verses of Protection**
The prayer includes Jacob's blessing to his grandsons Ephraim and Menashe: *"May the angel who has redeemed me from all harm bless these boys..."* (Genesis 48:16), along with the Priestly Blessing (Numbers 6:24-26).

### 7. **Adon Olam**
Many conclude with this ancient hymn, whose final verse encapsulates the bedtime Shema's essence:
*"When I sleep, as when I wake, God is with me; I have no fear."*

## The Spiritual Significance

### **A Time of Vulnerability**
Our sages understood that sleep represents a form of death - a daily surrender of consciousness. The Talmud states that "sleep is one-sixtieth of death" (Berakhot 57b). This vulnerability requires divine protection, which the bedtime Shema provides.

### **Spiritual Cleansing**
The act of forgiveness before sleep serves as a daily spiritual purification. By releasing grievances, we prevent negative emotions from contaminating our subconscious mind and dreams.

### **Unity of Day and Night**
The bedtime Shema creates continuity between our waking spiritual practice and our unconscious state, ensuring that even in sleep, we remain connected to the Divine.

## Practical Implementation

### **The Simple Version**
For those beginning this practice, simply reciting the first line of the Shema suffices, as the Talmud states this fulfills the basic obligation (Berakhot 13b).

### **The Extended Version**
More elaborate versions include additional psalms, protective verses, and meditative prayers, creating a comprehensive spiritual preparation for sleep.

### **Family Practice**
Many families adapt the bedtime Shema for children, using simplified versions that introduce young ones to this sacred practice.

## Contemporary Relevance

In our modern world of stress and digital overstimulation, the bedtime Shema offers a powerful practice for mental and spiritual hygiene. It provides:

- **Emotional closure** for the day's experiences
- **Stress relief** through the act of forgiveness
- **Spiritual grounding** in timeless truths
- **Peaceful preparation** for restorative sleep

The practice transforms bedtime from a mere end to the day into a sacred transition, a nightly return to divine shelter. As we release the day's burdens and embrace God's protection, we model trust in divine providence and prepare for the gift of renewal that each new day brings.

*"Into Your hand I entrust my spirit; You will redeem me, Lord God of truth."*
