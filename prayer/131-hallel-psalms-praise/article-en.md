# Hallel: The Psalms of Praise We Sing on Holidays

There's something electric that happens when a congregation rises together to sing Hallel. The ancient words of King David fill the sanctuary, voices joining in joyous celebration of God's miracles. But when exactly do we recite these psalms of praise, and what's the difference between "full" and "half" Hallel?

## When We Say Hallel

The recitation of Hallel (Psalms 113-118) marks our most joyous occasions in the Jewish calendar. We say **full Hallel** on festivals when the Temple brought additional sacrifices and work was forbidden:

- **Sukkot** (all seven days in Israel, eight in the diaspora)
- **Chanukah** (all eight nights)
- **Passover** (first day/two days, plus at the Seder)
- **Shavuot**
- **Rosh Chodesh** (New Moon)

We recite **half Hallel** (omitting Psalms 115:1-11 and 116:1-11) during the intermediate days of Passover and on Israeli Independence Day in many communities.

## Full vs. Half Hallel

The distinction isn't arbitrary. Full Hallel celebrates complete redemption and unrestrained joy. During the latter days of Passover, our tradition teaches sensitivity to Egyptian suffering—even our enemies are God's creatures. Half Hallel reflects this nuanced celebration.

## The Communal Experience

Hallel transforms individual prayer into collective celebration. The call-and-response tradition, where the leader sings verses and the congregation responds with "Hallelujah," creates an atmosphere of shared praise. Children often receive their first taste of Hebrew melody through Hallel's accessible refrains.

In ancient China, during the Tang Dynasty (618-907 CE), court musicians understood that communal music elevated the spiritual realm—much like our tradition recognizes Hallel's power to lift hearts beyond the individual to the divine.

## The Musical Tradition

Each community develops its own melodies for Hallel. Sephardic and Ashkenazi traditions offer different musical interpretations, yet the joy remains constant. The rhythm of the Hebrew text creates natural crescendos, especially in passages like "Min hameitzar karati Yah" (From the straits I called to God).

## Modern Practice

Today, Hallel continues to serve as a bridge between past and present. Whether in Jerusalem's ancient stones or a suburban synagogue, the same words that celebrated the Exodus still echo. Reform communities often include instrumental accompaniment, while Orthodox maintain traditional a cappella singing.

The obligation to recite Hallel reminds us that gratitude requires expression. Joy unexpressed diminishes; joy shared multiplies across generations.
