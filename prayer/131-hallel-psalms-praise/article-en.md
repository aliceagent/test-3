There's something electric that happens when a congregation rises together to sing Hallel. The ancient words of King David — composed nearly three thousand years ago during the period corresponding to China's Western Zhou Dynasty (西周, ~1046-771 BCE) — fill the sanctuary, voices joining in joyous celebration of God's miracles. Elderly voices blend with children's high notes, creating a tapestry of sound that has echoed through millennia. But when exactly do we recite these psalms of praise, and what makes Hallel so central to Jewish celebration?

## What is Hallel?

The word "Hallel" (הלל) comes from the Hebrew root meaning "to praise." It refers specifically to Psalms 113-118, a collection known as the "Egyptian Hallel" because Psalm 114 explicitly recalls the Exodus from Egypt. These six chapters form a unified liturgical unit that has been recited during Jewish festivals since the days of the Second Temple — approximately during China's Han Dynasty (汉朝, 206 BCE - 220 CE).

Unlike regular daily prayers that an individual can recite alone, Hallel is fundamentally communal. The Talmud teaches that Hallel requires a minyan (quorum of ten) to be recited with its full blessings, emphasizing that true praise amplifies when shared. This concept mirrors the Confucian principle of 礼 (lǐ) — ritual propriety that binds community through shared ceremonial practice.

The psalms themselves overflow with gratitude: "Not to us, O Lord, not to us, but to Your name give glory" (Psalm 115:1). They recount national redemption while simultaneously expressing personal salvation: "From distress I called to God; God answered me with expansiveness" (Psalm 118:5). This dual layer — national and personal — makes Hallel resonant across circumstances.

## When We Say Hallel

The recitation of Hallel marks our most joyous occasions in the Jewish calendar. Jewish law distinguishes between occasions that merit **full Hallel** (all six psalms) and those that warrant **half Hallel** (omitting two significant passages). Understanding this distinction reveals deep insights into Jewish values.

### Full Hallel Occasions

We recite complete Hallel with its full blessing on festivals when the ancient Temple brought additional sacrifices and when all work was biblically forbidden:

- **Sukkot** — All seven days in Israel (eight in the diaspora). Each day marks continued celebration of the harvest and divine protection during the forty-year wilderness journey.

- **Chanukah** — All eight nights. Remarkably, Chanukah is the only rabbinic (not biblical) holiday that merits full Hallel. The Talmud explains this was legislated to commemorate the miracle of the oil and the Hasmonean military victory. That a later, non-biblical holiday receives full Hallel status demonstrates Judaism's living tradition of recognizing God's ongoing presence in history.

- **Passover** — The first one or two days (depending on location), plus at the Seder itself. During the Seder, we split Hallel into two parts: Psalms 113-114 before the meal, and 115-118 after, creating a bracket of praise around our festival feast.

- **Shavuot** — Celebrating the giving of the Torah at Mount Sinai, approximately 3,300 years ago during what corresponds to China's late Shang Dynasty period.

- **Rosh Chodesh** — The New Moon, marking the beginning of each Hebrew month. While Rosh Chodesh isn't a full festival, we recite full Hallel (though some customs omit the introductory blessing) to acknowledge the moon's renewal as a metaphor for Israel's spiritual renewal.

### Half Hallel Occasions

We recite **half Hallel** — omitting Psalms 115:1-11 and 116:1-11 — during specific circumstances:

- **The intermediate days of Passover** (days 3-6 in Israel, 3-7 in the diaspora)
- **Israeli Independence Day** (Yom Ha'atzmaut) in many Israeli and diaspora communities, though this remains a matter of contemporary debate among Orthodox authorities

## The Meaning Behind Half Hallel

The distinction between full and half Hallel isn't arbitrary or a matter of convenience. It reflects profound Jewish values about empathy and the limits of celebration.

The Talmud (Arachin 10b) asks why we don't recite full Hallel throughout all of Passover, unlike Sukkot where every day merits complete Hallel. The answer provided is striking: during Passover's latter days, we recall that Egyptians drowned in the Sea of Reeds. The Midrash famously teaches that when the angels began to sing praise as the Egyptians drowned, God silenced them: "My creatures are drowning in the sea, and you sing praises?"

This sensitivity to enemy suffering — even enemies who enslaved and murdered our ancestors — distinguishes Jewish ethics. The principle echoes the teaching in Proverbs 24:17: "Do not rejoice when your enemy falls." Half Hallel embodies this nuanced moral stance: we celebrate our liberation, but we temper our joy out of respect for human life, even the lives of our oppressors.

This concept resonates with aspects of traditional Chinese thought. The Daoist principle of compassion (慈, cí) and the Buddhist teaching of compassion toward all sentient beings both recognize that true wisdom includes empathy even for those who wrong us.

## The Communal Experience

Hallel transforms individual prayer into collective celebration. The traditional call-and-response format, where a prayer leader sings verses and the congregation responds with "Hallelujah!" or repeats key phrases, creates an atmosphere of shared spiritual elevation.

In many communities, Hallel includes distinctive customs:

**Waving the lulav and etrog** during Sukkot Hallel — at specific verses praising God, worshippers shake the Four Species in six directions (four compass points, plus up and down), symbolizing God's presence throughout creation.

**Processional circuits** — On Sukkot, during Hallel's conclusion, the congregation circles the Torah scroll (or the reader's platform) while holding the lulav and etrog, reenacting the ancient Temple practice of circling the altar.

**Musical variation** — Different communities employ distinct melodies. Sephardic traditions often feature Middle Eastern modal systems (maqamat), while Ashkenazi melodies incorporate Eastern European folk influences. Yemenite Jews preserve what scholars believe may be among the oldest continuously transmitted Jewish musical traditions.

**Children's participation** — Many families encourage even small children to attend Hallel services, particularly on Chanukah and Sukkot, when festive joy fills the atmosphere. Children often receive their first taste of Hebrew melody through Hallel's accessible refrains like "Hodu l'Adonai ki tov" (Give thanks to God, for He is good).

During China's Tang Dynasty (唐朝, 618-907 CE) — roughly contemporary with the period when Hallel liturgy became fully standardized in Babylonian Jewish communities — Chinese court musicians understood that communal music elevated the spiritual realm beyond individual capacity. Similarly, our tradition recognizes Hallel's power to lift hearts from the personal to the transcendent through collective voice.

## The Structure and Themes

Each of the six psalms contributes unique dimensions to Hallel's message:

**Psalm 113** — Opens with pure praise, calling on God's servants to bless His name. It celebrates God who "raises the poor from the dust" — divine concern for the vulnerable and marginalized.

**Psalm 114** — The only historical narrative in Hallel, recounting the Exodus and the splitting of the Sea. "When Israel went out of Egypt... the sea saw and fled."

**Psalm 115** — Contrasts the living God with lifeless idols: "They have mouths but cannot speak, eyes but cannot see." This psalm establishes monotheism's fundamental critique of pagan worship.

**Psalm 116** — Intensely personal: "I love God, for He hears my voice, my supplications." Here individual salvation merges with national redemption.

**Psalm 117** — The shortest chapter in the entire Bible (just two verses!), calling on all nations to praise God. This universalist vision reminds us that Jewish redemption ultimately serves humanity's redemption.

**Psalm 118** — The grand finale, with its triumphant declaration: "This is the day God has made; let us rejoice and be glad in it." These words have become synonymous with celebration itself.

## Modern Practice and Meaning

Today, Hallel continues to serve as a bridge between past and present. Whether recited in Jerusalem's ancient stones, a suburban synagogue, a university Chabad house, or a home Chanukah celebration, the same words that celebrated the Exodus from Egypt nearly 3,300 years ago still echo with vitality.

Different Jewish denominations approach Hallel with varying practices:

- **Orthodox communities** maintain traditional a cappella singing and the full traditional text
- **Conservative synagogues** generally follow Orthodox practice but may include occasional instrumental accompaniment at festivals
- **Reform communities** often incorporate musical instruments, contemporary melodies, and sometimes gender-neutral language adaptations
- **Reconstructionist and Renewal movements** may add creative readings or meditations between psalms

Yet across all these variations, the essential experience remains: a community rising together in gratitude, linking arms across millennia with ancestors who sang these same words in the Temple courtyards, in medieval European ghettos, in North African mellahs, in Persian Jewish quarters, and in modern-day Jerusalem.

The obligation to recite Hallel reminds us that gratitude requires expression. Joy unexpressed diminishes; joy shared multiplies across generations. In reciting Hallel, we don't merely commemorate ancient miracles — we proclaim that the capacity for redemption, both national and personal, remains alive in every generation.

## Key Takeaways

- **Hallel (Psalms 113-118)** is recited on major Jewish festivals to express communal joy and gratitude
- **Full Hallel** is said on Sukkot, Chanukah, the first days of Passover, Shavuot, and Rosh Chodesh
- **Half Hallel** reflects moral sensitivity to enemy suffering during the latter days of Passover
- The **communal experience** of singing Hallel creates spiritual elevation through collective voice
- Hallel's themes blend **national redemption with personal salvation**, making it relevant across circumstances
