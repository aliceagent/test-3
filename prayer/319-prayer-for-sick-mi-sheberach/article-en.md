## Introduction

When someone we love falls ill, the human heart naturally turns toward prayer. In Judaism, praying for the sick is not just permitted—it is a profound mitzvah, a sacred responsibility that connects us to God's compassion and to our fellow human beings. Whether you're standing beside a hospital bed, sitting in a synagogue on Shabbat morning, or quietly reciting Psalms at home, Jewish tradition offers specific prayers and practices designed to channel our worry into hope, our fear into faith, and our helplessness into action.

The two central pillars of Jewish prayer for healing are the *Mi Sheberach* (מי שברך, "May the One Who Blessed") prayer recited in synagogue, and the recitation of *Tehillim* (תהלים, Psalms) either individually or in groups. These practices date back thousands of years—to the time of King David, who composed many of the Psalms during what corresponds to China's Western Zhou Dynasty (西周, ~1000 BCE)—and they remain vibrant and powerful today, offering structure, comfort, and spiritual support during some of life's most difficult moments.

## The Mi Sheberach Prayer: A Public Request for Divine Mercy

### What Is Mi Sheberach?

The *Mi Sheberach* prayer for the sick is a formal blessing recited during the Torah reading in synagogue. The prayer invokes God's healing mercy, asking for a *refuah shleimah* (רפואה שלמה)—a "complete healing"—for someone who is ill. The person's Hebrew name and their mother's Hebrew name are included in the prayer, following the traditional Jewish practice of identifying individuals through the maternal line when seeking divine compassion.

The prayer has ancient roots but was formalized in the medieval period—during what corresponds to China's Song Dynasty (宋朝, 960-1279 CE)—when Jewish communities in Europe and the Middle East developed standardized liturgies for communal life.

### When and How Is It Recited?

The *Mi Sheberach* is typically recited on **Shabbat** during the Torah reading, though it can also be said on **Monday and Thursday mornings** (when the Torah is read) or on **Rosh Chodesh** (the new month). In most synagogues, there is a designated moment after each Torah aliyah (when someone is called up to the Torah) for the gabbai (synagogue warden) or the person who received the aliyah to request a *Mi Sheberach* for someone who is ill.

**The traditional text** (in English translation):

> "May the One who blessed our ancestors—Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob; Sarah, Rebecca, Rachel, and Leah—bless and heal [Hebrew name] son/daughter of [mother's Hebrew name], along with all who are ill among Your people Israel. May the Holy One, blessed be He, be filled with compassion for them, to restore their health, to heal them, to strengthen them, and to revive them. And may He send them speedily a complete recovery—a healing of the soul and a healing of the body—together with all the ill, and let us say: Amen."

### How to Request a Mi Sheberach

If you want to request a *Mi Sheberach* for someone who is ill, you need the following information:

1. **The person's Hebrew name** (e.g., Yaakov, Rivkah)
2. **The person's mother's Hebrew name** (e.g., Yaakov ben Sarah—"Jacob son of Sarah"; Rivkah bat Leah—"Rebecca daughter of Leah")

If you don't know the Hebrew names, ask a rabbi or knowledgeable community member for help. Many synagogues also have a *Mi Sheberach* list where you can write down names before services.

**Important note:** It is customary to request a *Mi Sheberach* with the sick person's knowledge and permission. In Jewish thought, privacy and dignity are sacred, and we don't publicize someone's illness without their consent.

### The Cultural Parallel: Public Prayer in Chinese Tradition

In traditional Chinese culture, there is a similar practice of communal prayer for the sick, particularly in Buddhist and Daoist temples, where monks or priests perform *gongxiu* (共修, collective practice) or *qifu* (祈福, prayer for blessings) ceremonies on behalf of individuals. Family members bring the sick person's name and birthdate, and the monks chant sutras or perform rituals to invoke healing and protection.

While the theological framework differs—Judaism emphasizes a personal, covenantal relationship with the One God, whereas Chinese religious practices often involve multiple deities or cosmic forces—the underlying human instinct is the same: when someone we love is suffering, we seek divine intervention, and we do so within a community that shares our values and hopes.

## Reciting Tehillim (Psalms) for Healing

### The Power of King David's Words

The Book of Psalms (*Sefer Tehillim*) is a collection of 150 poetic prayers composed primarily by King David during the early Jewish monarchy—approximately 3,000 years ago, corresponding to China's Western Zhou Dynasty. These ancient poems have been recited by Jews in every generation since, offering words of praise, lament, gratitude, and desperate plea.

Jewish tradition teaches that reciting Psalms carries unique spiritual power, especially for healing. The Talmud (compiled during what corresponds to China's Three Kingdoms and Jin Dynasty periods, 200-500 CE) describes how King David's harp would play by itself at midnight, waking him to compose and recite Psalms. The words were considered divinely inspired, capable of moving heaven and earth.

### Which Psalms to Recite

There is no single "correct" Psalm for healing, but Jewish tradition has identified several that are particularly associated with refuah (healing):

1. **Psalm 6** — A plea for mercy during illness  
   *"YHWH, do not rebuke me in Your anger... Heal me, YHWH, for my bones are shaking..."*

2. **Psalm 20** — A prayer for salvation in times of trouble  
   *"May YHWH answer you in the day of distress..."*

3. **Psalm 22** — A cry from the depths of suffering  
   *"My God, my God, why have You forsaken me?..."*

4. **Psalm 23** — The famous shepherd's psalm, offering comfort  
   *"YHWH is my shepherd; I shall not want..."*

5. **Psalm 30** — A song of thanksgiving for recovery  
   *"I will extol You, YHWH, for You have lifted me up..."*

6. **Psalm 88** — A raw expression of pain and despair  
   *"YHWH, God of my salvation, by day I cry out; at night before You..."*

7. **Psalm 102** — A prayer of the afflicted  
   *"Hear my prayer, YHWH; let my cry come to You..."*

8. **Psalm 121** — A prayer for divine protection  
   *"I lift my eyes to the mountains—from where will my help come?..."*

9. **Psalm 130** — A plea from the depths  
   *"Out of the depths I call to You, YHWH..."*

10. **Psalm 142** — A prayer in distress  
    *"With my voice I cry to YHWH; with my voice I plead to YHWH for mercy..."*

### How to Recite Tehillim for a Sick Person

**The traditional practice:**

1. **Say the person's Hebrew name and mother's Hebrew name** before you begin  
   Example: *"I am reciting these Psalms for the sake of Yaakov ben Sarah, that he may have a complete recovery."*

2. **Recite the selected Psalms** (you can recite one, several, or all 150)  
   Some people recite the entire Book of Psalms (*Sefer Tehillim Shalem*) over the course of a month, dividing it into daily portions.

3. **Conclude with a personal prayer**  
   After finishing the Psalms, many people add a short, heartfelt prayer in their own words, asking for healing, strength, and comfort.

**Group recitation:**  
It is considered especially powerful to gather a group to recite Psalms together. In many communities, women organize *Tehillim groups* that meet regularly to pray for the sick. The Talmud teaches that communal prayer is particularly effective because it represents the collective merit and concern of the Jewish people.

### The Acrostic Psalm: Psalm 119

Some people recite **Psalm 119**, the longest chapter in the Bible, using an acrostic method: they recite only the verses that begin with the letters of the sick person's Hebrew name. For example, if the person's name is *David* (דוד), you would recite the verses that begin with the letters *dalet* (ד), *vav* (ו), and *dalet* (ד) again.

This practice combines the power of the Psalm with the mystical significance of the person's name, creating a personalized prayer tailored to their unique spiritual identity.

## Judaism's View: Prayer and Medicine Together

### Not Either/Or, But Both

One of the most important principles in Jewish thought is that prayer and medicine are not opposed to each other—they are complementary. The Torah commands us, *"V'rapo yerapei"* (וְרַפֹּא יְרַפֵּא, "and he shall surely heal," Exodus 21:19), which the rabbis interpret as permission—even obligation—for doctors to heal and for patients to seek medical treatment.

The 12th-century rabbi and physician **Maimonides (Rambam)**—who lived during what corresponds to China's Song Dynasty—was himself a doctor who served the royal court in Egypt. He wrote extensively on the importance of both medicine and prayer, teaching that we must do everything humanly possible to heal (through doctors, medicine, rest, and proper care) while simultaneously praying to God, recognizing that ultimate healing comes from the Divine.

**The balance:**

- **Medicine** addresses the physical body (*refuah haguf*, רפואה הגוף)
- **Prayer** addresses the soul (*refuah hanefesh*, רפואה הנפש)
- **Complete healing** (*refuah shleimah*) requires both

This approach mirrors traditional Chinese medicine's holistic view, which sees health as the integration of body (*ti*, 体), mind (*xin*, 心), and spirit (*shen*, 神). In both traditions, healing is not merely the absence of disease but the restoration of wholeness.

## Practical Application: What to Do When Someone Is Sick

### 1. Visit the Sick (Bikur Cholim)

Judaism considers visiting the sick (*bikur cholim*, ביקור חולים) to be one of the greatest mitzvot. The Talmud teaches that when you visit a sick person, you remove one-sixtieth of their suffering. Your presence offers comfort, reduces loneliness, and shows that the person is not forgotten.

**Guidelines for visiting:**

- **Go at a convenient time** (not too early in the morning or late at night)
- **Be sensitive to their condition** (if they're very weak, keep the visit short)
- **Pray in their presence** if they are comfortable with it
- **Avoid saying things like** "This is punishment from God" or "Everything happens for a reason" (these can be hurtful)
- **Offer practical help** (bring food, offer to run errands, sit with them so family can rest)

### 2. Recite Psalms Regularly

Set aside time each day to recite Psalms for the sick person. Even just **Psalm 23** ("The Lord is my shepherd") takes only a minute or two and can bring great spiritual benefit.

### 3. Request a Mi Sheberach at Synagogue

Go to synagogue on Shabbat (or Monday/Thursday morning) and request a *Mi Sheberach*. If you can't attend in person, contact the synagogue office and ask them to include the person's name on the prayer list.

### 4. Give Tzedakah (Charity)

Jewish tradition teaches that giving charity in the merit of a sick person can tip the scales toward healing. The idea is not that we "buy" God's favor, but that performing a mitzvah generates spiritual merit (*zechus*, זכות) that can benefit someone who is suffering.

### 5. Add or Change a Name

In some cases, when someone is critically ill, a rabbi may suggest adding a new name to the person's existing Hebrew name. Common additions include:

- **Chaim** (חיים, "life") for men
- **Chaya** (חיה, "life") for women
- **Rafael** (רפאל, "God heals")
- **Asher** (אשר, "happy/blessed")

The new name is then used in all prayers (*Mi Sheberach*, *Tehillim*, etc.). This practice is based on the Talmudic teaching that changing a name can change a person's spiritual destiny.

## Historical Context: Prayer for Healing Across Jewish History

### Biblical Precedents

The Torah records several instances of prayer for healing:

- **Moses prays for his sister Miriam** when she contracts tzara'at (a spiritual skin affliction):  
  *"God, please heal her!"* (Numbers 12:13)—the shortest prayer in the Torah, only five Hebrew words, yet it was answered.

- **King Hezekiah prays when he falls deathly ill** (during what corresponds to China's Spring and Autumn Period, 春秋, ~700 BCE), and God grants him fifteen additional years of life (Isaiah 38).

These stories teach that prayer can change reality, and that even the briefest, most desperate cry from the heart has the power to move heaven.

### Talmudic and Medieval Practices

During the Talmudic period (200-500 CE, corresponding to China's Three Kingdoms and Jin Dynasty eras), the rabbis formalized prayers for the sick. They debated questions like:

- **Can you pray for healing on Shabbat?**  
  The answer: Yes, because saving a life (*pikuach nefesh*) overrides almost all other commandments. However, you should phrase it differently on Shabbat, saying, *"Shabbat hi miliz'ok, urfuah krova lavo"* ("It is Shabbat, when we refrain from crying out, but healing is soon to come").

- **Should you mention the sick person by name in private prayer?**  
  Yes, especially in personal supplications, because God knows each person intimately.

By the medieval period (corresponding to China's Song and Ming Dynasties, 960-1644 CE), the *Mi Sheberach* prayer had become standardized across Jewish communities from Spain to Poland to Iraq.

## Key Takeaways

- **The Mi Sheberach prayer** is recited publicly in synagogue during Torah reading, invoking God's healing mercy by mentioning the sick person's Hebrew name and their mother's name.
- **Reciting Tehillim (Psalms)** is a powerful individual or communal practice for praying for healing, with specific Psalms traditionally associated with illness and recovery.
- **Judaism teaches that prayer and medicine work together**—we must seek medical care while also turning to God in prayer, recognizing that complete healing (*refuah shleimah*) encompasses both body and soul.
- **Visiting the sick, giving charity, and requesting a Mi Sheberach** are concrete actions that support healing and demonstrate communal care.
- **Prayer is effective even when brief**—Moses' five-word prayer for Miriam shows that sincerity matters more than eloquence.
