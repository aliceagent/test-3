## Introduction

Three times each day, during the weekday Amidah prayer, something extraordinary happens in Jewish synagogues around the world. The congregation rises on their toes, swaying gently in unison, and proclaims in Hebrew: "*Kadosh, Kadosh, Kadosh*" — "Holy, Holy, Holy is the Lord of Hosts, the whole earth is filled with His glory." This brief moment, known as the *Kedushah* (קדושה), represents one of the most mystical and powerful sections of Jewish prayer. During these few minutes, Jewish tradition teaches that we literally imitate the angels who constantly stand before God's throne in heaven, offering eternal praise.

For those unfamiliar with Judaism, this practice might seem unusual or even theatrical. Why do we rise on our toes? Why repeat the word "holy" three times? What is the significance of this synchronized movement? The *Kedushah* is far more than a ritual—it's a spiritual technology, a moment when the physical synagogue becomes a gateway to the celestial realm, when ordinary people momentarily step into the role of heavenly beings. Understanding the *Kedushah* opens a window into Judaism's mystical dimension, revealing how Jewish prayer seeks not just to petition God, but to align ourselves with the cosmic order of creation itself.

## The Biblical Source: Isaiah's Vision

The *Kedushah* is rooted in one of the most dramatic visions recorded in the Hebrew Bible. The prophet Isaiah, living during what corresponds to China's Spring and Autumn Period (春秋时代, approximately 770-476 BCE), received a vision that would shape Jewish prayer for millennia:

> "In the year that King Uzziah died, I saw the Lord sitting upon a throne, high and lifted up... Above Him stood the seraphim... And one called to another and said: 'Holy, holy, holy is the Lord of Hosts; the whole earth is filled with His glory.' And the foundations of the thresholds shook at the voice of those who called, and the house was filled with smoke." (Isaiah 6:1-4)

This vision is remarkable for several reasons. First, Isaiah witnesses the seraphim—a category of angels—standing in the divine presence. The Hebrew word "seraphim" (שְׂרָפִים) literally means "burning ones," suggesting beings of pure spiritual fire. These angels aren't sitting or resting; they're standing in perpetual readiness, constantly engaged in the worship of God.

Second, the angels don't merely say "holy" once. They proclaim it three times: *Kadosh, Kadosh, Kadosh*. Jewish commentators have offered various explanations for this triple repetition. Rashi, the great 11th-century French commentator (living during China's Song Dynasty, 宋朝), suggests that the threefold "holy" represents God's holiness in three realms: the highest heaven, the earth below, and the heavens that are above the earth. The Talmud offers another interpretation: the first "holy" refers to God as He is above, beyond human comprehension; the second to God's presence in the highest heavens; and the third to God's immanence on earth—"the whole earth is filled with His glory."

Third, the angels call out "to one another." This isn't a solo performance but a responsive chorus. In Jewish mysticism, this teaches that even the most exalted spiritual beings achieve their purpose through community and mutual encouragement. No angel proclaims God's holiness in isolation; they inspire and respond to each other.

## A Second Vision: Ezekiel and the Living Creatures

The *Kedushah* also draws from another prophetic vision, that of Ezekiel (יְחֶזְקֵאל), who lived during the Babylonian exile—corresponding to China's late Spring and Autumn period (春秋时代晚期, around 586 BCE). Ezekiel's vision was even more elaborate:

> "And the living creatures ran and returned like the appearance of a flash of lightning. And I heard the sound of their wings like the sound of many waters, like the voice of the Almighty... and they said: 'Blessed is the glory of the Lord from His place.'" (Ezekiel 1:14, 3:12-13)

These "living creatures" (*chayot hakodesh*, חַיּוֹת הַקֹּדֶשׁ) represent another category of angels. Unlike Isaiah's seraphim who stand still, Ezekiel's creatures are in constant motion—"running and returning." This dynamic energy becomes part of the *Kedushah* recitation, where we declare: "Blessed is the glory of the Lord from His place" (*Baruch kevod Hashem mimkomo*, בָּרוּךְ כְּבוֹד ה' מִמְּקוֹמוֹ).

The phrase "from His place" is particularly intriguing. Where exactly is God's "place"? Jewish mysticism teaches that God is both transcendent (utterly beyond the physical universe) and immanent (present everywhere). The angels proclaim God's glory "*from* His place" because God's essence cannot be localized—He fills all places yet is bound by none. When we recite this line, we acknowledge the paradox at the heart of Jewish theology: God is simultaneously everywhere and nowhere, intimately close yet infinitely distant.

## The Structure and Meaning of the Kedushah

The *Kedushah* prayer varies slightly between different prayer services, but its core structure remains consistent:

**1. Introduction by the Prayer Leader:**
"We will sanctify Your name in the world, just as they sanctify it in the highest heavens, as it is written by Your prophet: 'And one called to another and said...'"

This introduction establishes the prayer's purpose: to mirror on earth what the angels do in heaven. Jewish prayer doesn't aspire merely to human excellence; it aims for angelic perfection. When we recite *Kedushah*, we're not just praying *about* holiness—we're actively participating in the cosmic chorus of holiness that reverberates through creation.

**2. The Congregation Responds:**
"*Kadosh, Kadosh, Kadosh Hashem Tzeva'ot, melo chol ha'aretz kevodo*"
"Holy, Holy, Holy is the Lord of Hosts, the whole earth is filled with His glory."

At this moment, the entire congregation rises on their toes. Why? The Talmud (Berachot 51b) explains that the angels have no knees—they stand perpetually upright. By rising on our toes, we imitate their unwavering, upright posture. Some commentators add that lifting ourselves physically represents our spiritual aspiration to ascend, to rise above our earthly concerns and connect with the divine.

There's also a tradition to sway slightly during *Kedushah*, moving forward and back. This mimics the movement Ezekiel described—"running and returning." We "run" toward God in spiritual longing, then "return" in humble recognition that we cannot fully grasp the divine. This oscillation captures the rhythm of Jewish spirituality: intense closeness followed by reverent distance, yearning followed by awe.

**3. The Second Response:**
"*Baruch kevod Hashem mimkomo*"
"Blessed is the glory of the Lord from His place."

Again, we rise, acknowledging God's transcendence. Even as we declare that "the whole earth is filled with His glory," we also affirm that God's essential "place" is beyond all physical location.

**4. The Final Declaration:**
"*Yimloch Hashem le'olam, Elohayich Tzion ledor vador, Halleluyah*"
"The Lord will reign forever; your God, O Zion, from generation to generation, Halleluyah."

This verse (Psalm 146:10) transitions from the present moment to eternal time. God's kingship isn't temporary or conditional—it spans all generations, past and future. By ending with "*Halleluyah*" (praise God), we return from the celestial realm to our earthly worship, carrying the angelic inspiration back into our daily prayers.

## The Mystical Dimensions: Imitating the Angels

Jewish mysticism, particularly Kabbalah, elaborates extensively on the *Kedushah*'s deeper meanings. The Zohar, the foundational text of Jewish mysticism (written during what corresponds to China's late Song Dynasty, 宋末, around 1290 CE), teaches that when we recite *Kedushah*, we don't merely imitate angels—we actually join them.

According to Kabbalistic teaching, there are multiple levels of reality, often described as "worlds" (*olamot*, עולמות). The physical world we inhabit is called *Asiyah* (עשיה), the World of Action. Above it is *Yetzirah* (יצירה), the World of Formation, where angels dwell. When we recite *Kedushah* with proper intention (*kavanah*, כַּוָּנָה), we temporarily elevate ourselves from *Asiyah* to *Yetzirah*, entering the angelic realm.

This elevation isn't metaphorical—it's understood as a genuine spiritual ascent. The Talmud (Chullin 91b) teaches that angels have specific times when they sing praise: some during the day, others at night, and still others at dawn and dusk. The *Kedushah* we recite during the morning service (*Shacharit*) coincides with the angels' morning praise. We're not singing a solo; we're joining an eternal choir that never stops.

The medieval work *Sefer Chassidim* (ספר חסידים), composed during China's Jin Dynasty (金朝, 1115-1234 CE), advises that during *Kedushah*, one should focus intensely on the words, picturing oneself standing among the angels. Some traditions recommend closing one's eyes to shut out physical distractions, creating an internal vision of the heavenly throne room. Other traditions keep eyes open but focused downward, maintaining modesty even as one aspires upward.

## The Physical Actions: Rising, Swaying, and Feet Together

Jewish law (*halacha*) prescribes specific physical actions during *Kedushah*, each carrying symbolic weight:

**Rising on Toes:**
As mentioned, this imitates the angels who "stand" without knees. The *Shulchan Aruch* (שולחן ערוך), the authoritative code of Jewish law compiled in the 16th century (corresponding to China's Ming Dynasty, 明朝), states that one should rise on the toes three times: once for each "*Kadosh*" (Orach Chaim 125:2).

**Feet Together:**
During the *Kedushah*, one should stand with feet together, not apart. This is based on Ezekiel's description of the angels: "And their feet were a straight foot" (Ezekiel 1:7), which the rabbis interpreted to mean the angels appear to have a single foot, standing perfectly upright. By bringing our feet together, we again emulate their posture.

**Looking Upward:**
Some communities have the custom to look upward at key moments during *Kedushah*, as if gazing toward heaven. This is based on the verse "I lift my eyes to the mountains" (Psalms 121:1). However, other authorities discourage this, preferring that one maintain a humble, downward gaze. The variation reflects different approaches within Jewish tradition: should prayer be characterized more by aspiration (looking up) or humility (looking down)? Many communities embrace both, looking up during "*Kadosh*" but down during "*Baruch*."

**Not Interrupting:**
Jewish law strictly prohibits any interruption during *Kedushah*. One shouldn't speak, move excessively, or even respond "Amen" to other blessings (except in specific circumstances). This total focus mirrors the angels' complete absorption in their praise. When angels say "*Kadosh*," they aren't distracted by other concerns—they're utterly consumed by the holiness they proclaim. We aspire to that same single-minded devotion.

## The Community Dimension: Why Kedushah Requires a Minyan

One of the most important halachic requirements of *Kedushah* is that it can only be recited in the presence of a *minyan*—a quorum of ten adult Jews. If you're praying alone or with fewer than ten people, you skip the *Kedushah* entirely. Why this restriction?

The Talmud (Megillah 23b) derives this from the verse: "I will be sanctified among the children of Israel" (Leviticus 22:32). The word "among" (*betoch*, בְּתוֹךְ) implies that God's sanctification requires a community, not just individuals. Certain aspects of holiness can only manifest in a collective setting.

This teaching resonates deeply with Jewish philosophy. Unlike some spiritual traditions that emphasize individual enlightenment or solitary meditation, Judaism insists that the highest forms of holiness emerge from community. The *Kedushah*—the very moment when we touch the angelic realm—requires the presence of others. We don't ascend to heaven alone; we rise together.

This communal requirement has practical implications. In small towns or isolated communities, gathering a *minyan* can be challenging. Yet this difficulty serves a purpose: it forces Jews to seek each other out, to build and maintain community even when it's inconvenient. The *Kedushah* becomes not just a prayer but a catalyst for connection.

There's a beautiful metaphor here: just as the angels call out "to one another," responding and inspiring one another in their praise, so too must we gather with fellow Jews to proclaim God's holiness. Isolation might work for certain spiritual practices, but holiness, Judaism teaches, is inherently relational and communal.

## Cultural Parallels: Chinese and Jewish Concepts of Holiness

For Chinese readers exploring Judaism, the concept of "holiness" might initially seem foreign. Chinese philosophical traditions—Confucianism, Daoism, Buddhism—don't typically use a concept directly equivalent to the Hebrew *kedushah* (קדושה). However, there are intriguing parallels that can bridge understanding.

**The Confucian Concept of *Li* (礼):**
In Confucian thought, *li* refers to ritual propriety, correct behavior, and the cosmic order. When performed correctly, rituals align the individual with the larger harmony of society and cosmos. Similarly, *Kedushah* is about alignment—aligning human speech and posture with the eternal worship of angels, aligning earthly reality with heavenly truth. Both systems teach that physical actions (standing, swaying, speaking precise words) can have cosmic significance.

**Daoist Reverence for the Dao:**
Daoism speaks of the Dao (道) as the ultimate reality, ineffable and beyond human comprehension yet present in all things. The *Kedushah*'s proclamation—"Holy, Holy, Holy... the whole earth is filled with His glory"—expresses a similar paradox: God is utterly transcendent ("holy") yet entirely immanent ("fills the earth"). Both traditions grapple with the mystery of divine presence that is simultaneously everywhere and nowhere fully graspable.

**Buddhist Chanting and Meditation:**
In Buddhist practice, particularly in Mahayana traditions popular in China, group chanting serves to focus the mind, create merit, and establish connection with higher realms (such as the Pure Land). The *Kedushah*'s communal recitation, its rhythmic repetition, and its aim to transcend ordinary consciousness share functional similarities with Buddhist liturgy, though the theological frameworks differ.

**The Heavenly Hierarchy:**
Chinese folk religion and Buddhism feature elaborate celestial hierarchies—jade emperors, bodhisattvas, various orders of deities and spirits. Jewish angelology also describes a hierarchy: seraphim, cherubim, *chayot hakodesh*, and other angelic classes. The *Kedushah* acknowledges this hierarchy while asserting a radical idea: humans, by reciting these words, participate in the worship of the highest beings.

## Theological Implications: Humans and Angels

The *Kedushah* raises fascinating theological questions about the relationship between humans and angels in Jewish thought. On one hand, angels are portrayed as superior to humans—they're purely spiritual, sinless, constantly engaged in divine worship. The Talmud (Chullin 91b) teaches that angels have no *yetzer hara* (יצר הרע), no evil inclination. They simply obey God without internal conflict.

Yet Judaism also teaches that in certain ways, humans surpass angels. Angels are static—they don't grow, develop, or change. Humans, with our freedom to choose between good and evil, have the capacity for spiritual growth that angels lack. The Midrash (Bereishit Rabbah 8:5) even suggests that when God created humans, the angels objected, saying, "What is man that You are mindful of him?" (Psalms 8:5). God's response, according to some interpretations, is that humans can achieve a form of service that angels cannot: the service of struggle, growth, and freely chosen devotion.

The *Kedushah* embodies this paradox. We imitate the angels' praise, acknowledging their spiritual excellence. But we do so as humans—creatures of flesh and blood, burdened by physical needs and moral temptations—which makes our praise even more precious. An angel has no choice but to say "*Kadosh*." A human can choose to remain silent or distracted. When we choose to rise on our toes and proclaim God's holiness, we exercise a freedom that angels don't possess.

This is why the Talmud (Shabbat 88b) teaches that God prefers the study of Torah on earth to the songs of angels in heaven. Torah study requires effort, confusion, debate, and growth—all human qualities. Angels simply know; humans must learn, struggle, and choose. The *Kedushah*, then, isn't about becoming angels. It's about humans bringing angelic worship into the earthly realm, sanctifying the physical world through spiritual aspiration.

## Practical Application: Bringing Kedushah into Daily Life

While the formal *Kedushah* prayer occurs during communal services, its principles can inform our daily spiritual practice:

**1. Moments of Elevation:**
Just as we rise on our toes during *Kedushah*, we can create daily moments of spiritual "rising"—pausing to acknowledge something beyond our immediate concerns. This might be a moment of gratitude, wonder at nature, or conscious connection to the divine. The *Kedushah* teaches that even brief moments of elevation are valuable.

**2. Community and Isolation:**
The requirement of a *minyan* reminds us that spiritual life isn't meant to be solitary. Make efforts to connect with community, whether through prayer services, study groups, or acts of service. Holiness, Judaism teaches, emerges most fully in relationship.

**3. Physical and Spiritual Integration:**
The *Kedushah*'s specific physical requirements—standing, rising, swaying—demonstrate that Judaism doesn't separate body from spirit. Our physical actions can express and even generate spiritual states. Consider how your posture, movement, and physical habits affect your inner life.

**4. Imitation and Aspiration:**
The *Kedushah* is fundamentally about aspiration—reaching toward a higher standard. In your own life, identify models worth emulating. Just as we imitate angels, we can learn from teachers, mentors, and exemplars who embody the qualities we wish to develop.

**5. The Power of Repetition:**
The threefold "*Kadosh*" teaches that repetition can deepen meaning rather than diminish it. In prayer, meditation, or affirmations, don't shy away from repetition. Each recitation can carry new depth and intensity.

## Variations Across Jewish Communities

While the core of *Kedushah* is universal, different Jewish communities have developed their own customs:

**Ashkenazi Communities:**
Ashkenazi Jews (those with Eastern European heritage) typically rise on their toes three times—once for each "*Kadosh*." They may also rise during "*Baruch kevod*."

**Sephardi Communities:**
Sephardi Jews (from Middle Eastern and Mediterranean regions) have slightly different melodies and may have different customs regarding when to rise. Some Sephardi communities rise six times during *Kedushah*.

**Chassidic Practices:**
Chassidic communities often emphasize intense *kavanah* (intention) during *Kedushah*. Some Chassidic rebbes would close their eyes, sway dramatically, and appear completely absorbed in a state of spiritual ecstasy (*hitlahavut*, התלהבות).

**Modern Adaptations:**
In some contemporary synagogues, particularly those serving elderly or disabled congregants, accommodations are made for those unable to stand or rise on their toes. The principle is clear: the spiritual intention matters more than the precise physical execution.

## Conclusion: Between Heaven and Earth

The *Kedushah* represents one of Judaism's most profound liturgical innovations: a moment when the boundary between heaven and earth becomes permeable, when ordinary people in a physical building momentarily join the eternal chorus of angels. It teaches that holiness isn't confined to heaven—it can be accessed, proclaimed, and embodied right here on earth.

For beginners exploring Jewish prayer, the *Kedushah* offers a concentrated experience of prayer's essential purpose: to align ourselves with something greater than ourselves, to rise above the mundane, and to participate in the ongoing sanctification of the world. It's theatrical, mystical, communal, and deeply human all at once.

The next time you attend a Jewish prayer service and see the congregation rise on their toes, swaying and singing "*Kadosh, Kadosh, Kadosh*," you're witnessing an ancient practice that connects this moment to prophetic visions from thousands of years ago, that links this small gathering to the infinite worship of angels, and that affirms a central Jewish belief: that humans, fragile and flawed as we are, can participate in the divine work of making the world holy.

The angels never stop singing. When we recite *Kedushah*, we add our voices to theirs—not because we must, but because we choose to. And in that choice lies the uniquely human contribution to the cosmic symphony of praise.

## Key Takeaways

- The *Kedushah* is a prayer where we imitate the angels' eternal praise of God, reciting "*Kadosh, Kadosh, Kadosh*" (Holy, Holy, Holy)
- It's based on prophetic visions by Isaiah and Ezekiel, who saw angels constantly worshipping God in heaven
- Physical actions—rising on toes, swaying, standing with feet together—embody spiritual aspiration and angelic imitation
- *Kedushah* requires a *minyan* (ten adults), emphasizing that the highest holiness manifests in community, not isolation
- While angels are spiritually superior in purity, humans possess free choice, making our praise uniquely meaningful
- The practice teaches that we can access transcendent experiences through communal prayer, physical action, and conscious intention
- It bridges the earthly and heavenly realms, affirming that holiness can be proclaimed and embodied in the physical world
