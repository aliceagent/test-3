## Introduction

Six Hebrew words. Two recitations a day. One eternal declaration of faith.

The Shema is not just a prayer — it is the heartbeat of Jewish identity. For nearly four thousand years — since the time corresponding to China's Shang Dynasty (商朝, ~1600-1046 BCE) — these ancient words have been whispered by Jewish mothers tucking their children into bed, chanted by scholars in study halls, and proclaimed by martyrs facing death. "Shema Yisrael, Adonai Eloheinu, Adonai Echad" — "Hear, O Israel: the Lord our God, the Lord is One."

If you could learn only one Jewish prayer, this would be it. The Shema encapsulates the core of Judaism: the belief in one God, the commitment to live according to His commandments, and the connection between the Jewish people and their heritage. Whether you are exploring Judaism for the first time or deepening your practice, understanding the Shema opens a door to the very essence of Jewish faith.

## What is the Shema?

The Shema is technically not a prayer in the traditional sense — it is a declaration of faith, a pledge of allegiance to One God. The word "Shema" (שְׁמַע) means "hear" or "listen" in Hebrew, calling upon every Jew to pay attention to this fundamental truth.

The complete Shema consists of three paragraphs taken directly from the Torah:

1. **Deuteronomy 6:4-9** — The first paragraph declares God's oneness and commands us to love God with all our heart, soul, and might. It instructs us to teach these words to our children and to remember them constantly — when we sit at home, when we walk on the road, when we lie down, and when we rise up.

2. **Deuteronomy 11:13-21** — The second paragraph emphasizes the consequences of our choices: if we follow God's commandments, we will be blessed with rain and sustenance in the Land of Israel; if we turn away, we will face hardship. It repeats the instruction to teach these words to our children and to place them on our doorposts and gates.

3. **Numbers 15:37-41** — The third paragraph introduces the commandment of tzitzit (the fringes worn on the corners of a garment) and recalls the Exodus from Egypt, reminding us that God redeemed us from slavery to serve Him.

Together, these three paragraphs express core Jewish beliefs: recognizing that God is One, living a life guided by mitzvot (commandments), and understanding that Jewish life is intrinsically tied to the Land of Israel and our history.

## The Opening Line: A Declaration That Changed the World

"Shema Yisrael, Adonai Eloheinu, Adonai Echad."  
"Hear, O Israel: the Lord our God, the Lord is One."

This single sentence is the most famous line in all of Jewish liturgy. When our patriarch Abraham — living during what corresponds to China's Shang Dynasty — first proclaimed monotheism to a polytheistic world, he sparked a spiritual revolution. The Shema continues that revolution today.

Monotheism maintains that God is infinite, existing beyond time and space as an eternal, absolute Being Who is the continuous source of all creation. There is no power outside of Him; He is the transcendent source of morality, omniscient and good. Since an infinite Being is perfect and has no needs, His creation is ultimately for our benefit, and the Torah and its commandments serve as the blueprint to forge a relationship with Him.

In traditional Jewish practice, when reciting the first line of the Shema, we cover our eyes with our right hand. This custom helps us block out the distractions of the outside world and focus on these words with our entire being, feeling our connection with God that they convey.

### The Hidden Letters

If you look at the Shema in a Hebrew prayer book or Torah scroll, you'll notice that the final letters of the first and last words are enlarged. The first word "Shema" (שְׁמַע) ends with the letter ayin (ע), and the last word "Echad" (אֶחָד) ends with the letter dalet (ד). Together, these two letters spell the Hebrew word "Ayd" (עד), meaning "witness."

By reciting the Shema, we become witnesses — testifying to the existence of the Divine presence in the world. This is not mere recitation; it is an active declaration of what we know to be true.

## When Do We Say the Shema?

The Torah itself tells us when to recite the Shema. In the first paragraph (Deuteronomy 6:7), it says: "You shall speak of them... when you lie down and when you arise."

Our sages understood this to mean that we have an obligation to say the Shema twice daily:

- **Morning (Shacharit):** From the time you can distinguish between blue and white threads (approximately dawn) until the end of the third hour of the day (approximately 9-10 AM, depending on the season)
- **Evening (Ma'ariv):** From nightfall (when three stars are visible) until midnight

Many Jews also recite the first paragraph of the Shema just before going to sleep each night. This bedtime Shema (Kriat Shema al HaMitah) is a way to end the day with a declaration of faith and to place oneself under God's protection through the night.

## The Meaning of Each Paragraph

### First Paragraph: Love and Commitment (Deuteronomy 6:4-9)

This paragraph begins with the declaration of God's oneness and continues with the commandment to love God "with all your heart, with all your soul, and with all your might." What does it mean to love God with everything we have?

The sages teach that "with all your heart" means with both our good and evil inclinations — we must serve God even when our desires pull us in other directions. "With all your soul" means even if it costs us our life — we must be willing to give everything for our faith. "With all your might" is often interpreted as "with all your resources" — our money, our talents, our time.

The paragraph continues with instructions to make these teachings central to our lives:

- "Teach them thoroughly to your children" — Pass on the tradition to the next generation
- "Speak of them when you sit in your home and when you walk on the way" — Torah should be part of our daily conversation
- "When you lie down and when you arise" — Remember God morning and night
- "Bind them as a sign on your hand" — This refers to the mitzvah of tefillin (phylacteries)
- "Let them be for frontlets between your eyes" — Also referring to tefillin, worn on the head
- "Write them on the doorposts of your house and upon your gates" — This refers to the mitzvah of mezuzah

These are not poetic metaphors — they are actual commandments that observant Jews fulfill every day. Tefillin are worn during morning prayers, and mezuzot are affixed to doorposts in Jewish homes.

### Second Paragraph: Reward and Responsibility (Deuteronomy 11:13-21)

The second paragraph introduces a crucial concept: our actions have consequences. If the Jewish people follow God's commandments, the land will receive rain in its proper season, crops will grow, and there will be abundance. If we turn away from God and worship other powers, the land will not yield its produce, and we will be exiled from the good land God has given us.

This paragraph speaks directly to the connection between the Jewish people and the Land of Israel. It emphasizes that our residence in the land is conditional — it depends on our spiritual fidelity. For Jews living during the Spring and Autumn Period of Chinese history (春秋, 770-476 BCE), this was not theoretical; it became painfully real with the Babylonian exile in 586 BCE.

The paragraph repeats many of the same instructions as the first: teach these words to your children, speak of them constantly, bind them on your hands and between your eyes, write them on your doorposts. The repetition emphasizes the importance of these practices in creating a life saturated with awareness of God.

### Third Paragraph: Tzitzit and Remembrance (Numbers 15:37-41)

The final paragraph focuses on the mitzvah of tzitzit — the fringes worn on the corners of four-cornered garments. God commands the Jewish people to make these fringes and to look at them as a reminder of all His commandments.

The purpose of tzitzit is stated clearly: "That you may remember and do all My commandments, and be holy to your God." In a world full of temptations and distractions, the tzitzit serve as a physical, visible reminder to live according to God's will. The paragraph warns against following "after your heart and after your eyes, after which you go astray" — acknowledging the human tendency to be led astray by our desires and urging us to remember our higher purpose.

The paragraph concludes by recalling the Exodus from Egypt: "I am the Lord your God, who brought you out of the land of Egypt to be your God." This is a reminder of the historical event that forged the Jewish people into a nation and bound them in an eternal covenant with God. Every generation of Jews is taught to see themselves as if they personally left Egypt — the liberation is not just ancient history, but a living reality that defines Jewish identity.

## A Line Between the Paragraphs

In the traditional liturgy, immediately after the opening line "Shema Yisrael, Adonai Eloheinu, Adonai Echad," we whisper a second line: "Baruch shem kevod malchuto le'olam va'ed" — "Blessed is the Name of His glorious kingdom for all eternity."

Why do we whisper this line? The Talmud teaches that this phrase was said by Jacob's sons when their father, on his deathbed, asked if they all remained faithful to the one God. They responded with this declaration, and ever since, Jews have included it in the Shema — but whispered, because it is not explicitly part of the Torah text. (The one exception is on Yom Kippur, when we recite it aloud.)

## The Shema in Times of Joy and Tragedy

Throughout Jewish history, the Shema has been recited at life's most intense moments.

### Rabbi Akiva's Final Shema

One of the most powerful stories involves Rabbi Akiva, who lived in Israel during the Roman occupation in the 1st-2nd centuries CE (corresponding to China's Han Dynasty, 汉朝). When Roman authorities forbade the teaching of Torah, Rabbi Akiva risked his life to continue running a Jewish school and teaching classes.

When he was eventually arrested, Roman soldiers tortured him to death in an arena, scraping his skin with iron combs. Somehow, even in excruciating agony, Rabbi Akiva managed to recite the Shema with great enthusiasm and even joy. His students, witnessing their teacher's torture, cried out: "How can you pray at a moment like this?"

Rabbi Akiva responded: "All my life I have been troubled by the verse in the Shema that says you should love God 'with all your soul' — meaning even if it costs you your life. I wondered when I would have the opportunity to fulfill this commandment. Now that the moment has come, should I not fulfill it with joy?"

Rabbi Akiva drew out the final word "Echad" (One) until his soul left his body. His death became a model for Jewish martyrdom — facing death with a declaration of faith in the One God.

### The Shema in the Holocaust

During the Holocaust (1939-1945), countless Jews recited the Shema as their final words before being murdered. One remarkable story involves Rabbi Menachem Mendel Taub, the leader of the Kaliv Chassidic group.

Born in Romania in 1923, Rabbi Taub survived the Nazi death camps, but his entire family — including six siblings — were murdered. Rabbi Taub was kept alive only because Josef Mengele subjected him to sadistic medical experiments. Just before the camp was liberated, Nazis were throwing Jewish prisoners into bonfires. Rabbi Taub cried out the Shema and said: "Creator of the World, this might be the last time I will say Shema Yisrael. If You give me life, I promise I will say Shema Yisrael again and again, declaring Your eternity."

He survived. For the rest of his long life (he passed away in 2019 at age 96), Rabbi Taub devoted himself to encouraging Jews around the world to recite the Shema. His legacy continues today.

### Finding Lost Children with the Shema

After World War II, Rabbi Yosef Shlomo Kahaneman (the Ponevezher Rav) traveled through Europe searching for Jewish orphans whose parents had been killed in the Holocaust. Many Jewish children had been hidden in Christian convents and orphanages during the war, and after the war, some priests were reluctant to return them to the Jewish community.

In one orphanage, the priest insisted there were no Jewish children there. Rabbi Kahaneman stood in front of all the children and called out loudly: "Shema Yisrael, Adonai Eloheinu, Adonai Echad!"

Immediately, several young children who had last heard those holy words years before — when their parents tucked them into bed — began crying and calling out "Mama! Mama!" Their unconscious memory of the Shema revealed their Jewish identity and enabled Rabbi Kahaneman to take them to new lives in Israel.

## Cultural Parallel: The Great Learning and the Shema

Chinese philosophical tradition has a concept that resonates with the Shema's call to constant mindfulness. In the Confucian classic "The Great Learning" (大学, Daxue), there is an emphasis on self-cultivation through constant reflection. The text teaches: "The ancients who wished to illustrate illustrious virtue throughout the kingdom first ordered well their own states... first cultivated their persons... first rectified their hearts... first sought to be sincere in their thoughts."

Similarly, the Shema calls for a life of continuous awareness. "You shall speak of them when you sit in your home, while you walk on the way, when you lie down and when you arise" — in other words, make consciousness of God's presence a constant throughout your day. This is not compartmentalized religious practice reserved for temples or special occasions; it is a way of life that transforms every moment.

The parallel extends further: both traditions emphasize teaching the next generation. Just as the Shema commands "teach them thoroughly to your children," Chinese culture has always placed enormous value on filial piety (孝, xiao) and the transmission of wisdom from parent to child. Both traditions understand that the continuity of a people depends on education — not just intellectual knowledge, but moral and spiritual formation.

## Practical Application: How to Begin Saying the Shema

If you are new to Jewish practice, here is how to start:

**1. Learn the Opening Line**

Begin with the first line: "Shema Yisrael, Adonai Eloheinu, Adonai Echad." You can find audio recordings online to learn the proper pronunciation. When you say it, cover your eyes with your right hand to help you focus.

**2. Say It Morning and Night**

Make it a habit to recite this line when you wake up and before you go to sleep. Even if you don't yet recite the full three paragraphs, this opening declaration is the essence of the prayer.

**3. Gradually Add the Full Text**

Once you are comfortable with the opening line, begin learning the three full paragraphs. You can find the complete Hebrew text with English translation in any Jewish prayer book (siddur) or online.

**4. Understand What You Are Saying**

Don't just recite by rote. Read the English translation and reflect on the meaning. The Shema is a declaration of your relationship with God — let it be intentional and heartfelt.

**5. Incorporate It Into Daily Life**

The Shema instructs us to "speak of them when you sit in your home, while you walk on the way." As you go about your day, take moments to remember the core message: there is one God, He created the world, and He has given us a path to live a meaningful life.

## Key Takeaways

- The Shema is the central declaration of Jewish faith, proclaiming that God is One
- It consists of three paragraphs from the Torah (Deuteronomy 6:4-9, Deuteronomy 11:13-21, Numbers 15:37-41)
- Jews are commanded to recite the Shema twice daily — morning and evening
- The Shema expresses core beliefs: God's unity, the obligation to follow commandments, and the connection to the Land of Israel
- Throughout history, Jews have recited the Shema at moments of joy, danger, and martyrdom
- The Shema is not just words — it is a way of life, calling us to constant awareness of God's presence
- Beginning your Jewish journey with the Shema connects you to thousands of years of faith and millions of Jews around the world
