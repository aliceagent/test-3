## Introduction

Three times a day, in synagogues from Beijing to Brooklyn, Jerusalem to Jakarta, Jews recite the same psalm. It's called *Ashrei* (אַשְׁרֵי), and it appears in every single daily prayer service—morning (*Shacharit*), afternoon (*Mincha*), and evening (*Maariv*, during *Uva Letzion*). That's more than any other chapter in the Book of Psalms. In fact, the Talmud makes a remarkable promise: "Anyone who recites *Ashrei* three times a day is assured a place in the World to Come" (Talmud, Berachot 4b). What makes this particular psalm so powerful? Why has it occupied such a central place in Jewish prayer for nearly two thousand years—from the late Second Temple period (corresponding to China's Han Dynasty 汉朝, 206 BCE - 220 CE) through today?

The answer lies in its structure, its content, and its theology. *Ashrei* is Psalm 145, an acrostic poem where each verse begins with a consecutive letter of the Hebrew alphabet—almost. One letter is conspicuously missing, and that absence has puzzled and inspired commentators for centuries. This isn't just a random selection of verses; it's a carefully crafted meditation on God's relationship with the world, combining praise, trust, and gratitude in a pattern designed to shape the soul of the one who recites it.

## The Structure: An Alphabet Prayer (With One Missing Letter)

*Ashrei* is an alphabetic acrostic (*akrostichon* in Greek, similar to the Chinese tradition of *zàng tóu shī* 藏头诗, hidden-head poetry). Each of its 21 verses begins with a consecutive letter of the Hebrew alphabet: *Aleph*, *Bet*, *Gimel*, and so on, all the way through *Tav*. This literary device served a practical purpose in ancient times—it made the psalm easier to memorize in an era before printed prayer books. But it also carries symbolic weight: covering the entire alphabet represents completeness, totality, the full range of human language employed in praising God.

Yet there's a startling gap. The letter *Nun* (נ) is missing. After verse beginning with *Mem* (מ), the psalm skips directly to *Samech* (ס). Why?

The Talmud offers a striking answer: "Why is there no *Nun* in *Ashrei*? Because the *Nun* begins the word for *falling*—*nefilah* (נְפִילָה)" (Berachot 4b). The Talmud connects this to the verse in Amos 5:2: "Fallen (*naflah*, נָפְלָה), no more to rise, is the maiden Israel." David, the psalm's traditional author, deliberately omitted *Nun* to avoid introducing the theme of irreversible collapse into this meditation on God's sustaining power.

But then, the Talmud continues, King David *did* include a promise of rising in the very next verse (the *Samech* verse): "**The Lord upholds all who fall** (*Somech Hashem l'chol hanoflim*, סוֹמֵךְ ה' לְכָל הַנֹּפְלִים), **and raises up all who are bowed down**" (Psalm 145:14). Even when we fall, God lifts us. The missing *Nun* becomes a lesson: there is no permanent fall in the presence of a God who constantly sustains and raises.

## The Opening: Not Part of Psalm 145

Interestingly, when we say *Ashrei* in the prayer service, we don't actually begin with Psalm 145. We start with two earlier verses as an introduction:

1. "**Praiseworthy are those who dwell in Your house; they will yet praise You, Selah**" (Psalm 84:5)  
2. "**Praiseworthy is the people for whom this is so; praiseworthy is the people whose God is the Lord**" (Psalm 144:15)

Only then do we begin Psalm 145 itself, starting with: "**A psalm of praise by David: I will exalt You, my God, the King, and I will bless Your name forever and ever**" (Psalm 145:1).

Why these introductory verses? The word *Ashrei* (אַשְׁרֵי) means "praiseworthy" or "fortunate" or "happy"—it's a state of blessedness. By beginning with *two* verses containing this word, we reach the required *three* mentions of *Ashrei* at the start of the psalm, which the sages considered especially auspicious. This also explains the prayer's name: though it's technically Psalm 145 (*Tehillah l'David*), we call it *Ashrei* after these opening words.

## Themes: God's Kingship, Providence, and Accessibility

### God as King

*Ashrei* begins with kingship and never lets go of it:  
- "I will exalt You, **my God, the King**" (v. 1)  
- "**Your kingdom is a kingdom for all eternities**" (v. 13)  
- "**The Lord is King forever and ever**" (v. 13, though this is a verse from Exodus 15:18 sometimes added)

This is a universal kingship—not just over Israel, but over all creation, all time, all realms. It's a counter-narrative to the earthly empires that dominated Jewish history. When this psalm was being recited in synagogues during the Han Dynasty (汉朝) in China and the Roman Empire in the Mediterranean, both vast political structures claiming eternal dominion, Jews quietly declared that the only true eternal kingdom is God's.

### God's Sustaining Providence

The psalm moves from cosmic kingship to intimate care:  
- "**You open Your hand and satisfy every living thing with favor**" (v. 16)  
- "**The Lord is good to all, and His mercies are upon all His works**" (v. 9)  
- "**The eyes of all look to You with hope, and You give them their food in its proper time**" (v. 15)

This is not a distant, abstract deity. This is a God who feeds birds, who opens a hand to nourish every creature. The Talmud calls verse 16 (*Potei'ach et yadecha*, "You open Your hand") one of the most important in the entire psalm. Some authorities say it should be recited with special concentration (*kavanah*), visualizing God's hand opening to sustain the world at every moment.

There's a fascinating custom: In many communities, when the prayer leader reaches "You open Your hand," the congregation responds aloud. This active participation underscores that we're not just reading about God's provision—we're acknowledging our dependence on it right now, in this moment.

### God's Accessibility

*Ashrei* concludes with a promise that might surprise those who imagine Judaism as a religion of strict law and distant divinity:

"**The Lord is close to all who call upon Him, to all who call upon Him in truth**" (v. 18)

Not close to the righteous only. Not close to the learned only. Close to *all who call*—provided they call *in truth* (*be'emet*, בֶּאֱמֶת). The Hebrew word *emet* means truth, sincerity, authenticity. God is accessible to the honest seeker, regardless of status or knowledge.

The psalm ends: "**My mouth will speak the praise of the Lord, and all flesh will bless His holy name forever and ever**" (v. 21). David's personal praise (*my mouth*) becomes a vision of universal praise (*all flesh*). The individual voice merges into the cosmic chorus.

## Why Three Times a Day?

The Talmud's statement that reciting *Ashrei* three times daily guarantees a share in the World to Come (Berachot 4b) requires unpacking. The sages weren't offering a mechanical formula for salvation—Judaism doesn't work that way. Rather, they were making a psychological and spiritual observation: if you truly recite this psalm three times a day, with attention to its meaning, you will become a different person. You will internalize:

1. **God's sovereignty** (and therefore the relativity of all earthly powers)  
2. **God's providence** (and therefore gratitude rather than anxiety)  
3. **God's accessibility** (and therefore the possibility of relationship)

Recited at morning, afternoon, and evening, *Ashrei* becomes a rhythm that punctuates the day. Before work begins, during the midday break, as night falls—each recitation is a moment to step out of the current of busyness and remember what is constant. The Chinese concept of *sān shí* (三时), the three times of day for contemplation and practice, resonates here. Buddhist tradition also recognizes morning, midday, and evening as spiritually significant moments. *Ashrei* transforms these natural daily transitions into theological anchors.

## The Missing Nun: A Deeper Look

Let's return to the missing *Nun*. The Talmud's explanation—avoiding the word "fall"—is the classical interpretation. But later commentators offered additional layers:

- **Nachmanides (Ramban, 1194-1270, Song Dynasty 宋朝 in China):** The absence teaches humility. Even in a complete, perfect acrostic, there's a gap. Human works are never absolute.  
- **Kabbalistic tradition:** Each letter corresponds to a spiritual reality. *Nun* represents descent (*yeridah*) into materiality. Its omission in *Ashrei* signals that this psalm operates on a plane where such descent is already being redeemed by the following *Samech* verse—God's constant support.  
- **Chassidic interpretation:** The missing *Nun* is an invitation. We are meant to notice the gap, to wonder, to ask. A perfect, seamless psalm might wash over us. The absence creates a pause—a place for personal reflection.

Some prayer books actually include a verse for *Nun* from other psalms, or leave a deliberate blank space with an annotation. This visual reminder says: something is missing, and that's intentional.

## Practical Application: How to Say Ashrei

Knowing the content of *Ashrei* is one thing. Praying it is another. Here are practical suggestions for making this thrice-daily recitation more than rote repetition:

### 1. Slow Down at Key Verses
Don't rush through. Pause especially at:
- **Verse 13 (*Malchut'cha*)**: "Your kingdom is a kingdom for all eternities..." (Reflect: What does God's eternal kingship mean for today's anxieties?)  
- **Verse 16 (*Potei'ach*)**: "You open Your hand..." (Visualize: God's hand opening, sustaining everything, including you right now.)  
- **Verse 18 (*Karov Hashem*)**: "The Lord is close to all who call..." (Ask yourself: Am I calling in truth?)

### 2. Make It Personal
David wrote, "I will exalt You, *my* God." The psalm uses first person. This isn't a corporate script—it's a personal declaration. Try substituting "I" consciously. "**I** will bless Your name." "**My** mouth will speak Your praise." 

### 3. Notice the Acrostic
If you can read Hebrew, watch for the alphabetic progression. When you hit the missing *Nun*, pause. Remember: even in falling, God upholds.

### 4. Use a Translation with Commentary
Many siddurim (prayer books) include brief notes on *Ashrei*. The *ArtScroll Siddur*, *Koren Siddur*, and various online resources provide verse-by-verse explanations. Even reading one commentary point per day deepens engagement.

### 5. Connect It to Your Day
Morning *Ashrei*: Set intention for the day under God's providence.  
Afternoon *Ashrei*: Mid-day reset—remember the bigger picture.  
Evening *Ashrei*: Gratitude review—God opened His hand; what did you receive today?

## The Talmudic Guarantee: What It Really Means

"Anyone who recites *Ashrei* three times a day is assured a place in the World to Come."

This isn't a magic spell. The Talmud immediately qualifies: "Provided one recites it **with proper intent** (*kavanah*)" (Berachot 4b). The Maharal of Prague (1520-1609, Ming Dynasty 明朝 in China) explains that *Ashrei* encapsulates the entire relationship between God and creation. To say it with intent is to affirm:

- God's existence (*Aleph*: "I will exalt You")  
- God's governance (*Malchutcha*: "Your kingdom")  
- God's goodness (*Tov Hashem lakol*: "The Lord is good to all")  
- God's responsiveness (*Karov Hashem*: "The Lord is close")  
- Human purpose (*Tehilat Hashem yedaber pi*: "My mouth will speak God's praise")

A person who genuinely believes and lives these truths is already inhabiting the World to Come in some sense—a life oriented toward the eternal, the good, and the true. The Talmud isn't offering a legal contract; it's making an observation: this is what a soul formed by *Ashrei* looks like.

## Historical Context: From Temple to Diaspora

*Ashrei* (Psalm 145) was likely composed during the First Temple period (957-586 BCE, corresponding to China's Spring and Autumn Period 春秋时代), attributed to King David. But its elevation to a thrice-daily recitation happened later, during the late Second Temple and early Rabbinic period (1st-2nd centuries CE, Han Dynasty 汉朝). 

Why then? After the Second Temple's destruction in 70 CE, the rabbis had to reconstitute Jewish worship without sacrifices, without priests, without the physical center in Jerusalem. Prayer became the new service of the heart. Psalms, originally sung in the Temple by Levites, migrated into the daily liturgy. *Ashrei* became a portable temple—wherever Jews were, three times a day, they could open this gate to the presence of God.

The Talmud (compiled 200-500 CE, corresponding to China's Three Kingdoms and Jin Dynasty periods 三国-晋朝) formalized the practice and added the theological commentary that shaped how *Ashrei* has been understood ever since. By the medieval period (Rashi, 1040-1105 CE, Song Dynasty 宋朝), *Ashrei*'s place in the liturgy was absolutely fixed across all communities—Ashkenazi, Sephardi, Yemenite, Eastern, Western. It's one of the most universal elements of Jewish prayer.

## Cultural Connection: The Power of Repeated Text

The idea of reciting the same sacred text multiple times daily has parallels in other traditions. In Buddhism, particularly Mahayana Buddhism practiced in China, the repetition of sutras and mantras (*niàn jīng* 念经, *niàn zhòu* 念咒) is a core practice. The *Heart Sutra* (*Bōrě Xīnjīng* 般若心经) is recited daily by millions. In Daoist practice, sacred texts are chanted as part of the liturgy (*gōng kè* 功课).

What all these traditions recognize is that repetition is not mere rote. Repetition is *formation*. The texts we return to again and again shape our inner landscape. They become the language our soul speaks when under pressure, in joy, in confusion, in gratitude. *Ashrei* becomes the vocabulary of faith. When you've said "The Lord upholds all who fall" thousands of times, it's there when you fall.

This connects to the Confucian emphasis on ritual (*lǐ* 礼) as formative practice. Confucius taught that repeated enactment of proper forms shapes character. Similarly, *Ashrei* three times daily is a spiritual discipline (*riyāḍah* in Arabic Sufi tradition, *avodah* עבודה in Hebrew) that molds the one who prays it.

## Key Takeaways

- **Ashrei** is Psalm 145, recited three times daily in Jewish prayer services—more than any other psalm.  
- It's an **acrostic poem** covering the Hebrew alphabet, with the letter *Nun* deliberately omitted to avoid the word "fall," immediately followed by a verse about God upholding those who fall.  
- The psalm **combines praise of God's eternal kingship with intimate descriptions of God's providence and accessibility** to all who call in truth.  
- The Talmud promises that **reciting it three times daily with intention leads to the World to Come**—not as magic, but because it shapes a soul oriented toward God.  
- The **thrice-daily rhythm** turns the natural transitions of the day into moments of spiritual recentering, similar to contemplative practices in Buddhist and Daoist traditions.  
- The **missing letter** is an invitation to reflection: even in what seems incomplete, God's sustaining presence is already at work.
